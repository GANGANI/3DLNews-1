PASCAGOULA, Mississippi -- Huffington Post has released a list of

The 1 Weirdest Thing You Never Knew About Your Home State.

Most folks around Jackson County, however, know all about Mississippi's selection: The Phantom Barber of Pascagoula.

More than 70 years ago, Pascagoula was terrorized by a series of break-ins in which an intruder cut the hair of sleeping victims and his prime target was young girls with blonde hair.

William C. Dolan was arrested and convicted of the crimes, but many believe he was innocent.

Book retells chilling story of Pascagoula's 'Phantom Barber'

The Mississippi Press printed the story below on Halloween in 2009.

By Michael Dumas

Staff Reporter

PASCAGOULA -- Many believe that the best scares are those with deep roots in reality. This idea fuels huge box-office returns each year for Hollywood films such as "Saw," "Halloween" and this year's surprise hit, "Paranormal Activity." It inspires groups of people to spend months conjuring up ways to make haunted houses, gory hospitals and graveyards out of back yards, hallways and dusty attics.

But what happens when fright has no season, occurs without need of imagination, and is in every sense real?

Sixty-seven years ago, Pascagoula's 18,000 residents found out as the city was gripped by a terror operating under cover of shadow and slumber. It attacked children in their sleep, causing fathers to stay home on hot nights, joining other adults as they gripped weapons behind heavy eyelids and windows nailed shut.

Mysterious, malicious and thought to be a man "kinda short and kinda stout," the terror's name was whispered by neighbors and shouted by newspaper headlines across the country.

He invaded homes, even a convent, for three weeks in the summer of 1942.

He had residents shooting at strangers in their yards.

He was known as The Phantom Barber, and he might still be creeping through the dark, scissors silently opening and closing, hungry for more hair.

It all started on June 5, when two young girls were awakened at Our Lady of Victories Convent by a figure cutting off chunks of their locks, whispering "Shh!" as he escaped through a nearby window, according to newspaper reports.

Less than a week later, he broke into a residence, removing hair from a young girl sleeping near her twin brother.

Sandy footprint

He cut through window screens and even left a sandy footprint on the young lady's bed, an image that haunted parents and grandparents all across town.

In all, four people would have their hair removed by the Barber, the last a woman who was chloroformed three weeks after the initial attack. It is even thought that the specter of the Phantom Barber inspired a crime spree that also plagued Pascagoula during that time.

Residents were so cautious that, even though wartime production was reaching its height here on the Coast, Ingalls Shipbuilding and the Jackson County Mills were unable to fill their night shifts due to their workers staying home to protect their families.

"It was pretty frightening," said Sandra Moncrief, who was a young child in Pascagoula during the summer of 1942. "My dad worked at the shipyard, and he made this big, old, iron Billy stick, and they would take turns sitting up around the clock because the story was (the Phantom Barber) was cutting the hair off of blond children, and I had blond hair."

That was just a rumor, she said, but it illustrated the level of unrest the attacks caused throughout the community.

"Because everybody was so frightened, being a child of 3 or 4, I really felt that," Moncrief said.

Police arrest Dolan

By August, police had arrested a man they claimed was the Phantom Barber, William A. Dolan, though he was charged with attempted murder for the attack of a young married couple in their sleep. Police had found a bundle of hair near Dolan's Pelham Point residence, which they linked to the third child attacked.

The headlines trumpeted the Barber's capture, and though there were no reports of similar attacks after Dolan's arrest, and subsequent conviction, many in the area thought the police had the wrong man and that the Phantom Barber was still at large. Jackson County Sheriff Guy Krebs even spoke on Dolan's behalf when, five years into his term at Parchman Prison, Governor Fielding L. Wright considered pardoning the New York native.

Coiffeur captured?

So, did the mysterious coiffeur move to another part of the country looking for hair with new and strange accents?

Was he skirting the shadows in towns like St. Petersburg, Fla., Marysville, Ohio, or San Antonio, Texas, where local newspapers reported on Pascagoula's terror?

Was William Dolan the Phantom Barber, after all?

Will you dream tonight of a trim only to wake up to cold, steel shears sliding shut and hair on your cheek?

Only time will tell. Happy Halloween.
