County parolee problem, there’s no easy solution

  • By Jim Clark
  • Nov 19, 2014
  • Nov 19, 2014

There are nearly 400 individuals on parole from the Mississippi Department of Corrections (MDOC) in Lee County. They are out on charges like grand larceny, possession of methamphetamine, selling cocaine, jail escape, and theft of a vehicle.

“I honestly don’t know what you do to not get on probation,” said Lee County Sheriff Jim Johnson. “I don’t know that you can be bad enough not to get on it.”

The decision to release an inmate early to parole is within the complete discretion of the Mississippi State Parole Board. A list of all inmates eligible for parole is generated each month and sent to the Parole Board.

Current parole board members are Steve Pickett, chairman; Kathy Henry, Nehemiah Flowers, Betty Lou Jones and Butch Townsend.

“The parole board decides when a person, who is physically in custody, is released and how they are released,” said Capt. Bryan Hall, who oversees the Lee County Adult Detention Facility. “Probation is someone who is not incarcerated. They’re out there running around and there is a probation officer monitoring them.

There are between three to four probation officers overseeing all of these Lee County parolees.

“If you were operating a nursery, a daycare, with that many kids (400) and three people to watch them. Just watch who would shut you down,” Johnson said. “There’s no way, even with common core math, to calculate three or four watching over 400 and get it to work out.

“I understand it’s about one officer per 150 parolees,” Hall added.

The parole board considers things like amount of time served, crimes committed while incarcerated, escape history and more.

“If you’re in jail and behave, that can waive in your favor of cutting your time down,” Johnson said. “My opinion is - how else are they going to act? They’re locked up in a cage. There’s not going to be any victims because there’s none inside (prison) to get.”

This number is expected to grow as MDOC continues to look for ways to fight overcrowding.

Mississippi’s prison population has grown by 17 percent in the last decade. In July of 2013, Mississippi prisons housed 22,600 inmates. Mississippi now has the second-highest imprisonment rate in the country, trailing only Louisiana

“The only reason I can find for these numbers being so high is the expense of incarceration,” Johnson said. “That’s the only thing I hear is we just can’t afford to house these people.”

The sheriff isn’t positive about what the solution is but knows “the first thing we do when there’s a crime committed is go to this list.”

“All I can tell you is the way I was raised. If I knew for one minute that my Mom or Daddy was going to warn me 40 times before something happened, I’d guarantee you 39 times were coming,” Johnson said. “I was going to play it until I got to 40. The punishment has to meet the crime.”
