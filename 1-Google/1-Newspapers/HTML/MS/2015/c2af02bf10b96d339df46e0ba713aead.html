Kathryn Kelly

The Saltillo gal who made Public Enemy Number One

  • By Jim Clark
  • Dec 22, 2015
  • Dec 22, 2015
  • 0

Behind every good man is a good woman. So does that mean — behind every bad man is a bad woman. In the case of George “Machine Guy” Kelly it certainly was true. It’s doubtful Kelly would have been called Public Enemy Number One by FBI director J. Edgar Hoover if it wasn’t for Kathryn Thorne Kelly.

Cleo Mae Brooks was born in Saltillo to James and Ora (Coleman) Brooks in 1904. In the eighth grade, she decided she didn’t like her given name and called herself “Kathryn.” She thought it sounded more glamourous.

By 15 she married for the first time to a laborer named Lonnie Fry. They had a daughter, Pauline. Shortly after the birth, she divorced him.

She re-married Allie Brewer, but left him before the ink on the wedding certificate was dry.

About this time, her mother, Ora, also divorced and she married Robert K.G. “Boss” Shannon. He owned a ranch near Paradise, Texas where he rented rooms to criminals on the run for $50 a night. Ora took up bootlegging to help the family’s income. Kathryn moved in with them.

Kathryn married a bootlegger Charlie Thorn, trying matrimony for a third time.

Later she found out he was cheating on her. Officers found him dead of a gunshot wound to the head. Even though he was illiterate he left a note saying, “I can’t live without Kathryn. I am departing this life.” A corner’s jury ruled it a suicide.

Over the years she was picked up for shoplifting, robbery, receiving stolen goods and prostitution.

Her family was always close by. Her aunt was a prostitute. Her cousin was a bootlegger and she had two uncles serving time at Leavenworth, one for stealing automobiles and one for counterfeiting.

It was when she was visiting those uncles that she came into contact with George Kelly.

After being released from Leavenworth Kelly began working for bootlegger “Little Steve” Anderson in Oklahoma City. “Little Steve’s” mistress was Kathryn. Sparks flew, Kelly and Kathryn had an affair, and took off stealing Anderson’s new Cadillac and his prized English bulldog.

“I didn’t mind him taking my wife and my car,” Steve would say. “but I wish he’d left that dog.”

They married in 1930. It was Kathryn who couldn’t bear the thought of being hitched to another petty criminal.

She is the one who purchased his first tommy gun at a pawn shop and made him practice with it. She handed out spent cartridges at parties, and started a rumor that Kelly had been an excellent machine gunner during World War I. The truth is he conjured up a medical deferment so he wouldn’t have to serve, and besides that the Thompson sub-machine gun was not used during the first world war.

She said, “He can write his name in lead and knock walnuts off a fence post at 25 paces.”

His strong suite was bank robberies. He even pulled one off in Tupelo on Nov. 30, 1932. It was Citizens State Bank. In fact, that would be his last robbery of any kind.

Kathryn talked him into a kidnapping millionaire Charles Urchel in July 1933. She worked out the intricate details. They got away with the $200,000 ransom. They reportedly buried most of the money. Half of it has never been found.

They had been put on the new FBI’s most wanted list and ended up holed up im Memphis. In September 1933, when agents found the house and entered — Kelly was ready to give up.

“I’ve been waiting for you all night,” he told the FBI.

At first Kathryn found all the attention exciting, posing for newspapers and taking the stand in a black satin dress. But when she realized she was going to do serious time, recanted.

“I don’t want to say anything about that guy Kelly,” she told reporters. “But he got me into this terrible mess and I don’t want anything more to do with him.”
