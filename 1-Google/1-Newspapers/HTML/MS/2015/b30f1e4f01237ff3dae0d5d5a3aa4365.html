Elections, shootings among top Jackson County news stories of 2015

  • Updated: Dec. 31, 2015, 6:30 p.m.|
  • Published: Dec. 31, 2015, 5:30 p.m.
By
  • Warren Kulo | wkulo@al.com

JACKSON COUNTY, Mississippi -- It was a memorable year in Jackson County, to say the least, and for reasons both good and bad.

County elections, multiple shootings -- many domestic-related, government controversies and the Singing River Health System pension saga dominated headlines throughout the year.

Here are the top 10 Jackson County news stories for 2015, as selected by The Mississippi Press/GulfLive staff:

1. County elections -- it was a year of change in Jackson County politics. Many called for the ouster of the entire five-man board of supervisors in the wake of

the SRHS pension troubles, but ultimately only two -- John McKay and Mike Mangum -- would incur the wrath of the voters and lose in their bids for another term. Both were defeated in the Republican primary and were replaced by political newcomer Randy Bosarge and former Gautier Mayor Ken Taylor. Troy Ross, Melton Harris and Barry Cumbest all retained their seats.

The two supervisor seats were not the only high-profile county positions changing hands. Longtime circuit clerk Joe Martin and chancery court clerk Terry Miller had earlier announced their intentions to step down, with Randy Carney and Josh Eldridge, respectively, elected to replace Martin and Miller.

But several familiar names earned another term in office.

Sheriff Mike Ezell, who had won a special election a year earlier to replace Mike Byrd, won again in the regular election to earn his first full term in office. Jackson County school superintendent Barry Amacker retained his post, as did district attorney Tony Lawrence and prosecuting attorney Mark Watts.

All of the county's elected state officials retained their seats, with several running unopposed.

All of the local officials have been sworn in and will take office/begin a new term on Jan. 1.

2. Domestic shootings -- Domestic disputes or disturbances are commonplace occurrences for police, but 2015 saw a definite uptick in the number of such incidents involving shots fired, including several in which one or more people were killed.

Among the more high-profile cases: in March, police found the bodies of Eric and Monda Jewess in their Ocean Springs home in what police ruled a murder-suicide. Police also said Monda Jewess was the apparent shooter -- the first time they could remember such a case where the woman was the shooter.

In another domestic-related case, Zachary Stanford drove from downtown Ocean Springs, where he was employed at a restaurant, to Pearl, Miss., in late June. When he arrived, police say he shot and stabbed to death his grandparents, Robert and Peggy Faries. Stanford was later arrested in Magee, Miss., after being pulled over in a traffic stop. The Ocean Springs resident is awaiting trial on two counts of capital murder.

In August, Vancleave resident Michele Moore shot and killed her husband, Biloxi firefighter Richard Moore, in what police believed was an act of self-defense.

A month later, Chester Jerome Raine shot and killed his wife, Sonja Wells-Raine, inside the WIC food distribution center in Pascagoula, where Wells-Raine worked. Chester Raine turned the gun on himself and later died at Singing River Hospital.

3. Brutal assault -- A September assault on a woman from a prominent Ocean Springs family grabbed the attention of the entire community and many others due to its brazen and brutal nature.

Jalanivich was attacked while at work in the office of an Ocean Springs apartment complex. She told police she was sexually assaulted and the attack left her with multiple injuries, including swelling of the brain, a jaw broken in multiple places, an ear partially torn from her head and numerous cuts and abrasions around her body, according to investigators.

After a massive investigation, police ultimately identified 24-year-old Cameron May of Biloxi as the primary suspect. May was located in Okaloosa County, Fla., on Sept. 21 and taken into custody. He refused to waive his right to an extradition hearing, but through a warrant issued by Mississippi Gov. Phil Bryant and signed by Florida Gov. Rick Scott he was brought back to Ocean Springs.

May made his initial appearance in Ocean Springs Municipal Court on Oct. 28 and Judge Matthew Mestayer ordered May held without bond and the case turned over to the Jackson County Grand Jury.

Jalanivich is the daughter of former Ocean Springs alderman and current Ocean Springs Harbor Master Danny Jalanivich.

4. Search for new Ocean Springs police chief -- When Ocean Springs Police Chief Lionel Cothern announced his retirement in January and aldermen appointed his longtime deputy chief Mark Dunston to the interim post, most believed Dunston's permanent appointment to the job just a formality.

It wasn't that simple. Aldermen opted to open the job up to qualified candidates within the department. Dunston and Captains Terry Harris, Chuck Jackson, Mike Ducote and Derek Hoppner all applied and were deemed qualified by the city's civil service commission.

In a process often tainted by acrimony, four aldermen favored Hoppner, while Mayor Connie Moran and three other aldermen supported Dunston.

After multiple meetings and some closed door deal-making, Dunston was appointed to the chief's position. Two weeks later, Hoppner was made the deputy chief.

5. SRHS pension saga -- The Singing River Health System pension plan controversy, which first came to light in 2014, continued full force throughout 2015, with no final solution in sight.

Among the key developments this year:

In June, special counsel Billy Guice, hired to represent Jackson County's interests in the case, released the findings of an inquiry into the pension fund. The probe found no evidence of "theft, embezzlement or pilfering from fund assets," Guice said, but the examination did find that trustees and administrators fostered an atmosphere of secrecy that kept its pension plan woes hidden from employees and even from the system's own retirement committee.

That same month, a Jackson County Grand Jury also found no evidence of criminal wrongdoing on the part of the health system.

It was also in June that SRHS announced a proposal under which retirees would receive 88 percent of their expected benefits -- a plan summarily rejected by both the county and attorneys representing the retirees.

In August, Special Judge L. Breland Hilburn ruled the pension plan is a debt which must be paid by SRHS, contrary to the health system's claim that SRHS contributions were voluntary. Hilburn, however, did no set an amount for which the system would be liable.

Then, in October, word leaked that Jackson County supervisors had agreed to pony up $13.6 million to SRHS for indigent care and to avoid bond default.

In December, at their final meeting of the year, supervisors signed off on the deal, which includes the appointment of a turnaround firm to oversee health system operations and the resignation of five members of the system's board of trustees.

Guice said after the agreement was signed that the county cannot legally contribute directly to the pension fund, but that county officials are hopeful the contribution will help facilitate the health system begin to make contributions into the pension fund, which is stopped doing in 2008.

Retirees in attendance for the meeting were clearly opposed to the plan, shouting "No!" and "Don't do it!" prior to the supervisors' vote. Mike Mangum was the lone supervisor to vote against the plan.

Attorney Harvey Barton, who represents about 200 SRHS retirees, said his clients were not a party to any agreement between the county and the health system.

Two days later, the SRHS board of trustees ratified the proposal.

6. Gautier/Delta State shootings -- in a mid-September story which captured national attention, Gautier resident and Delta State University instructor Shannon Lamb shot and killed his girlfriend, 41-year-old Amy Prentiss, in the Gautier home the two shared, and then drove to the DSU campus in Cleveland, where he shot and killed fellow professor Ethan Schmidt.

After news of the DSU shooting broke, the campus was placed on lockdown as law enforcement searched for the shooter. No motive for the two slayings was ever found and Lamb would later that night shoot himself in the head as law enforcement closed in on him in a wooded area in Greenville, Miss.

7. JJ Spa bust -- In mid-November, law enforcement agencies conducted a raid on JJ Spa in the St. Martin community after uncovering evidence of prostitution and human trafficking being conducted there.

Jackson County District Attorney Tony Lawrence said the raid came as the result of a year-long investigation, prompted by complaints from citizens. Two people were arrested, including spa manager Yeon Sook "Jackie" Hwang, who was indicted by a federal grand jury on six counts of transporting individuals from at least six other states to Mississippi for the purposes of engaging in prostitution.

During Hwang's arraignment in federal court, an FBI agent testified that nearly $1 million went through the small spa in a two-year period, adding that some 9,000 condoms had been ordered by the business and approximately 50 women had worked as prostitutes there.

U.S. Magistrate Louis Guirola Jr. ruled Hwang posed a flight risk and ordered her held without bond. Her trial date is set for Jan. 4.

8. The prodigal son returns -- About 5,000 adoring fans gathered on Pascagoula beach to welcome home favorite son Jimmy Buffett, who came home for the dedication of a bridge painted and renamed in his honor.

In addition to renaming a small beachfront bridge over Baptiste Bayou in honor of the famed singer, the City of Pascagoula also proclaimed March 6 of every year going forward as "Jimmy Buffett Day" in the city and presented him with a key to the city.

After the presentations, Buffett thrilled the crowd by grabbing a guitar and, accompanied by Coral Reefer Band member and fellow Mississippian Mac McAnally, performed several acoustic songs, including "Pasacagoula Run" and "Son of a Sailor."

9. Psycamore no more --After more than four years, multiple court cases, a U.S. Justice Department investigation and two lawsuits, the City of Ocean Springs finally brought to a close the Psycamore issue.

A controversy which erupted in November 2011 when Ocean Springs aldermen voted to deny a use permit to the Psycamore mental health clinic to operate in a commercially-zoned building near a residential neighborhood came to an end in mid December when the City agreed to settle a lawsuit filed by businessman Roger Applewhite -- who owned the building where Psycamore wanted to locate -- for $107,500. In return, Applewhite agreed to drop his $405,000 suit against the City.

Coupled with the $437,000 settlement with Psycamore principal Dr. Sudhakar Madakasira and the $123,000 in legal fees spent since 2011, the City's final tab for the Psycamore decision comes to $667,500.

10. Close but no cigar -- A pair of runs to the state championship round by two Jackson County high school sports teams were highlighted the sports year in Jackson County.

The football team at Resurrection became just the second prep football team ever in Jackson County to start a season at 13-0, as the Eagles of head coach Scott Sisson captured their first ever south state championship in the process and earned themselves a spot in the Class 1A state title game against fellow unbeaten Hollandale Simmons.

Resurrection came up just short in a bid for its first ever state crown, falling to Simmons 25-9 on the sun-splashed turf at Vaught-Hemmingway Stadium on the Ole Miss campus.

The George County baseball team also played for the Class 5A state championship, but fell to Oxford in the best-of-three series that took place at Trustmark Park in Pearl the home of the Mississippi Braves. It was the first appearance for the Rebels in the state championship on the diamond in two decades.

In basketball, Moss Point's Devin Booker had quite a year. On the collegiate level, the true freshman earned All-SEC honors as he helped lead Kentucky to a number one ranking and an spot in the Final Four. Then, after declaring for the NBA Draft, he was selected with the 13th overall pick in the first round by the Phoenix Suns.

In November, tragedy struck the Jackson County sports community.

About the time Friday night high school football games were wrapping up on Nov. 6, word began to circulate that two of the best ever to don the red and blue of Pascagoula High School were gone.

Keith Joseph Sr., 44, and Keith Joseph Jr., 18, were traveling from Starkville, where the younger Joseph was a freshman on the football team and the elder had starred as a player years earlier, to attend that night's Pascagoula-Gautier game when the vehicle they were riding in left the roadway on Mississippi 57 near Leakesville and overturned several times. Both were pronounced dead at the scene.

The tragedy unleashed an outpouring of grief in both Starkville and Pascagoula, where both men had been star athletes and widely admired for their spirit and attitude.

Services were held on both the MSU campus and in Pascagoula, where father and son were laid to rest.
