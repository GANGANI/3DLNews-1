Editor's Pick

Opinion: Confronting our own shameful newspaper histories

  • Ryan Phillips SDN Editor
  • Feb 23, 2020
  • Feb 23, 2020 Updated Sep 3, 2020
  • Comments
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Print
  • Save

Even before I got to the Golden Triangle, I had an ill-informed assumption our newspapers likely had reputations that were earned well before I was ever born.

Meh, what can you do? Right?

At least that was my initial thought when I came here. Best to leave well enough alone.

Even in the years since I took the helm of our editorial operations, I’ve always tried to move the needle forward and not get bogged down in how things were done before — something those in the community will no doubt attest to, whether they like us or not.

But in doing my most recent research on black history and lynchings in our area for today’s special edition of the paper, I knew there would be numerous difficult and complicated truths for us to face at the Starkville Daily News and Daily Times Leader — even if neither newspapers were the incarnations of the brands they are today.

When I set out to chronicle the history of lynchings in the Golden Triangle, I initially hoped to lean on our precursors for information since so little academic reference material exists with respect to the lynchings in the Golden Triangle. This includes the Starkville News, East Mississippi Times (Starkville) and the West Point Leader as primary sources, but also countless other Mississippi newspapers and outlets from across the country.

I was sorely disappointed and confused starting out looking at our papers, but after further digging, the reality of the situation set in.

Once I had gathered the exact dates of lynchings, I was able to find numerous accounts in bigger papers across the state, but when looking at the corresponding dates in our own newspapers, it was business as usual — John Deere ads and wire stories on the front pages.

In examining the news printed during this time in area newspapers, it was as if the killings never happened. And each story presented in today’s edition was independently verified in a similar way, but most importantly, not from local newspapers.

With an experienced eye, it becomes apparent someone with training and access was sending out detailed information over the wire, which is absolutely jarring to think about in the present.

The two lynchings in Cedarbluff were violent, shrouded in publicly-discussed mystery and reported in cities across the state and southeast. Neither were mentioned in newspapers by correspondents covering the area. However, the information managed to make it out of Clay County and take on narratives on both sides of the ideological spectrum, bringing into question each detail reported.

But every now and then, in the countless newspaper editions I examined, you might find mention of a regional lynching in the three papers papers in Oktibbeha and Clay counties, but the accounts were far from the only ones I examined.

For example, the only mention I could find in a Starkville newspaper of a lynching that occurred in Oktibbeha County came in 1915, when the newspaper published a verbatim account of the double-hanging written by the Chicago Tribune. The Starkville News then says it published the article solely to show the people of the area the “slander” that had been committed by the Tribune and other large papers. Instead of acknowledging the atrocities, the Starkville News instead plays the victim and demands an apology for the coverage.

Sadly, there is no other way to view our own coverage of these incidents than highly suspect when seen through the lens of modern journalism techniques and ethics — and I’m not being pretentious in my speculation, but rather comparing it to other newspapers of the time.

The reports from the newspapers that would become the one you’re reading today used racially-charged language that I’d rather not repeat here (you can see them in the lynching story) and provided vivid firsthand details of lynchings, exercising editorial license to paint the events as chivalrous and justified, while completely bypassing any mention of concrete evidence or due process.

Across the board, newspapers in Mississippi reported details that could have only come from those involved in the mobs, while also publishing damning — and likely fabricated — details of the accusations levied against eventual lynching victims.

We are taught early on in journalism and law today that accusations are just that until proven.

When examining the lynching reports in our papers, the details that are provided about individual incidents — which are limited only to those accounts of lynchings outside of each individual town — are presented as if they are stone-cold facts, written with absolute certainty in an authoritative, and at times, celebratory, voice.

The accounts are completely unsourced and nearly each one makes open admissions that there was no effort given to identify those in the bloodthirsty mobs who committed the acts.

What’s more, in all of the accounts, there was no effort mentioned with regards to a jury trial.

I recently spent half a day at the Mitchell Memorial Library in Special Collections burning through microfilm in a desperate, but failed, attempt to find some kind of coverage of the Cedarbluff lynchings in the West Point Leader - the paper of record in the county and the only potential local resource I had left at that point. For hours I combed through racist ads and divorce notices, and could only drop my head when I hit the year 1917, finally having the realization that we had failed as an institution during one of the most violent and critical times in American history.

We are an accomplice to hate crimes that resulted in incomprehensible violence and it is our duty to acknowledge it when our predecessors wouldn't.

The Starkville News had a far worse public reputation at the time, publishing front page editorials with such titles as “The Negro Problem,” and after looking through their coverage, I was ignorantly laboring under the assumption that maybe the paper just either wasn’t very good or was too busy on the days of either lynching in Cedarbluff.

Surely, there has to be some good, right?

After trying to consider every conceivable possibility of why reports of the incidents, especially the highly-publicized murder of Jeff Brown, which was commemorated on postcards, had not appeared in local papers, I was left with no other conclusion than the bald-face fact that we covered it up, only taking ownership of the events when the integrity of the narrative came into question, as seen with the double-lynching in Starkville in 1915.

I take pride in the numerous connections I have to prestigious institutions, but this revelation honestly broke my heart and momentarily shook my faith in our newspapers.

But then I had another realization — the editors and publishers who printed these stories are likely rolling over in their graves at the fact that a redneck white kid like myself would expose them for fanning the flames of hatred in our communities, leading to senseless violence that was then covered up to prevent bad publicity.

I’m also re-energized in the sense that I think this local discovery can provide a framework for other papers in Mississippi and across the south to face down their own complicated histories.

I implore newspaper editors and my fellow members in the Mississippi Press Association to put their institution’s history under the microscope and try to understand how they each reported our state’s violent and oppressive history.

If nothing else, doing this will provide our communities and the next generation with insight as to how far their communities have come, while not letting the important stories fade away.

The readers of this newspaper also have my sworn promise that as long as I’m the editor, we will work tirelessly to rectify our own history and stand firm on the front lines against oppression in our communities.

No more cover-ups. No more embellishment.

We all deserve more.

Editor's note: This column appears as part of an expansive package published in our edition on Sunday, Feb. 23 as Black History Month comes to a close. To read the exclusive special edition, subscribe to the Starkville Daily News or to our E-Edition.
