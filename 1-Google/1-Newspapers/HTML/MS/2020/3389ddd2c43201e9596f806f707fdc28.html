Alabama-based law firm’s Super Bowl commercial parodies ‘Godfather,’ whacks insurance mascots

  • Updated: Feb. 07, 2020, 2:04 p.m.|
  • Published: Feb. 07, 2020, 12:15 p.m.
By
  • Mark Heim | mheim@al.com

As you know, the Super Bowl is all about the commercials, halftime performance, and, from time to time, football.

While Bill Murray’s re-enactment of ‘Groundhog Day’ in a Jeep commercial won the day during football breaks, viewers in the Mobile and Biloxi markets got a look at one of the best spots of the night.

David J. Maloney of Maloney-Lyons starred in “Godfather of Personal Injury,” a 30-second spot which parodies 'The Godfather’ while poking fun at some of the biggest insurance marketing campaigns out there by gunning them all down in a tip of the cap to 1972 classic starring Marlon Brando and Al Pacino.

In the spot, Maloney declares he has “decided to become Godfather of personal injury.” Over the next 27 seconds, characters resembling Flo from Progressive, the Geico gecko and caveman, Mayhem from Allstate and LiMu Emu of Liberty Mutual all meet untimely deaths.

“I was trying to think what we can do that would be amusing, funny, but a parody that can inject these characters - or lookalikes," said Maloney, who joined Lee Shirvanian and me on “The Opening Kickoff” on WNSP-FM 105.5 on Friday. "‘The Godfather’ was the first one to come to mind, but it seemed rather ambitious.”

It appears the ambition paid off.

Uproxx included the spot among the “good” in its “Good, Bad and Ugly” recap of Super Bowl commercials. In fact, the spot was one of only two “local” commercials to make the list.

“The best Super Bowl commercials every year are the local ones that run in regional markets only, in large part because they’re the only ones having real fun,” Uproxx editor-at-large Brian Grubb wrote.

Maloney, who didn’t want to disclose how much he paid for the airtime, said the production cost of the spot was $25,000, up 40 percent over past projects. The reason, he said, was the parody. The ad mirrored “The Godfather” as close as possible.

“The Super Bowl is the springboard,” he said. “The cool think about this is the comparison video. I anticipated the commercial would have some local appeal, but the comparison video would go viral.”

Every project will have detractors and certainly there were those that didn’t care for the spot, he said. But, overall, the spot was a hit.

“Once you took it out of the context of the game and everybody started seeing it online, the feedback has been like 99.5 percent positive.”

Maloney screened it for a 15-person focus group in Mobile three weeks before the Super Bowl, along with three other “conservative” ads.

“They all laughed,” he said. “They all felt like it was a little over the top for viewing but perfect for the Super Bowl and perfect for late-night viewing.”

The spot also got some attention from Above the Law, a web site that focuses on the “world of law.”

“I don’t want to say it was surprising, but it was kind of surprising that the ad itself got national attention,” Maloney said.

Leave the gun, take the cannoli and the 30-second spot.
