San Diego build quality praised on eve of christening at Northrop Grumman in Pascagoula

  • Updated: Jun. 12, 2010, 11:19 a.m.|
  • Published: Jun. 12, 2010, 10:19 a.m.
By
  • Harlan Kirgan

PASCAGOULA, Miss. -- The workmanship quality of the San Diego was praised by the shipbuilder and a former Secretary of the Navy on the eve of the ship's christening at 10 a.m. today at the Northrop Grumman Shipbuilding's Pascagoula shipyard.

View full sizeHarlan Kirgan/Mississippi PressThe LPD 22 will be christened as the San Diego today at the Northrop Grumman Shipbuilding shipyard in Pascagoula. A pre-christening news event was held at the shipyard Friday. From left, are former Secretary of the Navy Donald Winter, the ship's sponsor Linda Winter, the prospective commanding officer Cmdr. John Haydel and Northrop Grumman Shipbuilding President Michael Petters. The San Diego is about 77 percent complete and is scheduled to be delivered in the third quarter of 2011.

The 684-foot-long LPD 22 is the sixth of the San Antonio-class amphibious transport dock ships.

Michael Petters, president of Northrop Grumman Shipbuilding, said the ship demonstrates the advantages of building the same ship "again and again."

"We call that serial production, the opportunity to go and have our craftsmen move to the next ship and do it again," said Petters during a pre-christening media event at the shipyard Friday. "It creates efficiencies. It gives them the opportunity to say, 'Hey, we've got a better way to do this,' and that becomes part of our build plan."

Former Navy Secretary Donald Winter's wife, Linda, is the San Diego's sponsor.

Winter served from January 2006 to March 2009 as Navy secretary.

"I have been living with the development of this ship class and deployment for at least four years," said Winter. "I will say it has come a long way."

Winter said the Pascagoula shipyard "really is starting to figure out how to build this ship in a good, efficient and effective manner."

The first of the class, the USS San Antonio, was plagued with problems. In 2008, Winter ordered the San Antonio docked in Bahrain because of leak problems.

The ship's construction began at the Avondale, La., shipyard and was continued in Pascagoula where it rode out Hurricane Katrina in 2005.

Winter said he toured the San Diego about six weeks ago and saw a difference. "You get a good sense of the quality of work," he said.

The ship's prospective commanding officer, Cmdr. Jon Haydel, served as executive officer on the USS San Antonio and was on the ship when it rode out Katrina in the Pascagoula shipyard.

"The only true effect was it proved the seaworthiness and durability of this platform," he said of the Katrina experience.

"This class is transformational, and some growing pains are expected," he said. The San Diego is "far ahead of the previous five ships in her class in both quality and the time it has taken to construct."

Ryane Rawls of Wade, sheet metal foreman, said the shipbuilders "knew what we were up against when we started."

The lessons learned from building previous San Antonio-class ships resulted in better schedules, said Rawls. "There are more things in sequence to not lock out our other crafts. We are having a better-quality product."

Rawls is one of about 1,600 people who have worked on the ship, said Bill Glenn, a shipyard spokesman. There are about 12,000 people employed at the Pascagoula shipyard, he said.

Linda Winter said that, through her husband's tenure as Navy secretary, they feel that they are part of the Navy.

"Being the sponsor of the San Diego, I feel in touch with the Navy, I feel in touch with the Marines and I know the shipbuilder part of it," she said.

The role of the sponsor is to help build the spirit of the ship, she said.

"The other thing is, we support the crew," she added.

The ship will have a crew of 360 and is capable of transporting about 800 Marines, four CH-46 Sea Knight helicopters, two tilt-rotor aircraft and landing craft.
