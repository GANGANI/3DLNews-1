George County leaders say no to allowing alcohol sales

  • Updated: Apr. 06, 2012, 9:38 p.m.|
  • Published: Apr. 06, 2012, 8:38 p.m.
By
  • Beverly Tuskan, The Mississippi Press
View full size

LUCEDALE, Mississippi -- Though lawmakers are pushing to give cities in Mississippi's dry counties a say on allowing alcohol sales, city and county leaders don't see it happening in George County.

House members voted earlier this week 67-52 for Senate Bill 2497, which would allow cities of 5,000 or more people in dry counties to have elections on the sale of beer, wine and liquor.

The elections would be called when 20 percent of a city's voters presented a petition.

Of Mississippi's 82 counties, 27 are dry and 10 are partially dry, according to the state Department of Revenue.

Lucedale Mayor Doug Lee is not too concerned about the bill.

"We are a dry city," Lee said. "I don't anticipate it ever becoming a wet city. It was tried, I'll say, in the 1990s and that's the last time the issue was brought up, and it failed then."

The mayor said if he was remembering correctly, the threshold for the city at that time was 15 percent of voters needed for a petition.

"I think that's what prompted the referendum, was the fact that the 1990 Census said we were about 2,564 people. We just made it over the threshold in order to petition for a referendum. Of course, it failed then," he said.

"I think now we're somewhere around 3,310, if they bump that number up to 20 percent and 5,000 people, it wouldn't pass."

The mayor is OK with his city being in a dry county.

"As for me, personally, I've got the willpower to walk by it," he said.

But from a business standpoint, it could possibly help with economic growth, he added.

There were previous opportunities to bring in steakhouses and chain restaurants, but once they discovered the county was dry, "they backed off," Lee said.

"But we have our strong points. We have our natural resources, our rivers, hunting and fishing, that type of stuff. We have a lot to offer without the alcohol."

But should George County become wet, then it's left up to the officials of the city or the county to regulate it, he said.

District 2 Supervisor and Board President Kelly Wright sees the bill as a push for lawmakers to help the folks who are wanting to get alcohol into these dry counties to have a better chance of getting it passed.

"We already have a way and a means of that happening if the majority of the folks want it done," Wright said. "As for George County, it's been tried several times and never been successful."

"Sounds to me like the state legislators up there are trying to go at another route to make it easier to have it happen down here, when they probably ought to focus more on some hotter issues in the state that'll save the taxpayers some money, rather than to get their nose into alcohol being sold in a dry county," Wright said.

He said he met with Perry Lee, R-Mendenhall, the Senate committee chairman of local and private, on Wednesday and that they "won't let George County get a bill out to let the people of George County vote on whether they want a sports complex or not. But now they're talking about supporting something up there to make it easier to sell alcohol in dry counties. Sounds like to me their focus is in the wrong place."

County supervisors will neither vote to allow alcohol sales, nor will they get the opportunity to vote, Wright said.

"That will be a majority of the people's vote, but the board's going to stand with the people around here. We're a dry county, a mostly Christian community, and I think George County's a long way from having a majority of people here vote for something like that."
