The news, the people, the community of 2023

  • By John McBryde • Content Manager and Sarah White • Staff Writer
  • Jan 3, 2024
  • Jan 3, 2024 Updated Jan 16, 2024

A certain election, a new venue for entertainment and a couple’s efforts to save an iconic building in the heart of Franklin. These are just three of the stories that staff at the Williamson Herald covered in 2023, which has now given way to expectations for 2024.

Still, before we head into the unknown of a new year, there were enough stories from the previous 52 weeks to justify our making a list. And with the possible exception of the lead-off item, these are in no certain order.

Humans compiled this list with no assistance from AI, so please let us know if you feel we’ve missed stories that should have been included. Email us at contact@williamsonherald.com.  

An election like none other

Through a campaign season that was controversial and at times was dotted with bizarre twists and turns, Franklin Mayor Ken Moore defeated challenger Gabrielle Hanson by a whopping margin of 12,822 votes to 3,322 in the Oct. 24 municipal election. 

Hanson, former alderman at large, touted herself as a savior of sorts for a city that’s “beginning to crumble,” but voters showed in record numbers that would not be the case.

Also winning easily were Alderman-at-Large Brandy Blanton, who defeated Gary Moore, and Alderman-at-Large Ann Petersen over Jeff Feldman. Greg Caesar takes over the alderman-at-large seat vacated by Hanson by beating Patrick George and BK Muvvala. Clyde Barnhill ran unopposed for his alderman-at-large seat.

The campaign drew national attention for its Movie of the Week scenarios, especially when Hanson refused to disavow the friendly support she was granted by neo-Nazis who appeared at a campaign forum.

But through it all, a large majority of voters agreed that Franklin is doing fine and is in no need of a takeover. 

“I think ideally people need to understand all the good things going on in Franklin,” Petersen said on election night.  “I think, based on the results, the voters have seen what good things are going on. I wanted to focus my campaign on this, not the negative.” 

Hanson’s antics

They began last spring when then-Alderman-at-Large Gabrielle Hanson appeared on a podcast and claimed she knew the motive behind the March 27 shooting at The Covenant School in Nashville that left six people dead. And for the next several months leading up to her running as Franklin mayor at the October municipal election, there were other Hanson incidents that ranged from head-scratchers to her facing an ethics committee that eventually recommended censure.

Hanson sent a letter to Nashville International Airport President Doug Kreulen that basically lambasted him for providing monetary support to the Franklin Justice and Equity Coalition in its role as host of the Juneteenth festival in downtown Franklin. She admitted in a video on her campaign Facebook and Instagram pages that she was arrested in Dallas in the mid-1990s for promoting prostitution. 

And then her most infamous move was her association with self-identified Nazis who showed up at a campaign forum to support her. Though she had stated through social media she had nothing to do with “any white supremacy or Nazi-affiliated group,” she later said, “I am not going to denounce anybody their right to be whatever it is that they want to be whether I agree with what they do in their personal life or not.”

A ‘Bedford Falls moment’ as Five Points post office reopens

After months of work returning the Franklin Five Points post office to its former glory, new owners Julie and Matt Brown reopened the location in December. 

The pair hosted a ribbon cutting that featured speakers from the community and a large turnout of customers of the iconic facility in downtown Franklin. The event was a particularly emotional one for Julie Brown, who will lead business operations.

“When we walked out, and you all were here clapping and cheering, it was my Bedford Falls moment,” she said, referencing the Christmas classic movie “It’s a Wonderful Life.” “This is my snow globe. This is why I wanted this post office, not for the glory but because of this community. You all are amazing.”

New to the post office is a general store with miscellaneous items for purchase, such as snacks, drinks, custom apparel and small necessities. Post office lobby hours will remain 8:30 a.m. to 5 p.m. Monday-Friday and Saturday 8:30 a.m. to 1 p.m.  Stamps, shipping, metered mail and post office boxes are still available at the location. Four sizes of boxes are available. They can be rented for up to a year, and payments remitted online for customer convenience.

“We really wanted to turn this back into a place where the community wanted to come,” Matt Brown said.

Pride festival exceeds expectations crowd-wise

Franklin Pride returned in June for its third year at The Park at Harlinsdale Farm with more than 6,500 attendees, the festival’s biggest crowd yet. 

The Franklin Pride Festival is a free, community-led and family-friendly event designed to celebrate, support, engage and unite the LGBTQ+ community in the greater Franklin area, according to a press release from the organization. 

At a meeting in April, the Franklin Board of Mayor and Alderman granted the event a permit with a tiebreaking vote from Franklin Mayor Ken Moore. Locals voiced support for and outrage at the festival during public comment sessions leading up to the meeting. The polarization received national media attention. 

“Pride events and LGBTQ events are inherently sexual by definition,” said Jack Mooring, a pastor at Celebration of Life Church in Franklin. “This makes this event completely different than the vast majority of events that would apply for permits

Robert McNamara, Franklin Pride’s former and founding president, was ecstatic about the turnout considering the challenges he encountered while making the event a reality.

“It was a struggle getting here,” he said. “We didn’t know if we were going to be here or not. We love it that we are. A ton of people have shown up for us, and it’s amazing.”

Studio Tenn finds a home at Turner Theater

The beloved regional theater company, Studio Tenn, moved into its new permanent home at the Turner Theater at The Factory this year. The company broke in the new space with the production of “Million Dollar Quartet” in October. 

The shift to a purpose-built theater, complete with box office and beautiful marquee, was a welcome relief for the company that spent years performing on make-shift stages and moving between theaters. Now, Studio Tenn and the developers of the Factory hope their partnership will add to the quality of life in Williamson County. 

Middle Eight gets go-ahead

The Middle Eight, a multifamily project led by 30-year Franklin residents Toby and Amanda McKeehan and their partner, Charles Rulick, completed its final hurdle in November when the city’s Historic Zoning Commission approved architectural design of the development’s combination of single-family homes, townhomes, duplexes and apartments. 

It culminated a nearly two-year process for the proposed development located in the emerging Factory District walkable to downtown Franklin.

The McKeehans and Rulick of Truland Development Company are developing the 7.1-acre property behind the McKeehan’s historic home on the corner of Franklin Road and Liberty Pike directly across the street from The Factory at Franklin. The Middle Eight will provide 275 housing units to Franklin’s emerging Factory District. With an anticipated groundbreaking in January 2025, The Middle Eight is expected to have a project cost of more than $100 million. 

Residents and neighbors near the development were largely divided on whether it was a gain for the community. 

Some argued that the Middle Eight will increase traffic, that it’s construction threatens the last green corridor leading into Franklin and that it will negatively affect the character of nearby areas. 

Others argued that the Middle Eight will create much needed diverse housing in Franklin, attract diverse people and professions and support nearby small businesses.

Franklin’s amplification ordinance amended

At a meeting in March, the Franklin Board of Mayor and Aldermen voted 7-1 to amend an ordinance limiting public gatherings and amplification on Franklin’s Main Street.

Over the previous months, aldermen heard arguments from participants of a Christian worship service regularly held Saturday nights at Franklin’s square that plays amplified music.

Under the ordinance, the definition of a public gathering and expression event included events with amplification, or that occurred more than once per month if over 20 people attended.

It read that “no permits shall be granted for any Public Gathering and Expression Event after dark any place within the City. In addition, no permits shall be granted within the downtown historic district on weekend evenings/early mornings, Friday at 5 p.m. through Saturday at 7 a.m. and Saturday at 5 p.m. through Sunday at 7 a.m.”

Now amended, the ordinance allows amplification from 9 a.m. to 9 p.m. daily as long as what is amplified cannot be heard beyond 50 feet. It also grants permits for amplification after 9 p.m. and generally prohibits events from 9 p.m. to 6 a.m. instead of any time “after dark” except in some cases.

Multiple events held per month would not require a permit, but “no public gathering or expression event shall take place during a permitted special event in the permitted space,” the item report reads.

Tennis, anyone? 

After the Brentwood YMCA announced its closure, city officials and local tennis enthusiasts began looking for options to replace the dozens of tennis and pickleball courts that will eventually disappear with the Y. 

The Brentwood Board of Commissioners was split over the possibility of a partnership with the county to create a new tennis facility. Heated debates led the commission to delay a vote on the partnership and create an ad hoc committee to research options. 

Residents can expect more discussions and public hearings on the committee’s findings in 2024. 

New WCS school named after longtime Black educator opens

The Williamson County Board of Education voted unanimously in February to name one of the district’s newest schools after a Black educator who had a profound impact on education in the county.

Amanda H. North was the last African-American principal at Thompson’s Station School and a longtime Franklin resident, and the school named after her opened in September on Wilkes Lane in Spring Hill. North began her teaching career in 1934 in a one-room schoolhouse and, after rising through the ranks to become a principal for WCS, retired in the early 1970s serving Evergreen School. North died in 2011 at 98 and is survived by daughters Maggie Brown and Dorothy Jackson, who taught for over 40 years.

Also opening in September was Arrington Elementary School.

Plans for new Franklin City Hall get green light

Through a tiebreaker that came from Mayor Ken Moore, the Franklin Board of Mayor and Alderman passed a resolution authorizing the completion of the new City Hall’s schematic design on its existing site on Third Avenue adjacent to the town square.

The resolution, which saw a 4-4 tie among aldermen at a meeting in May, “solidifies the direction” for a new City Hall on the site and authorizes work toward completion of its design with periodic check-in points with BOMA.

It also allows city staff to secure the services of an owner’s representative to assist with value engineering through the remainder of the design phase and the finalization of construction drawings, and lets staff obtain two independent construction cost estimates to be provided to BOMA when design is completed. 

Westhaven Indigenous Site

An indigenous landmark hidden on the outskirts of the Westhaven golf course received permanent protection with the signing of a conservation easement. Decades of exploring and excavating artifacts have left two mound sites from the Woodlands era in need of conservation. 

Westhaven developers and representatives from Tennessee archeology groups and Native American organizations gathered at the site on Indigenous People’s Day to commemorate the moment in history.
