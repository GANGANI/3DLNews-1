Bringing magic to the classroom

  • By Abbey Nutter anutter@lebanondemocrat.com
  • Feb 10, 2024
  • Feb 10, 2024

The Lebanon Democrat is beginning the process of running question-and-answer profiles of the Wilson County educators who have been selected as the teacher of the year in their respective schools.

Those individuals, from the Wilson County School System and the Lebanon Special School District (in addition to two of the county’s private schools), are in contention for the Wilson County Teacher of the Year Award, which will be announced later this spring.

The series of profiles continues with a glance at Madison Lien, a Exceptional Education Kindergarten and 2nd grade ELA and Math teacher at West Elementary School...

Name: Madison Lien

School: West Elementary School

Age: 38

What grade/subject do you teach (of course, this will vary depending on whether you teach at the elementary, middle school or high school level)?

Exceptional Education Kindergarten and 2nd grade (ELA and Math, but I also am the case manager for all Exceptional Education students in these grades).

How long have you been in education (total years)? 14

How many years have you taught at your current school? 8

What other schools have you taught at prior to your current school? Carroll-Oakland Elementary

What is something unique about you — whether it’s a hobby, skill or past accomplishment — that most people likely wouldn’t be aware of?

I am the crazy Harry Potter teacher at our school, as it is a huge obsession of mine. Over the years, I have been slowly trying to transform my room to look as though it’s from Hogwarts, including my room number being changed to 9 ¾.

What do you enjoy doing in your free time (hobbies, etc.)?

Outside of school, I enjoy crafting, baking, spending time with my husband and two boys, and watching movies. I’m also a fan of cringe-worthy puns and love a good school dress-up day! Those closest to me would tell you if you can’t find me at school, I am probably either at Burn Boot Camp or in the aisles of ALDI.

Is there anything unique about your teaching situation that you’d like to detail?

I am fortunate to be part of a school team that I truly feel stands above the rest in our ability to enact change and progress with our exceptional education students.

How would you describe your teaching style?

I set high expectations for my students, no matter what their abilities are. My students know that they are expected to do their best, whatever that looks like for them. That being said, I am also not afraid to be silly, over-the-top, and “extra” to make my kids laugh and be engaged in what we are doing.

Could you share a couple of strategies for how to keep students engaged and motivated?

As stated above, you pretty much have to leave your pride at the door! In all seriousness, kids don’t want to learn anything from someone that they don’t like or that they think doesn’t like them, and they can sense the authenticity of how you feel about them. If you are excited to teach, and truly value them, that energy can be the best motivator. I also really like to draw and like to incorporate student interests into individualized drawing and things for them to work for so they can value the personal aspect of something uniquely made for them.

Have you ever encountered a challenge in teaching that required you to rethink your teaching methods and/or approach?

Every year. Every Month. Every Day. With my students, challenging behaviors and learning disabilities are unique to each student. What works one day likely won’t work the next. However, there is great validation and growth in being able to accept that challenge every day and grow right alongside my students as we figure out together how to move forward in the best possible ways, both behaviorally and academically.

What is different, unique and/or enjoyable about the school that you are currently teaching at?

The best things about my school are my people. The kids that make the job interesting and challenging and hilarious every day. My Ex. Ed. team is truly that — a team. We support each other, day in and day out, to do the best we can for our students. My gen ed teachers are unmatched in their ability to jump in and collaborate, include, celebrate, and pivot at a moment’s notice. Lastly, I’m incredibly fortunate enough to have parents that are engaged in the process and approach our plans as a collaborative, respectful team.

Why did you choose teaching as a career path?

As the daughter of a special education teacher of 42 years, this was honestly the last job I thought I would have. However, in the interim from undergraduate to graduate school, I subbed and worked in a pre-K classroom focused primarily for students’ with more severe disabilities and learning deficits. I fell in love with it and never looked back.

What is the most fulfilling part of teaching?

Honestly, feeling like I actually make a positive impact in another person’s life. Whether that is a student, a fellow teacher, or a parent, the moments where you can really see a change are immeasurably moving.

What is the most challenging part of teaching?

Finding the time to do everything you need and want to do while also maintaining the balance between home life and school life.

How has your view of teaching changed since you first embarked on your teaching career?

I feel like I have more of an understanding of just how valuable each and every year is and what limited time we have to make an impact. This is also probably, in large part, to the fact that I am also now a parent as well as a teacher.

How have you seen the profession change over the course of your career, and how do you see it continuing to evolve going forward?

Each year, I feel like more and more is asked of teachers because we will rise to the occasion and do what is best for our students, regardless of the ask. While that is undoubtedly an incredible descriptor overall for a profession and one I am proud to adhere to, I also feel like it continues to be undervalued by society in general. I would hope moving forward that teachers are valued at the level of educated professionals that they actually are.

Could you share what has been one of your most memorable moments in teaching?

Honestly, there are too many to count, but that is probably because I have a different measure of what is memorable to me. Countless little moments of watching student after student achieve something that was thought impossible, or that they (or their families) were told they would never do. I have been lucky enough to witness these incredible moments time and again over the last 14 years, and each is just as special as the last.

What is the most meaningful thing a student could say to you?

“Look, I did it!”

How would you ideally like to be characterized or remembered as a teacher?

If I am fortunate enough to have made an impact enough to be remembered at all, that’s enough for me. I want to be remembered as a teacher who showed a student that school CAN be a place where they can succeed.
