Traffic stop leads to seizure of fentanyl, cocaine, cash

  • Aug 1, 2023
  • Aug 1, 2023
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Print
  • Save

A traffic stop recently led Livingston Police to seize a large amount of drugs and cash.

“This is one of the largest drug seizures that has taken place in quite a while,” Livingston Police Chief Ray Smith said.

For several months, LPD had been investigating drug trafficking in and around the Town of Livingston, and this investigation had been initiated by several days of surveillance, along with traffic stops, suspect interviews, and undercover operations.

While conducting surveillance on Robert Mckay, detectives with Livingston Police Department contacted Overton County Sheriff’s Department’s K-9 handler Deputy Caleb Young to assist them with a traffic stop. During the stop, K-9 Figo gave a positive indication to the presence of illegal narcotics.

After the positive indication, Sgt. Terry Frady of OCSD allegedly recovered a black plastic bag containing approximately 1.3 pounds of methamphetamine, 1.7 ounces of fentanyl, 1.5 ounces of cocaine, several illegally obtained prescription narcotics, and 10 ecstasy pills.

Along with the illegal narcotics were cash, scales, and multiple zip-lock baggies, according to authorities, who noted that all are items commonly used in the distribution and sale of illegal narcotics.

As the epidemic of fentanyl in the area increases, Chief Smith promised that he and his department “will work diligently to protect the citizens of Livingston against anyone that attempts to distribute this drug or any other illegal narcotic in our community.

“The DEA recognizes that as little as 2 milligrams of fentanyl can be fatal. Fentanyl itself can be absorbed through the skin, and some forms of this drug can be colorful (as is shown in the photo). This makes the drug even more dangerous to children because of the colors.

“So, what appears to be a seemingly harmless pink or blue powder could easily cause a deadly overdose to a child.”

Transporting and handling these types of drugs are dangerous to not only citizens but also to the officers involved in working these cases, according to Chief Smith.

“An exposure to just a small amount could potentially cause death to the officer or to whoever is transporting,” he said.

LPD takes several precautions to try to protect themselves when dealing with drugs of this nature.

“But what worries our officers the most are the individuals that, in an attempt to hide or destroy the drugs in order to get away from law enforcement, may try to throw out the drugs or evidence when being pulled over in our community,” Chief Smith said. “This is when a citizen or child could find it and become exposed, which could possibly cause an overdose and even death.”

Chief Smith encourages all citizens who see any type of suspicious substances or packages to call the Police Department.

“Let our officers come and identify and collect these dangerous materials,” he said.

Chief Smith stated that he is thankful for the citizens of Livingston who call in their concerns on a daily basis.

“All tips are welcomed and can remain anonymous,” Chief Smith said.
