Dr. Royal Was From A Better Era - And Response (2)

  • Sunday, December 28, 2008

Perhaps it is because I am getting old myself now, but I am spending a lot of time reflecting on some "good old days." It also could be that with all of the negative reports on our society, economy and humanity (or lack of it), I am reminded of a time when I personally knew the world to be a better place than many know today. As I have been catching up on headlines and local news after being absorbed by the holiday season, I saw the local obituary for Dr. James Richard Royal, who is retired from the Brackett-Royal Clinic that used to be at the intersection of Spring Creek Road and Brainerd Road.

Doctors Brackett and Royal were my family doctors growing up. They were my personal doctors from the time I was born until they retired. They diagnosed many cases of strep throat and mononucleosis, wrapped up many sprains, sewed up many contusions and even set many many broken bones for me and members of my family. I am the youngest of nine children, so I know we had at least a page in their client list.

I recall that the summer before I was six years old I was running to our station wagon as we were leaving my grandparents' driveway. I tripped on the sidewalk and fell, headfirst, into the open car door, immediately gashing open my forehead. We were to leave in the wee hours of the next morning for our annual vacation. Dr. Royal met us at the office and put 21 stitches in my head as my entire family sat in the waiting room, cringing with every blood-curling scream. It's a memory that we all share to this day.

Many times I entered that waiting room over the years. There was a smell of sterility - alcohol and other cleansers, I assume - in the air. It was a distinct scent unique only to the Brackett-Royal Clinic waiting room. The excellent care they took with each and every patient who entered their doors was a testimony to not only the fact that both of these doctors were born and raised to be complete gentlemen and men of faith, but that health care in that day and age was still such a noble profession. These men 'were' doctors, they weren't just "practicing" medicine.

But more than the tremendous care they took with us - much of it after hours or with house calls - was the relationship that developed over the years. They men were not only prominent physicians in our community, they were our friends. When my father passed away 12 years ago, both of these men took time to personally express their condolences.

While I have no doubt that Dr. Royal is in a much better place now, this world and our community isn't better. The days of old hold many memories of going to the doctor, then stopping off at Lott's to get a sundae (especially if I had gotten a shot!), picking up a new set of guitar strings at Al Miller's, shopping for a Christmas present or even, eventually, an engagement ring at Rone Regency in Brainerd Village, going to a movie at the Showcase Cinema, listening to the barbershop quartet at Shakee's Pizza, canoeing through Eastgate Mall after the flood waters rose out of control... oh, I could go on and on... so many good memories. So many relationships...

If we really want America to be a better place, we need to rely less on Uncle Sam sending us stimulus checks and work harder to have relationships with the people around us. For better and for worse, our community is worth saving. And I believe that there are good people who want to reclaim that type of heritage and build a brighter future for our own children and grandchildren to enjoy. Too often we're too busy just surviving. We can thrive if we adapt an attitude of developing deeper community relationships again... so that era's like this do not have to end.

Joel Davenport
jdr1ru@gmail.com

I too remember Bracket and Royal. I was just a little boy living across the street from them both (they were neighbors) and got to know them by just being a nosy little boy. I knew Dr Brackett better as he would let me ride on his old Ford tractor. I always had a million questions for him as I learned all about this tractor that I was so impressed with. I would even help Sissy Bracket plant strawberry plants in the garden. Wow what fun I had in during those times.

Like Mr. Davenport I too can go on and on about the memories of childhood and the way lives were lived then. I remember Butch's Ham and Egger in East Brainerd the Piggly Wiggly and the Food King. May both Drs Bracket and Royal rest in a much better place now and know they are fondly remembered.

John Thatcher
JLThatcher@code3pse.com

I too, was sad to hear of the passing of Dr. Royal. My family were patients of Dr.'s Royal and Brackett since the early 1960's, until I moved away after college. I remember Dr. Royal coming to the office early on a Sunday morning after I awoke swollen and with rashes all over my body - it turned out I just had a very bad case of poison ivy, acquired from standing in the path of smoke from burning leaves that had poison ivy mixed in with it. With all their patients, Doctors Royal and Brackett must have spent most weekends in the office, handling one emergency or another.

Now, if we need a few stiches, an ingrown toenail corrected, a broken limb set and cast, or some other such problem, we are instructed to proceed to the emergency room of the local hospital, where they will take care of it after a wait of a few hours and considerable expense. But at the Bracket-Royal Clinic, such ailments were treated routinely with a minimum of fuss or bother.

My condolences to his family.

Rick Pierson
Everett, WA
rhp6033@hotmail.com
