Hixson High In The News Again - And Response

  • Tuesday, April 22, 2008

More problems in the school. Why? Is it a mindset that nothing will be done? Below is what troubles most parents and people.

"The teacher told police that "when he noticed something was going on, he stopped it and sent (female student) to the nurse."

The incident was reported the "next day," and Asst. Principals Patty Lane and Chris Earl began taking statements.

This action by the teacher should never be excused. Why wasn't it reported as soon as the teacher noticed something was wrong? Apparently many students feel they run the schools and not the educators and the staff seems to go along with it. Where are the teachers and students demanding that this type of conduct and oversight be dealt with a strong lesson of accountability?

If I were superintendent these students would be gone and so would the teacher. It is very plain that none should be in the school. The charge should be attempted rape and the teacher for failing to report the crime when it happened. Maybe Hixson High needs new top leadership as they seem not to be up to the task.

N.D. Kennedy Sr.
Ooltewah

* * *

I am a 1978 graduate of Hixson High School. Thirty years ago our principal was B.E. Edwards. Mr. Edwards was a white-haired, barrel-chested man who was reported to be a graduate of U.S. Marine Corps training. He was a very imposing and intimidating figure who could stop any student in his tracks just by glaring at them. Assisting him were two capable lieutenants: Dr. Tom McCullough, now headmaster at David Brainerd, and Gerald Bailey.

Notre Dame was an educational option for us, but my sister and I were only a year apart, so my parents couldn't afford for both of us to attend that school financially. We were hardly short changed, however, as Hixson was chosen as a National School of Excellence while we were there. My fellow classmates have gone on to distinguish themselves in a tremendous variety of disciplines.

Mr. Edwards had a saying: "This is (or is not) in keeping with the way we do things here at Hixson." He used this phrase often when he referred to his hard and fast disciplinary rules as well as a proudly acknowledging our academic and athletic achievement. While he was often gruff, he was first on the school intercom system to pray on behalf of the whole student body to guide a surgeon's hands when one of our own was sick or undergoing surgery. Likewise he acted as school-wide counselor and was quick to pray for the repose the souls of our few school mates who suffered untimely deaths as the result of tragic automobile accidents.

Our teachers were well credentialed and widely respected. A few of us may have occasionally snickered behind their backs at their eccentricities, but our comical "disrespect" amounted to wise cracking jokes repeated privately among ourselves and near went over the line that students cross today. Paddling did occur as I recall but only rarely. The result was a well-rounded student body in academics, the arts, physical education, and moral development.

So what happened? The ACLU, frivolous lawsuits, disconnected parents, and moral bankruptcy. Now chaos in part rules the day in all of our schools, and the teachers (God bless their continued efforts) are handcuffed. So today I find myself hesitant to admit that I am an alumni of a once proud school.

As a result, private schools in our area are booming. Turns out that a place where morals are taught, teachers are respected, and high academic scores the norm is still in great demand. Too bad I could never allow my kids to attend my high school alma mater. Instead my family will have to forego vacations and sacrifice greatly to fork over many thousands of dollars so my two youngsters can attend a parochial school where our Lord is still the ultimate headmaster.

Meanwhile, my tax dollars are languishing to fund a disaster that was once a distinguished public school. A flicker of light is still there, but will it ever come back? Not likely unless some of "old" ways become "new" again.

Mark Simpson
Chattanooga
