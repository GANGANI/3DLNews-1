Remains Found in National Park identified as those of Michael Cocchini
By Jeaneane Payne

Human remains recently found in Great Smoky Mountains National Park have been identified as those belonging to Michael Giovanni Cocchini who went missing in the park in March. Investigators worked with the Sevier County Medical Examiner's Office, Knoxville Regional Forensic Center, and the Knox County Sheriff's Forensic Unit to make the official identification through dental records.

The Great Smoky Mountains National Park Service says there will be no way for park investigators to determine an exact cause of death, but evidence found at the scene indicates that Cocchini had intentions of taking his own life.

Twenty three year old Cocchini was a Nashville resident staying temporarily in Gatlinburg. He was last seen by friends in Sevierville on Sunday, March 18.

Rangers became suspicious on March 20 when they noticed that a vehicle had been parked since Sunday at a quiet walkway along Newfound Gap Road approximately 1 mile south of the Park's Sugarlands Visitor Center. The walkway does not connect to the Park's trail system, so there would be no reason that backpackers would leave vehicles there overnight. The walkway is a short, easy trail that extends into the woods a short distance off the road and then dead-ends at the West Prong of the Little Pigeon River.

A small group of Rangers and a tracking dog conducted a quick search of the trail and a portion of the riverbank but did not pick up any definite signs of Cocchini. Rangers were later aided by eight Tennessee State Department of Corrections officers and Tennessee State dog team but found no trace of Cocchini at that time.

Cocchini's skull was found last week, only three tenths of a mile from the trail entrance. This was an area that had been thoroughly searched previously. Park rangers also found his backpack. Although the backpack was damaged by weather rangers found notes on the backpack which indicate he had intentions of committing suicide.
