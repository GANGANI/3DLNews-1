Brother-Sister Team Opens Unique Clinic

By Rosalind Guy

TEAMING UP: Angela Williams and brother Jerry Lovelace recently opened Midtown Injury and Physical Therapy Center at 1488 Madison Ave. -- Photograph By Rosalind Guy

Like all young children who picture themselves growing up to be lawyers, firemen, policemen and doctors, Angela Williams and her brother, Jerry Lovelace, had big dreams for their future careers.

Lovelace's plan was to become a doctor, and Williams wanted to work in his office as a nurse. They have come close to fulfilling their childhood predictions.

"Now, sure enough, we have our own office," Williams said.

Unique relationship. The brother-sister team recently opened Midtown Injury and Physical Therapy Center at 1488 Madison Ave. Instead of the doctor/nurse collaboration they dreamed of as children, the two have partnered as doctor/physical therapist.

And that, they said, is what makes their practice stand apart from others in the city.

"Usually you have to see a doctor and he has to refer you to a physical therapist," Williams said. "But here, you can have it all in one location."

Williams added that offering both services in one place cuts out the possibility of miscommunication that commonly occurs between therapist and physician. At the Midtown center, which opened in ­­October, Lovelace can keep a close eye on the progress of patients as they receive rehabilitation therapy from Williams. In addition, the pair is able to provide continuity of care for patients, helping them achieve the center's goal of restoring patients' ability to function.

Service offerings. Williams said the center's major focus is on patients in need of injury-related rehabilitation.

"We treat patients who have received sports injuries, workplace injuries and automobile accident victims," Williams said.

The center also offers a drug rehabilitation program.

Forming partnerships. Williams and Lovelace recently set out to contact attorneys in the Downtown area who specialize in workers' compensation claims and automobile accident cases. As a result, they have secured some agreements that ultimately will lead to partnerships with legal professionals to provide services for clients who need rehabilitation services. The center's proximity to Downtown law firms, in fact, was a consideration for Lovelace and Williams when they were scouting a location for the center.

Williams said the pair went in search of a high-traffic area located in the heart of the community that was easily accessible. And the recently extended trolley line runs right outside their door.

"The building has its own parking, so therefore it makes it easy for people to get to," Williams said. "We looked at offering all types of conveniences. People don't have to worry about having to go try and park their car somewhere and walk back."

Another consideration was the proximity to the Medical Center. Lovelace noted that the center also refers clients to hospitals or other specialty facilities when needed.

Besides Williams and Lovelace, the center employs Arthur Price as business manager and Eric Soler as rehabilitation technician.

As rehabilitation tech, Soler serves as Williams' assistant. But he is more than that - Soler, who is fluent in Spanish, also provides interpretive services.

"So we can treat someone who only speaks Spanish," Williams said.

Crossing career paths. Williams and Lovelace didn't follow a clear-cut path to arrive where they are today. They were born and raised in New York, but attended Oakhaven High School in Memphis. In high school, they both served as editors of the high school newspaper.

The childhood vision of going into business together forgotten, Williams said she enjoyed working at the newspaper so much that she thought that's what she would do with her life. Instead, after graduating high school, she spent five years in the U.S. Navy, where she worked as a dental technician.

Eventually, she headed back to school to get a physical therapy degree.

Williams attended the College of Allied Health at the University of Tennessee-Memphis. She is licensed to practice physical therapy in Tennessee and Mississippi, and she spent about six years working as a physical therapist at different outpatient facilities, including nursing homes.

Lovelace earned a doctorate in biochemistry at the University of Tennessee College of Medicine. He moved back to New York, where he spent two years in a practice group that provided services similar to those provided at the Midtown center. He then spent two years as a solo practitioner. He maintains his office in New York and travels there once a week.

Family business. The treatment center doesn't yet have a Web presence, but it is among the duo's plans for the future. Other plans, albeit distant, include possible expansion and bringing their children into the practice, further cementing it as a family business.

Williams and Lovelace will host an open house at the new facility from 4 p.m. to 7 p.m. Friday.
