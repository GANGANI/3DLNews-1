Police Department Forms in Piperton

Fayette town takes steps to prepare for coming growth

LANCE ALLAN | The Daily News

GETTING STARTED: Piperton Mayor Buck Chambers and patrolman Carl Hendricks, L to R, hope that Piperton's City Hall eventually will house the city's new police department. -- Photograph By Lance Allan

Watch out Piperton law breakers - a new sheriff is in town.

The southwest Fayette County community, which reflected a population of just under 700 in the 2000 U.S. Census, recently established its own police department.

"We might be the only city in the state of Tennessee that's incorporated that didn't have a police department," said Piperton Mayor Buck Chambers. "Our city in the next few years, the whole complexity is going to change. The majority of people in the community are happy we have a police force in the community for safety reasons, due to the fact that Highway 72 is about to become four lanes and the (state Highway) 385 interchange is targeted to be open by the last of December. We are trying to make sure we have everything in place when this happens."

Community cooperation. For now, the department's new officers are moonlighting while serving on the nearby Gallaway Police Department.

Gallaway officers will help Piperton get its new force off the ground, serving both cities until Piperton can establish a full-time staff. Gallaway Police Chief Billy Phillips is serving Piperton as interim chief. Eventually, Chambers expects the department to employ five officers to cover the community.

That change won't be immediate. However, Chambers said the town is diligently searching for a permanent chief and has begun the interview process. A new chief and at least two officers should be hired within three months, he said.

Preparing for growth. The decision to create the new department was laid out in January, along with several other priorities meant to prepare the town for growth. Those priorities also include a decentralized sewer system, which Chambers hopes will be introduced in coming months.

Beginning the process of establishing a police department has taken time for city officials, due largely to the costs involved. But the department became a reality following a March meeting of mayors from Fayette County communities. At the meeting, Phillips presented the idea of bringing in Gallaway officers to get the process started.

"It would have almost been cost prohibitive if we would have just, from day one, done it," Chambers said. "But through their generosity, they worked with us. They've done a beautiful job coming in here - furnishing the cars and the manpower to work with us on different shifts to make sure our police department is implemented properly. That's why it's so important to have good working relationships with other communities."

Building a police force. Piperton soon will have its own police cars. The city will receive two donated vehicles that are currently being delivered from Texas. Until those cars are ready, the town is using Gallaway police cars with placards on the sides to transform them into Piperton police cars.

Piperton officials currently are examining properties to eventually build a new city hall, which would allow the current facility to be used by the city's fire and police departments.

"We're just in the infant stages of it," Chambers said. "We're trying to be a proactive community rather than a reactive community."

Coming soon. Creation of its own police department is part of Piperton's proactive approach. Piperton soon will be a busy community. State Highway 385 ultimately will extend into Fayette County, with an interchange at state Highway 57 opening by the end of the year. That extension, along with the expansion of U.S. Highway 72 to four lanes within the town's limits, eventually will bring new residential and commercial growth to the community.

Jim Gallagher, a Memphis attorney and Fayette County resident, has been named to serve as judge of Piperton's new city court, which holds its first session Sept. 16. The court will handle municipal offenses; misdemeanor and felony cases that carry jail sentences will still be heard by General Sessions Court in Somerville, Gallagher said.

Gallagher, who is set to be sworn in next week, has been working with Chambers to set up ordinances for the town.

"The mayor and I rode around for about three and a half hours with him pointing out several things he felt were problems - he wanted to make sure there were ordinances in place that could address those problems," Gallagher said.

New laws. Those issues ranged from environmental and traffic problems to alcohol and nuisance concerns. Ordinances are now in place covering such issues as public drunkenness, disorderly conduct and open alcohol containers, Gallagher said.

"I think it's a big step in Piperton's growth for them to have this," he said. I think they are definitely going to need it. The sheer volume of citations that have already been issued at 15, 20, 25 miles an hour over the limit is staggering. They just need to get that under control."

For now, Gallagher's court will meet about twice a month until the docket is under control.

Working together. Chambers said the relationship between Piperton and Gallaway is indicative of the future he envisions for Fayette County.

"I think this is a great step toward mending cities in Fayette County to be working together as one unit rather than be all separate communities," he said. "Say a company comes in looking for property in Fayette County and we don't have something to suit them. I put them with the Oakland Chamber of Commerce or any of those cities because they might have something they can use.

"It's not a matter of how much it helps the city of Piperton, but how much it can help the western part of Fayette County. If it helps Fayette County, it helps us."
