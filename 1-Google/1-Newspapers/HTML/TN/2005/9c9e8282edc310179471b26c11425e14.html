Cowley coming into her own at Belmont

Former Cascade Lady Champion and District 11-A Player of the Year Laura Cowley is heavily involved in the future plans of Belmont.
Posted Wednesday, March 9, 2005 12:00 am
By DANNY PARKER

NASHVILLE -- The step from high school to college is never an easy one. Make that step from tiny Wartrace to a university in our state's capital and the adjustment is that much more complicated.

Former Cascade star basketball player Laura Cowley faced such a task as she is finishing off her first season as a member of the team at Belmont University.

"This year has been the toughest year that I have ever had," Cowley said. "Going from a little school to a (Division I university), just learning stuff is just really overwhelming. I have learned so much more this year than I have ever learned. It's just a big learning experience."

As does anyone who parts ways with the cumfy nest of home, Laura still thinks back to her friends and former teammates at Cascade.

"We were like the starting five. I miss them so much. I miss our team. We saw each other every day at school. Here you don't go to classes with the same people. We just did everything together. I miss the closeness, and I miss our close, close friendships."

Venturing to the bright lights and big city of 2nd Avenue in downtown Nashville from Bell Buckle Cafe can lead a young person away from their responsibilities in the maturation process.

Cowley, who turned 19 in February, has kept her faith at the Baptist school and maintained her academic standing as she was recently named to the Dean's List.

On the court, her first experiences came at the age of 4 in church league basketball in Tullahoma.

Her family moved to Wartrace while she was in sixth grade but her feats on the hardwood never slowed.

Cowley credits current Lincoln County Lady Falcon coach Herman Harden among various AAU coaches who aided in her development in basketball. Lady Champion coach Tracy Ferrell aided mostly with her "learning how to be a leader. She made you feel like you needed to take over and be in charge of the team when you're out there."

The 5-foot-8 guard started and was named All-District all four years at Cascade. In her senior year, she averaged 17.9 points per game en route to being named District 11-A Player of the Year. Her career ended with a total of 1,384 total points scored.

Belmont coach Tony Cross credits Cowley's athletic ability with being among the first things he noticed about her.

"I thought if she was a player with that much athletic ability, she could continue to get better. Then, when I found out what such a good student and person she is, that sold me that, 'Hey, she belongs here at Belmont, in our program because she can grow as a player and as a person here at Belmont.'

"She thinks of herself as a small-town girl. In my mind, that small-town girl has already done well and has got even better days ahead."

Belmont seemed like an easy decision for Cowley as it offers much of what any young lady with strong religious beliefs and a thirst for education would look for.

"It's just a great school," said Cowley, who has attended Grace Baptist Church in Tullahoma for much of her life. "The academics, then there's a Christian atmosphere and there's good coaches. It's just a solid basketball program.

"I think it was just God's will that I came here because we prayed about it."

Cowley is majoring in Exercise Science and, like many of her teammates, plans on going to physical therapy school after finishing her undergraduate work at Belmont.

For an athlete that was counted on so much and received extensive amounts of playing time in high school, college athletics have been an adjustment. Although she has played in 24 of the Lady Bruins' 26 games, she has averaged only around 11 minutes per game.

However, Cowley has had a shoulder to lean on in the person of senior guard Hollie Davis.

"If I were to look up to somebody, it would probably be Hollie," Cowley said. "She's just such a good, Christian girl. I've learned a lot from her. She's helped me out. When coach yells at me and stuff she's just like, "I went through the same thing.'"

Laura knows her role though with this year's team and already speaks much like a consummate team player.

"We have three seniors and a couple upperclassmen below them too," Cowley said. "So, I didn't expect to come out there and take it over. But, I just knew this year was going to be my learning year. Yeah, it's different and I wish I could be out there, but I know that I am supposed to be supportive to the upperclassmen."

"She's played more and better as a freshman than what I anticipated," Cross said. "Certainly she's still having some growing pains as a freshman, but she has been in games where she really made a difference.

"The best thing that can happen to her right now is when she becomes a sophomore and she's on her way. But, she's contributed a lot to our team. We started out early with a lot of injuries and she filled in and did a great job for us. (She) showed some offensive ability. She's getting better all the time. She's learning the game, learning our system. So, I'm very proud of her."

Her future looks bright as a Lady Bruin. Cross forsees a path of contribution from his freshman.

"I certainly think she's gotten the experience this year to be in the regular rotation next year. If not, I would be surprised."

She pinpoints certain aspects of her game that Cross and his staff have refined, among those are court awareness and the more cerebral parts of the game.

"I have learned how to defend anything. I learned all our offenses. I think I have gotten faster. My shot has gotten a whole lot better."

Cowley still has some parts of her game to work on. Both she and Cross agree on the where a majority of her improvements can be made.

"I would have to say court awareness with that as well," Cowley said. "I just need more experience being aware because I think everything else is behind me."

"Just court awareness and learning the game, learning our system," Cross said. "Regardless of how good players are sometimes, it's a new system and an overload on learning things. Sometimes, all of a sudden, that light will come on. Then they just play and use that athletic ability to help try to think through everything.

"So, I'm really excited about her future because she's very coachable. She is coachable and she realizes she's got some things to learn. She will continue to get better."
