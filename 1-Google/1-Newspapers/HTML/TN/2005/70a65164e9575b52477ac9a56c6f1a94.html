Medtronic Buys Swinnea Road Distribution Center     

     Medtronic's spinal and biologics division has purchased property at 4340 and 4380 Swinnea Road southeast of Memphis International Airport from Airport Logistics Center LLC for $11.4 million.
     The nearly 30-acre property includes two warehouses totaling 210,000 square feet on the east side of Swinnea Road west of Tchulahoma Road and south of East Raines Road.
     The warehouses serve as distribution centers where orders are processed and distributed for assorted Medtronic products, primarily surgical tools and implants used in spinal surgery, said Medtronic spokesperson Bert Kelly.
     Kelly said Medtronic had been leasing the property before the recent purchase. The company is looking to nearly double the Swinnea Road facility's current size over the next 18 months.
     "For us, the purchase of the land gives us a little more flexibility. We were leasing roughly 15 acres, but now that we have purchased close to 30 [acres] with this, it gives us a little more flexibility to do with it what we want to do," he said.
     Kelly said the project is covered under a payment-in-lieu-of-taxes (PILOT) agreement that was approved by the city-county Industrial Development Board in December 2005.
     This PILOT was intended to cover not only the current construction going on at Medtronic's administration campus at Pyramid Place by Airways Road and Interstate 240, but potential renovations to the Swinnea Road site.

Simon Transfers Wolfchase Between Holding Groups, Refinances Mall for $225 Million

     A quitclaim deed filed in the Shelby County Register's office last week shows ownership interest in the Wolfchase Galleria mall has been transferred between limited liability companies both within Indianapolis-based Simon Property Group Inc., according to The Daily News Online, www.memphisdailynews.com.
     The holding group Wolfchase Galleria Limited Partnership transferred the property to Galleria at Wolfchase LLC.
     In a related transaction, the new ownership group refinanced the property for $225 million through General Electric Capital Corp.
     Simon spokesperson Lee Morris said the transfer was part of the refinancing process. Simon Chief Financial Officer Stephen Sterrett signed the quitclaim and related deeds.
     The transferred property includes 62 acres on and around the Wolfchase Galleria site. It does not include anchors Macy's, JCPenney, Dillard's or Sears. However, a related transaction shows Wolfchase Galleria LP also transferred to Galleria at Wolfchase an operating agreement with parties owning those four anchors: Rich's Department Stores Inc., J.C. Penney Properties Inc., Dillard Tennessee and Sears, Roebuck & Co. Inc., respectively.
     Wolfchase Galleria, at the corner of North Germantown Parkway and U.S. 64, opened in 1997. It is consistently among the Shelby County Assessor of Property's highest-dollar property appraisals across the county.

Dunavant Award Winners Announced

     The Rotary Club of Memphis East on Wednesday announced that David Barber and Judge Larry E. Potter are the recipients of its fourth annual Bobby Dunavant Public Servant Awards.
     Barber is deputy administrator for administration and finance at the Shelby County Division of Corrections.
     Potter serves in the General Sessions Court Division XIV Shelby County Environmental Court. The court, which Potter helped administratively create, is the first county-wide court of its kind in the nation.
     Potter and Barber will receive the awards at the Rotary Club of Memphis East luncheon, which will be held April 18 from 11:45 a.m. to 1 p.m. in the Grand Ballroom at The Peabody Hotel, 149 Union Ave.
     Former U. S. Sen. Howard Baker will be the keynote speaker. Tickets to the luncheon are $30 per person or $300 per table (10 seats per table).

Morton Joins GTx As Chief Medical Officer

     Dr. Ronald A. Morton Jr. has been appointed chief medical officer at GTx Inc.
     GTx is a Memphis-based biopharmaceutical company.
     Morton previously worked as professor of surgery and chief of urology at the University of Medicine and Dentistry of New Jersey-Robert Wood Johnson Medical School. He also was director of urologic oncology for the Cancer Institute of New Jersey.
     Morton also held the Conzen Chair for Clinical Research and was director of the New Jersey Center for Clinical and Translational Sciences.
     Prior to joining Robert Wood Johnson Medical School in 2004, Morton held a dual faculty appointment at the Baylor College of Medicine in the Scott Department of Urology and in the Department of Molecular and Cell Biology, was clinical director of the Baylor Adult Urology Program, chief of urology at the Houston Veterans Administration Medical Center and director of the Baylor Prostate Cancer Center Research Laboratories.
     "Ron Morton's appointment as chief medical officer further strengthens GTx's management team and he will provide outstanding medical leadership as we execute our commercialization plans for Acapodene and Ostarine," said Dr. Mitchell S. Steiner, GTx CEO, in a statement. "Dr. Morton is an excellent fit for our company."

Saint Francis Receives High Ratings

     The gastrointestinal (GI) surgical program at Saint Francis Hospital-Memphis has been ranked among the top three in Tennessee and best in the Memphis area, according to a study conducted by an independent provider of hospital ratings.
     HealthGrades Inc. recognized Saint Francis' GI program for clinical quality excellence based on findings released in October. The hospital also received several five-star ratings for its cardiac program.
     "Saint Francis Hospital is committed to saving and improving the lives of people in our community," said David Archer, CEO of Saint Francis Hospital-Memphis. "This recognition from HealthGrades recognizes the care our doctors, nurses and staff provide to our patients."
     Hospitals are rated for specific services by assessing the national outcomes data collected on all hospitals by the U.S. Center for Medicare and Medicaid Services.
     The HealthGrades ratings for hospitals nationwide are available at www.healthgrades.com.

Roger This: Despite Recent Hangar Expansion, DeWitt Pilots Want More Space for Planes

     In late February, the Memphis-Shelby County Airport Authority (MSCAA) filed a $900,000 building permit for two new hangars at General DeWitt Spain Airport, according to the Daily News Online, www.memphisdailynews.com.
     It didn't take long for the project to take flight.
     Crews recently broke ground on the pair of 10-plane "T-hangars" to be built at DeWitt Spain, a general aviation airport five miles north of Downtown Memphis at 2787 N. Second St.
     The hangars will provide storage space for 20 small airplanes, which can be stored back to back, forming a row of interlinked Ts.
     The added space is sorely needed, although it hardly will make a dent in the waiting list of pilots who have been holding out for years to store their planes under cover instead of tied down outside.
     At last count, 57 plane owners are waiting for hangar space, said airport director Walter Cathey.
     "We will have the hangars filled before they are complete," he said.
     
Dead reckoning
     DeWitt Spain opened in 1972 on Mud Island but was forced to move the following year because of construction of the Hernando DeSoto Bridge. The airport found a permanent home just five miles north of Downtown, along the Mississippi River.
     For most of its early years, DeWitt Spain was run by a fixed base operator - a private vendor that offers aircraft maintenance, fuel and tie-down services and aid to pilots for checking weather and organizing flight routes.
     Now those services are handled by MSCAA - specifically Cathey - who has been at the airport for 20 years and began serving as director in 2003. Cathey also runs Charles W. Baker Airport in Millington.
     Both small airports are operated by MSCAA, which doesn't generate much revenue from either facility.
     "The Airport Authority operates these airports for two purposes," said Walter White, MSCAA director of operations and public safety. "One, it promotes general aviation in the community, and that's a vital asset to any community to be able to have a general aviation aircraft. Two, it gives aircraft an alternate location to go to if they don't want to mix in with traffic and the larger aircraft at the international airport."
     Planes flying into the two airports use visual flight rules (VFR) to land and are not directed by air traffic control, as they would be at larger airports such as Memphis International.
     Also, there are no landing fees at the airports, so pilots can buzz into Memphis, take a taxi into town and grab dinner at the Rendezvous - something they do often, Cathey said.
     
Every purpose under heaven
     Pilots like to keep their planes inside to protect them from the elements because heat and exposure can wreak havoc on the planes' sensitive electronics.
     "The avionics tend to get very sensitive," White said. "If you have to be out in the sun for an extended period of time, it's a kind of greenhouse effect inside your plane. It actually wears on your plane.
     "It helps to retain value and reduce depreciation if you're able to hangar it properly and keep it in proper conditions. So most people want to try to do that if they can. Most people have significant investments in their aircraft."
     White said the airport has been trying to add hangar space for years, but financial restraints have prevented plans from moving forward. The cost for storing a plane in the new hangar will be $235 a month; storage in the old hangar is $215 a month, but the benefits of added hangar space have nothing to do with extra revenue.
     "It's going to encourage more people to utilize the airport," White said.
     
Plane to see
     Jay King has owned a flight school called Downtown Aviation since 2003, although the company had been operating at DeWitt Spain for close to 20 years, he said. In the past four years, he's seen the amount of traffic truly take off.
     "Since I've been here in '03, it's grown tremendously," said King, whose school has 40 to 50 active flight students. "I think it's been the change in the flight school and the services and the airport management."
     King has played a big role in the airport's growth. Last summer his company became a fully licensed Cessna Service Center, further enhancing DeWitt Spain's complement of services for pilots based in or passing through Memphis.
     "We do work on all kinds of service and maintenance work on general aviation aircraft, airframe and engine work," King said.
     
For landlubbers only
     If MSCAA officials had their way, they would build many, many more hangars at DeWitt Spain. But while the demand exists for more hangar space, the land does not.
     "We don't have a lot of room to expand," Cathey said.
     Bound by a city sewage plant that abuts the Mississippi River to the west and North Second Street to the east, DeWitt Spain and its lone runway are locked into place.
     There would be room for one more hangar, Cathey said, but only a smaller one to fit about six to eight planes.
     So, unless the pending construction of Interstate 69 reroutes North Second Street and frees up land, the airport will be looking to do more with less - as it has for 35 years.
     "We're always looking for opportunities to provide or enhance services to our customers," White said.
