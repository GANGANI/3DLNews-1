Gaining Speed

Momentum builds for collaborative efforts to improve Whitehaven

NICKY ROBERTSHAW HITCHING | Special to The Memphis News

A new visitor center sits at the corner of Brooks and Elvis Presley Boulevard, the entrance to Whitehaven.  Photos: Lance Murphey

For years the Whitehaven community has been fighting against urban decay, crime and economic decline, including the effects of the recent recession.

But with ambitious plans for Graceland in the offing, committed political leadership in place and cooperation from the major players in the area, Whitehaven seems poised to turn an important corner.

“We have the opportunity to catch and stabilize a highly significant neighborhood,” said Memphis Mayor A C Wharton Jr., pointing to Whitehaven’s subdivisions and property values.

He notes that while Whitehaven is nowhere near that point, all too often troubled areas are allowed to further run down. When that happens, reviving them after the fact is much harder. Wharton has made Whitehaven a priority. That includes residential areas, the Brooks Road corridor and the area around Graceland, one of the city’s biggest tourist draws.

A.J. Reddy and Patrick Neale of Atlanta pose for a snapshot outside Graceland.

Whitehaven, the largest neighborhood in South Memphis, starts around Brooks and extends south to the Mississippi state line and east to Memphis International Airport. Elvis Presley Boulevard is the main artery, and home to Graceland and Southland Mall. Major corporations including FedEx Corp., Smith & Nephew and Medtronic Inc. have operations along Brooks and near the airport, while residential neighborhoods are found throughout. In the 1950s and early 1960s, upscale Whitehaven was the logical place for newly-wealthy Elvis Presley to buy his home. Since then, while there are pockets of stability, the area has been plagued by economic decay, crime and other problems.

In considering the future of Whitehaven, the planned expansion of Graceland is the biggest wild card. The attraction’s corporate owner, CKx Inc., announced in 2007 an ambitious $250-million expansion that would include one or two hotels, a convention center, and related businesses. Since then, the company, citing the economy and other factors, has been mum about specifics, other than to say its expansion will likely not match the original plan.

Now, the company is a takeover target, which could work for or against the Graceland expansion. A major suitor is Robert F.X. Sillerman, the former CKx chairman and CEO who announced the original Graceland plan but resigned from CKx in May. He owns more than 20 percent of the stock and has said he’ll try to take over the company.

Visitors tour the Lisa Marie airplane at Graceland.

Last week Sillerman offered to buy Elvis Presley Enterprises Inc., revealing he values the company at between $512 million and $535 million. In an SEC filing, Sillerman said he plans to offer between $5.50 and $5.75 per share for at least another 30 percent worth of CKx on top of the 21 percent he already holds.

There are other suitors as well, so how committed is the company’s current management to the Graceland plans?

“Elvis Presley Enterprises and parent company CKx continue to evaluate plans to expand its existing operations at Graceland,” said CKx chief financial officer Thomas Benson in a statement. “The size and scope of any redevelopment program will be dependent upon a number of factors including general economic conditions. With the continued support of local and state government, future plans will serve to enhance what is already a world class attraction that draws hundreds of thousands of visitors annually from all over the world.”

‘Above and beyond’

The company has already acquired property around Graceland, even if no announcements have been made as to what it intends to build there. For its part, the city plans to spend as much as $47 million in state, local and federal funding to improve Elvis Presley Boulevard between Brooks and Shelby, which includes Graceland, said Wain Gaskins, city engineer. Already, field surveys are under way, as is the process of piecing together the funding. The next step is to hire a consultant/design team for the project, he added.

“We are committed to a complete transformation on what we may call the front door of Whitehaven,” Wharton said of Graceland.

The new Lit Junior restaurant retail store at 1665 Winchester Road is one of a few new businesses that have opened a location in Whitehaven recently.

One longtime Whitehaven booster is excited about the road improvements, and believes that entrepreneurs will get busy once Graceland moves forward with its expansion.

“If they see shovels turning around Graceland, they will turn a shovel on their own projects,” said Chuck Strong, president of the Whitehaven Economic Development Corp. and owner of Piano’s Flowers & Gifts. “They don’t want to jump the gun, but when somebody shoots the gun, they are going to be ready to go.”

He points out that once the hotels and convention center at Graceland are built, visitors who once spent a day and a half in Whitehaven will stay four to five days. They will want upscale places to eat and other amenities. Already, things are picking up in Whitehaven, says Strong, who rattles off a long list of recent developments: The new Lit Jr. restaurant store, the razing of shabby apartments, recent expansions of Medtronic and Smith & Nephew operations and the city’s commitment to improving Whitehaven.

“You don’t see businesses closing,” Strong said. “You see businesses coming.”

Truck dealer Diamond International finished a $6 million expansion of its Brooks Road property in Whitehaven.

Significantly, some committed Whitehaven businesses have invested more heavily in the area. Earlier this year, the truck dealer Diamond International finished a $6 million expansion of its Brooks Road property, developing a 10-acre facility for truck sales and service. Chuck Hutton Toyota, which had operated next to Diamond, chose to relocate in Whitehaven at I-55 and Shelby in 2008 rather than to leave the area. Methodist Le Bonheur Healthcare has spent $7 million in recent years for renovations and equipment upgrades at Methodist South Hospital, including improving its emergency-room facilities.

Strong said he believes Whitehaven has a lot going on demographically to support the businesses there now as well as the future businesses likely to spring up once Graceland begins its expansion.

“We’ve been here a long time, and we’ve got the body count: 600,000 (yearly visitors to Graceland),” said Strong, a Whitehaven resident since 1959. “That’s above and beyond what lives here.”

From the inside out

While small businesses are critical to the well-being of Whitehaven, plenty of other entrepreneurs have an interest in improvement. This includes myriad corporations with operations in the area that formed the Memphis Airport Area Development Corp., a nonprofit group focused on improving the airport area, including Whitehaven.

This year the group hired Applied Real Estate Analysis Inc. (AREA), a Chicago-based consulting firm with expertise in upgrading airport areas, noted Jo Ferreira, a FedEx executive and MAADC president. AREA is now working on the Memphis airport area with plans to issue recommendations as well as a master plan later this year. MAADC also has retained the Greater Memphis Chamber, to work with local code enforcement agencies as a way to bring improvements to Whitehaven’s primary commercial corridors.

“We are hopeful about the future of this area, and we feel we’ve picked excellent resources,” said Ferreira, managing director of hub area business development for FedEx.

An enhanced Whitehaven is also a factor for promoting aerotropolis, a local economic development initiative that highlights the strength of the airport with the city’s other transportation and logistics capabilities. Memphis boasts the world’s No. 1 cargo airport. And Memphis International generates some $28.6 billion in annual economic impact as well as provides 220,000 jobs, according to a recent study.

“Strong neighborhoods are a big part of the aerotropolis concept,” said Jim Covington, the chamber’s vice president of logistics and aerotropolis development. “The closer the neighborhoods are to the airport, the better we want them to function. These things are all linked together.”

Memphis airport officials also are concerned about improving the neighborhood.

“Visitors who come into this community need to not only be impressed when they come in, but also when they leave,” said airport board chairman Arnold Perl. “So appearance around the airport is of critical importance. We at the airport have an interest in making sure that what’s on the outside of the fence is as appealing as what’s on the inside.”

He points to the $1.6 million in funding approved by the Memphis City Council last year for beautifying the two-mile stretch of Plough Boulevard leading into and out of the airport. Backed by council chairman Harold Collins (who represents Whitehaven on the council), the funds are being used to plant some 1,500 trees and to add landscaping, new lighting and signage in the area.

Wharton is upbeat about the prospects for Whitehaven. He cites the Unified Development Code, which was approved last week, an important weapon for stabilizing the neighborhood and encouraging sustainable development. He also cites the leadership of council chairman Collins as well as his own.

“He is very much attuned to what’s needed in the area,” Wharton said, “so we will be working hand and hand.”
