Peaches and the Cream

Investigating the Simpkins job search

  • Bruce Dobie and Liz Murray Garrigan
  • Nov 14, 1996
  • Nov 14, 1996
  • Facebook

Peaches Simpkins is deputy to the governor of the state of Tennessee. That job makes her the second highest-ranking official in the executive branch of state government. Her office is mere steps away from that of Gov. Don Sundquist. In many ways, she is the governor’s eyes and ears. She is also his right-hand aide.

Simpkins, who is paid $88,600 a year, has a reputation for making fearless use of her power. Her language is rough; her style is challenging and combative. Whenever Peaches Simpkins steps into a process, controversy invariably seems to follow in her wake.

In recent days, Simpkins’ name has popped up in association with a highly politicized struggle at the Tennessee Higher Education Commission (THEC), which oversees the University of Tennessee and Board of Regents college systems. THEC’s executive director, Bryant Millsaps, a Democrat, is under fire from the Sundquist administration. In the assault on Millsaps, Peaches Simpkins has led the charge.

Simpkins has stated that the administration would be pleased to receive a resignation letter from Millsaps, who was the Sundquist team’s own pick to head the commission. Millsaps, in a noteworthy display of bravery, has said he will not quit unless the THEC board asks him to. Meanwhile, a Republican board member who was a Sundquist appointee, has resigned over the issue. In her resignation letter, the board member said she had “concerns over Ms. Simpkins’ conduct.”

In the recently concluded political races, Simpkins, a nominal Democrat, alienated some of her old political friends by assisting Republicans in state legislative races. Meanwhile, she alienated some Republicans by maintaining a hands-off approach in races where Republicans had hoped to be more competitive.

The truth of the matter is, ever since she helped Sundquist in the 1994 gubernatorial race, Simpkins has been under fire for her wavering political allegiances. In the 1994 election, she attracted much attention as a prominent member of the “Democrats for Sundquist” group. Then, after being named deputy to the governor, she took batterings from the Republicans and the Democrats, both of whom questioned her loyalty.

Simpkins has frequently been mentioned as a potential gubernatorial candidate in some future election, but her ties to Sundquist have turned out to be something of a political burden, both for her and for the governor. Nonetheless, when it comes to getting things done, Simpkins seems to know no obstacles. Her supporters and her critics may squabble about her effectiveness—or her ineffectiveness, depending on the point of view. But it’s clear that it is Simpkins who manages the political side of the governor’s life. Whether they like her or not, important figures on Capitol Hill know she is a force with whom they must deal.

“If there is a hill, and nobody can take the hill,” commented one of Simpkins’ closest colleagues, “you can send Peaches to take the hill and be assured that it will get done.”

Networking

Rumors have long circulated that Simpkins is preparing to leave public life. The rumor mill continues to grind steadily away.

Reports have surfaced that Simpkins has sought a job here in Nashville at Baptist Hospital. Sources in the health-care business say she was offered a $265,000-a-year post—plus benefits—at Baptist. For whatever reason, Simpkins did not take the job at the not-for-profit hospital.

But Simpkins seems much more tempted by the chance to be involved with Women’s Health Partners Inc., a for-profit health care company. Simpkins acknowledges that she will probably have some involvement in Women’s Health Partners. Already, her potential affiliation with the Nashville-based company has stirred up a storm of controversy and has led to charges of a potential conflict of interest.

Women’s Health Partners’ mission, described in the curious vernacular of health-care entrepreneurs, is to “carve out” and “roll up” obstetrical and gynecological doctors’ groups. In other words, the company hopes to create a network of gynecologists in Tennessee and Kentucky. Once all those ob-gyns are interconnected, they will have substantial leverage in negotiating with insurance companies and hospitals.

At least in theory, consolidation into a network would also allow doctors to be doctors, freeing them of many of the worries and hassles of paperwork and offering them the benefits of better computer technology and more efficient administration. At the same time, all those combined firms would add up to a sizable organization, one with considerable revenues and a substantial appeal on Wall Street. Such firms often double, triple, or quadruple in value when they go public.

Women’s Health Partners is currently attempting to line up a substantial group of doctors. When the combined revenues of their practices hit the $50 million mark, health-care industry officials say, the company will be big enough to go public. That step could take place sometime in 1997. Then the doctors participating in the network—and the entrepreneurs who put the deal together—would be able to share the wealth.

As far back as three months ago, at an official presentation made to doctors in Knoxville by officials from Women’s Health Partners, Deputy Gov. Peaches Simpkins was listed as both “vice chairman” and a “director” of the company. At least one prominent health care executive in Tennessee has confirmed to the Scene that in recent months he discussed with Simpkins, both by telephone and in person, the possibility of doing business with the company.

Long-distance telephone records from Simpkins’ office, from July through September, show numerous calls placed to major health care providers in the state. Meanwhile, some in Nashville’s tight-knit circle of health care entrepreneurs say they are familiar with Simpkins’ work on behalf of the company.

Simpkins denies that Women’s Health Partners has paid her anything—neither salary, consulting fees, stock options, nor equity. She denies that she is a director of the company, and company officials say it was a “misprint” to list her as “vice chairman” and a “director” in company literature. Still, Simpkins acknowledges that she very likely will join the Women’s Health Partners board. She acknowledges that she has held a few conversations with persons who might be interested in doing business with the company. But she insists she has not been extensively promoting the venture.

Women’s Health Partners Inc. is a project of Andrew “Woody” Miller, a well-known health care entrepreneur who has contributed $20,000 to Sundquist’s campaign fund.

Miller was a co-founder of Surgical Care Affiliates (SCA), a Nashville-based venture. Even before he helped start SCA, Miller was involved with Hospital Corporation of America, and, later, with Quorum, a hospital management company. When SCA was sold this past January, Miller, the firm’s largest shareholder, had reaped over $50 million from the venture. He is currently capitalizing some of the costs—about $15 million—of Women’s Health Partners.

“It is the most exciting company I’ve ever been involved with,” Miller says.

Hospital bedfellows

Miller’s new company has competition from two other local firms attempting to accomplish similar goals. The intertwining of leadership at the three firms demonstrates the incestuous nature of Nashville’s health care businesses.

Symmetry Health Care, which intends to establish an ob-gyn network, is headed by Bill Hamburg, who was president of Surgical Care for four years until its acquisition early this year. Hamburg says it was Miller who hired him to come to Surgical Care. Mayor Phil Bredesen is a passive investor in Symmetry; thus, his interests in that venture would be in direct competition with Simpkins’ interests in Women’s Health Partners, should she actually become involved in the firm.

It is a well-known fact that there is no love lost between Bredesen and Simpkins. Their relationship disintegrated during the 1994 governor’s race, when Simpkins sided with Sundquist in his race against Bredesen. In a much-publicized confrontation, Bredesen was accused of pushing Simpkins. Sundquist’s supporters could be seen sporting buttons that read, “Phil Pushed Peaches.”

Meanwhile, Pat Ryan is heading up PrincipalCare, another attempt to establish an ob-gyn network. Ryan has a long history in local health care circles; working with him at PrincipalCare is Frank Gordon, the son of Joel Gordon, who, along with Miller, was a founder of Surgical Care.

Wall Street watchdogs who keep an eye on Nashville’s health care business say all three companies are part of a national trend. “All three are pretty much the same,” says Catherine Gemmato-Smith, managing director of corporate finance at J.C. Bradford. “They all have the same strategy, which is to roll up ob-gyn practices, enter into arrangements whereby the company would buy the assets of the practice. The company would manage the practice for the physicians and be paid a management fee.”

The leading model for these companies is PhyCor, which has been in existence for almost a decade. PhyCor manages the practices of physicians in multi-specialty groups. Firms like Women’s Health Partners, on the other hand, are specifically targeting ob-gyns. Other companies around the country are specializing in serving opthamologists and even dentists.

While the business goals are similar at all three ob-gyn firms, their strategies are different. At Women’s Health Partners, the primary selling tool is the promise that the doctors themselves will have a role in running the company. “We have a company that is physician owned and controlled,” Miller says, adding that 75 percent of Women’s Health Partners is owned by physicians and that physicians will fill 75 percent of the seats on the board.

“There are real advantages to coming together and working in partnership with your colleagues,” says Dr. Mike Bishop, whose practice, called Women’s Health Group, was one of the first to participate in Women’s Health Partners. Nearly 30 ob-gyns are involved in Women’s Health Group.

Bishop says that, by joining a network, doctors can practice medicine and let the company worry about management. He also mentions the “L word”: leverage. “We’re just a fragmented little industry out there,” he says. “This gives us leverage—getting ourselves in a position to be at the negotiating table. We are getting hammered every day with the hospitals that own physicians and control the health care dollar.”

From a strictly financial standpoint, the potential rewards for a venture like Women’s Health Partners are immense. Tiny physician’s practices make up the largest mom-and-pop industry in America. And if a company can come along and combine those private practices into one big business, and then take the business public, the rewards could be enormous.

(Financial) health issues

Miller says he has asked Simpkins to join his board of directors. Even though he says she has not actually consented to join the board so far, he says it is very likely she will become a company director. “She has indicated she is probably inclined to do that,” Miller says. “We are checking out to make sure there are no conflicts of interest before we do that next step.”

On Aug. 13, Women’s Health Partners’ top company officials traveled to Knoxville to meet with a gathering of ob-gyns at Club LeConte, a private downtown club atop the First Tennessee Bank Building. There, company officials made a presentation about the benefits of joining such a physicians’ network. Among the speakers was Simpkins, who spoke about the “statewide significance of Women’s Health Partners.” Unfortunately, Miller says, it was a goof for her to be listed in the program as a director and vice-chairman of the board. Simpkins explains the confusion this way: “To be honest with you, those are the kinds of things they would like for me to consider doing, and that just haven’t been consummated yet.”

When it comes to describing Simpkins’ activities on behalf of Women’s Health Partners, Miller says, “She has, I guess, attended a few meetings to observe what we are presenting to the doctors. She has indicated her enthusiasm for becoming a board member with no time frame or commitment.” But he adds that, even though there has been talk of “the possibility of her joining the company on a full-time basis,” there have been no formal agreements.

A review of Simpkins’ telephone records from July through September indicates that she has placed calls from the governor’s offices to several firms that are targets of the Women’s Health Partners’ business plan.

Phone records for July and August show long-distance charges on Simpkins’ office line to Fort Sanders Regional Medical Center in Knoxville, Methodist Hospital in Memphis, and Baptist Hospital in Memphis. Miller said his company is in discussions with all three about entering into a business arrangement.

Alan Guy is president of Fort Sanders Alliance, which owns Preferred Health Partnership of Tennessee Inc., one of the largest managed care organizations in the state. Preferred Health Partnership provides coverage to about 300,000 people. Guy confirmed to the Scene that he has talked and met with both Simpkins and Miller to discuss Women’s Health Partners.

Not only have Simpkins and Guy spoken about Women’s Health Partners, but they have also spoken about other health care matters, including TennCare, the massive state-financed insurance program upon which the fortunes of numerous large health care providers in the state depend. For some, this mix of interests—both public and private—is troubling.

Situation ethics

Simpkins says that her role with Women’s Health Partners has not yet been solidified, that she has not officially taken a leadership role, and that she sees no problem with having occasional conversations about the company when she is on state time or when she is using state facilities.

“If you’re asking me if we have signed documents, no. Have we come to final terms? No. Have we become very serious? Yes,” Simpkins says. An involvement with Women’s Health Partners, she says, would be “an incredible opportunity for board service, if that’s something that they and I choose to do. I think they’re the premier group in this area.”

Accepting the board position, she adds, would not necessarily require her to leave the governor’s staff. “Do I have to leave the governor’s office to do it? No. Do I expect to have to disclose it in January? Yes.” Simpkins says the governor is aware of her activities.

As for the two dozen long-distance phone calls made from her office to various hospitals and health care interests during the last several months, Simpkins says, in many of those cases she was simply returning phone calls placed to her by friends with questions about TennCare or other matters.

“I get calls from all over the state from people who have TennCare questions or people who have any number of questions,” Simpkins says. She concedes that TennCare is not one of the programs for which she is responsible.

“I receive calls on most any subject that comes up in the state of Tennessee,” she says. “You can’t have a 20-year history in something without getting a call. We don’t live in a vacuum.”

Simpkins does have a long history of involvement in health care in Tennessee. At various times, she has worked as a consultant to Baptist Hospital, as the executive director of the nonprofit Hospital Alliance of Tennessee, and as the executive director of the state’s Health Facilities Commission.

“I’m real relaxed [on those occasions when] a hospital administrator calls me and Women’s Health Partners comes up for three minutes of a conversation,” Simpkins says.

Simpkins says she’s the deputy governor all the time, not just while she’s at work. So, she says, she doesn’t feel bad about making brief long-distance phone calls at work or about meeting with her accountant about personal business matters in the Capitol. “If you’re talking about 30 calls at a minute a call, that doesn’t give me any heartburn,” she says.

Most health care principals hesitate to go on the record about the potential conflict of interest that might result from Simpkins being involved with Women’s Health Partners while she still holds the second most powerful post in the executive branch of state government and while she is still in a position to influence state health care policy. But Don Welch, associate dean and associate professor of law at Vanderbilt University and the author of Conflicting Agendas: Personal Morality in Institutional Settings, says a situation like Simpkins’ could pose serious ethical questions.

“If a company is trying to use an influential public position to achieve private gain, then there’s something a little troubling about that,” Welch says. “In Washington, D.C., they’ve tried to deal with these types of things by having regulations to prevent people from going from a government position to a lobbying position. And those regulations don’t even deal with simultaneous conflict of interest. They just talk about trying to convert someone from public to private life.”

Welch says “a real problem” arises in cases where an official like Simpkins might actually use her position “as a way of getting some leverage with the people [she’s] calling. What we really want to be careful of is people using their positions in government as a way of achieving some personal gain.”

Returns and investments

This is not the first time Simpkins has been accused of business dealings or investments that smack of a potential conflict of interest. In the early days of the Sundquist administration, Simpkins took some heat when the governor announced plans for Corrections Corp. of America to build and contract with the state for a new prison somewhere in Tennessee. CCA is a publicly traded company that manages correctional institutions. Simpkins is an investor in CCA.

If Sundquist had retained a disclosure policy for his staff like the one former Gov. Ned McWherter established for his cabinet and senior aides, the public would know much more about Simpkins’ investment in CCA and a host of other companies. McWherter’s office policy demanded that his cabinet and senior staffers go beyond what is required by state law—a simple disclosure of the names of any companies in which they owned at least $5,000 in stock. McWherter also required his top aides to go public with their income tax returns, so that any profit derived from those investments was a matter of public record. He went public with his own records too.

When Sundquist took office, he did not continue the McWherter policy; his staff is only required, twice a year, to file the simple disclosure mandated by state law. Simpkins’ most recent filing indicates that she owns stock in about a dozen companies and has real estate investments in North Carolina.

When Lamar Alexander left the governor’s office in 1986, it was revealed that he was lining up meetings for a company that would be engaged in the operation of child-care facilities around the country. Alexander took some heat for those activities, but in the end the court of public opinion decided that it was all right for him to make plans for the days when he would no longer be governor.

In Simpkins’ case, it can be argued that she is simply making plans for the days after she leaves the governor’s office. At the same time, it’s fair to ask about the ethics of the No. 2 official in the executive branch of state government promoting a private company. After all, government officials are supposed to be there to serve the people. They are not there to take sides in a highly competitive marketplace.
