By JENNIFER MURLEY

Making it through ranks

By JENNIFER MURLEY

The Daily News

Edward Labry III first met his mentor Dan Palmer in the mid 80s. Labry was a college student burning up the phone lines pitching Palmers financial products.

Palmer was the president of EFS National Bank. Labry was an EFS telemarketer.

Just 15 years later, the seemingly unlikely duo transformed the small company once centered on matching private label gas cards with fleet trucking companies, into an innovative company credited with making credit card acceptance available at grocery stores, pay-at-the-pump conveniences available at gas stations and sprinkling automated teller machines across the nation.

Or, to put it more simply: what was once a $10 million market capitalization company is now a $14 billion market capitalization company.

And Labry, 38, who has tackled nearly every job within company since then, is now the president of both the Concord EFS Inc. holding company as well as its subsidiary EFS National Bank.
In recognition of outstanding career accomplishments, Labry is being presented with the Award of the Phoenix from his alma mater, Cumberland University, tonight at the schools inaugural Alumni Awards Banquet in Lebanon, Tenn. The award is the highest honor given by the university.

Its an honor, definitely, to be recognized, Labry said. This is everybodys dream, to be a part of something in its infancy stages, and be a part of it all the way through its bad times, through when it begins to make money, to its tremendous successes.

Looking back, Labry credits his formative Cumberland years, during which he was both a full-time student and employee, with instilling his drive for success.

College takes discipline, he said. It teaches you that you get out of something what you put into it. And, you carry that on into your relationships, your marriage, your family, your children you get out of something what you put into it.

Equally heartening as his philosophy on life, is Labrys workplace ethic.

When discussing the success of his company, he immediately defers credit to his support staff, and, as a result, goes the extra mile to accommodate their needs.

I got the luxury of being a telemarketer, a road salesman, a manager, a director, a senior vice president, so you really learn how everybody wants to be treated because you were one of those people, he said. People really have to be happy where they work, recognized for what they do and compensated accordingly.

Although he is wholeheartedly committed to his career, Labrys first priority these days is his family.

I just want to be a good dad, he said, shifting to a decidedly softer tone after referring to his two young cotton-topped children. I love my two daughters more than anything in this world.

And then theres his dad, with whom he has remained close through the years, and who also links Labry to his favorite pastime, duck hunting. Each year, the two men travel to their Alligator, Miss.-farm for what is now deemed their opening day tradition. Mississippis duck season opens Dec. 1.

Its something my father and I have done ever since I was 2 years old, he said, flashing a sentimental smile. He used to drag me and now I drag him.

Although Labry, a youngster by professional standards, appears to be on top of the world early on, hes more than willing to share his recipe for happiness.

Just because other opportunities come along that might seem to provide a little bit more at the time, the grass is not always greener on the other side of the fence, he said. If you get to a place and you fall in love with it, you should stay there, because youve got to love what you do.
