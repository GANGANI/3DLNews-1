Bagel bonanza Whether boiled or steamed, bagels becoming hot bakery items By SUZANNE THOMPSON THE DAILY NEWS For a bread, bagels have caused a lot of commotion. That crazy, circular, chewy, cousin of a roll is sweeping the country slathered with cream cheese, substituted for sandwich bread or munched with coffee en route to offices. "Bagels are habit forming," said Vadit Pahboo, owner of The Bagel Company of Memphis, located at 6698 Poplar Ave., across from the Carrefour. Pahboo contends that it is the chewy texture that makes the doughnut-shaped bread so irresistible. And that chewiness can only be attained one way by boiling the dough prior to baking it, according to Pahboo. At The Bagel Company, this is the method that has been used to make the bagels for the last 13 years. The business operated for about nine years under the name Johann Sebastian Bagel and was Memphis first bagel shop. Pahboo bought the business four years ago and changed the name to The Bagel Company. The shop still has the original dough maker it used when Johann Sebastian Bagel opened and employs the same baker, Reginald McCray. After the dough comes out of the dough machine, McCray said it is fed through the bagel maker, which forms the dough into that well-loved, round shape. The next step is proofing, which is done naturally, with the dough being allowed to rest and leaven in the warmth of the kitchen. After the dough has risen, it is put into a refrigerator, which retards the rising process. The bagels remain in refrigeration for four to 24 hours. Then comes the controversial part, the boiling, which some claim can toughen the dough. The bagels are submersed in the vat of hot water for about a minute. "Characteristic of a bagel is its shine and skin," said McCray. "Ours are produced the old-fashioned way." McCray contends that boiling the bagels results in the shiny appearance that he said others imitate cosmetically by applying an egg wash before baking. McCray carefully arranges the steaming boiled bagels on paper sheets where they will be lifted on trays into the huge rotary oven. Pahboos wife, Daksha, said McCray starts baking each day at 5 a.m., so the first customer who walks through the door at 7 a.m. will get a fresh, hot bagel. The Bagel Company offers at least a dozen different types of bagels and also has an adjoining bakery stocked with a variety of freshly baked, round-loaf breads, such as onion-parmesan-garlic and cinnamon-raisin. Daksha Pahboo explained that the company is committed to using only fresh ingredients and never uses flavorings. Instead of using a lot of sugar to sweeten the cinnamon-raisin loaves, the dough is loaded with raisins for added sweetness. The Bagel Company has a full-service restaurant and also offers a variety of cream cheeses as well as a catering service. One of the next bagel baking businesses to enter the Memphis market is Bagel Street Cafe. Bagel Street Cafe, which operates two stores, one at 2037 Exeter and the other at 4735 Southern, steams the bagels rather than boiling them. Owner Mike Vogler said this process gives the bagels a softer crust instead of the more crisp crust found on boiled bagels. It is a difference that Vogler said his customers appreciate. Offering 14 types of cream cheeses, all hand blended, and 20 different bagel flavors, including pumpkin and cranberry rum, Vogler puts together a line of gift baskets available for delivery. Vogler is big on customer service and takes fax orders to deliver lunch or bagel orders to his clients. He said there is not a set radius for delivery, and his employees have delivered as little as one bowl of soup from his shop on Southern to a customer on Lamar Avenue. "It was not cost effective, but thats part of the game with customer service," he said. Vogler also starts with fresh ingredients and makes his dough from scratch daily. After making the bagels, they are proofed in "proofing boxes" and put into a low-velocity, high-humidity chamber to retard the leavening process. Then Bagel Streets bagels are baked in a steam-injected oven. The process takes 24 hours to produce a batch of bagels. "Theres really no short-cut," Vogler said. A counter-service style cafe, Bagel Street has "schmeared" bagels, which are sliced bagels slathered with cream cheese. Deli-style sandwiches, tuna and chicken salad, and soup are part of the lunch fare served at both locations. Bagel Street also caters functions varying in size from five to 500. Vogler said he has a healthy list of corporate clients, and he sells his bagels wholesale to many area Kroger bakeries. Vogler brought Bagel Street Cafe to Memphis from St. Louis, where he opened his first two stores. Vogler has been in Memphis for three years. He said he is looking at opening additional cafe locations soon. Another bagel vendor, the newest in the Memphis market, Bronx Bagel Bar, located at 4724 Poplar Ave., offers 26 different types of bagels. Bronx Bagel opened two years ago, and like Bagel Street Cafe, has walk-up counter service. Scott Anderson, director of operations, said he is pleased with the growth the company has experienced. Bronx Bagel Bar is preparing to open a second location at 121 Union Ave. Anderson said the new Downtown location should be open in about two weeks. Bronx Bagel Bar offers a variety of luncheon sandwiches and gourmet coffees. Anderson said that the bagels are baked fresh daily from dough that is not made on the premises. The steam-baking method is used at Bronx Bagel Bar. Anderson said he feels the steam-baking process provides a softer bagel. "We call them more eater friendly," he said. The fitness craze of the 90s makes bagels a perfect choice for breakfast or a quick snack, according to Anderson, who said Bronx Bagel Bar bagels have only one gram of fat and also are offered "schmeared." Anderson said he was confident that there would soon be more competition in the Memphis bagel market, which he called "untapped." "I think youll see a lot more bagel places coming to Memphis," he said.
