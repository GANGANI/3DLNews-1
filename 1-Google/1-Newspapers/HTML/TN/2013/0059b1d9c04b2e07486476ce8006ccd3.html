Chattanoogan: Callie Starnes – We Are Still Human

  • Wednesday, April 17, 2013
  • Jen Jeffrey
Nightly news anchor at WRCB Callie Starnes, shares about the human side of being a journalist.
Callie's family at the 2011's Dare to Dance in which she had competed, sponsored by the Kidney Foundation.
Election night live shot.
Greg Glover and Callie interviewed Ms. Betty White in 2010.
Live shot during the April 27th storms in 2011.
Radio show with Tonya Craft after she was aquited.
Reporting from the White House for the Top Cops presentation.

On the morning of the terrorists’ attacks of September 11th, Callie (McGinnis) Starnes knew that she wanted to be a journalist. It was her sophomore year in high school and she had elected to take a broadcasting class. The class held closed circuit announcements and Callie was anchoring that week.

“My teacher came in during that broadcast and he explained to me what was happening. He basically told me to inform the entire student body what was happening and that the principal was allowing teachers to turn on their televisions in the classroom, if they so chose,” Callie remembers. “I had an opportunity at that young age to know what it felt like to deliver the news of such a big event. From that moment on, I had the bug and that was where it started for me.”

Growing up in the Atlanta area along with her younger brother Alex, Callie was brought up fairly well-rounded. Her minister father Darrell could be somewhat strict, while her mother Peggy (who had a California upbringing) brought an easy-going attitude to the mix. Occasionally, the family would relocate as Pastor McGinnis would be called to various churches.

“My first job was hand-washing bone china in an English tea room. Preachers don’t make a ton of money so when I wanted to go to the movies, I washed china on the weekends and the lady eventually hired me to teach tea etiquette for little girls with tea parties and things like that,” Callie recalls.

“I wanted to be a producer. It seemed to fit because I was organized with everything in my life and putting together a newscast was more up my alley. After anchoring the closed circuit announcement on 9/11, I just caught the bug early and loved it. I was encouraged by others to go on air,” Callie says.

After graduating from Berry College in Rome, Ga., she married her husband Brian Starnes. “He knew that if we didn’t marry right away that I would end up moving somewhere far off and it would make things more difficult,” Callie says.

The hopeful journalist had opportunities to intern, beginning with a sports talk show with Southern Broadcasting and was promoted as the show’s co-host of the Saturday morning sports show. She also interned at WSB-TV in Atlanta and later joined the team at WMGT in 2007.

“I had a story that year that aired in several states and my former co-worker Justin Young, who was working at WRCB, had put in a good word for me. I got the interview and took the job in 2008. I was the morning show reporter working with Jed and Latrice and getting up in the middle of the night to go to work. I was that person standing on the mountain top with snow flurries at 4 a.m.,” Callie laughs.

After her first year as a reporter in Chattanooga, Callied received a promotion moving into the role of weekend anchor, and she currently co-anchors the eleven o’clock news with Greg Glover.

Callie admits that while reporting the news it involves more than just notifying the public of needed information. There is the human aspect in which a journalist cannot remove themselves, yet they must still remain professional.

The April 27th tornadoes were an assemblage of events that made a great impact on Callie’s life.

“Paul Barys knew it would be a rough day. We had emails that were sent to us telling us to make sure we had our gear, make sure we had snacks - that it will be a long night,” Callie conveys.

“It was afternoon when it hit and all the crews were already out in the field. The first line that hit was in Tiftonia. There was some damage, but nothing that I hadn’t seen before. When I worked in Macon, there was a tornado that had come through but nothing too alarming and there were not a lot of injuries,” Callie says.

David Carroll was in the field anchoring when another line came barreling through the area. Unable to get a live shot because of the strong winds and pouring rain, the crew had to bring down the mast on the live truck and they all took shelter along with several local officials at the nearby fire hall.

“I had never seen anything like this in my life. As I watched the hail fall, there were birds actually falling from the sky. Every now and then you would see a bird plummet to the ground. That was when I thought, ‘This is a little different’,” Callie expresses.

“We packed up and went back to the station and they sent a photographer and I out towards the Collegedale/Apison area. That is when the line that did so much damage (and unfortunately took a lot of lives) came through. We were in the car when it hit and the best we could do was to not park under a tree,” Callie attests. 

“I remember the hail coming from both directions. I was sitting in the car a little scared not knowing what to expect,” Callie admits.

Her fear had passed over when the phone rang. It was the news room relaying what they were hearing on the scanners. Being a field reporter at the time, Callie was not experienced with the newsroom scanners.

“Those who were listening to them have said that the things they were hearing were life altering - people looking for children, the screams of families and the screams of first responders …it was pretty bad,” Callie confirms.

The news team spent the next hours trying to get to the damaged areas, but most of the roads were blocked.

“We did the best we could, but it wasn’t until the next day that we really got a look at just how bad it really was. From there it was non-stop for the next week,” she says.

Safety was a big concern as several in the newsroom kept their field reporters well-informed.

“When we were in Collegedale we had gotten a call not to go any further because it was obvious the storm was moving near us,” Callie says. “We were never out there by ourselves. There was a lot of correspondence and that is what kept us feeling comfortable with the situation.”

Callie wasn’t afraid that a tornado may touch down on the car, but just knowing what was happening around them was a bit unnerving.  There were definitely precautions taken as the newsroom crew made sure they knew where field crews were at all times that day.

“There were not a lot of opportunities for interviews in those moments because it was extremely dark with no power and with pouring rain. You go into this mode where you almost stop thinking about the interview because you are thinking, ‘what can I do to help?’ We definitely wanted to get out the information, but we didn’t want to get in the rescue crews' way. It was chaos as they were trying to climb into trees, using their flashlights, calling out to people and using hand saws trying to cut into trees,” Callie remembers.

That night was mostly reporting what the crews were seeing and hearing. The next few days Callie was scheduled to be off, but she went in to work instead.

“I couldn’t stand the thought of not being there - it was kind of an ‘all hands on deck’ situation,” Callie says.

The next period after the disaster had struck was recovery-mode. Instead of being sent back to Apison, Callie was dispatched to Ringgold where she would witness first-hand the destruction that made national news.

“I can still kind of close my eyes and put myself there,” she says. “When I saw Cherokee Valley Road for the first time, it was hard to even capture on camera. You would stand in place and shoot in one direction, but what you were seeing was literally all the way around you. It was hard to show on camera exactly what we were feeling, while not seeing anything left standing around us,” Callie expresses.

One of her first interviews with a man on Cherokee Valley Road that day who told her that his wife was taken to the hospital after a tree limb or some sort of debris had hit her. They were next door to the Black family who did not survive.

“I remember standing on the road talking to him and, as he recounted the moments he remembered, he just started to cry. It was one of those moments where you just had to stand there and let him cry and cry a little bit with him. It was an overwhelming experience just to imagine that he was there when it actually happened,” Callie admits.

“There were times when I would talk to somebody and I remember taking a few minutes to just be quiet. Occasionally I would take a walk or take a few minutes just to collect myself. You want to put out the information to people, but at the same time you are a human being. You are listening to parents who have lost their children and those who lost other members of their family. I think it is impossible not to feel their grief,” she acknowledges.

One year later, Callie reported on families who survived the tornados. She empathized as she saw new homes replacing damaged ones, but when she saw the trees were still snapped in two she knew recovery was still ongoing.

Another big event that Callie covered was the Tonya Craft trial. Callie had expected the trial to last a week or more, but it was ongoing for 23 days. “It was a time that I learned a lot about my life as a journalist and the court system in general. It was an incredible experience. Journalistic rights were tested on what kind of access we could have and a lot of the time we had to consult with our attorney to make sure we were getting fair access to what was going on. It was definitely a learning experience for me,” Callie insists.

Colleague David Carroll has been impressed with Callie’s dedication during her career and says, “Callie has accumulated more sources in four years than some reporters do in their whole careers. She comes up with her own stories and has an incredible work ethic.”

David has often told her that if she wanted, she could easily switch from being an anchor/reporter to being a news director. “She has great managerial potential leading the way in everything she does,” he confirms.

Callie and husband Brian had planned to stay in Chattanooga for a few years with the idea to reach a bigger market and to make their way back to Atlanta.

“We really fell in love with this city. It has a lot of character and it fits our personality,” Callie confides, “…and I won’t lie; I don’t miss the Atlanta traffic - that’s for sure!”

They have built a house and moved in last September. 

With the personality of an organizer and planner, Callie says, “I have learned you can’t always plan it all. Will I be in Chattanooga forever? I don’t know, but right now - it feels like home.”
