When the Klan came to Blacksburg

A KKK rally in Blacksburg in 1991 served as a reminder of Virginia Tech's complicated history with white supremacy.

  • Matt Jones, editor in chief

    Matt Jones

    Editor in Chief

The streets of Blacksburg were relatively quiet for most of the day on Jan. 20, 1991 — the day before the birthday of Martin Luther King Jr. The only people downtown at first were a handful of reporters and a number of state and local police officers.

By 1:15 p.m., uniformed police officers deployed along already-barricaded major roads downtown. Over 250 officers from different agencies were present, including a SWAT team, K-9 units and plainclothes officers. A helicopter hovered overhead.

In a few hours, 500 people filled the otherwise quiet streets for an event unlike any Blacksburg had seen since the late 1920s. At about 1:30 p.m., a Collegiate Times reporter watched about 35 state police officers walk to the parking lot of the Blacksburg Municipal Building.

A few minutes later, eight cars pulled into the parking lot, led by a white van flying the Confederate battle flag on top. The letters “KKK” had been painted on the side doors.

The Ku Klux Klan was coming to Blacksburg.

Blacksburg and white supremacy

The first iteration of the Ku Klux Klan was started in 1866 in Pulaski, Tennessee. The organization soon spread to local chapters called “klaverns” throughout the South, quickly becoming one of the most prominent white nationalist, violent organizations.

According to Encyclopedia Virginia, there were several violent incidents in 1868 attributed to the Klan. However, by the end of the year, public opinion had turned squarely against the secretive organization. Many newspapers that had initially condoned the group quickly shied away as the Klan’s violence came to light, and its prominence in the state faded for the time being.

The Virginia Agricultural and Mechanical College, now Virginia Tech, was established in 1872, after the first Klan’s peak in Virginia. But, references to the Klan can be found throughout the university’s early history.

The first commandant of cadets, former Confederate Gen. James H. Lane, later became a professor and commandant at Alabama Agricultural and Mechanical College, now Auburn University. A klavern based in Auburn, Alabama, that was in existence by 1923 was named after the general, according to “Mapping the Second Ku Klux Klan, 1915–1940,” a project of the Virginia Commonwealth University Libraries.

The Bugle yearbook from 1896 includes a page for a KKK student organization, although multiple historians have suggested that it may have been a distasteful joke. That page lists Claudius Lee, the namesake of Lee Hall, as the “Father of Terror.” However, the Klan was not active in Virginia at the time and would not be for several more decades.

The second iteration of the Klan, however, was active in Virginia and Blacksburg by the 1920s. According to a chapter from “A Special Place for 200 Years,” a historical book written for Blacksburg’s bicentennial, Klan activity in Blacksburg peaked in the late 1920s.

During those years, the KKK held bonfires on what is now Alumni Mall and the sports field for the Blacksburg High School. The KKK also burned a cross at a house on Lee Street. “Mapping the Second Ku Klux Klan” also found nearby active klaverns from around that time in Parrott, Christiansburg and Roanoke.

Virginia Polytechnic Institute (VPI), then the name of the university, also had a chapter of the Anglo-Saxon Clubs of America (ASCOA) during that period. The ASCOA was founded in 1922 by John Powell, a University of Virginia professor and musician, and Earnest Cox, a Methodist preacher. Powell, unsatisfied with the Klan, set out to create a white supremacist organization based on racial pseudo-science.

The organization slowly gathered steam and gained coverage in Virginia newspapers. In October 1923, the 15 posts of the ASCOA sent representatives to a convention in Richmond to hammer out the details of a national organization. An article about that first convention in the Oct. 12, 1923, issue of The Collegian, the student newspaper of the University of Richmond, lists chapters at many major Virginia universities: VPI, UVA, the University of Richmond, the College of William and Mary, the Virginia Military Institute, and Washington and Lee University.

The ASCOA spent much of its efforts in its first years lobbying for a racial integrity bill that would strictly prohibit marriage between people of different races. It succeeded with the Racial Integrity Act of 1924, the law that would be overturned in the landmark 1967 U.S. Supreme Court decision in Loving v. Virginia.

The UVA chapter had been founded by Powell and his friend Dr. Paul Barringer, a member of the school’s medical faculty and president of VPI from 1907 to 1913. Barringer had risen to national prominence in 1900 for a presentation to the Tri-State Medical Association in Charleston, South Carolina, titled “The American Negro: His Past and Future.” In that essay and other presentations, Barringer advocates racial eugenics and suggests that African Americans had been better off as slaves.

The VPI chapter was active as well, though somewhat briefly. Included in the papers of Powell, held in the University of Virginia Library, is a letter dated Feb. 2, 1924, noting M. O. Williams resigned as president of the V.P.I. chapter and from the club. Melvin Owens Williams Jr. was the secretary of the class of 1924, president of the campus YMCA and a mechanical engineering major, according to the Bugle yearbook from that year.

Although Klan membership nationwide declined over the next few decades, it regained ground in the 1950s and 1960s during the Civil Rights Movement. A church bombing in November 1966 was cited as possibly Klan-related. On Dec. 8, 1966, the Klan held a cross-burning near the Executive Mansion in Richmond, leading then-Gov. Mills Godwin Jr. to threaten a police and National Guard crackdown on the Klan in Virginia. Afterward, the group faded from prominence somewhat in the state.

New River Free Press

“As a long-term and national strategy to defeat racism, prejudice and chauvinism, the ‘ignore them’ strategy is doomed to fail.”

Editorial board of the New River Free Press

In 1985, the Klan began an organized recruitment drive, holding marches in multiple localities. In most instances, residents and journalists mostly ignored the activity. But in Blacksburg, there were red flags that made officials worry about what would happen.

Preparations

The Christiansburg chapter of the Christian Knights of the Ku Klux Klan filed for a parade permit in December 1990, scheduling its rally in Blacksburg for the day before Martin Luther King Jr. Day in January. The permit required approval by the Blacksburg Town Council, which voted on the measure as part of its consent agenda on Dec. 11.

Almost 100 Tech students showed up to the meeting that night to voice their concerns, according to The Roanoke Times. The public comment session for the consent agenda lasted an hour as students argued that allowing the march would irreversibly harm the university and the town.

“Action will be taken if action is invoked against black students on campus,” said Mark Walker, then-president of the campus chapter of the NAACP at the meeting.

Several students told the council that allowing the rally, in addition to the risk of violence, might drive minority students away from Tech. Some said that they planned to protest at the rally.

“Virginia Tech has gone through some severe, covert racism this year, and I think this would only put the icing on the cake,” Walker said, according to The Roanoke Times.

Three self-identified members of the Klan showed up as well, although they didn’t address the council. Larry Moore, a Christiansburg man who called himself the grand dragon of the Klan in Virginia, told The Roanoke Times that “we’re not here to harass anyone” and that Blacksburg residents had asked them to come.

The Town Council approved the consent agenda, including the parade permit, unanimously. Ronald Secrist, Blacksburg town manager, told the Collegiate Times afterward that the town’s hands were tied legally. The Klan had a right to peacefully protest.

However, the town could still put reasonable restrictions on the march. The event would be limited to two hours, marchers would have to consent to searches for weapons, distributing literature or recruiting during the march would be prohibited, and the route would be shortened by five blocks from the Klan’s initial application.

Meanwhile, university and town officials and community members set about their next task — avoiding a conflict.

The university spent time over the winter break brainstorming how to draw attention away from the Klan, including an alternative event or prominent speaker. David Nutter, then associate director for public affairs at Virginia Tech, told the Collegiate Times that the university would ignore the march. The only event would be a religious service, called a “Celebration of Unity,” at 10 a.m. that Sunday in Burruss Hall.

“People like that thrive on attention,” Nutter said. “By not giving them the attention that they want, we will deprive them of what they want the most.”

Initially, the Blacksburg Police Department scheduled a private meeting between the university and police on Jan. 11 as well to coordinate a response. However, it was canceled on Jan. 8 because of fears that the meeting would draw more attention to the event, which was already getting heavy coverage in the Collegiate Times and other media outlets.

And in the days before social media, newspapers and letters were one of the few ways to reach a large volume of people. Oscar Williams, president of the Montgomery County NAACP, sent a letter to the Collegiate Times published Jan. 11 asking residents, on behalf of the organization, to stay away.

“While certainly there are some problems at Tech, and NAACP is well aware of these problems, these problems, however, would be only exacerbated by hoodlums and outlaws such as the types the Klan is rallying and seeking to recruit,” Williams wrote. “Thus, NAACP asks the support of all right-thinking individuals (community citizens as well as students) and organizations to refrain from supporting the Klan’s efforts to form a Klavern in the area.”

T-shirt guy

“They got their hoods. We have our T-shirts”

Tim Clements

Blacksburg resident

Tech President James McComas and Blacksburg Mayor Roger Hedgepeth wrote a joint open letter to the community beforehand asking residents to stay away from the rally. Instead, they encouraged people to show up to the unity service in Burruss Hall.

The Blacksburg Town Council and Montgomery County Board of Supervisors passed resolutions condemning the Klan and supporting the boycott. The Blacksburg Ministerial Association also published a letter in The Roanoke Times supporting the boycott of the march.

However, others were not as sure of the boycott. The New River Free Press, a small monthly publication based in Blacksburg that was known for its social and environmental advocacy, ran an editorial advocating peacefully confronting the Klan. It noted that recent rallies in southwest Virginia had been met with resistance.

“To boycott the march, to turn away and withdraw to the campus or to the shelter of our homes and hand over the streets… is to grant power to a group of people that lusts for power and thrives on fear,” the editorial read. “As a long-term and national strategy to defeat racism, prejudice and chauvinism, the ‘ignore them’ strategy is doomed to fail.”

Meanwhile, the Blacksburg Police Department was preparing for different possible scenarios. Chief of Police Don Carey spent six weeks reviewing video from previous Klan marches in nearby areas and planning a response. Officers from Radford, Christiansburg, the Montgomery County Sheriff’s Office and the State Police would be brought in to help.

Others had their own way of preparing. Blacksburg resident Tim Clements designed and sold several dozen T-shirts for $10 each with a cartoon of a Klan member with a slash through it and the words “Just Say No.”

“They got their hoods. We have our t-shirts,” Clements told The Roanoke Times.

And then the Klan came

About 500 people showed up for the “Celebration of Unity” service honoring King and opposing the Klan in Burruss Auditorium at 10 a.m. on Jan. 20. President McComas quoted lines of “You’ve Got to Be Carefully Taught” from the musical “South Pacific” in his opening remarks, saying that intolerance is something people are taught.

“We show by our participation and presence here that we reject racism,” McComas said.

During the service, community members and university officials spoke, sang hymns and prayed with the group. Leaders from minority and religious groups across campus spoke to the assembled group, including Walker speaking for the NAACP, and Randy Lucas, chair of the Black Organizations Council.

“Will we be extremists for hate or love?” Walker said to the audience. “The decision is yours to make.”

Police began assembling in downtown Blacksburg before 1:15 p.m., spacing themselves out along the march route. Officers could be seen every 15 to 20 feet up Main Street, along Roanoke Street and down Draper Road in downtown Blacksburg.

The Roanoke Times likened the police buildup to the Gulf War. Operation Desert Storm had begun on Jan. 17; 4,000 showed up to a convocation that afternoon in Cassell Coliseum. A SWAT team and an armored vehicle were on hand in case violence broke out.

Meanwhile, Virginia Tech police officers stationed themselves on campus to attempt to dissuade students from getting involved. Thomas Goodale, vice president for student affairs, estimated that they convinced about half of the people heading toward the march to turn around.

Goodale, Secrist, Hedgepeth and Town Attorney Richard Kaufman watched the crowd assemble in the parking lot from the top floor of the Blacksburg Municipal Building. Thirty KKK marchers showed up in the eight cars, many of which had license plates from North Carolina or South Carolina.

Some of the Klan members put on white, green, purple or peach-colored robes before letting police search them for weapons. Several wore cone-shaped hats, but hoods were prohibited — if violence broke out, hoods would make it difficult to prosecute Klan members.

After a prayer, the marchers raised five Confederate battle flags and an American flag and walked into the street. Protesters lining the streets held up signs; many held small “End Racism” ones handed out before the Klan started. Some yelled “Go home” and other (sometimes crude) anti-racist messages at the marchers.

The Klan members chanted as they walked down Main Street as well: “What do we want?” “White supremacy!” “When do we want it?” “Right now!”

After 15 minutes, the Klan and crowd surrounding it found themselves in Draper’s Park, next to the Blacksburg Police Department. Police surrounding the marchers pushed the counter-protestors back as several Klan members spoke on a bullhorn, though they were sometimes drowned out by others yelling.

The Klan returned to their cars and left after only 22 minutes, Carey told a columnist for The Roanoke Times.

Moore, the grand dragon, told The Roanoke Times that there had been 256 supporters of the Klan in the crowd of between 500 and 600. However, he said none of the marchers were from Blacksburg. He also pinned the number of Klan members in Virginia at 5,600, telling the paper that “we have a lot of secret members.”

At the time, officials were publicly optimistic about how the rally had turned out. Police arrested only one person, a man who had thrown an egg at a Klan car. Carey told The Roanoke Times that Blacksburg’s approach could serve as a good model for other municipalities.

Goodale in 1991 had a regular column in the Collegiate Times, expressing his and the administration’s views on events on campus. His column on Jan. 25 recapped what had been a busy week.

On Jan. 16, Operation Desert Shield started in Iraq. On Jan. 20, Goodale attended the “Celebration of Unity” service before watching the march with other officials from town hall. Later that week, Goodale went to Richmond with President McComas and other leaders to attend a summit on “campus civility” called by Gov. Douglas Wilder.

“In the course of a week, our university community mobilized to talk of war at a quickly called convocation… to protest a group bent on violence and destruction of democratic principles, and to attend a meeting on campus civility at the seat of state government.” Goodale wrote. “Once again, we did it as a coalition of concerned persons.

“This is the stuff of what universities are made. I am proud to be part of it.”

The Southern Poverty Law Center, which tracks hate groups, says that there were 130 active KKK groups in the U.S. in 2016. These include groups in Richmond, Martinsville and Danville, Virginia. About 50 Klan members rallied in Charlottesville in July 2017, predating the “Unite the Right” rally in August that brought far-right groups and white supremacists together. Klan flyers appeared in neighborhoods in Leesburg, Staunton and Waynesboro in October and November 2017.

Blacksburg has seen other controversies related to white supremacy in recent years. In March 2017, leaflets with hand-drawn swastikas were left on the lawn of the Chabad Librescu Jewish Student Center at Virginia Tech. Several protests were staged during the fall semester regarding a graduate teaching assistant linked to white supremacist and threatening posts online. But, no Klan rallies.
