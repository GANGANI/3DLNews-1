Anger over pipelines spills into General Assembly

  • Michael Martz
  • Apr 18, 2018
  • Apr 18, 2018
  • 0
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Print
  • Save
Michael Martz

Intensifying public anger over the pending construction of two massive natural gas pipelines through Virginia boiled over into the General Assembly on Wednesday, when more than a dozen Democratic lawmakers asked Gov. Ralph Northam for more oversight of stream crossings and tree cutting and to protect the rights of landowners protesting the projects.

Organized by Del. Mark Keam, D-Fairfax, a news conference conducted before the assembly reconvened in its annual veto session brought lawmakers into the fight from other parts of the state that aren't affected directly by construction of the Atlantic Coast and Mountain Valley pipelines.

Del. Danica Roem, D-Prince William, was among a group of Northern Virginia Democrats who joined the protest, dismissing what she called "NOVA versus ROVA (Rest of Virginia) BS."

"It is our obligation to stand with them," Roem said.

The legislators joined protesters in waving posters that said "I stand with Red" in solidarity with a 61-year-old woman in the Roanoke Valley known publicly as Red, who, with her daughter, has been camping in trees on her Bent Mountain property to prevent crews from cutting trees in the path of the pipeline.

Longtime neighbor and family friend Genesis Chapman said he has spent last week "camping under Red's tree," with temperatures dipping into the mid-20s and "spitting snow." He accused Roanoke and state police of denying the women access to food and water, and "spotlighting (them) at night like animals.

"My community is under siege," said Chapman, a lifelong resident of Bent Mountain.

Sen. Chap Petersen, D-Fairfax City, a longtime critic of Dominion Energy, the managing partner of the Atlantic Coast Pipeline project, said he comes to the fight as a supporter of environmental protection and a lawyer for landowners to protect their private-property rights.

"Those trains are coming together," he said. "Yes, the governor needs to be involved."

Dominion spokesman Aaron Ruby issued a statement early on Wednesday that said the proposed 600-mile Atlantic Coast Pipeline had received "the most thorough regulatory review of any infrastructure project in Virginia history" and claimed that opponents' "delaying tactics" would "will cost consumers and businesses hundreds of millions of dollars in higher energy costs."

"It will slow down our region’s transition from coal to cleaner energy sources, delaying improvements to our environment," Ruby said.

Legislators and protesters accused the Virginia Department of Environmental Quality of doing too little to ensure that construction of the proposed pipelines will not irreparably harm stream crossings the state decided to let the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers oversee under a nationwide construction permit.

"The people who should be watching this is our Department of Environmental Quality," said Del. Chris Hurst, D-Montgomery, one of several freshmen delegates who joined the protest and one of three Roanoke-area legislators who were thwarted in legislative efforts this year to impose tighter controls over the projects.

Sen. John Edwards, D-Roanoke, said the proposed 303-mile Mountain Valley Pipeline "could ruin our way of life," but said Northam had "pledged to look at the science" in determining whether the projects would harm the environment.

Spokesman Brian Coy defended the state's regulatory process for the pipeline projects and said the governor has "directed (agencies) to follow the law and meet the most rigorous standards the law allows."

In its 25-year history as an agency, DEQ has never conducted a review of a project on the scale of either the Mountain Valley or Atlantic Coast pipelines, a DEQ spokeswoman acknowledged earlier this month. And the Virginia State Water Control Board has opened up a public comment period on whether the Army Corps of Engineers permit for stream crossings is adequate to protect Virginia waters, with some board members pushing for the DEQ to do its own review.

David Sligh, an environmental lawyer for Wild Virginia and leading opponent of the Dominion-led pipeline, responded that public utility customers "will pay dearly for the cost of this unnecessary project," which he contends would produce "huge profits" for its developers but few benefits for utility ratepayers.

"The state of Virginia will only be stepping up to do the job it was obligated to do from the start if they refuse to accept the Corps of Engineers’ rubber stamp approvals for waterbody crossings," Sligh said.

Dennis Martire, mid-Atlantic vice president of the Laborers International Union of North America, which represents many of the workers who hope to help build the projects, derided the call for more review of water crossings as an attempt to "distort and politicize the regulatory process" and to "take food off the tables of working-class men and women in Virginia."

"No doubt, the next thing they’ll demand is a pebble-by-pebble analysis," Martire said.

Natalie Cox, a spokeswoman for EQT Midstream Partners, which is building the Mountain Valley Pipeline, said there “may be individuals who will always oppose the safe construction” of the project, which she added has been subject to major review efforts by officials and regulatory agencies and “hundreds of route adjustments and modifications” over the past three years to address landowners’ and community concerns.

“We respect opponents' right to peacefully and lawfully protest,” Cox said. “At the same time, however, it is important to note that all work for the MVP project has been authorized by federal and state agencies, and the Virginia DEQ has imposed on MVP the most stringent oversight of a natural gas pipeline project in the department's history.”

Cox said the company’s top priority “remains the safety of our workers, contractors, law enforcement officers, and people in communities along the route.”
