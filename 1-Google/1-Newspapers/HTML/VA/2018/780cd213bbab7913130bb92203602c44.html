GMU law school under scrutiny for possible Federalist Society ties

  • By Angela Woolsey / Fairfax County Times
  • May 11, 2018
  • May 11, 2018 Updated May 11, 2018

George Mason University’s Antonin Scalia Law School has allowed the Federalist Society to influence hiring, program development, and student funding and admissions, GMU law school alumni say.

The activist campaign UnKoch My Campus released more than 700 pages of emails and other documents on April 30 that suggest strong ties between the Fairfax-based public university’s law school and the Federalist Society for Law and Public Policy Studies, a nonprofit that promotes conservative and libertarian principles.

Compiled through Freedom of Information Act requests filed by GMU Law School alumna Allison Pienta and the student group Transparent GMU, the documents show Antonin Scalia Law School Dean Henry Butler and other university administrators discussing the law school’s renaming, faculty hires, prospective students, and candidates for federal clerkships with members of the Federalist Society, including executive vice president Leonard Leo.

“As an alum, and as a Virginia resident and taxpayer, I think it’s a really serious intrusion into the academic independence of a public institution,” Pienta said. “That’s what we need to remember: that this is a public institution and they need to be fully transparent to their faculty, their students, their alumni, and to their taxpayers about what they’re doing and who has an influence within their school.”

Pienta, who graduated from GMU’s law school in 2004, says that she started researching the role of donors in the school after George Mason announced on March 31, 2016 that it had received $30 million in gifts, which prompted the university to rename the law school after the late Supreme Court justice.

The donation combined $10 million from the Charles Koch Foundation and $20 million from an anonymous donor who contacted the school through Leo and requested that the law school take on Scalia’s name, according to a news release from the law school.

Reported to be the largest combined gift in GMU history, the money went toward establishing three new scholarships for law school students.

When it announced the law school’s renaming, the university also released a copy of the grant agreement that it had signed with the George Mason University Foundation, which manages private donations on the university’s behalf, and the anonymous donor, who was identified solely as a U.S. citizen since their name had been redacted.

According to the agreement, the donation would help create a merit-based scholarship program for approximately 57 students per academic year through 2021, and the law school would provide funding for at least 10 years for 12 new faculty positions, additional staff positions, and support for its Center for the Study of the Administrative State and the Center for Liberty and Law.

According to their websites, the Center for the Study of the Administrative State examines “the problems of administrative accountability” in the federal government, while the Liberty and Law Center challenges “government encroachment upon liberty.”

The anonymous donor’s $20 million grant was divided into installments of up to $4 million each that would be contributed once a year through May 1, 2020, according to the payment schedule dictated in the grant agreement.

The agreement also contains a section for third-party beneficiaries. Antonin Scalia’s estate was given the right to direct GMU to stop the use of the justice’s name if “the school has changed such that its continued use of the name would reflect unfavorably upon the reputation or legacy of the Justice.”

A second third-party beneficiary was included in the agreement, but as it was originally released, the party’s name was redacted.

Pienta says that she and another GMU Law alum sent FOIA requests that eventually led George Mason to release a version of the grant agreement with the anonymous donor that showed the name of the redacted third-party beneficiary.

The two alumni and Transparent GMU, a student-led campaign that has been advocating for more transparency around the university’s relationship with donors since 2014, provided the un-redacted grant agreement to BuzzFeed News for an article on the Federalist Society that reporter Zoe Tillman published on Nov. 20, 2017.

The un-redacted agreement, which is still online, identifies a Virginia non-stock corporation called the BH Fund as a third-party beneficiary, saying that the anonymous donor had previously agreed to give the fund “the right to enforce all of the donor’s rights” in the agreement.

The agreement also allowed the BH Fund to “review and approve the text of any proposed publicity” about the law school’s conversion to the Antonin Scalia Law School, and the donor was given the right to terminate the agreement if they determine the school “is no longer principally focused on the school’s mission.”

According to the Virginia State Corporation Commission, the BH Fund became an active corporation on Jan. 14, 2016, and its principal office is located at 45 North Hill Dr. in Warrenton. Its registered agent or office is identified as North Rock Reports LLC.

A 2012 article in the Boston Globe describes North Rock Reports as a Federal Election Commission compliance consultant to Republican candidates and causes. VSCC listings show that the group is also linked to a corporation called Protect America’s Consumers, which a Jan. 7, 2016 Politico article describes as a “front group taking aim at the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau.”

Pienta’s research showed that the BH Fund was organized by the law firm Holtzman Vogel Josefiak & Torchinsky, which is located at the same address listed for the corporation. The firm counts former George W. Bush deputy chief of staff Karl Rove, Sen. Marco Rubio (R-Fla.) and the National Republican Congressional Committee among its past clients, according to Politico.

According to Pienta, the BH Fund’s president is Leo and its secretary-treasurer is Jonathan Bunch, the Federalist Society’s vice president and director of external relations.

Leo confirmed his connection in the BuzzFeed News article, where he explained that he is “responsible for making sure that the law school remains true to its principles over the course of the gift.”

Neither Leo nor the Federalist Society returned Fairfax County Times requests for comment.

GMU Foundation president Janet Bingham, who also serves as vice president for GMU’s Office of University Advancement and Alumni Relations, says that the foundation allows for the inclusion of third-party beneficiary provisions in gift agreements as a mechanism for the donor or a party designated by the donor to enforce the terms of the agreement.

In other words, the provision gives the donor or their designated beneficiary the ability to take legal action if they determine the university is not honoring the terms of the agreement.

“This language is used in donor agreements from time to time,” Bingham said. “Normally, without this language, donors do not have the right to enforce the terms and conditions of a gift like this one…There have been lawsuits around the country by donors who contend universities are not [or] have not done what was agreed to.”

Pienta, Transparent GMU, and UnKoch My Campus, which includes GMU graduate and former Transparent GMU president Samantha Parsons among its founders, say that the released email communications between Leo and other Federalist Society members and university officials demonstrate that the conservative organization was involved in developing law school programs, directing grant funding, selecting faculty, and determining student admissions.

The grant agreement between GMU, the foundation, and the anonymous donor to the law school’s name change specifies that “the school has sole and absolute discretion to determine and carry out all selection, research, scholarship, teaching and service at the school.”

Emails between Leo and Scalia Law School Dean Henry Butler from Oct. 27, 2015 show the Federalist Society executive vice president recommending a prospect for an adjunct faculty position, though details about the person’s identity have been redacted.

Leo also sent an email to Butler regarding a potential hire on May 6, 2016, and he describes a man identified as Pat Hofer who is “under consideration” as “smart, affable, [and] strategic” in a Nov. 3, 2016 email to Butler.

Butler notified Leo on Sept. 15, 2016 that national and homeland security expert Rachel Brand had been unanimously approved as a new faculty member, noting that “bureaucratic reasons” prevented the university from formally offering her a job until Sept. 26 of that year.

Brand is now an associate professor at the Scalia Law School’s National Security Institute. She was sworn in as an associate attorney general by U.S. Attorney General Jeff Sessions on May 22, 2017 but resigned from the position about nine months later.

According to her faculty biography, Brand serves as chairman of the Federalist Society’s litigation practice group, and she is listed as a contributor on the organization’s website.

The released emails from UnKoch My Campus show that Butler intended to work with GMU’s admissions office to meet a prospective student after Leo sent him the student’s resume on Aug. 2, 2015.

In the email, Leo says that the student has “doing very good work” at “RAGA,” the acronym for the Republican Attorneys General Association, and that “his father is a senior executive at [redacted].” The student had already applied to Mason and was visiting that Friday.

The law school’s communications with Federalist Society representatives appear to also extend to coordinating on student programs.

An email dated May 2, 2017 shows GMU Law and Economics Center executive director Todd Zywicki introducing Bunch to Ryan Lodata, who served as the LEC’s outreach coordinator for the center’s Attorneys General Education Program and a prospective “Federal Judge’s Initiative.”

“Ryan will be the one keeping track of nominations, confirmations, and recruitment of judges for our programs,” Zywicki said in the email to Bunch. “He may be in touch with you from time-to-time to compare notes and the like. Thanks for your offer to help us out with these projects.”

Lodata, who is currently listed in GMU’s directory as a research coordinator for the Scalia Law School, then thanked Bunch for his “offer of assistance” on the initiatives and said that he looked forward to working with him.

In another email to Bunch dated June 5, 2017, Scalia Law School associate professor J.W. Verret connects the Federalist Society external relations vice president with career counselor Anna Francis, who runs the law school’s judicial clerkship program.

“We are hoping to place Scalia Law alumni who are current members of our Fed Soc student chapter, alumni who were active in Fed Soc, and other Scalia Law conservative and libertarian alums in federal clerkships,” Verret says in the email to Bunch and Francis.

Pienta says that she was aware that many professors in GMU’s law school had a “conservative bent” when she attended, but she was surprised to see how frequently administrators seemed to communicate with members of outside organizations like the Federalist Society and the Charles Koch Foundation in the emails she obtained through FOIA.

“There was a regular line of communication between Leonard Leo and Dean Butler,” Pienta said.

Butler disputed the notion that his communications with Leo amount to evidence that the Federalist Society has significant influence at the Scalia Law School.

“Any accusation that the Federalist Society has had undue influence on the law school is unfounded,” Butler said in a message posted on the law school’s website on May 4. “While we are pleased to receive recommendations from many sources, decisions on faculty hiring and student admissions and scholarships are independent and strictly the purview of the law school’s faculty and administrative leadership.”

The law school dean says that the prospective student whose resume Leo shared was admitted but decided to enroll in a different law school after not qualifying for merit-based support.

According to Butler, of the potential faculty hires discussed in the emails, only Brand was ultimately hired as a term professor after approaching the school and receiving strong support from the school’s faculty appointments committee.

In response in which Verret suggested helping conservative and libertarian students and alumni find judicial clerkships, Butler says that he is “not going to apologize for using our connections with the Federalist Society, or any other group or individual, to help our students receive clerkships. Networking plays a vital role in today’s legal market.”

Pienta argues that the communications between law school administrators and Federalist Society leaders go beyond mere networking, especially if Leo’s BH Fund has the power to terminate the school’s grant agreement with the anonymous donor.

“If he’s holding the purse strings and can withhold the funding installments of each year, that’s a little bit more than networking,” the GMU law school graduate said.

Butler denied that the Federalist Society was the anonymous donor and dismissed UnKoch My Campus’ allegations related to the BH Fund as “disjointed, including the bizarre assertion that the fund is somehow a third-party beneficiary.”

As previously stated, the un-redacted grant agreement for the law school renaming made available by BuzzFeed News last year names the BH Fund in the section regarding third-party beneficiaries.

GMU Global Antitrust Institute Executive Director Joshua Wright said in a Tweet on May 2 that he chairs the Scalia Law School’s faculty selection committee.

“I’ve never discussed with or been contacted by a donor about a potential faculty hire,” the former Federal Trade Commission member said before praising the quality of the school’s faculty and recent hires.

Wright singled out assistant professors Megan Stevenson, Jennifer Mascott, Jordan Neyland, Paolo Saguato, Murat Mungan, Eugene Kontorovich, and Caroline Cecot, associate professor James Cooper, professor Nuno Garoupa, and new hire Robert Leider as examples that the school “has a very bright future.”

All of those faculty members except for Garoupa, Mungan, and Neyland are listed as contributors to the Federalist Society, meaning that they have spoken or otherwise participated in the organization’s events, publications or multimedia presentations.

“A person’s appearance on this list does not imply any other endorsement or relationship between the person and the Federalist Society,” the organization’s website says of its contributors.

Wright did not return a request for comment from the Fairfax County Times.

UnKoch My Campus, which aims to show the influence of wealthy donors and corporations on academia, released the documents related to Scalia Law School shortly after GMU President Ángel Cabrera made some past gift agreements with donors involving the university’s economics department publicly available on April 27, admitting in an email to faculty that they “fall short of the standards of academic independence” he expects.

The university and the GMU Foundation are also in the middle of a lawsuit filed in February 2017 by Transparent GMU, which is seeking access to donor agreements involving the Koch Foundation under Virginia’s FOIA laws.

The foundation has denied the group’s past FOIA requests on the grounds that it is a separate, private entity and therefore not subject to freedom of information laws.

The case went to trial at the Fairfax County Courthouse on April 24.

“Legal counsel does not consider the documents released on Friday, April 27 to have an impact on the lawsuit,” UnKoch My Campus said in a press release distributed during a press conference held on May 4 by GMU students, faculty, and alumni.

Cabrera called for the university to conduct a full review of all active gift agreements in an email to faculty on April 30 and met with the GMU faculty senate on May 1 and 4.

According to GMU Faculty Senate Chair Keith Renshaw, a chair and associate professor for the psychology department, the senate unanimously approved a motion during its May 4 meeting that called on GMU to review all existing gift agreements that support faculty positions, student scholarships, or academic programs and curriculum, as well as the university’s current gift acceptance practices and policies.

The motion, which has been posted to the faculty senate’s website, says that the review will be conducted by a task force with representatives from the GMU Board of Visitors, the administration, student body, and faculty. It also requires that at least one person from a third-party entity independent of the university participate in the review.

“The faculty is deeply disturbed by the recent revelations of these gift agreements,” Renshaw said in remarks to the Board of Visitors during its May 3 meeting. “…In no instance should a philanthropic donor to a university have any influence over academics. That includes curriculum, faculty hiring, faculty firing, and faculty or student research and scholarship. The ideology driving the influence is not what matters. Academic independence, academic freedom and academic integrity are what matter.”
