    Locals remember Martin Luther King Jr. and his violent end

    • By EVAN GOODENOW | The Winchester Star
    • Apr 4, 2018
    • Apr 4, 2018
    • 0
    • Facebook
    • Twitter
    • WhatsApp
    • SMS
    • Email
    • Facebook
    • Twitter

    • Save

    WINCHESTER — Fifty years ago today, Tommy Dixon, then a 15-year-old freshman at Handley High School, arrived home from track practice and heard breaking news on television.

    CBS news anchor Walter Cronkite announced that the Rev. Martin Luther King Jr. had been assassinated in Memphis, Tenn., where King had been supporting striking sanitation workers.

    Dixon, who would go on to become Handley’s first black basketball coach, serving from 1986 to 2013, recalls feeling fear and sadness.

    Part of the class that helped integrate Handley, Dixon said his parents and brother had helped shield him from encountering racism firsthand. On April 4, 1968, he saw how it martyred the man who led the nonviolent struggle for racial equality and become the conscience of a nation.

    Dixon and three of his siblings would become the first in their family to attend college. He credits King with helping pave the way for opportunity for black people like himself and his family through the fight for the 1964 Civil Rights Act and the 1965 Voting Rights Act.

    Dixon said the election of President Barack Obama, the nation’s first black president, wouldn’t have been possible without King.

    “A lot of people owe him a lot because of what he stood up for and what he meant to this country,” Dixon said in a recent interview.

    Dixon said he never would’ve believed America would elect a black president, adding that there has been great progress in race relations. But he also acknowledged that the backlash against the civil rights movement by some white people during King’s time continues today.

    “They feel the civil rights movement was trying to take something from them and steal from them, which it wasn’t,” he said. “It was just trying to make sure everybody was on an even plane.”

    ♦

    In the decades since King was slain, some historians say his memory has been sanitized, with the main historical focus on his “I Have a Dream” speech and fight for racial equality for black people. They note there has been little mention of his fight against income inequality among all races through the Poor People’s March, which occurred after his death, and his opposition to the Vietnam War.

    “If America’s soul becomes totally poisoned, part of the autopsy must read: Vietnam,” King said in 1967.

    While King has become revered in the decades since his assassination, during his lifetime he had critics on the left and the right. Some conservatives branded him a Communist and rabble rouser. Some liberals, like President Lyndon Johnson, were bitter when King denounced the Vietnam War.

    Angry at continued institutional racism and stubborn resistance to the civil rights movement, some radical Black Power activists disowned King and the nonviolent resistance he modeled after Indian leader Mahatma Gandhi’s struggle for independence from British colonialism. After a March 28, 1968, march led by King in Memphis on behalf of striking sanitation workers turned violent, critics who accused King of being an “outside agitator” pounced.

    A syndicated editorial cartoon of King in the Winchester Evening Star that hit the streets April 4, 1968, hours before his death, echoed that sentiment. In the cartoon, King is seen firing a revolver while a fake halo is attached to his back.

    In clouds of gun smoke, the words “looting”, “violence” and “trouble” are spelled out. “I’m not firing it — I’m only pulling the trigger,” says the quote above King.

    John Copenhaver, head of the Winchester-based Valley Interfaith Council, which promotes religious solidarity and social justice, said ideas like those expressed in the cartoon mischaracterize King’s approach of “militant nonviolence.”

    Copenhaver, 69, a retired Shenandoah University religious philosophy professor who has studied King’s life and speeches, said that rather than provoking violence, King was exposing the violence that helped maintain segregation.

    “King believed, and so did Gandhi, that nonviolence was not some cushy kind of lovey-dovey thing. You’re exercising discipline and courage to do it, and your loving confrontation with violence is going to illicit anger,” Copenhaver said. “People don’t like to be confronted, and they don’t want to see their own racism.”

    Copenhaver said King’s approach inspired him to go from being an Army reservist to conscientious objector.

    Like Copenhaver, Donald Finley Sr., 62, was inspired by King’s religious convictions. Finley recalled being angry and sad after the assassination.

    “It felt like we had lost a voice,” he said. “We had lost someone who was actively making very positive change.”

    Finley, who would go on to become Winchester’s first elected black councilman, serving from 1992-98, said he didn’t know much about politics at the time of the killing. As he grew up and learned about King, Finley said he came to suspect that assassin James Earl Ray didn’t act alone. Finley is also critical of FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover, who led the FBI’s harassment and surveillance of King.

    Under the approval of then-Attorney General Robert Kennedy, the FBI had been wiretapping King since 1961.

    In the weeks after King’s assassination, Finley remembers being dismayed as he watched on television as riots engulfed many American cities, including Baltimore and Washington, D.C. But Winchester remained peaceful. Some residents honored King in a memorial service at the Market Street Methodist Church on April 7, 1968. It was part of a day of national mourning declared by President Johnson.

    ♦

    Reaction to King’s murder was mixed in letters to the editor in The Star. Two letter writers praised a speech by Democratic West Virginia Sen. Robert C. Byrd on the Senate floor that was published after the assassination.

    In the speech, Byrd, a former Ku Klux Klan member, condemned the assassination. But he then went on to blame King for his own death because King defied a federal injunction against a second march in Memphis.

    “He usually spoke of nonviolence. Yet violence all too often attended his actions,” Byrd wrote. “And at the last, he himself met a violent end.”

    But one letter writer praised King.

    “We know that his family misses him and we feel very sorry for this,” Winchester resident Vivian Kline wrote. “We are thankful though, for a martyr in our time who has made it possible for us to start down the mountain, on the other side, side by side and hand in hand, together.”

    Copenhaver, then a freshman attending Washington and Lee University in Lexington, remembers being one of the few white people to take part in a march honoring King in Roanoke, Copenhaver’s hometown.

    Judy Humbert, who would go on to serve two terms on the Winchester School Board, was a clerk-typist in Harrisburg, Pa., where she moved to from Winchester to find work. Like many black people, Humbert said she felt disheartened and disillusioned after learning of the killing.

    “We called him our leader because he looked like us and spoke like us,” said Humbert, 71. “We were just kind of disappointed in America that that would happen.”

    Humbert, who co-authored a book about the Douglas School, which she and other black children in Winchester attended during segregation and for 10 years after the 1954 Brown v. Board of Education Supreme Court decision outlawed segregated schools, said King’s speeches still move her now as they did then.

    Forbidden from using the segregated Handley Library as a child, Humbert said there has been significant progress since King’s death, but she still encounters racism.

    Humbert said in recent years white boys traveling by her house have screamed racial slurs at her as she sat on her porch in the summer. And she sees how some white people cringe when they encounter her.

    “We’re still fighting [against] racism,” she said. “It’s not as bad as it was, but let’s not forget it’s still here.”

    Finley said he tries to be color blind and loves Winchester. But he said progress has been slow.

    He noted that it wasn’t until 2015 that the Confederate battle flag, a painful reminder to many of slavery, was removed from the Winchester city seal. The removal was prompted by the killing of nine black people in a church in Charleston, S.C. Shooter Dylann Roof, a white supremacist, had prominently displayed the flag on social media before the killings. The Confederate battle flag was replaced on Winchester’s seal with a Confederate government flag, better known as the Stars and Bars. Three other flags, the American flag, the Virginia flag and the British Union Jack, also are part of the seal.

    While slow to change, Finley said Winchester, part of the “massive resistance” movement against desegregation, has had more positive change than some surrounding communities. Fifty years after his death, Finley said King is still a role model for that change.

    “Martin Luther King gave us hope,” he said. “We’re not where we want to be, but we sure aren’t where we used to be.”
