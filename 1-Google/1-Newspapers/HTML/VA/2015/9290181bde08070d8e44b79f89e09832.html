Natural Bridge owner outlines plan to correct money woes

  • BY LUANNE RIFE Roanoke Times
  • Oct 11, 2015
  • Oct 11, 2015
  • 0

BY LUANNE RIFE Roanoke Times

NATURAL BRIDGE — The 215-foot iconic arch carved out of limestone is timeless, as is the forest and streams that unfold across the property’s 1,600 acres.

But much of what people have built is stuck in a time warp waiting for its new owner to blow off the dust and pull the dated resort into a more modern era.

Last week, the Rockbridge County property’s former owner, Angelo Puglisi, donated the bridge and sold all the other buildings and land to the newly formed Virginia Conservation Legacy Fund. The transaction requires that once VCLF pays off a $9.1 million loan most of the property will be deeded to Virginia to be run as a state park.

The VCLF will keep the hotel, cottages and caverns. Plans are underway to update the hotel’s appearance and business model and to offer attractions for those seeking outdoors and historic quests rather than a chance to add to their tchotchke collection.

Tom Clarke, head of the VCLF, on Friday explained some of what he and his team has in mind.

The first round of consultants looked at the Natural Bridge Hotel and told its new owner to tear it down. “Most people had a vision of a Greenbrier, Homestead type of thing,” Clark said. “For us, that was a little too elitist. What we saw was a hotel that could be a destination for the working person, for the families.”

He had a different vision that counters exclusivity. “The whole thing we’d been thinking in our mind was marketing the family value package at $119.99. If you’ve got three kids, we’ll put a roll-away in the room. You get free attraction tickets, continental breakfast in the room.”

And he sure wouldn’t mind if travelers along Interstate 81 pulled into the Natural Bridge Hotel at $75 a pop instead of spending the night in a roadside motel.

Clarke stood in the hotel lobby explaining the quick changes (power washing the outside, updates to the lobby) that would occur before the hotel reopens March 14, and his vision for the surrounding acreage (trails, tennis and basketball courts, access to the James River for canoeing and fishing). As he spoke, the desk phone began to ring. He moved to answer it, jotting down a name and phone number to take a reservation.

“That’s revenue,” he said, rather pleased at working the desk. Clarke said four major events already have been booked, and though the hotel was closed in January to prepare for the transition, VCLF is honoring all previously booked events. The Rockbridge County Hunt Club is there today.

Clarke expects the hotel will remain open year-round, but will operate differently than in the past when 50 employees might be on hand to wait on five guests. It’s booked solid during the summer.

“The problem is come December, January, February, occupancy is less than 10 percent,” he said. The plan is to attract academic, government and organization meetings.

During the past several months, Clarke, who lives in Buchanan, has dropped by the Natural Bridge several times a week and noted many outdated, frustrating business practices. “There are so many ways to improve,” he said.

“I came on Labor Day, and the parking lot is filled, and I come into the gift shop. There must have been a thousand people in the gift shop, literally. And the grill was closed. I asked the lady, ‘Why is the grill closed,’ and she said. ‘Oh, we close at 2 o’clock.’ On Labor Day? 2 o’clock?”

Another time, he spotted a dozen motorcyclists who had checked in but were just standing outside while the Little Red Fox Tavern had already closed. It was 9:15 on a Friday night.

Perhaps one of the biggest changes visitors will notice is planned for the massive gift shop.

“I tease that there can’t be any more products from China than are in that gift shop — 40-foot containers, from Shanghai to Natural Bridge,” he said. Instead, Clarke plans to stock the space with the work of Virginia artisans, and envisions stocking wine from all 250 Virginia wineries.

All of these things could help to retire the note more quickly as 100 percent of the revenue from the bridge and its concessions goes to debt payments.

Clarke expects the bridge to reopen March 1 and the hotel two weeks later. When it closed, 80 to 90 people lost their jobs. Some have been rehired, and the process is continuing. Not all will return, Clarke said, as some positions are obsolete.

VCLF will bring the National Bridge Hotel into the Internet reservation age and electronic payroll, for instance.

By mid-March, he expects to have hired about 50 employees, with the number projected to grow to 200 as the business grows. VCLF already has sold 10 percent interest in the hotel for $750,000 to fund its startup, and expects to raise the $5 million needed for renovations this way.

As the business model changes, so, too, will the experience. Clarke is working closely with the state Department of Conservation and Recreation on interpretive signs, exhibits and planning a trail network.

“They are working with us and making sure that what they give us is what we want,” said Joe Elton, DCR interim director.

Elton said that a number of old roads and horse trails were uncovered during recent surveys and that DCR is working with Clarke’s team to determine which would work best for hiking, biking and riding, and even for the disabled to travel in motorized carts to enjoy the vistas.

Work is already underway on exhibits that fit into the state park system. Elton has introduced Clarke to the chief of the Monacan Nation so that their story can be better told in the village.

Both Clarke and Elton can rattle off many ideas for the park, such as having premiere biking trails, uncovering artifacts that have been tossed aside since Thomas Jefferson’s day and showcasing the story of electricity through the antiquated infrastructure and an old hydro-electric dam that was recently discovered.

“This is one of the best projects I’ve been involved in,” Elton said. “It’s early in the game, but it looks good.”
