WOODY: Redskins’ strength coach John Hastings gets a liver transplant

  • By PAUL WOODY
    Richmond Times-Dispatch
  • Nov 25, 2004
  • Nov 25, 2004

By PAUL WOODY
Richmond Times-Dispatch

Although you would never know it to look at him and would get no indication of it from talking to him, John Hastings spent almost every day for the past 28 years waking and wondering if it would be his last day of life.

Hastings, the strength coach for the Washington Redskins, was dangerously ill. When you live like that, you tend to get a little numb to the idea of death. You reach a point where you know how sick you are, but since you've kept going all those years, you figure you'll keep going for a while longer.

Last May, though, doctors at the University of Pittsburgh told Hastings he had three months to live. Hastings, 40, needed a liver transplant. Without it, the end he always knew was awaiting him was closing in at a more rapid pace than he realized.

Hastings had done nothing wrong. His liver problem was not self-induced. It came about because of a cruel quirk of fate.

When he was 12 years old, Hastings got sick. At first, doctors in his hometown of Lancaster, Pa., thought he had mononucleosis. When Hastings didn't get well, the diagnosis changed to colitis. At one point, the idea that he had Crohn's disease was put forward.

But no matter what the diagnosis, Hastings did not get well and stay well.

Hastings' parents, Jack and Monna Hastings, were frustrated but persistent. Their persistence led them to the National Institutes of Health, where a diagnosis finally was made.

John Hastings had chronic autoimmune hepatitis. His immune system saw his liver as a foreign body and attacked it.

Something in Hastings' genetic makeup that was not present in any other member of his family, past or present, had brought on the illness.

As a teen-ager, Hastings was in and out of the hospital for days and weeks at a time. He spent two years at home, receiving his education from tutors because he was too sick to go to school.

Somehow, he managed to play high school football. He graduated from Ohio University, where he did not play football, but he did play rugby.

"Against the doctor's wishes," Hastings said.

"You learn to cope. When it's all you know, you either make it work or you don't. You either stay at home and do nothing or you work and try to stay as active as you can"

There was a time in college, though, when Hastings' lack of liver function caused toxins to accumulate in his system to such an extent that Hastings could not even get out of bed.

"We would have to bring him home, take him to the NIH and get him stabilized, then take him back to college," Monna Hastings said.

"I frankly don't know how he got through college. His attitude and determination enabled him to do it He learned early on that the more exercise and physical stress he put his body, the better he felt. He'd find ways to keep himself going."

After one of his teen-age stays in the hospital, Hastings picked up a copy of Muscle and Fitness magazine. It occurred to him that weight lifting might help compensate for the wasting element of his illness. Hastings became a workout warrior. Eventually, he became a fitness trainer in a private gym, then a gym owner, then a strength coach for the San Diego Chargers.

"It's funny how things come full circle, how you are a product of your environment," Hastings said. "I've always had an interest in weight training, probably because of this disease."

Chronic autoimmune hepatitis does not go away. Hastings was taking 30 pills a day, but the disease took its toll. The medicine suppressed his immune system, which left him vulnerable to other infections, which would send him to hospital for as long as a week at a time.

"You don't want to think the worst, but sometimes you can't help it," said Lori Hastings, John's wife of 10 years.

Lori and John Hastings have two children, a son Alex, 8, and a daughter Taylor, 6

"When he would go in the hospital, sometimes I would think, 'What's going to happen if he leaves us?'" Lori Hastings said. "You think about the rest of our lives."

Hastings had been on a liver transplant list in San Diego, but he lost his job with the Chargers when Marty Schottenheimer took over as the head coach.

Schottenheimer had been fired by the Redskins and replaced by Steve Spurrier. Spurrier's new strength coach, Chip Morton, hired Hastings as his assistant. The hiring came just in time. Hastings' insurance coverage from his Chargers' contract was about to lapse.

To John Hastings, this all seemed to be part of some grander plan.

"For years, people had told me I'd never get a transplant on the West Coast," Hastings said. "There were too many people and not enough donations. People told me I needed to move to the East Coast, but I wasn't going to leave my job with the Chargers. And then I got the job with the Redskins."

Hastings spent two years on the transplant waiting list in Northern Virginia. Finally, at the urging of doctors in Northern Virginia, Hastings went to the University of Pittsburgh, one of the premier transplant programs in the country.

Hastings thought he was going to Pittsburgh for another evaluation and a spot on another waiting list. He had been waiting six years for a new liver; he figured he could wait a while longer.

What Hastings learned in Pittsburgh, though, was bad news. His disease had advanced to a critical stage. Hastings went to the top of the transplant list. Then came the good news. Ten days later, Hastings was told a liver had become available for him.

Hastings was one of the lucky few. According to Anne Paschke, a representative of the United Network for Organ Sharing, there are 17,489 people on the waiting list for a liver transplant. In 2003, 5,671 transplants were performed.

Hastings underwent 10 hours of surgery, followed by several days in a medically induced coma, seven days in intensive care and two more days in regular hospital care. He then spent a month at the hospital's "family house" so he could receive regular evaluations by the hospital medical staff.

Redskins coach Joe Gibbs called. Special-teams coach Danny Smith came to visit. Redskins owner Dan Snyder phoned several times. Snyder told Hastings not to worry about his job and not to come back to work until he was fully recovered.

"The Redskins," Lori Hastings said, "were awesome."

Two months after the transplant, Hastings was back in the Redskins' weight room.

"I feel better than I have in years," he said. "When I was a kid, I never imagined myself living this long. I know that sounds morbid. What it does is get you in touch ... when I went into surgery, I wasn't scared of dying at all. I wasn't praying to get through this. I was praying that whatever happens, I'm comfortable with it. I'm putting myself in Your hands. I wasn't panicked that I might die.

"An organ donation is a gift that is so precious. The families of donors have done something not only to help one person, but whoever that person touches. The donation gave me a second chance at life. It changes you forever."

For information on becoming an organ donor in Virginia, go to www.save7lives.org
