Virginia Tech notebook: Williams, DiNardo force turnover, change in momentum for Hokies

  • By Andy Bitter 381-1674
  • Aug 30, 2014
  • Aug 30, 2014 Updated Apr 24, 2019
  • 0
1 of 2

By Andy Bitter 381-1674

BLACKSBURG — Chase Williams hadn’t had a clean shot on the quarterback like that in quite some time, and Derek Di Nardo hadn’t run like that with the ball in his hands since high school.

Together, Virginia Tech’s pair of senior linebackers put together the play that gave the Hokies some breathing room in Saturday’s 34-9 win against William & Mary.

The Tribe had the ball at the Hokies’ 39-yard line in the second quarter, down 7-6 and nearly in field goal range. But on third-and-six, Williams got a clear lane on a blitz and clocked William & Mary quarterback Steve Cluley, who fumbled the ball.

“I wasn’t going to waste that opportunity,” Williams said. “That was in the game plan all week to try to get after the quarterback like that.”

Di Nardo, who got his first career start at whip linebacker with Ronny Vandyke still on the mend from a preseason groin injury, picked up the loose ball and took off down the field for 45 yards. He weaved through a couple would-be William & Mary tacklers before being dragged down at the 2-yard line.

“It’s kind of a blur,” Di Nardo said. “It happened so quick. I kind of just had a profile view of Chase laying out the quarterback and I saw the ball dribble down his chest. And I kind of got the Sunday hop into my chest.”

Receiver Willie Byrn razzed Di Nardo for being dragged down before the end zone, welcoming him to the “Inside the 5” club — Byrn got his comeuppance later, getting tackled at the 2 after a 26-yard reception — but the play was a big one.

“Anytime you can get a big turnover like that, especially at our end of the field, it’s a big momentum swing,” Di Nardo said.

Virginia Tech eventually capitalized on the play, getting an 8-yard touchdown catch by tight end Bucky Hodges to go up 14-6.

Fresh faces

Of the 17 Hokies freshmen who dressed, 10 played, including receivers Isaiah Ford and Cam Phillips, offensive linemen Eric Gallo and Braxton Pfaff, running backs Shai McKenzie and Marshawn Williams, defensive lineman Melvin Keihn, safety C.J. Reavis, cornerback Greg Stroman and kicker Joey Slye.

Ford and McKenzie had the biggest contributions. Ford started and caught four passes for 43 yards and a touchdown. McKenzie came off the bench and ran for 106 yards and a touchdown.

Kick start

Slye got some play too, a surprise pick to be the field goal kicker after a tight August battle with fellow freshmen Michael Santamaria and Carson Wise.

Slye, who was originally just supposed to handle long-distance field goals, instead handled all the kicking duties, making field goals from 20 and 27 yards in addition to kickoffs, where he put his high school linebacker skills to work with a tackle on a return to start the game.

“The kickoff, there was a lot of nerves,” Slye said. “With the band playing, your heart actually starts going with the rhythm. But after [the tackle], the nerves went away.”

Slye was the first freshman kicker to start for the Hokies since Carter Warley in 2000. The last true freshman kicker to start for Virginia Tech was Shayne Graham in 1996.

Injury updates

Tight end Kalvin Cline (knee) did not dress for the game. Last year’s leading rusher, running back Trey Edmunds (leg), played but mostly on special teams. He was in for one offensive play but didn’t get a carry.

Receiver Josh Stanford had a limited day after tweaking his hamstring early. He had a 7-yard catch on Tech’s opening drive and later a 12-yard run on a jet sweep.

Extra points

Former Hokies punter Hunter Windmuller had a 52.0-yard average on five punts for William & Mary. Tech punter A.J. Hughes had a 46.3-yard average on three punts, although he did draw a running into the kicker penalty that earned the Hokies a first down. … Head coach Frank Beamer awarded new athletic director Whit Babcock a game ball afterward.
