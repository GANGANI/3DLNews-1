On-on-one with... Jeff Mitchell: Commitment key to Blacksburg coalition

  • By Katelyn Polantz | 381-1669
  • Dec 25, 2010
  • Dec 25, 2010

By Katelyn Polantz | 381-1669

Blacksburg High School's gym collapse forced citizens to care about their local government's decisions this year.

One group of activists, the Blacksburg High School Coalition, formed a few months after the February disaster and has lobbied at weekly public meetings for the county to replace the 36-year-old school.

Last month, the group succeeded.

The Montgomery County Board of Supervisors doubled back on a September decision to repair the school, and now is working to fund $125 million in school construction projects that include a new Blacksburg High.

More than 200 subscribers have belonged to the coalition's online discussion for months, and a core group of about a dozen activists meet regularly at the Blacksburg Public Library to research and plan.

Jeff Mitchell -- an attorney, father of a Blacksburg Middle School student and former adviser to Gov. Mark Warner -- directs the group's lobbying strategies.

Mitchell explained the key components for the grass-roots group in a recent conversation with The Roanoke Times:

Q Talk me through how Blacksburg parents got so organized this year.

A I think that grass-roots organization requires committed individuals, and they just started to surface.

They were sending e-mails back and forth, they were digging for information, they were using the Internet to perform their own investigations and they were showing up for things.

All of a sudden, a core group of committed parents and business owners converged, and that's really what led to that Blacksburg High School Coalition.

The toughest thing about a grass-roots organization is lots of different reasons motivate people to be involved. Getting people to stay focused and stay on message toward the ultimate goal is sometimes hard.

But it's been pretty easy with this group because they're committed behind a single goal of getting a new high school in Blacksburg.

Q What has surprised you most since February?

A The complicated nature of getting anything done in Montgomery County.

It is clear to anyone who has followed this process, that for completely understandable reasons, there are so many intrajurisdictional issues and intraboard issues that it's complicated.

But as long as people are moving in the same direction, things happen. Progress can happen, but it's slow and meandering.

Q What are the necessary ingredients for a successful grass-roots campaign? What was the key in this one?

A The two most important things are to organize and to empower.

Organization has become so much easier with the Internet and Facebook and e-mail.

Empowering usually simply means educating -- finding people who are interested in supporting the cause and making sure they understand what the relevant issue of the moment is. So that when people show up at a public hearing, they can speak to the point at hand and be effective.

When people give their time and get involved in these things, they want to know that they're making a difference. Clearly now they are.

Q So speaking at public hearings and writing to your supervisors does make a difference?

AHuge difference. I think that whether it's contact at the grocery store or at church or in an e-mail or a letter, that's very significant.

People who are involved in grass-roots efforts have to remember that what they say is important. It's important when they raise the issue that they're concerned about.

Local government officials are the truest form of elected official that can have a real impact on your life.

If your trash doesn't get picked up, your kids don't have a school to go in -- the sort of basic responsibilities we look to government for -- those are usually local government responsibilities.

I can assure you not a single member of the [Montgomery County] school board, not a single member of the board of supervisors signed up for what they're dealing with right now. I think it's very important that people involved in grass-roots campaigns circle back and thank these folks for what they're doing for them.

They may not always agree with them, but they're showing up and doing their best. It's our job to work with them and keep them educated and hopefully get them going in the right direction.

Q You sometimes see larger scale grass-roots campaigns morph into other groups after it's over. After this issue is resolved, what do you do with this group of people that has bonded together over an issue?

A I think that grass-roots organizations continue to exist as long as they're relevant.

So the challenge is once we get a new high school, is there a reason for this group to be activated and continue? That will probably be decided at that moment in time.

There's a lot of work between now and then. Once we finish Blacksburg and Auburn, we have to turn to Christiansburg and look at their critical needs, which are just as real.

So this is a problem that's going to require a longer term solution.

But in the short term, I think folks can deal with the disruption as long as they know there's a plan and the end is near. That's what we're hoping for now.
