Lawmakers Vote For Budget To Create Oversight of DOC

  • By LISA LANDRAM Daily News-Record
  • Oct 12, 2023
  • Oct 12, 2023 Updated Mar 11, 2024
  • 0
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Facebook
  • Twitter

  • SMS

  • Save

Virginia lawmakers voted on a budget that includes funding for an independent office to oversee the Virginia Department of Corrections signaling the end of an era in which the largest agency in Virginia is entrusted with policing itself, according to the American Civil Liberties Union of Virginia’s news release on Sept. 6.

“We've been working on this for a long time," said Shawn Weneta, policy strategist for the ACLU of Virginia.

It has been in three general assembly sessions, 2021, 2022 and 2023.

“We had legislation in all three sessions … to create this independent oversight," Weneta said.

The new ombudsman office will monitor conditions at VADOC facilities and make recommendations, among other responsibilities, stated ACLU VA.

“VADOC has an annual budget of $1.5 billion and total control over the lives of approximately 25,000 Virginians, yet it doesn’t have to share almost any information with the people who fund it: taxpayers. Neither the public, the media, nor even elected officials really know what goes on behind prison walls,” said Weneta. “That’s why lawmakers’ first step towards creating meaningful oversight of VADOC is so significant. We’re pleased to see this year’s budget include funds for the establishment of an independent ombudsman’s office that will begin the work of rooting out waste, fraud, abuse and corruption in the prison system."

Weneta is hopeful that in the springtime, after the General Assembly session, “we will have codified all of the authorities of not just the ombudsman, but the committee as well.” This will take time due to creating a 13-person committee that needs to figure out its operating procedures. Then once that is completed, they can nominate an ombudsman, said Weneta. This will most likely carry over to the next budget year.

“Some of our biggest allies in sort of advancing this legislation and advancing the budget amendment have been the corrections officers’ unions,” said Weneta.

“Corrections officers are the working law enforcement in our prisons … this is a tool that corrections are asking for," said Weneta. “This is a bill that is pro law enforcement.”

That encourages transparency and accountability. It extends not just to people that are incarcerated, but it also protects people that are working in our prisons every day.

There has been a series of incidents within the DOC, a dozen unnatural deaths, fires, an escape, all sorts of incidents that signal that the department is not being run in a manner that is in the best interest of public safety, said Weneta. What is in place is not working.

Governor Glenn Youngkin has appointed Chadwick Dotson as the 10th Director of the Virginia Department of Corrections, according to a DOC press release in Sept.

“As VADOC director, I will strive to support department personnel, continue the fight against contraband entering our facilities, especially fentanyl, and uphold the VADOC’s mission of creating long-term public safety for the Commonwealth of Virginia,” Dotson said in the DOC press release. “I also am laser-focused on providing the highest quality re-entry services for inmates and probationers, in order to continue the effort of reducing Virginia’s 20.6 % recidivism rate, which is currently the second lowest in the United States.”

“We can't just rest on new leadership,” said Weneta. “We need an independent agency to be able to actually inspect these institutions and facilities, to evaluate policies and report that to the General Assembly and to the governor, because if we want to make good policy decisions, if we want the legislature to fix problems, they need to have good information to work on.”

Another opportunity that the ombudsman could offer is rather than having workgroups that have not been effective in the past, the ombudsman can study, create policy and make recommendations to the General Assembly. This can address complaints involving solitary confinement, profiteering and canine attacks in prison, according to Weneta.

We have a board that oversees our potato crop in Virginia, said Weneta.

“We should have the same thing for an agency that has full custodial control over 25,000 people," Weneta said. And some form of custodial control over an additional 70,000 who are on probation and parole.

“Everyone’s safety — both prison staff and people who are incarcerated — depends on independent oversight. It’s thanks to bipartisan support from legislators in both the House and the Senate that this budget, once signed, will finally give Virginians the first glimmer of access to what the largest agency in the Commonwealth does behind closed doors. To create a truly unbiased office, it will be crucial to ensure independent appointment of the ombudsman and that legislation is passed providing the office with the resources it needs to be effective. We look forward to continuing this crucial work with lawmakers and those in power to bring transparency and accountability to Virginia’s prisons,” said Weneta in the news release.

Attempts were made to contact the governor's office and no response was received by the printing of this article.
