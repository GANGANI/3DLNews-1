IN THE NEWS 

Lawmaker again eyes northern Virginia casino plan

Proposed legislation would allow Fairfax County to hold a referendum on building a casino.

State Sen. David Marsden, D-Fairfax, plans to re-introduce a bill that calls for a complex with gaming, a convention center, concert hall and hotel. Marsden prefers a location in Tysons Corner, but the legislation would allow county officials to select any site near Silver Line Metro stations, including Reston.

Gov. Glenn Youngkin has not indicated whether he would support the plan. Similar legislation by Marsden last year didn't advance. 

The move comes after Richmond voters in November voted against a referendum to build a casino complex. A ballot measure for a slots venue in Manassas Park also failed.

VIRGINIA BEACH: The U.S. Department of Transportation is providing $14.9 million for the Virginia Beach Trail. The 12-mile pathway is planned from the city's Norfolk border to the oceanfront along a corridor where a light rail project had been planned. 

BRIGHT LIGHTS: A historic downtown Marion theater has received a brighter look. A total of $60,000 was spent on installing modern signage at the Lincoln Theatre, a 500-seat venue that dates to 1929. The building, which houses community performances, was added to the National Register of Historic Places in 1992.

IN THE NEWS 

UVa president seeks committee for commenting 

University of Virginia President Jim Ryan is asking that a committee be formed to tell him when to comment on current events. The Daily Progress reported that Ryan asked the Board of Visitors in a meeting: “I think I would start first with a question of whether we should be saying anything. If the answer is ‘under certain circumstances’ then the question would be, logistically, what are the principles about when and how you’re going to say something?”

University presidents have come under fire recently for responses to the issue of antisemitism on college campuses, which was the topic of a Dec. 5 hearing on Capitol Hill. In that case, University of Pennsylvania President Liz Magill resigned days after testifying in front of the House Committee on Education, and leaders of Harvard and MIT also were scrutinized for comments made in testimony.  

FUNDS: Work is ramping up to distribute more than $500 million of prescription opioid settlement money. The Opioid Abatement Authority was created by Virginia legislators in 2021 to determine what agencies get money from a national settlement that manufacturers and distributors of opioids paid. Funds are going to clinics, medical treatment programs, public outreach and other efforts. 

“Virginia didn’t ask to be at the epicenter of the opioid crisis, but we were,” said state Sen. Todd Pillion, R-Washington County, and chairman of the authority board. “Now we are committed to be a national leader in bringing the crisis to an end.”

The Virginian-Pilot and Associated Press contributed to this report.
