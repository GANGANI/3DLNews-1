Schapiro: Even as a lame duck, Gov. Youngkin could still fly

  • JEFF E. SCHAPIRO Richmond Times-Dispatch
  • Nov 8, 2023
  • Nov 8, 2023 Updated Nov 21, 2023
  • 0

Executive Editor Chris Coates and politics columnist Jeff Schapiro discuss the outcome of Tuesday's election. Read about the results at go.richmond.com/electionoutcome.

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email

It's Day 1 of the end of Glenn Youngkin's governorship.

Repudiated in elections in which he aimed to restore full Republican control of the Virginia legislature, Youngkin — at the midpoint of his non-renewable, four-year term — finds himself and his conservative agenda where, as a candidate in 2021, he brashly claimed the state's economy remained despite a strong bounce-back from the pandemic: in a ditch.

"Why not run for president?" said Del. Marcus Simon, D-Fairfax, referring to the oft-mentioned, last-minute national candidacy by Youngkin that would have been powered by a GOP take-back of the Virginia Senate and an affirmation of the party's majority in the House of Delegates. "It's not going to get any better for him as governor."

Though badly weakened in a campaign that he, in effect, cast as a referendum on his administration, Youngkin need not become a lame duck - yet. He joins a pantheon of Virginia governors — since the state pivoted to two-party competition just over a half-century ago — for whom legislative midterm elections were disappointing, if not embarrassing.

But for several of these governors — Republicans and Democrats — the second half of their terms were surprisingly productive. One even found himself where Youngkin has been since his narrow victory over Terry McAuliffe in this blue-trending state - the least Southern in the South: in presidential orbit.

Former Gov. George Allen, a Republican who failed to secure GOP control the House and Senate in 1995 but on the downhill side of his term won an overhaul of the juvenile justice system and a parental-notification requirement for underage women seeking abortions, said Youngkin could still amass wins despite hostile, Democratic majorities.

"It depends on the issue," said Allen, for whom General Assembly midterm elections were represented as a test of his personal popularity following such first-half successes as parole abolition. "I think tax cuts are still possible. But a lot depends on the revenue forecast. Same with behavioral health and funding for law enforcement."

There is, however, a big difference between then and now, most notably that the legislature lacks nearly 30 years on was abundant in 1995: conservative Democrats and moderate Republicans. Their significant number created more opportunities to build the coalitions on which consensus programs depend.

Few of either were elected or re-elected Tuesday in freshly drawn districts, ensuring that in the House and Senate, both narrowly divided, one is either a liberal Democrat or conservative Republican. This underscores a trend that first emerged during the Allen governorship: the nationalization of state politics long shaped by local and regional concerns.

Del. Bobby Orrock, R-Caroline, who, having been in the House for 34 years, will be the senior member of the General Assembly seated in January, worries that Washington-like partisan polarization augurs in Richmond further impasse over the remainder of Youngkin's term.

"We've got to find some way to work together, otherwise we'll be spinning our wheels for the next two years," said Orrock, among the legislature's few comparative centrists. "Both parties have become so polarized, and that's going to make things difficult."

That may be an understatement, should confrontation prove the preferred approach of Youngkin and Democrats. Both have little to lose. Youngkin could wield with impunity his gubernatorial veto since Democrats haven't the two-thirds vote in both chambers to override it. Democrats, however, have enough votes to block anything with Youngkin's name on it.

Seemingly preoccupied with his presidential flirtation, Youngkin — as a newcomer to government and politics — showed little interest in mastering his day job and getting to know the personalities who could help and hurt him. Even Republicans complained publicly that his national focus had become a needless distraction.

That Youngkin finds himself uncertain what to do in the face of a humiliation made more so by the record $19 million he spent on his losing campaign — the final figure could approach the $26 million Youngkin steered from his personal fortune to his 2021 candidacy — was illustrated by his stubborn silence in the hours following the Democratic victory.

Youngkin was saying nothing publicly. His communications staff — in the governor's office and his political action committee — failed to return texts seeking comment. The closest Youngkin came to saying anything was a post on X, formerly Twitter, around midnight Tuesday by his chief strategist, Dave Rexrode that "we had hoped for a stronger outcome." (Youngkin later announced a Wednesday afternoon news conference.)

Youngkin may have, if only unintentionally, hinted in the finale to the House and Senate elections that all was not well; that his so-called conservative compassionate compromise on abortion — a 15-week ban — only managed to infuriate the voters Republicans desperately needed to win over: suburbanites, especially women.

Youngkin did not include a reference to his proposed abortion restrictions in a stump speech at a suburban Richmond rally Sunday. Also, his choreographers refused to say where he would spend Election Night and whether he would be commenting on the results.

We are still monitoring a couple key races and will fully assess where things stand in the morning. We had hoped for a stronger outcome this evening but are proud of the effort all of our candidates put in to these extremely competitive districts.

— Dave Rexrode (@DRexrode) November 8, 2023

Disappointing midterms did not discourage Gov. John Dalton, a Republican who served from 1978 until 1982, or Democrat Mark Warner, governor from 2002 until 2006. Both achieved breakthroughs that would seem improbable, winning tax increases from a General Assembly firmly controlled by the opposition party.

Dalton, determined to strengthen the GOP's ranks in the legislature that remained underwhelming despite its statewide dominance, saw pickups for his party of three seats in each chamber, still falling well short of his objective: one-third of the seats in either house, enough to sustain his veto.

Dalton, nonetheless, proposed a fuel tax rise to finance the state's then-struggling road-building program. Democrats went along with it, though they tweaked the scheme, substituting a cents-per-gallon increase for a percentage-based formula that Dalton said could generate more cash.

In 2004, Warner - having been thwarted at nearly every turn in his first two goes with a Republican legislature - broke a campaign promise not to raise taxes and won a $1.4 billion increase for cops, education and social services that were cash-starved because recession and the rising cost of the GOP car-tax rollback had blown a hole in the state budget.

That Warner - aided by defecting Republicans, including Bobby Orrock — outmaneuvered the GOP on taxes immediately generated chatter that the Virginia governor had presidential potential for 2008. Warner briefly tested the waters, ultimately saying he wasn't up to the task and endorsing Barack Obama.

Democrats are skeptical that such ancient history will inspire a Youngkin reset, noting that his cheery manner can't veil his own recent history of hitting hot buttons — racial, cultural, educational and financial — that arouse Republicans but aggravate Democrats and more than a few independents.

Said Sen. Ghazala Hashmi, D-Chesterfield, "He's burned those bridges."

Tougher on Youngkin were those who'd plumped for his candidacy, promoted his policies and seemingly pleaded he seek the presidency. Fox & Friends, one of the Murdoch cable programs that was a propaganda arm of the Youngkin administration, described the Tuesday legislative elections as an "epic failure" for the governor.

Some friends.
