Parents get ‘Friday the 13th nightmares’ from 4 more schools and School Board lawyer

  • By Asra Q. Nomani Special to the Fairfax County Times
  • Jan 13, 2023
  • Jan 13, 2023 Updated Jan 14, 2023
  • 9
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email

In a series of “Friday the 13th nightmares,” as one mother put it, the principals of at least four more Fairfax County high schools admitted they withheld National Merit awards from children, as a Richmond lawyer for the Fairfax County school board sent parents a letter requesting interviews that she is seeking “to record.”

“These are just Friday the 13th nightmares,” the mother said.

First revealed in a column in the City Journal on Dec. 21, and chronicled further in the Fairfax County Times on Dec. 29, with news of a $455,000 contract with a company preaching “equal outcomes for every student,” this scandal has slowly escalated into not only a tragedy for children and their post-secondary futures but also a potential institutional coverup, parents say. 

On  Jan. 3, Fairfax County Public Schools Superintendent Michelle Reid, walked around the library at Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology in Alexandria and told about 100 parents that the withholding of awards was a “one-time” event and “human error” at the school, led by Principal Ann Bonitatibus. Then, on  Jan. 10, after Langley High School Principal Kimberly Greer and Westfield High School Principal Tony DiBari admitted they withheld awards, Reid walked among parents in the cafeteria at Langley High School in McLean and said that “staffing shifts and changes” at TJ, Langley and Westfield in Chantilly led to withheld awards. 

But tonight, in nearly copy-cat messages, the principals of four more high schools published Friday the 13th missives, confessing they had withheld awards. In Annandale, Shawn DeRose at Annandale High School said the school “did not receive” the Commended Student awards from the National Merit Scholarship Corporation. In Alexandria, Pamela Brumfield at Edison High School said students got a celebration in “late November,” after early college deadlines had passed. In Springfield, Alfonso Smith at Lewis High School said students were “notified later that we would have hoped.” Also in Alexandria, Tanganyika Millard at West Potomac High School said the awards were “delayed” and marked with a “celebration” in “early November.” 

“Fairfax County Public Schools has spent far too much time on pet projects and not academics. It’s time for that to change,” said Shawnna Yashar, an intrepid mother at TJ who discovered the issue of awards withheld in late November. One day, her husband and she stumbled upon the National Merit certificate in their son’s papers, weeks after early college application deadlines had passed. Commended Students are among the top 3 percent of 1.5 million students who take the PSAT/NMSQT test in their junior years, and the honor opens the door to admissions, scholarships and other opportunities.

The new confessions makes it at least seven schools of 27 high schools in Fairfax County Public Schools that deliberately withheld awards from students. A running live analysis by Fairfax County Times on the racial demographic of students impacted, an issue that initiated a civil rights investigation by Virginia Attorney General Jason Miyares, reveals that the percentage of National Merit Semifinalists – a level above the Commended Students – who are of Asian American heritage at these seven schools total about 127 students of 156 students, or about 81 percent of the National Merit Semifinalist recipients. The same patterns appear among Commended Students, but Fairfax County Public Schools has refused to release the names of all of its Commended Students. 

Still, some politicians continued to try to diminish the impact of the scandal, even though it disproportionately impacts students of color. Friday night, Virginia Sen. Scott Surovell (D-Mt. Vernon), an attorney, published a tweet saying that his children’s school – West Potomac High School – had withheld the awards, revealing, he said, how “absurd this investigation is,” examining civil rights issues. He tagged Virginia Sen. Ghazala Hashmi (D-Chesterfield), Sen. Louise Lucas (D-Portsmouth) and Blue Virginia, a Democratic Party blog. Lucas is chair of the Virginia Senate Education and Health Committee. Earlier this week, in a tweet, Surovell called the investigation “fake.”

In this saga, today, at 4:15 p.m., Faith Alejandro, an employment attorney and chair of the Diversity and Inclusion Committee at Sands Anderson PC, a Richmond-based law firm, emailed local parents a letter, stating her firm had been “engaged” by the Fairfax County School Board to “investigate questions surrounding notification to students of awards or recognition from the National Merit Scholarship Corporation (“NMSC”). 

The late Friday afternoon delivery is the latest chapter in the debacle by Fairfax County Public Schools over the withholding of National Merit Commended Student certificates from seniors in at least three high schools. 

The letter from the law firm represents the first action by outside lawyers for the school district in completing a “third-party” investigation.

“Parents got their love letters, nice!” said Debra Tisler, a local mother and special education advocate who is assisting parents battling the law firm as they try to win rights for their children with disabilities. 

In special education circles, the firm has earned the moniker of “Sands Bagging,” said Tisler, for allegedly creating an “illusion of fair practices.” “However,” said Tisler, who is running for school board “parents that have had direct dealings with them know they are part of the UnFairfax mission,” with reports of lost documents, much like the withheld merit certificates, destroyed electronic records and complaints with state and federal agencies about issues related to the retention of education records. 

Alejandro did not respond to questions. Fairfax County Public Schools has not responded to a public records request seeking a copy of its retainer agreement and payment schedule with the law firm. And officials in its Office of Communications and Community Relations have refused to respond to numerous requests seeking comment.

The work by the law firm does not have the gravitas of the civil rights investigation that Miyares opened up on Jan. 3 into the withholding of the awards from students. That night, Reid told about 100 parents at TJ that she had retained Sands Anderson to conduct a “third-party” investigation. 

Many parents, including many attending a meeting earlier this week on Jan. 10 with Reid at Langley High School, aren’t buying the messaging by Reid that the law firm will be “independent,” considering the school district will be paying its bills.

It took the law firm until today, Jan. 13, to contact parents for the first time. A parent at Langley whose son was impacted by the withholding of the awards had not yet received a letter by late Friday. 

The new legal fees come amidst an already exorbitant tally that the Fairfax County Public Schools auditor Esther Ko recently released in a new Legal Audit Report, which concluded that the school district had spent $9.2 million on 10 legal and consulting firms, one firm only listed as “A Consulting Firm,” over a year and a half, between June 1, 2020, and Dec. 31, 2021, to provide “general legal advice and litigation services.” 

The No. 1 paid firm was Hunton Andrews Kurth LLP, which was hired by the school board to defend admissions changes to TJ that federal judge Claude Hilton ruled are “patently unconstitutional,” with discrimination against Asian American students. The next firms receiving the greatest fees are Blankingship & Keith PC and Michael E. Kinney PLC. 

The No. 4 firm on the list is Sands Anderson PC with bills totaling $288,571. The report noted the firm is “assigned to general legal advice and litigation services including special education and personnel matters.”

In the letter, Alejandro wrote: “We understand that you, or a student with whom you are associated, has concerns related to the notification process of NMSC recognition or awards. It may be, as well, that you have information concerning the notification process or Fairfax County Public Schools’ practices and procedures in this regard.”

“We would appreciate the opportunity to speak with you about the matter at your earliest convenience,” she continued. “If you are interested in discussing the matter with us, please email or call me to let me know your availability to speak with us.”

In making plans for the interviews, Alejandro said, “We will likely have this discussion by Zoom or other remote video conferencing tool. Please let us know if that is available to you. We will also ask your permission to record the conversation.

In an acknowledgment that the school administrators have likely put the school district in a hot legal mess, Alejandro noted, “Of course, if you are represented by an attorney in this matter, we ask that you please forward this letter to your counsel.”

In her auditor general’s report, Ko concluded that the school district’s  “controls and processing” for managing its external legal counsels were not “formally documented.” Shatter the Silence Fairfax County Public Schools, a volunteer-run nonprofit raising concerns about civil rights violations and sexual assaults in Fairfax County, found the report “deeply disturbing.”

At Sands Anderson, Alejandro has established herself in the language of the “equity” agenda that many parents in Fairfax County believe landed the school district in such hot water. A Class of 2001 graduate of another regional Governor’s School, Maggie L. Walker Governor’s School in Richmond,, Alejandro attended the University of Virginia before earning her law degree from the University of Richmond School of Law and later joining Sands Anderson PC. According to her LinkedIn bio, she was named chair of the Diversity and Inclusion Committee in February 2018, a position she continues to hold.

The law firm states: “Our team values and pursues diversity throughout our business from the employees we hire to the clients we serve.” 

For many parents in Fairfax County, the “equity” and “diversity and inclusion” agenda is partly what led to the trouble in which Fairfax County public school administrators now find themselves. This fall, as principals withheld National Merit awards, Reid hired a contractor, Performance Fact Inc., based in California, that she had employed in her previous school district of Northshore Public Schools in Washington State, preaching “equal outcomes for every student.”

At the meeting at Langley High School earlier this week, many parents said that the school district’s “equity” and “diversity” agenda was misguided and even cruel, withholding awards from mostly Asian American students who are not viewed as part of a “diversity” solution. 

“This is shameless, shameless!” Langley mother Amy Paladini told Reid.

Two months ago, as parents and students, most of them minorities, many from immigrant families, grappled with awards withheld Alejandro participated in an online lesson sponsored by Sands Anderson PC, titled, “The Implicit Bias Breakdown: The Importance of Eliminating Implicit Bias in the Practice of Law.”
