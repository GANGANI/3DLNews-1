Popular live Nativity returns to Afton after three-year, pandemic-induced hiatus

Bethlehem Village will be open Friday through Saturday

  • Derek Micah Armstrong
  • Dec 15, 2023
  • Dec 15, 2023
  • 0
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Print
  • Save

The Star of Bethlehem will be lighting up the night sky over the Blue Ridge Mountains this weekend for the first time since 2019.

After a three-year hiatus because of the pandemic, Bethlehem Village at Hebron Baptist Church in Afton is returning.

“We’re excited to be back doing it again this year,” said Ken Bryant, who helps market the event for Hebron Baptist Church, which he attends.

Bethlehem Village, located on the grounds of Hebron Baptist Church at 66 Tanbark Drive in Afton, will be open from 6 to 9 p.m. Friday and 5 to 9 p.m. Saturday and Sunday. There is no charge for parking or admission.

Stacey Johnson, co-director of Bethlehem Village and another member of Hebron Baptist Church, said the cast and crew have missed hosting the live Nativity and are eager to open the village to the public again.

“We felt like it was time to bring it back,” Johnson said. “We’re excited to be open again to share Christmas with everyone.”

Tracy Campbell, who plays an angel in this year's production, said she is glad Bethlehem Village is returning. She volunteered for the first time in 2019 and was disappointed the event had to take a hiatus.

“I loved it so much,” Campbell said. “I couldn’t wait for it to start again. We do this to glorify God.”

'The First Noel'

Bethlehem Village began 24 years ago, when Jim Ailor, then the pastor of Hebron Baptist, was inspired by a Nativity scene that his children had given him.

His children gave him pieces of the Fontanini Village collection each year until he had about 15 structures.

One afternoon in 1999, near the end of summer, Ailor was lying on his couch in his living room reading an article about how people are more open to spiritual things around Christmas. He pondered what Hebron Baptist Church could do to capitalize on it.

“At that time, many churches were doing cantatas or concerts,” Ailor said. “We didn’t have the talent for that.”

Thinking about his Fontanini Village collection sparked an idea.

“We did have a room full of people who could do carpentry,” Ailor said.

He decided that making a life-size Nativity scene would help bring to life the Christmas story and was something his congregation could do.

“Without his vision, I don’t think anyone would have thought of this,” Bryant said.

With little time to waste, the church members immediately began working on the project.

“We didn’t have a lot of time because December was coming up quickly,” Ailor said. “It was a big project, and only 100 people attended the church then.”

The biggest struggle was finding camels, something Ailor insisted was necessary. However, after calling around the country and unable to find some, a church member suggested using llamas and putting humps on them — something Ailor said he desperately wanted to avoid.

“I didn’t want to do a live Nativity without camels,” he said.

In the end, the church was able to rent three camels, two donkeys and some sheep for $3,000.

“I think they were a big draw in the beginning,” Ailor said. “I’m not sure it would have been successful without the camels.”

In December 1999, the church members set up four buildings in its parking lot and hosted its first live Nativity for 10 nights leading up to Christmas, with the final night on Christmas Eve.

“We had 2,500 visitors and thought it was great,” Bryant said. “We were this little country church in the middle of nowhere and didn’t know what to expect.”

“It was really neat,” agreed Ailor.

In the second year, Bethlehem Village was set up behind the parsonage, and Union Baptist Church got involved.

Ailor left Hebron Baptist Church in 2004, and Bethlehem Village was relocated to a permanent setting behind the church. The original four buildings were replaced. Bethlehem Village grew from there, becoming a popular attraction throughout the Shenandoah Valley and beyond during the holiday season.

In 2009, Piney Mountain Ministries partnered with Hebron Baptist.

“It began as a church project and has evolved into a community project,” Johnson said.

'Silent Night'

The annual event peaked right before the pandemic, with nearly 8,000 visitors showing up over five nights in 2019, Bryant said.

“COVID took some of the momentum out,” he said.

Having not hosted Bethlehem Village in four years, organizers are easing back into it this year with three nights.

“We’re getting back into the groove,” said Bryant, who is unsure how many visitors they will attract this year.

In some ways, it’s a rebirth, Johnson said.

“It’s almost like we’re starting from scratch,” she said.

Cast and crew have been warned it might get off to a rocky start, but things will work themselves out. With their help and patience, Bethlehem Village, which has sat empty for four years, will turn into a bustling town this weekend with live animals, shopkeepers, Roman soldiers, shepherds, wise men, angels and, of course, Mary, Joseph and the baby Jesus.

'O Come, All Ye Faithful'

Visitors enter the village through a tent, where they are given a brief explanation of what is happening in the town, handed a plastic gold coin and then sent on a journey through time.

“When visitors enter the city, Roman soldiers will admonish them to pay their taxes,” Johnson said.

After paying their taxes, guests will be invited to register for the census in compliance with the decree from the Roman emperor. Guests can then visit the shops and speak with Bethlehem citizens, including bakers, basket weavers, blacksmiths and many more.

Emma Craig, a 16-year-old student at Nelson County High School, is working in Bethlehem as a baker responsible for making flatbread with olive oil and seasonings, which guests can sample.

“I’m a little nervous,” she admitted.

But she’s also excited because she enjoys cooking pastries and hopes to be a professional baker one day. She has even taken in-person classes at King Arthur’s Baking School in Norwich, Vermont.

Emma, who participated in 2019, said its fun to participate in the live Nativity.

“You get to meet new people,” she said.

Thomas Arthur, who is playing an angel, is also excited because the professional performer gets to sing “Away in a Manger,” “Silent Night” and “O Come, All Ye Faithful.”

“Those are all beautiful songs everyone knows, and they’ll be singing along with us,” he said.

Live animals are essential, Johnson said. This year, Bethlehem Village will feature camels, sheep, goats and a donkey from Infinity Acres Ranch in Ridgeway. Although the animals may be petted, they are not to be fed.

During the hiatus, a synagogue has been added to the village.

“Every year, we add a little something different,” Johnson said.

Something that won’t be different is the event’s most iconic component: its electric spotlight that shines a bright beam of light into the heavens, illuminating the way to Bethlehem Village from miles away.

“We have had people come off the interstate wanting to know what the light is about,” Johnson said. “They have no idea what is happening and just show up.”

One of Bryant’s favorite memories regarding the event is an encounter over a decade ago with a family traveling over Afton Mountain on their way home to North Carolina.

“One of the kids in the car saw the bright light out the window. He said to his dad, ‘What is that?’ And the dad jokingly said, ‘That’s the star of Bethlehem.’”

The family pulled off the interstate and drove down Route 6 until they saw a large banner beside the road advertising the live Nativity.

“They ended up at Bethlehem,” Bryant said.

'Joy to the World'

The tagline for Bethlehem Village is “Find your place in the real Christmas story,” Bryant said.

“You get as much out of the village as you want to,” Johnson said. “Everyone has to find the true meaning of Christmas by experiencing it themselves.”

However, Bryant said you don’t have to be a Christian or religious to enjoy the live Nativity, because volunteers do not force beliefs on visitors.

“Christmas means different things to different people,” Bryant said. “We’re not going to push you into anything.”

Though, he does think Bethlehem Village offers a peaceful break from the commercialism and stress of the holiday season.

“Life often gets in the way this time of year,” Bryant said. “But you can come here for an hour or two and enjoy being away from all the hustle and bustle.”

That said, organizers and volunteers hope Bethlehem Village will bear fruit.

“If just one person is impacted, that’s enough,” Bryant said. “It’s better to impact a few than have 15,000 people come through and nothing happens.”

Ailor moved back to the valley five years ago and lives in Waynesboro. Soon after returning, friends kept telling him to visit Bethlehem Village, not knowing his role in its creation. Surprised it was still going strong all these years later, Ailor visited it before the pandemic.

“I was amazed it lasted this long,” he said. “What they have done with it is really good.”
