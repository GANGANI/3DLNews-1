Quake Shakes Sitka, Thousands Evacuate, Tsunami a No-Show

Sitkans evacuate to the high school early Saturday. (Sentinel Photo)

By SHANNON HAUGLAND
Sentinel Staff Writer
    An earthquake shook the ground in Sitka for several seconds at 11:58 p.m. Friday, and the resulting tsunami warning caused thousands of residents to head for higher ground.
    The earthquake, centered off Dixon Entrance 110 miles south of Sitka, was measured at 7.5 on the Richter scale.
    Within minutes, the Alaska Tsunami Warning Center in Palmer issued a tsunami warning for Sitka. A tsunami warning is the highest level of alert, indicating a high risk that low-lying coastal areas will be inundated by one or more earthquake-generated ocean waves.
    It wasn’t until 2:10 a.m. Saturday that the “all-clear” was announced. The tsunami resulted in a tidal variation of less than one foot as measured in Sitka.
    Sitka High School, Koot Gooshi Heen Elementary School and the Kimsham ball field, which previously had been designated as evacuation centers, were sites of mass gatherings of young and old heeding the warning to evacuate their homes.
    Sirens were activated, and warnings of the impending tsunami were spread by Sitka police using loudspeakers on their patrol cars as they cruised through town. Sitka’s emergency mass telephone calling system was also activated, sending recorded messages to home telephones throughout the community.
     The sirens and the recorded announcements to evacuate started about 15 minutes after the earthquake was felt shortly before midnight.
    The warning ended at 2:05 a.m. Saturday, and fire officials activated the all clear signal – the Westminster chimes tune – at 2:10 a.m.
    Fire officials said no earthquake-related damage was reported in the community.
    Emergency officials said today that they felt prepared for the emergency, and for the most part followed the plan already in place.
    It was the first mass evacuation of the town since the late 1980s, they said.
    “For us, it went pretty good,” said Fire Chief Dave Miller, who is the emergency manager for Sitka. “We talked to all of our people about what to do, a few days before. We had a good team of people at the fire hall within a few minutes. ... Are there things we can do different? Yes. There are and we’ll work with (agencies) on that.”
    Residents had various estimates of how long the quake and aftershocks lasted – from seconds to more than a minute – but the National Weather Service’s official report is that the quake lasted six seconds at 11:58 p.m. Friday. An aftershock of 5.2 on the Richter scale was felt at 3:28 a.m. Saturday, the Alaska Tsunami Warning Center said.
    Assistant Fire Chief Al Stevens, who lives in the Thimbleberry neighborhood out Sawmill Creek Road, said cars were heading out of Price Street toward the high school within minutes of the quake. That is not a bad idea, since tsunami warnings generally follow large earthquakes, Miller said. The chief said the tsunami sirens should be taken seriously.
    “We’re only going to set off the sirens if we feel it’s truly an emergency,” Miller said. “We’re not going to set it off for every earthquake around the world.”
    Also within seconds of the earthquake, hundreds of calls, including 911 calls, flooded the police department. Some asked whether it was an earthquake, others about the evacuation plans, police said.
    “That caused quite a bit of a delay,” SPD Lt. Barry Allen said. “Everyone was calling at the same time.”
    “If people feel an earthquake, and it’s for a long time, they shouldn’t be calling us,” Miller said. “We won’t be able to tell them any more than they know. ... If they call, they’re tying up the phone lines for calls from the Coast Guard, National Weather Service, police and Coast Guard. The best thing to do is go to higher ground.”
    The calls delayed activation of the call-burst system, which was at 12:26 a.m. and reached 93 percent of the 4,586 phone numbers on the list, police said. The call-burst system was set up in recent years to notify residents of just such emergencies.
    Lyn Blankenship, Sitka police dispatch supervisor, went in to the station off Lake Street when the earthquake occurred, and recorded the message for the mass callout.
    The streets leading to Sitka High, the official Emergency Operations Center, as well as the main evacuation center and KGH, were jammed with bumper-to-bumper traffic. Hundreds of fire hall volunteers, Sitka police officers, Alaska State Troopers, Sitka police officers and jailers, U.S. Forest Service officers and Sitka Mountain Rescue reported for duty within minutes.
    Prisoners in the jail were detained at KGH and police officers directed traffic at main intersections. All of the city fire trucks, and other emergency vehicles, were moved to KGH. Public works vehicles were driven to higher ground up Jarvis Street from the Public Works complex Street. Two Coast Guard Aids to Navigation boats were towed on trailers to the high school.
    Police arrived on the main streets to direct traffic, by which time traffic was already backed up on Lake Street and Sawmill Creek Road. Traffic moved at a snail’s pace to the parking lots at Sitka High. All parts of the school, including the gym and the performing arts center auditorium, were opened to accommodate the hundreds of refugees seeking shelter there.
    “It’s going to be warm and dry there,” Miller said. “They could also go to higher ground.”
    To avoid the crowds, he suggested that people make their individual plans.
    “You can talk to friends on higher ground, and make plans with them in advance,” he said. He said residents could go a step further in their preparations, by putting together an airtight box with all the supplies they need for an evacuation, and storing it in a friend’s house on higher ground.
    “We want people to go to higher ground,” Miller said. “If they can walk, it would be a lot better.”
    Sitka School Superintendent Steve Bradshaw said he felt the earthquake at his house on Swan Lake as he was getting ready for bed, and received a call from authorities soon after, as part of the emergency evacuation plan.
    “I thought it was a big gust of wind, but it didn’t take me long to figure out what it actually was,” Bradshaw said. Within 10 minutes he got a call from Miller asking him to open KGH and Sitka High.
    At the high school, people napped, read and watched movies in various classrooms, the commons, hallways, stairwells and the gym. The performing arts center is the designated evacuation center for Mt. Edgecumbe High School, but since the Japonski Island boarding school is still closed for the year-end holidays, the general public was allowed in.
    Bradshaw said other school officials arrived before he did to open up the school. He said everyone was well-behaved during the emergency. He said that it helped that he knew what to expect in this situation, and that the plan was followed.
    “A lot of kudos to the fire chief and his people,” Bradshaw said. “I thought they did an excellent job. ... Everything went as well as expected.”
    The tsunami warning, advisories to evacuate and updates were broadcast at regular intervals on KCAW-FM through the Emergency Alert System, KCAW General Manager Ken Fate said today. KCAW, whose studio is on the downtown waterfront, played recorded programming and evacuated its personnel from the building until the warning was over.
    Larry Snider, manager of Sitka’s AM radio station, KIFW, broadcast information for the public, staying at his microphone throughout the evacuation, relaying information about the earthquake and the tsunami, between such specially selected songs as “Shake Your Bootie,” “Shake Your Groove Thing,” and “The Tide is High.”
    Snider said he felt the quake when it happened, and knew what it was from his time in San Francisco. “I certainly knew what the heck it was,” he said. Snider went directly to the station on Swan Lake, gathered information as it came through and stayed on the air until the warning was over.
    He said his training with Armed Forces Radio to “stay at our post” probably carried over to the experience on Friday night. “I wanted to get out as much information as I could,” Snider said.
    Snider also fielded calls on the air from the public. One woman called asking for advice on how she could get to the high school, and another called to advise Snider to take care of himself. Snider said being on the air for two other tsunami advisories, Nov. 5, 2011, and Oct. 27, 2012, were good preparation for Friday night.
    In accordance with their emergency plan, the Sitka Pioneers Home and Sitka Community Hospital did not evacuate or relocate their residents and patients. SEARHC officials said Mt. Edgecumbe Hospital patients were evacuated to the third and fourth floor of the hospital.
        Stevens said the tsunami warning was broadcast on VHF Channel 16, and that Sitka emergency workers kept villages in the region informed.
    Miller, Stevens and others with duties during the tsunami warning will be evaluating the response this week to discover ways to improve if a similar event occurs again.
    News of the earthquake made national headlines. Sentinel photographer James Poulson’s photos of the evacuation scene at Sitka High School were distributed by the Associated Press and published by the New York Times, USA Today and Britain’s national newspaper, The Guardian, as well as newspapers throughout the U.S.
