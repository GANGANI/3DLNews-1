Sitka Park Supt. Miller Loses Position - Again

By TOM HESSE and
SHANNON HAUGLAND
Sentinel Staff Writers
    Nearly three years after being reinstated as the superintendent of Sitka National Historical Park, Mary Miller has once again been fired.
    Her official notification came on New Year’s eve, the result of a federal court ruling upholding the original grounds for her dismissal in 2010.
    Miller could not be reached for comment by press time today.
    The National Park Service said Miller was fired in 2010 because she refused reassignment to a new position with the NPS in Anchorage.
    Miller, who had been superintendent of the park since 2008, appealed to the Merit Systems Protection Board, which held a hearing on the issue. On Jan. 6, 2012, the administrative law judge found in favor of the Park Service. But in the spring of the following year the board reversed itself on an appeal by Miller and ordered Miller reinstated.
    Miller returned to her position at the Sitka park in April 2013 after the Merit Systems board modified its ruling in favor of Miller.

Mary Miller. (Sentinel Photo)


    The National Park Service then petitioned the U.S. Court of Appeals for a review of the Merit Systems Protection Board’s decision.
    In the now-overturned decision the board abandoned the framework cited by the administrative law judge and sided with Miller, who contended that she was unqualified for the reassignment. Additionally, court documents say, Miller said her removal action involved discrimination based on her gender, race, and physical disability, and that the reassignment was reprisal for equal employment opportunity complaints she had filed with the agency.
    As superintendent of SNHP Miller was under Joel Hard, the deputy regional director for National Parks Service in Alaska. Hard, who took over that position shortly before Miller was reinstated in 2013, told the Sentinel that Miller’s removal last Thursday had nothing to do with her recent performance.
    “That’s old business, this has nothing to do with the job that she did for the better part of three and a half years working with me,” Hard said. “She’s been a good employee. She’s done a good job here.”
    In the most recent court ruling, the U.S. Court of Appeals remanded the case back to the Merit Systems Protection Board with instructions to reinstate the initial ruling by the administrative judge upholding Miller’s firing.
     In short, the court agreed that the reassignment request was legitimate and that adequate notice had been given to Miller under the precedent established in the 1980 ruling in Ketterer v. Department of Agriculture. That ruling established a framework by which employees may be terminated for refusing a reassignment. In Miller’s case, the proposed reassignment was to an Alaska Native liaison position with the National Park Service in Anchorage.
     The reinstatement of the initial decision was issued on Dec. 31, with the notation that the action was “immediate,” which meant Miller had to leave that day, Hard said. At the time Hard was in Anchorage and therefore had to deliver the news to Miller by telephone.
    “When the order was conveyed on Thursday morning to us from the National Park Service and the MSPB it said ‘immediate,’ which was that day. I was of course in Anchorage and the only flight I could get on was the evening flight,” Hard said. “When you work with a professional for a number of years, removing them by telephone is not what you want to do.”
    Hard said he asked if he could wait to carry out the order, but was told the action had to be immediate.
    Hard was in Sitka today, and said he was to meet with Miller to discuss some of the transition and to allow her to get her personal effects from the park headquarters building.
    As for the park itself, Hard said Chief of Maintenance Mike Trainor is taking over until an acting superintendent can be found. Hard expects an acting superintendent will be in place by the end of the month, and serve until the position can be filled permanently in the next few months.
    “It can be a long process, within 90 (days) to a six-month period, but I’m hopeful that it won’t be that long,” he said.
    Hard, who expects to leave tonight for Anchorage, said he has been helping with the transition and giving reassurance to the staff at the Historical Park.
    “Much of it has been to explain to new employees what happened. This has nothing to do with what she did last week or last year. This is old business,” he said, adding that the mood at the park is mixed.
    “There are clearly people that are unhappy with this and there are people who are looking forward to moving forward without the uncertainty of the courts involved,” Hard said. “It’s a new day at the park, and not clouded by litigation.”
    Miller has other avenues to appeal the decision through the civil court system, or through an Equal Employment Opportunity Commission claim, Hard said.
