A Ferguson Vacation, Big Delta to the Yukon

  • Judy Ferguson

    Judy Ferguson

  • Jun 19, 2014
  • Jun 19, 2014 Updated Nov 20, 2014

Most people take cruises for vacations, roam in their RVs, or at least trailer a camper to parts unknown. Not the Fergusons. We go on wilderness river trips and set up camp every day. Over the years we have done a lot of rivers, including the Yukon and the Kobuk.

My desire with this column – as time allows – is to document Delta history. However, in between, this summer I will share installments of some of the Ferguson river trips.

Almost 40 years ago in 1975, our oldest son, Clint, made a little boat of cottonwood bark. When he finished, he and his dad walked to the river.

“I want to let it go, Dad,” he said, and then let his boat drift down the Tanana’s current. “I’m sending a message to the villages, like the Indian boy did in my book.” (Paddle to the Sea by Holling Clancy Holling)

Reb smiled, “Okay, but, Clint, why don’t we put our own canoe in and follow your little boat to the sea?”

Over the next several days, we packed our canoe with supplies to go from our upriver home near Big Delta, north to the Yukon River and west toward the Bering Sea, a trip of 700 miles.

That night, Reb tossed and turned, unable to sleep as he pictured our loaded, 17-foot canoe. He finally decided it was not boat enough to make the trip to the coast. We delayed our trip for a day to go to Fairbanks and buy a 19-foot Grumman freighter canoe.

While we were in town, I contacted a new friend, experienced canoeist Charlie Wolf. At 76, Charlie was an old student of the French-Canadian voyageurs, the boatmen who had freighted goods for the early fur companies. He knew some French, had a voyageur’s hat, and had canoed many rivers in Canada and Alaska many times.

Charlie showed me some oar strokes, explaining in both French and English, and I was all set. He cautioned about Squaw Crossing, an unprotected stretch of water just above the confluence of the Tanana and Yukon Rivers. He warned if the wind came up, the shallow water could quickly become boiling waves. We were glad we had the longer boat, and at home we transferred the load from our shorter boat to the larger one.

June 21st, we fastened splash covers made from surplus Army canvas across the mid-section of the canoe to protect the food and bedding, and we started out. Reb was near the outboard at the back of the canoe. Clint sat in his canvas porthole in front of his dad. Sitting in his camping chair, he could duck under the cloth skirt any time and escape the wind and spray. In the bow, under my seat, I had slipped Lieutenant Castner’s 1898 account of his Tanana-Goodpaster river expedition as well as Archbishop Hudson Stuck’s book “Voyages on the Yukon and its Tributaries.” The archbishop had called the Tanana, “a bad river.”

“Oh, great,” I thought as I saw Bates Rapids open in front of us, a large, undulating water where the Delta River tumbles into the Tanana.

We traveled up the white caps, and then down the two-foot troughs, and up again. Our boat knifed through the swells powered by our six-horsepower motor. A shipwrecked steamboat, the Nabesna, lay up a side slough where Captain Clarence O’Flannigan had run it aground many years ago: a testimony to the challenge of the Tanana. In his early days, O’Flannigan had prospected near Tolovana Roadhouse, downstream from Fairbanks, Nenana, and Tanana.

On our fourth day out, we stopped at the original Tolovana Roadhouse. It sat in a sea of grass, its floors covered with flood silt, but its log walls and ceiling were still beautiful and intact. As we continued downriver, we passed a series of ghost towns, a reminder of the formerly busy Tanana, echoing now with emptiness. Gone from the river’s edge were the Native villages that had once saved the life of Lieutenant Allen, an Army explorer, in 1898. The buildings of Old Minto and Cos Jacket were shells of the once thriving river settlements.

In the 1950s, the era of the sternwheelers faded, taking with it a source of the Natives’ income from cutting and stacking firewood to feed the ships’ boilers. Up and down the river, the steamboats had loaded their piles of wood.

Cos Jacket, downstream from Old Minto, was an old relation to the village of Tanana. Exploring the Cosna (Cos Jacket) ruins, we found that all that was left were old brass beds, two Victrola phonographs, Edison records, a time sheet, and a 1939 National Guard album.

That night, as we pitched our tent in a bed of pungent Labrador Tea, I worried about facing Squaw Crossing. I was tense in the morning as we approached the bottleneck on the Tanana that was twice as wide as the river had been before it emptied into the Yukon. Hidden sandbars divided the Tanana into a myriad of shallow channels. Wind could easily turn the rolling water into erratic white caps, but, thankfully, only a breeze stirred that day.

The mouth of the Tanana emptied into the vast arm of the mighty Yukon River, sweeping in from Canada. Broad, green mountains framed the endless sky of the river valley. We were entering a different country.

That night after the family was asleep, I sat in the moonlight and realized we had passed through an invisible door of a hidden Alaska. We were leaving behind roads, phones, and cement. Like a little boat going into wide waters, we only sensed the wind, sand, and salmon – a world where the river is the only road. A hidden Alaska.

Judy Ferguson, a 46-year Big Delta resident, is the author of “Windows to the Land, An Alaska Native Story, Volume I: Alaska Native Land Claims Trailblazers,” “Bridges to Statehood,” “Parallel Destinies” and “Blue Hills” and children’s books “Alaska’s Secret Door,” “Alaska’s Little Chief” and “Alaska’s First People.” http://alaska-highway.org/delta/outpost
