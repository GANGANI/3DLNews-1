Dairy Farming in Jasper

By J.S. "Chick" Wilson•
Prior to the 1940’s there were several of the old type dairy farms in Jasper County. These earlier “Flat Barn” dairies usually consisted of a big 20 stanchion building with a concrete floor and 10 or so head gates on each side.

At about 3 a.m. the cows were rounded up and guided into the barn. Dairy herds at this time consisted of Jersey and Guernsey cows with a very high butterfat content of 4 % but less gallons per cow.

Feed that usually consisted of cotton seed meal and hulls was already in the troughs behind the head gates and the cows knew where to go. Once one row of cows was all in place the head gates were closed locking them all in place. The second row of cows entered the barn and were done likewise.

The milk workers would then wash the cow’s udders with warm water and then start milking each cow by hand. This was a slow and tedious job that took several hours and a strong grip. The milk now in the milk pail was taken over to the milk house adjacent and poured into 10 gallon milk cans.

When full, these cans were lifted into the cooler which was a vat of cool water usually cooled by a refrigeration unit attached. Milk trucks came by later and lifted the cans out of the cooler and onto the truck. Empty cans were swapped out daily. The trucks then carried the milk to creameries in the Atlanta area.

This process of milking was a twice a day job starting about 3 a.m. and again at about 3 p.m. Years before this and before rural electricity, cooling was a problem and milk always had to be distributed pretty fast to keep from spoiling.

Most milk customers served were in the cities with milk bottles deposited at customers doors daily.

In the country and further out from cities there were few dairy operations because nearly each family had a milk cow for personal milk.

In about 1946 just after World War II, veterans were coming home and looking for a way to make a living. With the financing help of Farmers Home Administration many entered the dairy business. There were now 30 to 40 dairy farms in our county and milking had progressed quite a bit. Each farm averaged about 200 acres and the milking heard averaged about 30 cows.

Cows were now big Holstein cows that gave a lot more milk even though the butterfat content was lower.

Milk barns now were smaller with raised ramps and stanchions where the cows now entered and were closed into a metal stanchion with a feed trough in one end usually automatically fed.

The cows now were standing about three feet higher than the milk pit which made it much easier on the workers to wash them off and attach vacuum milkers. Water lines were nearby to furnish fresh water for cleaning up the barns. Heated water was now used to wash up all milking equipment daily.
Stainless steel pipelines were now used to move the milk from the cow to the cooler uninterrupted. Instead of the water coolers with big heavy 10 gallon cans there was now the stainless steel refrigerated milk tank which held several hundred gallons depending on the size of the herd. All of this could now be washed and cleaned automatically which kept bacteria count lower.

Now big refrigerated tank trucks came by and pumped the milk out of the barn tanks and into their trucks for the same trip to Atlanta.

Side Note: Cows have to be bred once each year by a herd bull or by artificial insemination. In nine months the cow gives birth to a calf. If it’s a bull calf he goes to the sale barn after several days of colostrum consumption. If a heifer calf she is usually raised for herd replacement. After several years cows go “dry” and cannot breed or produce milk so they too go to the sale barn. Good cows can produce up to 10 gallons or more of milk each per day.

Two dairies have advanced even more but the hard dirty work seven days a week is still necessary. Cows still love to graze on green pastures and eat good hay and produce good quality milk but they still have to be caught up early each morning and again later in the same day for milking. Some even milk three times a day now.

Cleanliness is still important and a government inspector checks this very often. Things have changed too; dairies now have several hundred acres of grazing land, milk 100 to 400 cows and produce much more milk per cow with a butter fat of 3.2%. The cost of producing milk is much higher now.

However, there are now only three dairies in Jasper County—Charlie Lane, Phil Harvey, and Warren Camp. The picture above is of the Phil Harvey dairy on Hwy. 212. He is milking about 370 big Holstein cows daily that average about eight gallons of milk per cow. His milk tank holds up to 4,000 gallons of milk.

He milks twice a day, 6 a.m. until noon and 7 p.m. until midnight with two shifts of workers, usually Hispanic.

A tank truck picks up his milk daily and takes it to Atlanta.

When I was dairying in the 50s I vividly remember the hard before day and late afternoon work, seven days plus field work and I also remember the quote once made by the late Gene Talmadge, Governor of Georgia, saying that “a dairy farmer don’t need a Sunday suit of clothes.”

I’m sure that is not true now, or is it?
