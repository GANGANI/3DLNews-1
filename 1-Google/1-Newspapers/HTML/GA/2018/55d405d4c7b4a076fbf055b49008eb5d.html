BREAKING NEWS: Brian Crow Surrenders Teaching Certificate
By Editor Becky Watts

ATLANTA - Pike County Times has followed the May 23, 2017 complaint of inappropriate contact between Brian Crow, who is no longer a teacher in the Pike County School System, and a female student since the story became public in 2017. Crow resigned as a teacher in the Pike County School System, and the case was presented to the Grand Jury but No Billed due to a lack of cooperation from the victim in this case.

The case was then sent by the Pike County School System to the Georgia Professional Standards Commission (PSC) to determine whether there would be sanctions against Crow's teaching certification. Crow then requested for the Attorney General's Office of State Administrative Hearing to hear his case in February of 2018.

The case was finalized when a Consent Order was filed on September 13, 2018. Cases are listed by number in the minutes and uploaded after being approved the following month.

The Case

Here is the investigative case in a nutshell. There was a complaint that "the educator allegedly had inappropriate physical contact with a female student." The Finding of Fact outlined the case in which the educator denied the allegations and the student denied the allegations as well as any wrongdoing on the part of the teacher. However, according to the investigation notes from this case, investigators from the Pike County Sheriff's Office subpoenaed Crow's phone records and found over 100 calls between the teacher and student over the span of four weeks with calls "at various times throughout the day and night" ranging from 3 to 20 minutes. The student and teacher both told investigators that the calls were about softball. The Finding of Fact noted that the teacher was not arrested and no criminal charges were brought against him.

The Standard, Rules or Laws in Complaint include the following (included verbatim from the report):
PSC Rule 505-6-.01 (3b) - The educator failed to maintain a professional relationship with students by committing an act of child abuse, including physical and verbal abuse; committing an act of cruelty to children or any act of child endangerment; committing any sexual act with a student or soliciting such from a student; engaging in or permitting harassment of or misconduct toward a student that would violate a state or federal law; soliciting, encouraging, or consummating an inappropriate written, verbal, electronic or physical relationship with a student; furnishing tobacco, alcohol, or illegal/unauthorized drugs to any student; and failing to prevent the use of alcohol or illegal/unauthorized drugs by students who are under the educatorâs supervision (including but not limited to the educatorâs residence or any other private setting).
PSC Rule 505-6-.01 (3a) - The educator violated federal, state, or local laws or statutes.
PSC Rule 505-6-.01 (3j) - The educator failed to demonstrate conduct that follows generally recognized professional standards and preserves the dignity and integrity of the teaching profession by demonstrating conduct that impairs and/or diminishes the certificate holderâs ability to function professionally in his or her employment position, or behavior or conduct that is detrimental to the health, welfare, discipline, or morals of students;
PSC Rule 505-6-.01 (5a7) - The educator is unfit for employment as an educator for other good and sufficient cause.

The Consent Order lays out the Findings of Fact that Brian Crow holds a Georgia certification and that he "allegedly engaged in inappropriate communication and conduct with a student." It further says that Crow wishes to "resolve this matter without a hearing and voluntarily surrender his certificate."

The Conditions of Surrender are plainly laid out in this order. First, Crow acknowledges the surrender of certification and "forfeits and relinquishes all right, title and privilege" of being able to teach in the public schools in the State of Georgia unless his certification is reinstated. He cannot serve as a volunteer or be employed as an educator, paraprofessional, aide, substitute teacher, or in any other position in the State of Georgia. [Note from the Editor: I called the PSC to ask if this Consent Order also applied to private schools and was advised that this certification does not apply to private schools--only to schools in the public school system. Private schools have rules that they must abide by but educators holding a public school certification is not one of them.]

Second, Crow shall not be eligible to petition for reinstatement or for a new certificate for three years. This three years began on August 7, 2017 and will continue through August 6, 2020. If Crow decides to petition for reinstatement or a new certificate, the PSC will hear his petition and will have access to the entire investigative file for this case.

Finally, the PSC accepts the voluntary surrender without condoning Crow's conduct and understands that this Consent Order may be disseminated as a disciplinary action. There is an Affidavit attached to the Consent Order where Crow swears that he is over the age of 18 and competent to testify in this matter and swears that he did not work as a educator in the public school system in Georgia from August 7, 2017 through the signing date of June 15, 2018.

Closing

In short, Crow turned over his teaching certification because of the allegations of inappropriate conduct with a student in 2017 and will not be allowed to teach in the public school system through August 6, 2020. This is the last of the information that was needed to close out this series of articles.

Pike County Times extends its gratitude to the Pike County School System, the Pike County Sheriff's Office, the District Attorney's Office, and the Professional Standards Commission for answering my (many) questions and sending open records as requested.

Acts of Sexual Abuse or Sexual Misconduct

(a) Any student (or parent or friend of a student) who has been the victim of an act of sexual abuse or sexual misconduct by a teacher, administrator or other school system employee is urged to make an oral report of the act to any teacher, counselor or administrator at his/her school.

(b) Any teacher, counselor or administrator receiving a report of sexual abuse or sexual misconduct of a student by a teacher, administrator or other employee shall make an oral report of the incident immediately by telephone or otherwise to the school principal or principalâs designee, and shall submit a written report of the incident to the school principal or principalâs designee within 24 hours. Thus, one clearly knowledgeable witness may provide a preponderance of evidence over a dozen witnesses with hazy testimony, or a signed agreement with definite terms may outweigh opinions or speculation about what the parties intended. Preponderance of the evidence is required in a civil case and is contrasted with "beyond a reasonable doubt," which is the more severe test of evidence required to convict in a criminal trial. No matter what the definition stated in various legal opinions, the meaning is somewhat subjective."
