BOE sees new design plans for Jonesboro High

Updated Dec 28, 2018

JONESBORO — The Clayton County Board of Education got its first look at the proposed design for Jonesboro High School last week.

Architects Cooper Carey and Associates presented the design for building during the Jan. 9 BOE meeting.

The $37 million construction project is expected to begin in the summer and be completed by August 2020. Monies from SPLOST IV and V will pay for the new construction.

The two-story building will expand from 213,000 square feet to 265,000 square feet, adding an additional 52,000 square feet to accommodate 1,600 students. The student population, as of October 2017, is 1,343.

The plans call for razing the majority of the school including the library, tech lab, cafeteria, gym, green house, lockers and vocational building. That would leave the classrooms in the center of the building and the gym constructed in 2010.

All new facilities will be rebuilt on the school’s current footprint.

Sophia Tarkhan, associate design principal with Cooper Carey, said the school’s current layout and facade reflect several additions and modifications that have been built over the years.

“They’re not coming together well,” Tarkhan said.

The building’s front entrance will also change. It currently faces Mt. Zion Parkway. The new proposal calls for the front entrance to be placed on North McDonough Street. It will feature separate drop off locations for buses and car riders, but each will funnel to the same front entrance.

Tarkhan said changing the entrance orientation will help alleviate traffic concerns at the corner of Mt. Zion Parkway and North McDonough Street.

Additionally, she said it will create a sense of pride for students.

“They are going to be very proud of the school,” Tarkhan said. “That’s our desire.”

As for safety, visitors to the school will not be able to access the building in the new design without first entering through the reception area. Students transitioning from classes will no longer have to exit and re-enter the building.

“We made sure all the circulation is interior for safety purposes,” Tarkhan said.

The new building will include a mock trial courtroom, separate cosmetology and barber shop and health information facilities and medical office. The gym will be two stories with access on the second level.

Several board members expressed their content with the new design.

“You have worked a miracle,” said Mary Baker. “It’s fantastic. I can’t wait to see it.”

As a former teacher at Jonesboro High, Ophelia Burroughs said the design was a blessing.

“I came in 1979,” she said. “I’m really happy about the design and how modern it is.”

Jessie Goree agreed.

“I love the design. It is beautiful, and I applaud you on it,” Goree said.

“It is beautiful,” said Judy Johnson. “Jonesboro needs a new school to represent Clayton County and the wonderful basketball team.”

Design principal Jerry Cooper said that when design began in October, they wanted to create a sense of pride in the hearts and minds of students and the community.

“This is not just another red brick school house,” Cooper said.

Two dozen horses dead in Noah’s Ark Road stable fire

Updated Dec 28, 2018

JONESBORO — Investigators are still working to determine the cause of a massive fire at a Noah’s Ark Road stable that killed two dozen horses late Wednesday night

When firefighters with Clayton County Fire and Emergency Services arrived shortly before midnight, the stable was fully engulfed in flames. Only one horse survived.

“One of them escaped, but 24 of them did not,” Battalion Chief Laura Richardson said.

A family renting a home on the property called 911 at about 11:45 p.m. to report the fire. The stable is located near the intersection of Noah’s Ark Road and Dixon Industrial Boulevard.

Richardson said that crews responding to the scene worked first to contain the blaze at the stable, but it had already spread to a small hayloft nearby, and prevention efforts were also required. There was concern of the fire spreading to the woods.

The stable was privately owned by a Clayton County family. “They were pretty devastated,” Richardson said.

It was a tough call for the Fire Department as well.

“It doesn’t matter if it’s a human, it doesn’t matter if it’s an animal. These things are hard,” she said.

Forest Park man steps up as nail salon owner

Updated Dec 28, 2018

FOREST PARK — Clayton County has no shortage of nail shops. But it’s not every day that you find an African-American man with an MBA doing mani-pedis.

Scott Lewis, 44, owner of D’yVonne’s Nails and Beauty Bar, says he is the first African-American man to own a nail shop in Forest Park.

Lewis has been operations manager at the Federal Reserve Bank in Atlanta since 1998. But he’s always loved the nail business and credits his godsister, Stacie Brown, with supporting his passion.

“She’s always been in my corner,” Lewis said.

After earning his MBA in 2012, Lewis dipped his toe, so to speak, into the world of nail salons with a booth in a flea market off I-285.

Now, he’s moved up to a storefront on Jonesboro Road, where Lewis named the shop after his mother, Yvonne. He pointed to a framed article, featuring a beautiful woman kneeling at a utility meter.

“My mother was the first female meter reader in Macon, Georgia,” Lewis said.

Raised by a single mother who excelled at breaking barriers, Lewis is at home in his shop.

“Being a male is a big advantage because women want to go to a man,” he said. “It’s totally different because men are very rare in this industry. So to be one of the very few is a very big accomplishment.”

Lewis also says it’s becoming more socially acceptable for men to enjoy pedicures. “Some don’t because they think it’s more of a woman’s thing,” he explained. “But we try to present it as ‘foot maintenance’ as opposed to a pedicure.”

An entire wall of the shop showcases a rainbow of polishes, some containing tiny dots, shapes, or bits of glitter.

Kesha McCartha, 44, a local business owner, chatted with Carrie Mason, 42, as Mason did her nails in a bright hot pink shade called YouthQuake.

“To be frank, it’s American-owned, especially black-owned, so it was a treat to come here for me,” McCartha said.

Mason, who rents a booth in the shop, has been in the nail business for about a decade, became a master cosmetologist in 2012.

“I met Scott at the Bronner Brothers hair show. We linked up, and he got stuck with me,” she joked.

In between clients, she practices new designs on a series of model hands that seems to claw its way up from her station.

Lewis has taught at the nationally-recognized Bronner Brothers International Beauty Show, which is coming up again in Atlanta next month, as well as Elaine Sterling in Roswell.

The shop offers a range of services, including manicures, pedicures, massage, nail wraps and more. High-end service includes a glass of champagne.

“I want people to relax, kick back and enjoy the services,” Lewis said.

Teacher among alleged gang members charged in 2016 murder of Coates children

Updated May 15, 2020

JONESBORO — Clayton County authorities announced charges Thursday for 11 alleged gang members, including one DeKalb elementary school teacher, in connection with the 2016 murders of two children shot while they slept in their Libby Lane home.

Daveon Coates, 15, and his 11-year-old sister Tatiyana were killed Oct. 22, around 5 a.m. on a Saturday, during a home invasion. Four other unrelated children, ages 6 to 13, were also staying in the home without parental supervision when someone broke in and began shooting.

The brother and sister were found fatally wounded in separate rooms, according to Clayton County police.

Eleven members of one DeKalb County gang are now facing charges related to their deaths, with eight accused of malice murder. Among them is Michael Desean White, 26, a former fifth-grade teacher at Toney Elementary in south DeKalb. White was arrested at the school Friday, March 23.

“Today is a good day. It is a good day for the Coates family, the Clayton County law enforcement family, the Clayton County Police Department, and the citizens of Clayton County,” said Police Chief Kevin Roberts during a press conference announcing the 54-count indictment.

Roberts is the third police chief to head the investigation into the Coates murders over a year and a half, what he called a “trying time” for the agency as they sought justice for the family. The effort was a collaboration between the PD, the Clayton County District Attorney’s Office, the Sheriff’s Office and agencies in DeKalb County and Chattanooga, as well as state and federal investigative agencies.

“There is some small piece of solace that they’ve received from the progress of the case,” Roberts said on behalf of the Coates family, who was not present for the conference.

It was initially believed that the intended target was another 15-year-old boy, Deundre Demond Mitchell, who was sharing the Libby Lane home with the family after a recent move to Georgia from Tennessee. Police said Mitchell was associated with a DeKalb gang and allegedly stole several guns from an apartment in DeKalb County.

When days before the shooting he got word that the gang, alleged in an indictment to be a subset of the Crips, was planning retribution, Mitchell fled to Chattanooga.

Roberts said Thursday it is unclear whether the shooters were aware it was innocent children they targeted. “There was no way that they could know the harm that was coming their way,” he said of the sleeping children.

Mitchell was taken into police custody after he was reportedly involved in a drive-by shooting in Tennessee the Sunday afternoon following the shooting.

It was that incident that led to the first break in the case.

“During that drive-by shooting, a weapon was dropped at the scene,” said Capt. Stefan Schindler, who heads the Major Felony Unit for the CCPD. “That weapon subsequently was linked to our homicide.”

Mitchell will face a charge of felony murder for the theft that led to Tatiyana’s death. According to the indictment, Mitchell was given a deadline to return the stolen guns, or a female family member would be killed.

White, Devin Dunson, Melvin Allen Crockett, Jamar Reshaad Mitchell, Christopher Leonard Spencer, Vernon Jerome Beamon, Jamon Marqice Bynam and Sterlin Obche Pate are accused of malice and felony murder as parties to the crime in the Coates siblings’ deaths. Daveon Coates was shot three times, while Tatiyana was shot twice.

The eight are also accused of home invasion, associated charges of aggravated assault and cruelty to children in the third degree for allegedly committing the crimes in the presence of the Mitchell children. They are each accused of criminal gang activity, including White.

According to a statement from the DeKalb County School District obtained by FOX5 Atlanta, White was removed from his position during the investigation. FOX5 has a news-sharing agreement with the Clayton News.

“All employees must submit to a thorough background check with DCSD’s Public Safety division before being hired in any capacity,” the district said in the statement. “This specific employee was checked twice in 2017; and on both occasions, nothing indicated this employee may be involved in criminal behavior.”

Schindler said his investigators were shocked to learn of White’s involvement.

“We were absolutely flabbergasted — I’ll be honest with you — to come to the realization that we had a fifth-grade teacher in a public school, teaching children, as part of this,” he said. “It took our breath away.”

Sherman Thomas and Rashad Christopher Character are also charged in the case — Thomas with a racketeering charge for allegedly giving false statements to police, and Character with a weapons charge.

“That indictment serves as notice to criminals and more specifically gang members who may hide in Clayton County that they will be apprehended and prosecuted to the greatest extent,” Roberts said.

He encouraged all law enforcement agencies across the metro area and the state to acknowledge gang violence in their communities and take steps to mitigate any future tragedies.

“We cannot pretend that these things don’t exist,” the chief said.

5 arrested after Rex drug house raid

Updated May 15, 2020

REX — Five suspects are in jail after Clayton County Sheriff’s deputies raided a drug house in Rex.

According to Sheriff Victor Hill, officers from CCSO’s Cobra Unit got an anonymous tip via Nixle, a public safety website, that led them to the house off Hartford Drive. There, they allegedly found a stolen Honda Civic in the front yard. Deputies secured a search warrant and raided the house.

Inside, they reportedly found 10 ounces of methamphetamine, 10 ounces of GHB, and two semiautomatic pistols.

Those arrested on July 5 in connection with the case include Casey Michelle Birchfield, 37, of Morrow; Angela Marie Britt, 40, of Hampton; Nicholas Ryan Dix, 26, of Griffin; Robert Kier Narikawa, 48, of Stockbridge; and Brenda Kay Williams, 33, homeless.

Birchfield made her first appearance in court at 10 a.m. Friday and is charged with driving with a suspended or revoked license and with having an expired or missing tag. She is being held on $2,000 bond.

Britt, who is being held without bond on one charge of trafficking methamphetamine or amphetamine, was denied bond at her first appearance Friday at 10 a.m. Britt faces a preliminary hearing at 8 a.m. July 19 and a bond hearing at 8 a.m. July 2o. According to Clayton County inmate records, Britt also goes by the aliases Angel Marie Britt, Angela Marie Glisson, Jamie Snyder, and Sherry Smith.

Dix is charged with trafficking methamphetamine or amphetamine and giving a false address or date of birth. Bond was set Friday at $2500. Dix’s preliminary hearing is scheduled for Thursday, July 12, at 8 a.m., with a bond hearing Friday, July 13, at 8 a.m. Narikawa and Williams each face one charge of violation of the Georgia Controlled Substances Act.

Narikawa’s bond was set at $10,000 at his first appearance Friday at 10 a.m. His preliminary hearing is scheduled for 8 a.m. July 19. He also has a case pending in traffic court for failing to signal a turn.

Williams also had bond set at $10,000 at her first appearance Friday at 10 a.m, and is scheduled for a preliminary hearing at 8 a.m. July 19.

$45.8 million contract awarded to renovate Jonesboro High School

Updated Dec 28, 2018

JONESBORO — The Clayton County Board of Education awarded MEJA Construction Inc. the contract to renovate Jonesboro High School.

The $45.8 million project is set to begin this month and be completed by August 2020. Additional contingency funds as well as architectural and engineering fees bring the total expected project cost to $49.5 million.

The renovations will be funded using Special Purpose Local Option Sales Tax V and Capital Outlay Funds and future monies from SPLOST VI.

The school district is banking on the approval of the proposed $280 million SPLOST VI by voters in March 2019 to help fund the project. If approved, SPLOST VI collection will start on Jan. 1, 2020.

The new Jonesboro High will not resemble the current structure when completed. The majority of the facility will be razed, including the library, tech lab, cafeteria, old gym, green house, lockers and vocational building. The only thing left standing will be the classrooms in the center of the building and the gym constructed in 2010.

The new school will expand from 213,000 square feet to 265,000 square feet, adding an additional 52,000 square feet to accommodate 1,600 students. As of October 2017, the student population was 1,343. The new building will include a mock trial courtroom, separate cosmetology and barber shop, health information facilities and medical office.

The biggest change will be the location of the front entrance, which currently faces Mount Zion Boulevard. It will shift to North McDonough Street and feature separate drop off locations for buses and car riders with each funneling to the same entrance.

In addition to the project contract, Hibernia Enterprises Inc. was awarded the contract to remove and dispose of asbestos materials at the school. The total cost with anticipated expenditures is $227,062.67.

Jonesboro toddler shot dead

Updated May 15, 2020

JONESBORO — Clayton County Police say a toddler has been accidentally shot and killed in Jonesboro.

A source with Clayton County Fire and Emergency Services says the victim was in cardiac arrest at the scene and transported to a hospital from the Hunters Ridge Mobile Home Park in the 300 block of Black Hawk Trail.

The shooting was accidental, “just an unfortunate, devastating situation,” according to CCPD spokesman Captain Scott Stubbs.

The father, Johnathan Butler, 31, was asleep around 12:11 p.m. Thursday when the two-year-old boy came in and got hold of the gun “around the pillow area,” Stubbs said, and pressed the trigger, suffering a gunshot wound to his body. The boy’s mother, Naterika Pearman, 24, was asleep in the front room at the time.

The family is “devastated” and cooperating with investigators, Stubbs said.

The GBI Medical Examiner will conduct an autopsy. Stubbs said the district attorney’s office will determine whether to press charges.

As news crews gathered outside the home, family members asked for privacy. An unidentified man asked a television videographer and the Clayton News to leave the park.

This is at least the third accidental shooting in Clayton County in the past five months:

  • On June 6, Sakhya Bentley, 12, Riverdale, was shot in the chest by another juvenile in her own home when two boys, 13 and 14, were messing around with a gun. Both boys were charged with involuntary manslaughter, reckless conduct and possession of a weapon by a minor.
  • On Oct. 10, Davon Triplett, 23, Forest Park, was shot in the head by Chinequah Hawkins as they hung out with friends at the A & H Apartments. Triplett was about to go to his toddler son’s birthday party and was expecting a daughter. Hawkins faces charges of involuntary manslaughter and reckless conduct. The family’s GoFundMe page shows $3,996 out of the $7,600 needed to pay for Triplett’s burial. His funeral was Oct. 20.

A new study in the Journal of Urban Health found that one in three U.S. homes has at least one firearm, and that 7 percent of children in the U.S. live in homes with at least one loaded, unlocked firearm. According to the study, that figure more than doubled between 2002 and 2015, the last year for which data was available.

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, 208 people in Georgia died in firearms accidents between 2004 and 2015.

Second grocery parking-lot shooting in Stockbridge this week

Updated Dec 28, 2018

STOCKBRIDGE — Clayton County Police detectives are investigating a shooting in a grocery store parking lot, the second in a week for Stockbridge.

On Monday around 9:10 p.m., Clayton County Police were called to the Krystal for a man who had been shot in the parking lot of the Kroger at 3618 Ga. Highway 138. According to police, the man had been shot several times.

Witnesses told police five or six men had been standing around a car, helping a woman who had locked her keys inside. A second woman in a different car got out and yelled at the men to move.

The men and the woman got into a shouting match, and another man got out of the second woman’s car, allegedly brandishing a gun. The argument escalated, and one of the men who had been helping the first woman reportedly shot the other man with the gun several times.

The wounded man was transported to a hospital, where police say he WAS being treated “for what is thought to be non-life-threatening injuries.”

Police are not releasing any names at this time and have not determined whether or not charges will be filed. The investigation is ongoing.

This is the second shooting in a Stockbridge grocery store parking lot this week. Clayton County Police Department spokesperson Officer Marcena Davis said the two incidents are unrelated.

On Oct. 1, at least one man was hospitalized in serious condition after a shooting around 5:06 p.m. near the gas pumps in the Ingles parking lot on Walt Stephens Road. That store is in Henry County Police jurisdiction.

At the time, Henry County Police said that it was “unclear whether the incident was robbery or domestic related.”

Jonesboro High Class of 75 opens time capsule

Updated Dec 28, 2018

JONESBORO — For a moment, if you will, close your eyes and imagine the year is 1975.

Gerald Ford was president and “All in the Family” was the No. 1 show on television.

Depending on the radio station you listened to, Barry Manilow was belting out “Mandy,” David Bowie was singing about “Fame” and John Denver was thanking God he’s a country boy.

“Jaws” and “The Rocky Horror Picture Show” were playing on the big screen, and kids were caring for their pet rocks.

At Jonesboro High School, the Class of 1975 was planning their next steps in life and looking into the future, but still counting on remembering their high school days.

To do so, Jonesboro High’s Key Club members and graduating students created a time capsule and buried it in front of the then-new Career, Technical and Agricultural Education building.

In a large metal container made to hold a helicopter engine, students placed letters to their future selves, class pictures, yearbooks, magazines, a handmade flag and a Bible. The capsule was originally to be opened in the year 2000, but there was one problem — nobody could remember where it was buried, except for one man. Former history teacher Wayne Boddie was the school’s Key Club advisor in 1975. He now lives in Washington state, but when he heard that Jonesboro High is set to undergo a $45.8 million renovation he knew the capsule had to be saved. The majority of the school will be razed including the vocational building.

Boddie wrote a letter to the Kiwanis Club letting them know where to find the time capsule. Then-President Bob McCullough headed up the search. With the help of a metal detector he found it.

“He gave me pretty good directions as to where to start,” McCullough said.

The plan was to dig up, what initially was thought to be a small metal box from the Class of 1976. But what McCullough and others found was actually the time capsule from the Class of 1975. And it was a lot bigger than anyone had remembered.

The digging started on Nov. 30 with McCullough and Jonesboro High’s head custodian Jared Johnson. Current students picked up a shovel and joined in. After two hours of digging, Principal Felicia Brown called on the construction crew at the school. It took a skid steer to get the capsule out of the ground.

The idea was to open it on Dec. 1, with former students and teachers present. But the Class of 1975 did such a good job sealing it, they were unable to open it. What’s another week of waiting after 43 years?

For a week, the school’s maintenance crew worked to remove 20 large, rusted bolts that held the memories all those years.

On the chilly and rainy morning of Dec. 8, faculty and students from several grades gathered to see what was put away for the future.

“The senior class was instructed to write a letter to their future selves,” said David Worley, member of the Key Club. He said the metal box sat outside the lunchroom and students could drop off their letters or trinkets in the box.

“I don’t remember it being that big though,” he said laughing.

As McCullough pulled the items out one by one, there was laughter and excitement. Nostalgia for days gone by bubbled up as did the memories of old friends. For just a moment the past became present.

Eddie Hunter, Class of 1976, found his signature on the first item pulled from the capsule — a roster full of student signatures.

“It just takes me back,” he said. “Those were some of the best days of my life. I had great friends, and we had great times together.”

Hunter reminisced about racing his 1969 Plymouth Road Runner in front of the school.

“We turned that road into a drag strip out there,” he said, referring to Mount Zion Boulevard.

Hunter pointed to his best friend’s signature, Richard Craig, right above his on the roster.

“We’ve stayed in touch all these years thanks to Facebook,” he said.

Though they’re using technology nowadays to keep in touch, Hunter said he was glad he attended school before social media came about.

“Back then, people had to actually talk to each other, and we made great friends,” he said.

Steve Lundquist, 1979 JHS graduate, said he attended the capsule opening out of curiosity.

“It was neat to see all this,” he said.

Lundquist said he recognized many of the names and faces as friends.

“Back then people moved here and stayed here,” he said.

Several items unearthed will remain at the school, while the letters will be mailed to students.

After a few stops and starts, finding and digging out the capsule, McCullough said the work was worth it.

“It’s always fun to go back in the past,” he said. “It’s been like a treasure-hunting adventure.”

One more treasure hunt may be on the horizon at Jonesboro High before construction begins. The Class of 1976, too, buried a time capsule on the property.

Carol Bridewell, Class of 76, said she’s sure her class created a time capsule because they were the Bicentennial class.

“I feel certain we buried our own in front of the flagpole,” she said.

Hill announces more arrests in Clayton County gang roundup

Updated Dec 28, 2018

McDONOUGH — Four alleged gang members were arrested last week at a home in Henry County, the Clayton County Sheriff’s Office has announced.

Clayton County deputies joined forces with their Henry counterparts April 11 to serve an arrest warrant for Nayya Laron Terry, 17. Terry has been wanted since March 12 on a charge of gang activity.

According to the Clayton County sheriff, Terry was arrested at the same address where deputies had previously arrested his older brother, Hassan Mohammed, on suspicion of involvement with the same gang. Both brothers are allegedly affiliated with Jahsiah James Lee, who is facing murder charges in what authorities have described as a retaliatory, gang-related shooting death.

“Members of Sheriff Victor Hill’s elite Vice Squad teamed up with Henry County Sheriff’s Office to arrest Terry for gang participation at the same address where they arrested his older brother,” the Sheriff’s Office said in a public advisory. “Upon arrival, they found a drug operation, weapons, and arrested three additional gang members.”

Israel Robinson, 23, of Hampton, Shameek Nichols, 23, of Ellenwood and 35-year-old Umm Hill of Ellenwood were arrested on various drugs charges related to marijuana possession and booked into the Henry County Jail. Nichols has been released on bond.

Terry was transported to the Clayton County Jail to face his charge. He posted a $10,000 bail on April 12 and was released to await a preliminary hearing on May 1.

“Sheriff Victor Hill’s elite squads will continue to target all gangs until they learn to stay out of and far away from Clayton County,” the Clayton Sheriff’s Office said.
