A joke that wasn’t funny: Student awaits trial

  • Laura James @laurajames225
  • Sep 25, 2014
  • Sep 25, 2014 Updated Jun 9, 2015
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS

The University of Georgia student who caused chaos on campus last week after he posted a “prank” threat on social media was released from jail on Tuesday, Sept. 23.

Ariel Omar Arias, 19, had been incarcerated with no bond at the Athens-Clarke County jail since Friday, when he was arrested on two felony counts of terroristic threats.

Judge Patricia Barron set his bond at $8,000 — $4,000 for each count — at a special bond hearing at the Athens-Clarke County Magistrate Court on Tuesday.

Police received several reports of a threat of violence directed at the Zell B. Miller Learning Center posted on the social media website Yik Yak around 11:30 a.m. on Friday.

Multiple students reported the post stated “If you want to live, don’t be at the MLC at 12:15,” according to a previous Red & Black article.

Although Arias was charged with two counts of terroristic threats for Friday’s events, Jimmy Williamson, UGA chief of police, said there was only one threat, but multiple people received it.

“We charged him with what we thought were the most appropriate charges,” Williamson said. “Could we have charged him with more? Yes, we could have charged him with more. We charged him with what we felt was most appropriate.”

Rafael Montano, sophomore international affairs major from Lilburn, met Arias in eighth grade, went to high school with him and lived two doors down from him in Russell Hall last year.

Montano said he felt complete shock when he found out he knew the guy who caused the evacuation of the MLC.

“I was in shock,” Montano said. “So were all my friends because we knew him. We hung out with him last year. We never thought he was capable of doing something like this.”

Arias was a psychology major planning on attending medical school.

“We used to go to the dining halls together,” Montano said. “We would do movie nights in each other’s rooms on the weekends and stuff like that.”

Although Montano has known Arias since eighth grade, they were not close in high school but became closer when they lived on the same hall freshman year.

At Parkview High School in Lilburn, where both Arias and Montano attended, Arias played baseball his freshman and sophomore years. A Parkview High School junior varsity baseball roster has Arias listed as a right-handed pitcher.

“He wasn’t involved in any clubs or anything really,” Montano said.

Montano said Arias was not involved in any clubs or extracurricular activities at UGA either.

“He was an introverted guy,” he said. “He could be a little intimidating when you first meet him, but if you get to know him well, you could build a relationship and talk and stuff.”

Arias liked to try to be funny, even in situations that weren’t humorous, Montano said.

“He likes to joke a lot. He was one of the guys who would take a joke to the next level, so I don’t really see how he could have done this [act of violence] legitimately,” he said. “We had a friend in common who said she was going on a date night last year, and he started joking about it. She got to the point where she was like, ‘I can’t handle this anymore. I don’t want to be near you right now.’ She didn’t talk to him for like a week or two, and then he apologized like, ‘I’m sorry for what I said. It was in the moment, and I was trying to be fun. I’m sorry.’”

Montano said Arias made sex-related comments about their mutual friend and the person with whom she was going on the date night. He said Arias eventually apologized.

“He apologized in front of all of us and she said ‘OK, yeah I know you take everything to the next level, but don’t do it again,’” he said. “When he jokes sometimes, he doesn’t realize the consequences of his jokes.”

Montano said he believes the threat was truly a “prank,” as Arias claimed.

“He was probably just joking, but didn’t realize the consequences of that joke,” he said.

At the special bond hearing on Tuesday, Dustin Smith, a detective sergeant with the UGA Police Department, said he did not “have any information that would lead [him] to believe that” Arias was a threat to UGA.

At the hearing, Arias’ attorney said his client was an “excellent student” with no previous criminal history.

The judge also barred Arias from Athens-Clarke County, with the exception of appearing in court or meet with his attorney, and ordered him to live with his father Edwin Arias in Lilburn. Arias is also suspended from UGA on an interim basis.

Barrett Malone, director of the UGA Office of Student Conduct, said Arias is barred from the campus until he makes an appointment to meet with the student conduct office.

“He’ll go through the conduct process and may receive one of the sanctions outlined in the code of conduct that could include expulsion, but it just depends on the facts and circumstances of the case whether he decides to go to a formal hearing, that kind of thing,” Malone said. “He’s interim suspended, pending the conduct process.”

— Editor’s Note: A previous headline of this story read that Arias was awaiting ”sentencing.” He cannot be sentenced without having stood trial. The Red & Black regrets this editorial oversight.
