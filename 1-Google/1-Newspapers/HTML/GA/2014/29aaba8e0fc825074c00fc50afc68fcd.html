Shaq tells Arabia Mountain High students to drive responsibly

April 10, 2014April 9, 2014 Andrew Cauthen 0 Comments

Retired NBA star Shaquille O’Neal said his mother encouraged him to give back to others.

That’s why he was at Arabia Mountain High School on April 3 encouraging students to refrain from driving drunk or distracted.

“Three things can happen when you drive recklessly,” O’Neal said. “One, you can be arrested. Two, you can cause serious or horrible injuries to someone else. Three, you can die. More teens your age will die from driving recklessly than from homicide or suicide.”

“So my question is, ‘Why risk it?’” he said. “I know how it is when you’re in your car and somebody texts you and you try to text back. Reckless driving: don’t do it.”

Sponsored by the DeKalb County District Attorney’s Office, DeKalb County Fire Rescue and Georgia Department of Highway Safety, O’Neal’s Arabia Mountain High visit was part of “Ghost Out,” an annual program that highlights the impact of dangerous driving practices and distracted driving. The event also featured a mock hearing for DUI defendant, portrayed by Arabia Mountain student Alexis Smoot.

“Our goal is to teach young student drivers the effects and consequences of driving under the influence, texting while driving and more,” said District Attorney Robert James in a statement. “I know we have all seen cars swerve on the road when a person is talking on the phone or trying to text and drive. A split second can change someone’s life forever. We see lives lost every day due to reckless behavior behind the wheel.

“This is a great message for young people to hear as we approach the prom season,” James added. “Hopefully our efforts will help change behaviors and save lives down the road.”

DeKalb County Chief Assistant District Attorney Nicole Marchand Golden portrayed a judge in the mock hearing in which the defendant was sentenced to 10 years in prison, five years of community service, five years of probation and fined $1,000 for causing a death driving while intoxicated.

“I’d like to go easy on you, but the family wants me to prosecute you to the fullest extent of the law,” Golden said during the mock hearing. “More importantly, I have to teach you a lesson. And through you, I have to teach a lesson to all of your friends and everyone in high school around you. This is not something that the court takes lightly.

“You made a bad decision to drink,” Golden told the minor. “And you made a bad decision to drive after you had been drinking.”

During a news conference, O’Neal said he talks to students all the time.

“Mom always told me if you can help other people, especially the children, just go out and do it,” he said.

“I think why I’m still relevant at the age of 42 is because I speak the language,” he said. “I have six children. I’m this cool guy, and I think the kids can relate to that.

“I speak to all the children. I speak all languages,” he said. “What I want them to get out of this is ‘Shaq can do it, I can do it.’

“I wasn’t the smartest kid in the class,” O’Neal said. “I wasn’t the most athletic kid in the class. What I did have was respect for my elders, and I did have dreams.”
