Savannah Morning News' Larry Peterson dies at age 71

Corey Dickstein  |  Savannah Morning News

Throughout his more than four-decade career that began in his native Southern California and ended in his adopted Coastal Georgia, Larry Peterson established himself as the kind of journalist his peers strive to be - one who held accountable those in power.

A dogged seeker of truth, Larry, who spent 15 years at the Savannah Morning News, used his skills as an investigator and his knowledge of political processes and the individuals he wrote about to carefully and clearly explain to the public the often-convoluted nature of the topics he covered.

Larry died late Thursday in his Tybee Island home about six weeks after being diagnosed with cancer. He was 71. Memorial arrangements are still being planned.

A fearless muckraker, Larry never shied from asking tough questions and welcomed controversial or difficult topics. In addition to his duties as the Savannah newspaper's chief political reporter from 2004 through his retirement in January 2013, Larry initiated and reported the majority of the Savannah Morning News' investigative coverage of what led to the 2008 Imperial Sugar refinery explosion in which 14 people were killed.

To those who knew him well, he was more than a respected byline. Larry was both endearingly quirky and exceptionally kind.

"Larry was a terrific reporter and a terrific editor, and he loved doing both very much," said Lanie Lippincott Peterson, his wife of 27 years. "He was a terrific man, a kind and loving man and a wonderful father to our only daughter Lindsay. I loved him very much."

Larry began his career in newspapers as a college student at California State University at Long Beach, taking his first full-time job in 1970 as a city government reporter at the Chula Vista (Calif.) Star-News. He spent 20 years at the Orange County Register as a political reporter and an assistant city editor.

It was through that job that he met Lanie, a rival political reporter with the Los Angeles Times.

They began to get to know each other during a 1986 dinner covering an event for then-California Gov. George Deukmejian.

"We were head-to-head competitors; we were always trying to scoop each other," Lanie recalled. "That evening we were sitting at the table and started talking … I realized I liked talking to him, and we started dating almost immediately."

Lanie's editors didn't like her dating a rival reporter. Eventually they pulled her off the political beat.

"It was worth it," she said. "We got married about a year later on the cliffs above the ocean in Laguna Beach. There were politicians - Republicans and Democrats, both - at the wedding. They all respected Larry."

In 1995, Larry joined the Savannah Morning News as metro editor. After a brief period working as an editor at a trio of other newspapers, he returned in 2001 to the Tybee home he and Lanie still own. He took a job as the assistant business editor at the Savannah Morning News before he returned to being a reporter.

He was respected as an editor, but he was revered as a political writer, garnering the trust of both his readers and public servants through his detailed stories and contextual columns in his Political Notebook series.

"As an elected official, it's always good to talk to a reporter who not only knew politics, but he loved it," said Eric Johnson, a former Georgia state senator from Savannah and a 2010 Republican candidate for governor. "You'd wish some stories didn't get written or that the reporter put a little different perspective on it, but Larry knew what he was doing, and you could always appreciate that."

Savannah Alderman Tom Bordeaux, who served eight terms as a state representative, noted that after more than a decade of knowing Larry and reading his articles he had not figured out whether he had any particular political leanings.

"He had a keen mind for politics, and he put it into understandable stories for the public," Bordeaux said. "He got the answers, not just the numbers.

"Larry understood what politics ought to be, and he asked questions that would make you think - he'd make us dig deeper into our own selves to answer the hard questions. That's an immeasurable loss (for the Savannah community) to not have anyone else who asks those questions that way."

Larry, who much preferred a time-worn University of Southern California Trojans football T-shirt and a pair of cargo shorts to a collared shirt and tie, seemingly spoke his own language, generally known to his colleagues as "Larryisms."

An event barely worthy of news coverage was a "Monkeyfish festival," a complicated, hard-hitting story was a real "thumb sucker" and computers, more often than not, were described as "hemorrhoids."

When those computers - often Larry's nemesis - were especially troublesome, Larry would snap to attention, render a stiff salute to the machine and loudly declare, "Gen. Fubarski is in command."

Apart from his colorful vocabulary - that, indeed, included unprintable terminology he may have picked up during his stint in the U.S. Army that included a tour in Vietnam and a Bronze Star - Larry had an unique ability to explain the often complicated nature of politics.

"He was guided by a gifted writer's hand when he put words to paper," said Burke Day, a former state representative from Tybee. "Whatever Larry wrote was always balanced and factual. You can be on opposite sides of the fence and take what he wrote to be the gospel.

"And if you disagreed, you could call and give your opinion and he'd listen.

"I don't think there's a higher compliment for a journalist than that."

He had an unerring instinct to sniff out a lie and would go sometimes to extreme lengths when he suspected an official was trying to dupe the public.

Savannah Morning News Executive Editor Susan Catron recalled an incident when a politician who was living in South Carolina tried to run for a Georgia Senate seat.

"Larry loved to hold politicians accountable. He believed in the process and what it means - he'd go to great lengths to keep it honest," Catron said. "A few years ago, he spent days watching a house and mailbox a politician claimed for his residence.

"In the end, he found the man … didn't even live in the state, own land here, had no car registered in the state and had not paid Peach State income taxes.

"He hated politicians who were cynical about the voters and the process and detested those who treated citizens as if they were stupid."

He had an equal disdain for the apparent carelessness of the officials found responsible for the fatal explosion at Imperial Sugar.

Fewer barks were more focused than his when he asked pointed questions of former Imperial Sugar President and CEO John Sheptor following the blast and fire that killed 14 people, recalled Anita Sue Hagin, who worked with Larry to plan coverage in the wake of the incident.

Larry's questions of Sheptor and other Imperial Sugar officials continued after the final reports were released by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration and the U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board alleging safety violations at the Port Wentworth plant.

Larry followed with an assessment of state and federal elected officials' efforts at putting legislation in place to prevent another disaster.

In fact, in 2010 the Savannah Morning News received three statewide first-place awards stemming from his investigation of the explosion, including an individual Story of the Year award for him.

"Never will I forget the countless hours I spent, Larry at my side, poring through hundreds of photos provided by the Bureau and of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms, the Georgia Office of the Insurance and Safety Fire Commissioner and the Savannah-Chatham police," said Hagin, the general manager of Savannah magazine and former Savannah Morning News assistant managing editor.

"We discussed each image - a mangled clock capturing the time '7:15 p.m.,' a metal wall with '3 BODIES FOUND HERE' scrolled across it, the remains of a disintegrated cardboard box atop a forklift that was used as a makeshift roof to keep sugar from falling on employees as they drove through the warehouse.

"As telling as those images were, they pale in comparison to the stories that Larry has told, the truths that he has exposed by way of his institutional knowledge and unflinching questioning."

His commitment to telling those stories, especially that of the Imperial Sugar catastrophe, continued even through his retirement.

Just before his diagnosis of bladder cancer, Larry had finished penning a 300-page book - his third; he'd written two journalism-based mystery novels - that looked further into the explosion. Lanie and Larry's agent Janice Shay are currently seeking a publisher for the work titled "Deadly Dust."

Just as Larry continued to commit journalism, as he would say, through the end of his life, he continued his love affair with the ocean.

Every bit a Southern California surfer, Larry continued his daily treks from his beach-front home to the Atlantic Ocean for a swim or to catch a wave on his 10-foot longboard even after he was diagnosed with cancer.

A sign posted on his cubicle wall until the day he left the Parkway Pantheon, as he'd dubbed the Savannah Morning News' Chatham Parkway headquarters, read, "The terrorists win if you don't surf."

His love of the water began when he was around 17, Lanie said, and although he was often complimented after gracefully navigating a wave, Larry never considered himself a particularly good surfer.

He loved life, Lanie said. He doted on their 26-year-old daughter Lindsay, and he loved his eight brothers and sisters. And through it all, she added, he loved his work and serving his community.

"I sometimes feel guilty that I haven't worked in any community service projects," Larry wrote about his retirement in his July 13 column for the Savannah Morning News.

"My rationalization for not having done it so far is that journalism - which, with or without consenting adults, I still commit - is a form of such service."

Larry made an impact on everyone who spent time with him. Here are more comments from those who worked with him, for him, and who loved him:

Jenel Few, Savannah Morning News reporter

Nineteen years ago I interviewed for a police reporter job at the Savannah Morning News with a tall lanky man wearing a University of Southern California letterman's jacket and wet hair. He had been surfing on his lunch break.

I told him how I had dreamed about attending USC since elementary school when I saw Cheryl Miller lead the women's basketball team to NCAA glory. I got emotional when I recounted how that dream was crushed because of a clerical error with my academic scholarship.

Much to my surprise, Larry was even more broken up about it than I was. We talked for over an hour about growing up in California and the tough transition from life in Southern California to life in South Georgia. A few days later Larry became my boss. I wouldn't have made it through the transition without him.

Eric Curl, SMN reporter

Some my fondest memories are of Larry yelling at his computer for non-compliance. Still, he managed to put that keyboard to good use by providing an informative look at the world of politics, as well as spinning entertaining yarns in his columns. The last time I saw Larry was about two or three months ago when he was surfing with his long board on Tybee's North Beach, bad back and all. He got stung by a jellyfish at one point. That is how I will always remember Larry, trying to catch a good wave, no matter what.

Michael Traynor, SMN publisher

Larry was a tremendous journalist and a consummate professional. He took on the tough stories and worked them hard to get to the facts. His writing and reporting skills were superb as he covered our local politicians while working on investigative and other news.

Later in his career, his reporting on the Savannah sugar refinery explosion brought clarity as he reported on the causes of the tragedy along with the many outcomes needed to make sure it will never happen again.

Larry's reporting was fair, accurate and sometimes hard hitting which caused hard feelings from some politicians, but the true measure was the high level of respect he had with these same people. He will truly be missed.

Susan Catron, SMN executive editor

Larry loved to hold politicians accountable. He believed in the process and what it means - he'd go to great lengths to keep it honest. A few years ago, he spent days watching a house and mailbox a politician claimed for his residence. In the end, he found the man running for a local state senate seat didn't even live the state, own land here, had no car registered in the state and had not paid Peach State income taxes. He hated politicians who were cynical about the voters and the process and detested those who treated citizens as if they were stupid.

Larry was loved in our newsroom, and he made everyone around him better at our work - the work he loved.

Anita Sue Hagin, general manager of Savannah magazine and former Savannah Morning New editor

As far back as I can remember, Larry never called me by name.

From the time I started at the Savannah Morning News on May 7, 2001, as a news planner/designer, every hello, good-bye or page proof I offered him was met with a spirited "Dog Named Blue."

Never did I ask him why.

Instead of appearing young and unknowing to this storied senior journalist and editor with a sign on his desk espousing "The terrorists win if you don't surf," I had enlisted the help of Google. An old folk song with unclear roots, "Old Blue" has allegedly been performed or recorded by a range of singers from America folk legend Gilbert Vandine "Cisco" Houston to Jim Jackson to Joan Baez.

The lyrics vary a bit; the gist is the same: a tale of a man with a beloved old hunting dog.

"I'll take my axe and I'll take my horn

And get me a possum in the new-grown corn.

Well Old Blue barked and I went to see

And he had a possum up in a tree

Well the possum crawled out on the limb

Blue barked, the possum, possum growled at him

Singing "Ya-ho Blue, you good dog you."

He may have thought I was a dogged visual journalist, but if ever there was "Old Blue," it was Larry.

His possums: gubernatorial and congressional candidates, local and state politicians, CEOs and business leaders.

Bret Bell, former SMN reporter

I worked with Larry while I was at the paper from 2000-2007 and had the privilege of reporting with him on a few congressional campaigns. The political operatives hated him, because he didn't tolerate their nonsense. Reporting with Larry Peterson on a congressional election was like batting next to David Ortiz in October. We were so lucky to have him, and he was so much better than the rest of us at this stuff.

I'll remember him in his cubicle under a mountain of paper at the old Bay Street offices, cranking out reams of copy as he wailed on his keyboard with two fingers, peering from beneath his oversized glasses, and muttering profanity under his breath. He was very much one of the last of the old school of journalists, though I don't recall too many of the old guard going home to surf at the end of each day. Larry Peterson was one of a kind, and the world is less interesting without him in it.

Josh Rayburn, SMN managing editor for continuous news

God help the PR flack or spokesman who tried to slip something by Larry or - even worse - wasn't completely honest with him.

He'd be on the phone and all would be well - until something would make his voice change. You'd start hearing words and phrases such as "No, you didn't" and "That's not what you said." Then you'd get that golden moment and hear something to the effect of "Well, let me tell you what I've found." That's when you knew our Mr. P was taking someone to school, and that if the person he was talking to wasn't going to be 100 percent upfront then he was going to point it out to his readers.

What he was too busy to see was how the rest of us reacted. While Larry grilled, we grinned. When you watch a master practice his craft, you can't help but smile.

Larry's been gone from our newsroom for almost two years, but his voice still echoes through our halls and in all our hearts. It always will, as it always should.

Pamela E. Walck, former SMN reporter

Larry Peterson was truly a gentle giant in the Savannah Morning News newsroom. He rarely spoke of his reporting life prior to SMN, but the careful listener would catch whispers of it now and then. It wasn't that he hid the fact he had been a political reporter for the Orange County Register, it was just that it had been some time and the youngsters in the newsroom, like myself, rarely took time to consider what that meant. Who he had covered. What moments his notebook and pen had captured for his readers.

For example, during an election cycle (I am thinking it must have been a presidential election year) we had a few down moments after deadline and the Government & Business desk staff were talking shop about great presidential speech moments. Somehow, Papa Bush's "A Thousand Points of Light" speech came up in the conversation. With a nonchalance that only Larry could muster, he said off the cuff, "Oh, yeah, that was a good one. I covered it, you know." The rest of us were gobsmacked. Kinda in the same way you feel as a kid, when you catch your kindergarten teacher at the grocery store.

Larry was a constant source of information in terms of journalistic professionalism and news gathering routines. Watching him conduct a phone interview with a politician was a kin to viewing an artist painting a masterpiece on canvas. He navigated the interview with a finesse that was simply masterful. What most sources never knew was that back pain often meant he was conducting the said interview while sprawled out on the newsroom floor, one leg crossed at the knee with a notebook carefully balanced as his pen flew, capturing the source's comments. His ability to negotiate through an interview - both on and off the record - and still get a "good quote" (as he liked to called it) was a thing of pure beauty. Journalism textbooks never teach you how to do that. Interns and newsroom youngsters alike learned just by watching Larry do his thing.

He was also a wonderfully, colorful addition to our newsroom, which was filled with eccentricity and creativity at its finest. In the early 2000s, the Morning News became a beta site for a software company developing an integrated word processing and page design program. None of us truly knew what this would mean to our daily lives until the system went "live" one day and right around deadline, all the computers crashed under the digital weight of that night's newspaper production. A collective groan rolled across the old Bay Street newsroom as each reporter, editor, photographer and designer individually began to process what this would mean to them as they attempted to complete their work that day. Then, that grumbling was interrupted. Larry suddenly leapt from his seat, stood at a sharp attention and saluted his computer, while declaring boldly: "Gen. Fubarski is in command!" Then walked out the door. That pesky general plagued our newsroom at lot those first six months as a beta site. And Larry - being the good soldier that he was, both during Vietnam and then as a reporter - would salute that four-star pest each time.

I think that sometimes Larry's colorful character made it easy to overlook just what a strong addition he was to our newsroom. I remain thankful that I not only had the chance to work with him, but to also call him a friend. In an environment filled with deadline pressures and harsh critics (both within and without the organization), Larry was a gentle spirit that knew how to break the tension with just the right comment - or a salute - at just the right moment.

Ben Werner, former SMN reporter

Larry first returned to Savannah, about 13 years ago, he joined the business team at the Savannah Morning News, where I worked covering publicly traded companies. He was a great editor to work with. I thought it was cool how he went out on his surf board each morning, whether there were waves or Tybee was like a lake. His Larry-isms were hilarious, usually as he shouted out while struggling with the aging computers and ancient operating system we were still working on back then, a sort of DOS-like program, often calling the crashing screen and terminal a "Jive turkey."

But what stands out most are his stories. The one I always think of is how he had just arrived from out west and was talking about getting the house on Tybee opened up. Lanie hadn't come yet, so it was just Larry making the preparations. He had someone come out to check out the HVAC unit, and as Larry told it, he explained, well there's some good news and bad news.

I was curious and asked what's the bad news?

Larry said, turns out there's a family of rats that's made a nest in the crawl space.

Yikes, that's pretty bad, so what's the good news?

Somebody's pet boa constrictor seems to have gotten loose and is also living down there. I guess if I let nature take it's course the problem will be solved in a few months. But I don't think Lanie will go for that, so I've got an exterminator coming out.

I've been gone from Savannah for 11 years now, but I still tell folks about that story. I know he'll be missed, but I hope he's out somewhere catching a wave.

Corey Dickstein, SMN reporter

The first time I was sent out with Larry to cover an assignment - I believe it was when I was an intern and he'd just asked if I wanted to go along with him to something and maybe snap a few photos - we finished the assignment and Larry walks over to a receptionists' desk: Can I use your phone?

I was dumbstruck.

Here I am two, three weeks out of college - this was 2009 - and here's this veteran reporter who did not own a cell phone. Turns out Larry simply hated them - smartphone or dumb-phone, he had little to no use for them. In fact, his wife Lanie told me, the day after he retired from the paper he donated the cell phone SMN eventually mandated he buy to Goodwill.

I sat next to or across from Larry for a couple of years, and listening and watching him was better than any schooling in journalism. He was truly a master of his craft. I soaked up all the advice he'd offer, and I felt very proud on the occasions when he'd offer me a, "Good read in today's paper."

Christian Livermore, former SMN reporter

It's hard to think of Larry, who was always so vigorous and youthful, being gone. I am sad he has left us, but grateful that I got to enjoy his friendship for as long as I did. I'm a bit in shock, so in no particular order, here are some things I remember: Suppers at Larry and Lanie's with Barkman begging from the kitchen where he was tethered; Larry's political stories from his California days; Sunday brunches at J. Christopher; barbecue and a ride in Larry's convertible; how you always knew there was a problem with Larry's computer because you'd hear him mutter threateningly from across the newsroom, 'Don't do it, [unprintable expletive]; the time in the Telfair that my friend Stephen, on seeing an artwork, said, 'This is what happens when you lose the instructions to your swing set', and a man standing nearby looking at us as though we were the worst kind of heathens but Larry thinking it was the funniest thing he ever heard. Rest in peace, Larry. You were loved and you will be missed.

Noelle Phillips, former SMN reporter

Larry was a true newsroom character. He was a good newsman but his quirky personality made him so interesting. I sat next to him and some days I could hear his voice but could not see him over his cubicle wall. I would stand to see where his voice was coming from and Larry would be lying on the floor while talking on the phone. That meant his back was acting up that day. This happened when the newsroom was still on Bay Street so you know he had to be in pain to stretch across that wretched, coffee-stained carpet.

He also had hilarious sayings and phrases, many pulled from his time serving with the military in Vietnam. Here are my three favorites:

1. If we don't surf, Lindsay, the terrorists win. (Said to his daughter on or around 9/11/2001 and then printed and hung on his cubicle wall.)

2. General Fubarski in command! (Shouted while standing at attention and saluting, often during a computer malfunction. As you can imagine, there were many with Larry.)

3. Oscar Foxtrot Sierra!

These sayings and other quirks kept us laughing and sometimes made us shake our heads. But the man had a good heart and loved newspapering. A newsroom just isn't a newsroom without a guy like Larry Peterson.

Mary Mayle, SMN reporter

Larry Peterson was one of the best - and most unassuming - reporters I've ever known. He was never one to talk about the major news outlets he worked for, the presidents he covered or the awards he won. It just wasn't in his nature. But any journalist fortunate enough to sit near Larry and listen as he conducted an interview - taking no prisoners but exercising unwavering fairness and objectivity - knew they were experiencing journalism the way it should be practiced. We were incredible lucky to have him in our newsroom.

Suzanne Donovan, former SMN editor

Larry was a first rate, stellar political reporter and thinker. The Savannah community was so very fortunate to have someone with his depth and breadth of experience and knowledge.

He brought a level of professionalism and such thoughtful political reporting and thinking that brought context to even local politics that allowed people to really make sense out of what was going on. We don't have anyone in our community that can do that now.

On a personal level, he had such a deep love of the water, and I think when he let people see that side of him in his columns - here he was this hardcore political writer in a hardboiled world, but he also had this great soft side. He loved people and animals, and he loved the world. He was deeply compassionate, and to me his surfing really embodies that.

He'd just get out there and literally be in the water and I think that was his way of communing with the universe.

He'd come in the office late and stay late, and that was so he could spend his mornings out on the beach. He just loved it.

He'd come in and bring me a shell from his walk that day. I still have them. He was a good friend.

Eric Johnson, former state senator and gubernatorial candidate

Every elected official and candidate for office - you want to control the story, but if you can't exactly control the story you want to work with a reporter who knows what's going on, who has a deep understanding of politics, of the issues, of those involved, someone who knows the background. You hope to work with someone who's going to write something that's not just a skim over the top, dramatic piece of writing. With Larry, it was really an inside look at the person or issue from someone who absolutely understood it.

When you get a call from a reporter - if it was from Larry you knew you'd better call him back. He was going to write a story, but he was also going to listen to you. That was the good part of Larry Peterson, he loved the subject matter. His level of expertise … that's rare these days.

Tom Bordeaux, Savannah alderman and former state representative

Larry is your perfect reporter.

I always enjoyed talking to Larry. He loved what he did, and understood the backstory. Sometimes reporters just want to know the vote. It's like someone asking for the score of a football game without getting any information on what happened in the game. Larry understood the game.

He was passionate. He was passionate about politics. He was passionate about life. He was passionate about his work. He managed to put that all together nicely. I just wish I had gotten to see him on a surfboard. When I heard he surfed, it was like 'No way. Wow.'

Burke Day, former state representative

My relationship with Larry - I considered it a fair one. There was things that he might write about that he included me in that I might take exception to, but Larry was the type of journalist I could call about it and he'd listen. In a nutshell, he was a very fair, knowledgeable journalist.

When I got a call from Larry it was, what is he going to ask me? What does he know that I don't? I never assumed it was good news or fun questions. But he'd always come from a well-researched place and it would always be balanced. There are still a few journalists out there whom you can call a friend. In agreement or disagreement the friendship prevailed always. That's how it was with Larry.

Charles Bullock, University of Georgia political scientist

I became one of his favorite sources, for some reason or another. He would always call with some story he was working on. He'd call it something like a 'thumb-sucker.' He'd call up and say 'I've got this thumb-sucker for the Sunday paper,' and we'd talk about some aspect of Georgia politics or national politics.

He was good. I've talked to - in an election year I might do 500 interviews with a whole lot of reporters. Some reporters call and it's clear they don't know much. Larry understood the basic politics. He was calling me about something very specific.
