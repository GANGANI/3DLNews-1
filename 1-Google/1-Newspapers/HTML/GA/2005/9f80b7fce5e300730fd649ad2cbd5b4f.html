Johnny Neil Smith To Retire

For the first time in 16 years Piedmont Academy will begin classes next fall without Headmaster Johnny Neil Smith at the helm. But retiring after 43 years as an educator doesn't mean the soft spoken Mississippi native is leaving Monticello.

"We'll take a summer vacation, then I may do something part-time...and I might give some time," said the deeply religious educator. "Whatever the Lord leads me to."

He didn't specifically mention work on his second novel until asked, nor did he mention using the musical talents that he enjoys but seldom displays publicly outside singing in the Monticello Baptist Church choir.

What he won't be doing is bearing the responsibility the institution he has loved and nurtured through lean times and, now, relative prosperity. Not that he shows signs of strain, nor of the fact that he turned 65 last fall, but he says he's ready to get away from the pressure.

He's also comfortable turning the headmaster's office over to James P. "Chip" Champion, assistant headmaster, basketball coach and teacher who has been groomed for the top spot for a couple years. Mr. Champion will officially take over after the current academic year is wrapped up.

Mr. Smith's career as a professional educator began in the early 1960s when he finished a two-year stint as a tank driver for Uncle Sam and returned to his hometown of Union, Miss. "We had five high schools," he said of the rural county in those days before consolidation. They asked me to start a band and football program at the Beulah Hubbard School."

It seems not an unreasonable assignment for one who had participated in both football and band in the same school system; band participation didn't include marching at football games. He had entered the Army after receiving his BS in Education from Mississippi College, which he attended on a band scholarship.

He thought of "a thousand things that might go wrong" when he took on the task. "I wasn't but 22," he recalls. "I wasn't much older than some of the kids in school." But the career launch was successful—for the next 43 years he's been a professional educator, 10 years in public schools and 33 in private education.

Along the way he picked up a master's degree in counseling from the University of Southern Mississippi and certification in school administration from Georgia Southern University. He is married to the former Susan Croce, whom he met when she was coaching an opposing basketball team while Mr. Smith was coaching for Ocmulgee Academy in Terrell County.

Mrs. Smith enjoys teaching third grade at Washington Park School and has no plans to quit when her husband retires, said Mr. Smith. The family includes three children and four grandchildren.

Mr. Smith has become Piedmont's longest serving headmaster since accepting the post in 1989 when enrollment was trending downward. The school was one of many "seggie academies" created in response to racial integration, but was shaking that image by the late eighties.

He notes that the organization, now the Georgia Independent Schools Association, with which Piedmont has long been affiliated, had agreed that member schools must maintain an "open door" policy. A number of minority students, including several African-Americans had been enrolled at Piedmont.

A typical class now includes a half-dozen or more African-Americans, and numerous other minorities. (Mr. Smith proudly points out that the Clark Atlanta Class of 2005 includes an African-American Piedmont graduate.)

More significantly, the school in the last couple decades has come something of a regional academy not without prestige as a purveyor of quality private education. Currently only about half its enrollment generally comes from Jasper County; probably a third, more or less, from Newton and the remainder from Butts and a smattering of other neighboring counties.

Enrollment for the 2004-2005 school year is 304, up from 197 in 1992-1993, the low point since the school was created in 1970. There was concern then that the school, like so many founded during the period of resistance to integration, would fade away. But with a cadre of supporters rallying to the cause of higher education Piedmont has become a robust facility with an annual budget of some $1.6 million, most of which is circulated through the local economy.

Mr. Smith does not foresee growth much beyond current levels since the physical plant is near capacity and overcrowding existing classrooms is not considered at option at Piedmont.

Mr. Smith attributes the school's success to "quality faculty." And how, one asks, does a headmaster attract quality personnel to such a school? Answer: "hard prayer." Then, he says, there's an environment where teachers aren't heavily involved with disciplinary matters, and aren't overly bogged down with paperwork. "Here, they're free to teach," he says.

Though he doesn't anticipate significant growth, he expects continued improvements beyond those he's seen over the past 16 years. A new science lab and additional pre-school classrooms are high on the want list.

A believer in extracurricular activities and spiritual guidance as part of the school experience, he doesn't conceal pride in the band he organized in 1993 and (surprise!) he directs personally. With trumpet as his primary instrument, the headmaster also strums a pretty good banjo. His musical tastes stray from traditional to Dixieland.

A novel, Unconquered, sequel to Hillcountry Warriors published several years ago, is in the editing stages. Both involve Southern family life in the Civil War. Add to the mix of music and writing his interest in woodworking, hunting, tennis and his eagerness to work with young people whenever he can.

And of course, there's the ubiquitous call to virtually all retirees: "There are some things to do around the house." The house in this case is the long-time family home on Amelia Drive (formerly Mockingbird) in Monticello. How much time he'll be spending there depends on...this new retiree might say whatever message he gets from a higher power.
