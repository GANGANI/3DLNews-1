Homelessness in Jasper County Is Explored, Explained

By Danita Dyess•
Imagine this: Getting evicted from your home and living in the woods when the temperature drops to 30 degrees without food or clean water? Another situation is a single mother fleeing domestic violence and living in her car with two children. Do these two scenarios seem incredible? They’re not.

According to the Georgia Alliance to End Homelessness, “10,234 people in Georgia are homeless as of 2020.” So what do those state statistics mean for Monticello and nearby counties?

Homelessness is more common than you might think in Jasper and surrounding counties. Once you get information from experts, you will understand what homelessness is and learn what you can do to help.

Definition of Homelessness

You probably think you already know what homelessness is. After all, the name says it all. However, the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) defines homelessness according to four categories.

1. People who are living in a place not meant for human habitation, in emergency shelter, in transitional housing, or are leaving an institution where they stayed temporarily

2. People who are losing their primary nighttime residence, including hotels/motels within 14 days

3. Families with children or unaccompanied youth who are unstably housed and probably will continue in that situation

4. People who are fleeing domestic violence

An accurate definition of homelessness affects not only who is considered homeless, but what types of services they can receive under federally mandated programs. These programs have strict guidelines that can disqualify an individual.

Who’s Homeless and Why?

Ask any expert and they will tell you that homelessness is a complex issue. Accordingly, the reasons for extreme poverty are as diverse as the individuals experiencing it. Sure, there are common reasons: alcohol and drug addiction, mental illness, divorce, and job loss. However, trauma, career burnout (similar to depression), rape and incest may not be discussed.

The National Alliance to End Homelessness said, “Race is a significant predictor...Higher unemployment rates, lower incomes, less access to healthcare, and higher incarceration rates are some of the factors likely contributing to higher rates of homelessness among people of color.”
Understanding the Reason for Chronic Homelessness

Of course, some people have settled for a life on the streets. This could be because they have tried to get off the streets multiple times but failed. In general, the problem is usually a lack of the right tools or services like the kind of professional guidance from a social worker or an attorney.

For instance, a person battling alcoholism may enter a 12-step program that may work for a lot of people, but not for this particular individual. Another person may have autism, Asperger’s Syndrome, ADD, or a learning disability. He may find it extremely difficult to get a job without the services of an organization dedicated to helping people with special needs.

People applying for disability usually do not receive it with their first application. A lawyer is needed to apply properly. Furthermore, a homeless person may not have a phone or mailing address and thus miss notifications requesting additional documentation or upcoming court dates. So, the process is prolonged.

Counting the Unsheltered

Every other year, HUD requires communities to count the homeless population. The goal is to evaluate need and track progress. As mandated by the McKinney Vento Act — a federal law that provides money for homeless shelter programs—all homeless service providers must conduct a census that must occur during the last 10 days of January in odd years. This process is referred to as the Point in Time (PIT) count.

The Georgia Department of Community Affairs published the Georgia Balance of state Continuum of Care Point in Time Homeless Count 2019 Report. Most data listed clusters counties, so it can be very difficult to isolate a specific area. However, Appendix B listed counties and totals for UHPs, Unsheltered Homeless Persons: Jasper – zero, Jones – 14, Newton – 29, Putnam – 5, and Monroe – 8.

Children and Homelessness

The Georgia Department of Education keeps records of homeless children. However, Katheryn A. Preston MSW, director, Georgia Alliance to End Homelessness, provided some figures. The number of homeless children living in Jasper County was unable to be verified by the Jasper County Board of Education at the time of this writing.

Finding Shelter & Services

In 1996, Pastor Clara Lett, Rainbow Covenant Ministries, Inc., started an outreach ministry for underprivileged youth. Eventually, she started helping adults and in 2001, she established the Garden of Gethsemane Homeless Shelter.

It is the only shelter that serves Butts, Jasper, Morgan, Newton and Walton counties. Previously, it served people in Rockdale County but that has been discontinued since Rockdale is considered a part of Atlanta.

Lett said, “Captain Edward Walker Jr. refers people who have been incarcerated to us.” She also said that there has been an increase from 1 percent -- 5 percent in homeless people from Jasper County. Her shelter houses women with children as well as men. Currently, she has 29 people at her shelter. However, “Elderly people, some 80 years old or older, account for a lot of the homeless people.”

Funding comes from grants and the $15/day fee for transitional housing. She does not receive federal or state assistance.

Gethsemane Homeless Shelter is located at 7133 Turner Lake Circle, Covington, Ga. 30014; 770-787-8519. Email - [email protected].

Vanessa Jackson is the executive director of Putnam Christian Outreach Inc. (PCO). It’s located at 151 Industrial Blvd., Eatonton, Ga, 31024. 706-485-4066. She said, “We serve all areas, not just Jasper County.” She said that her organization does not provide shelter,but does assist with a hotel for people experiencing extreme cold or hot weather.

Jackson said, “We work with Paid in Full Ministry for a program called Just For Now that provides 1 - 4 months of housing at the rate of $250 - $450 per month.”

On average, PCO helps two people (usually males) per month. However, they also assist single females who are often single mothers.

Homeless people can get medical treatment from Piedmont Newton Hospital. Willing Helpers Medical Clinic in Covington provides free healthcare services.
Solutions to Homelessness

So, is it realistic to think that homelessness can be eliminated? Preston said the following.

We will have to forget the “Every man for him/herself approach.” We must become educated about the real causes and gaps in the current systems of care. There needs to be social responsibility that is a shared approach and we all must commit too. There must be serious change with real commitment and about a $200 billion investment the paradigm as written about by Vissing, Hudson and Nilan in their book, Changing the Paradigm of Homelessness.

Here’s what you can do to help the homeless.

1. Donate to Research Organizations

When you think about homelessness, you probably don’t think about research. Your first reaction is probably a church, shelter, or nonprofit like United Way. However, it’s research that provides the data needed to develop programs that focus on needs and practical solutions. It debunks myths, gauges effectiveness, and increases public awareness.

The United States Interagency Council on Homelessness, National Alliance to End Homelessness, and the Department of Veteran Affairs are some organizations that conduct groundbreaking re-search.

Support research organizations. Preston said, “Research requires a statistical approach to measure the problem, identifying gaps and serves as systems of accountability.”

2. Choose Reputable Organizations

Verify the status of a nonprofit with the IRS Nonprofit Charities Database, Charity Navigator and Charity Watch. The Better Business Bureau also has a website, Give.org, that evaluates nonprofits.

Most 501(c)3 organizations have a Board of Directors. Some homeless organizations have opted to go through an accreditation program through the Georgia Alliance to End Homelessness.

3. Tell the Mayor

Contact members of the City Council and elected officials know. Every law starts with an idea from a legislator, staff member or concerned citizen.
It may not seem like your lone voice has any impact. So perhaps you can discuss your concerns and creative solutions with a team of like-minded neighbors. As a group, your advocacy will be noted.

4. Educate yourself

Read books like No Way Home by Joseph Tartakovsky and The Book on Ending Homelessness by Iain DeJong. The Journal of Social Distress and Homelessness can provide valuable research and insight.

5. Volunteer

Volunteer at your local church. Captain Walker said that Monticello Baptist and Springfield Baptist are often contacted to see if they know anyone who can help.

The Monticello community offers the kind of support that practically eliminates homelessness. The coordination of agencies like the Jasper County Sheriff’s Department, churches and shelters plays a role also. Perhaps Captain Billy Bryant described it best. He said, “If you are cold or hungry in Monticello, you won’t be cold or hungry for long. Someone is going to take you in or help you out.”
