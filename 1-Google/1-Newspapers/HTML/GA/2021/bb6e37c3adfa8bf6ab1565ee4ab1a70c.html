Common Causes of Stress in Nurses

Healthcare providers are some of the unsung heroes of our country. This is especially true of the nurses and CNAs who work tirelessly while exposing themselves to illness in order to help patients recover. But being a hero comes with a cost, and for countless nurses this includes a significant amount of stress. This stress can lead to burnout which can cause even the most devoted of healthcare professionals to leave their field. Knowing the common causes of stress in nurses can help these people recognize and avoid caregiver burnout. Coworkers Surprisingly, one of the more common causes of stress in nurses isn’t aggressive patients or the physical toll of constantly being on one’s feet—it is other healthcare providers. For some, this may involve feeling pressured by coworkers or not getting along with them (possibly because they too are experiencing burnout). But it may also include breakdowns in communication or poor cooperation. This scenario can cause stress in any setting, but when the health of others is on the line, this can multiply our stress levels and make even simple tasks difficult. Patients Patients in hospitals and nursing homes are often undergoing some of the most stressful moments of their lives. They are often in pain or dealing with conditions that can cause mood changes. Because of this, some patients are prone to becoming aggressive or belligerent, and nurses often have to take the brunt of these foul moods. Most patients are not trying to be difficult or bothersome. Just as often, kind and mild-mannered patients can add to a nurse’s stress, not because they have done something wrong, but because the nurse so badly wants to help them become well. The constant emotional strain of caring for people at difficult moments in their lives is why some call caregiver burnout "compassion fatigue.” Job Environment Nurses and CNAs can be found in a variety of environments such as hospitals, private practices, or nursing homes. These different environments all have different sources of stress. Hospitals have patients dealing with traumatic or life-threatening injuries and illnesses which are often the most difficult to treat. Nursing homes, on the other hand, involve working with dementia patients who need constant intensive care. Whatever the location, being a nurse or CNA is a physically challenging position. Constantly being on one’s feet, having ten- or twelve-hour shifts, and having to physically move patients can take a toll on the body and mind after a while. By understanding what nurses go through, we can adjust our healthcare facilities to help avoid burnout. This is the first step in taking care of the nurses and healthcare providers who care for us.

Retreat Gets High Marks

The Retreat Nursing Home has received a high ranking of five stars overall in U.S. News & World Report’s  2011 Best Nursing Homes, available online at www.usnews.com/nursinghomes. The rankings highlight facilities across the nation providing high-quality care and are a valuable tool for millions of elderly Americans and their families in search of a nursing home. U.S. News’s Best Nursing Homes profiles more than 15,000 facilities and ranks them by state, using data and quality ratings from the federal government. The rankings are updated quarterly.The rankings rely on information from the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services, the federal agency that sets and enforces quality standards for all homes enrolled in Medicare or Medicaid. Homes receive an overall rating of one to five stars from the government based on the number of stars earned in three other categories: health inspections, nurse staffing, and quality of care. Donna Holman, Administrator of The Retreat, says, “We are proud to have maintained the CMS 5 Star Quality Rating all year. This rating calls attention to the good work given on a daily basis by each one of our employees. This recognition is achieved by the daily teamwork and support of our employees and physicians every day, along with continued support from our patients’ families, our board members and our community.” “Choosing a nursing home for your parent or loved one is incredibly difficult,” says Avery Comarow, Health Rankings Editor at U.S. News & World Report. “Homes at the top of our rankings have repeatedly shown high quality and deserve consideration.” The Retreat is located in the Jasper Health Services complex and has 55 beds. The Retreat also earned this distinction in 2010.

Man Found Collapsed On Sidewalk

A Monticello police officer stopped on College Street last Thursday to check on a stopped vehicle occupied by two females, and found Terry Thompson, a 48-year-old male, collapsed on the sidewalk, according to Monticello Police Chief Bud Smith.The officer called the Emergency Medical Service (EMS), and EMTs responded immediately. Mr. Thompson had been taken to Jasper Memorial Hospital by ambulance, complaining of headaches, according to the medic call heard on the scanner earlier that day, and had been released at 11:01 a.m., records show. He was later declared dead after being returned to the hospital. According to County Coroner Billy Norris, Mr. Thompson’s cause of death is pending due to a toxicological report which could take from six to 12 weeks. Other than that incident, the Monticello Police Department (MPD) reported a fairly quiet week with only one arrest during the period of December 1-8: Jamie Leanne Blackmon, 29, of Eatonton was charged with speeding, reckless driving, fleeing or attempting to elude a police officer, aggressive driving, passing violation, stop sign violation and driving under the influence of alcohol or drugs.

Jasper Health Services Seeks Funding

Administrator Jan Gaston reported during the regular Jasper Health Sevices meeting Tuesday that the Primary Care Center (PCC) has received 200 doses of H1N1 intranasal flu vaccines. The report also indicated the injectable form of the vaccine should be available within two-three weeks, and that there has not been great demand for the vaccine to date. PCC is out of seasonal vaccine for all ages after having administering 900 doses to date, and are currently awaiting additional vaccine to be received by Nov. 5. However, there is vaccine available for hospital patients and nursing home residents. Mrs. Gaston’s report also included a copy of her letter to the Jasper County Board of Commissioners (BOC) concerning the discretionary funding support provided to Jasper Memorial Hospital (JMH), indicating the funds were a monthly stable cash flow which they counted on.
