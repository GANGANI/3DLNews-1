Dr. Frederick Funderburg: Monticello’s First African-American Physician

By JAMES CAMPBELL

Dr. Frederick Douglas Funderburg was born in 1889 as a native of Anniston, Ala. As a young man, he worked as a Pullman employee and a barber for eight years before entering medical school. And soon after graduating from Meharry Medical School, he moved and started his medical practice in Monticello.

It is still unknown as to why he chose Monticello, or maybe it was because there was no other black doctor in the area. When he arrived, he soon became known as “The Negro Physician” because he did not only treat black patients in Monticello and Jasper County communities, he also treated white patients as well for the next 50 years from 1922 to 1972.

During Dr. Funderburg’s short practice, as it would seem to some people in and around Monticello, he was the first and only black physician who has ever claimed Monticello as his home. And there has never been another African American physician to set up practice in this area since then. Before he died, he was called upon to be the physician for the white community as well as the black community.

In 1938, a flu epidemic hit Georgia and killed many people which included the only white doctor that was in the area between Jasper and Putnam counties. That was a very critical time for many white citizens who had not wanted a black doctor for their personal physician before but they had no other choice but to call on Dr. Funderburg.

During this epidemic period, Dr. Funderburg attended to as many as 60 white patients a day while he was also attending to black patients. As the epidemic subsided, he successfully treated all those who came down with flu. Many lives, white and black, were saved because of this one black physician who was not immediately accepted by many whites.

It is without a doubt, that the relationship between white and black physicians became very good in the South during those days, and it was all because of Dr. Frederick Funderburg competence. He convinced many white people that he was one of the best physicians in the entire state of Georgia regardless of the color of his skin.

During his many years in Monticello, he won the respect of many white people. Among the white people who visited Dr. Funderburg’s office on a regular basis were bank officials, school teachers, and several members of prominent white Georgia families.

Dr. Frederick D. Funderburg overcame prejudice and racism with his skills. He did not have to march or protest to get people’s attention. It was because he was so well respected due to his skills, and that opened the doors for other African American doctors simply because of his success with white Southerners.

It is believed that Dr. Funderburg eventually retired from medicine in 1982. However, his legacy still lives on in the development of the “Funderburg Park,” and the naming of Funderburg Drive that runs directly in front of the house he built and called home as well as his office in the Washington Park community.
FOOTNOTE: James Campbell is the author of “Beneath the Badge,” “Southern Gold,” and “29 Murders.” He also has a new novel coming out soon entitled, “Telling It Like It Is.” He is a native of Monticello who lives in Metro Atlanta where he continues to follow his dream of writing. Mr. Campbell has also submitted written material to be co-authored by fame novelist James Patterson in a book called “The Ghosts of Murder Creek,” a story about the ghosts that haunt Murders Creek in Jasper County.

James Campbell has an extensive and impressive background that goes back to his days at Jasper County Training School where he graduated as one of the school’s top all-around athletes in basketball and football.

He later spent four years stationed in Europe with the U.S. Army where he also played semi-pro basketball for the Army. After his discharge from the military, he became the first African American to fly police helicopters for the City of Atlanta. He retired from the police force after 37 years. In retirement, he now spends his time researching African American history.
