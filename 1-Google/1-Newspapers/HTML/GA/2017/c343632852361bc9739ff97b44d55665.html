Jasper County Guardians, Our Sheriff’s Office

By Janet Jernigan•
When was the last time you heard or read a positive story about law enforcement? You may not need them today, tomorrow or anytime in the past, but someday you may dial 9-1-1 and you will expect help immediately.

Jasper County has an estimated population of 13,900 people living in 5,162 households in a 373 square mile area. Twenty-five percent of our population is 18 years old and younger and a little over 15 percent of the population is 65 years old and older. Meeting the demands of all of these people puts a strain on a small law enforcement force such as ours. Not only do they serve county residents, but also, since the dissolution of the Monticello Police Department, they service the City of Monticello.

Each week in this newspaper, there is a long list of crimes committed against person and property requiring hours of investigation and records, officers required to stop drivers intoxicated or on drugs or operating an unsafe vehicle, many officers are required to serve warrants from the courts, control traffic during an accident or in the case of this county, loose farm animals roaming along the roads, transporting prisoners to and from court and for medical reasons, or sadly looking for missing loved ones. Each call to 9-1-1 requires action.

An officer can be put in a dangerous situation at any moment. The very day I began to write this article two Baldwin State correctional officers were killed transporting prisoners between Milledgeville and Jackson. The Jasper County Sheriff’s office went into action to protect their citizens and to help find the escaped convicts. There was no time for interviews.

Police forces around the country suffer from a heavy turnover including Jasper County. “Two and done” is the mantra as police chase the money. Jasper County sheriff officers’ starting pay is approximately $31,000 per year after graduating from Police Academy, $596 a week before federal and state income taxes, health, dental, vision insurance premiums. In contrast, the City of LaGrange Police department offers starting pay for someone without Police Academy certification at $40,872. Larger counties entice police officers with two years of experience under their belt to start at around $37,000. Many police officers must rely on Federal Earned Income Credit and state benefits to keep their family together.

Health and life insurance benefits for our officers are also lacking. They do receive group health insurance while paying from $18 to $40 a pay period and have a deductible of $1,500 per person. Vision and dental benefits are additional payments. The county offers a life insurance policy of $25,000 for an officer who loses his life.

After federal and state income taxes, health and other insurance premiums, many officers cannot afford to own a home unless their spouse has a steady income. Since officers have shift changes, getting children to school, attending their school activities, even going to a church or having family time is a struggle. To make ends meet, some officers have second jobs.

Officers interviewed for this article expressed similar answers when asked if they always wanted to be in law enforcement, overwhelming yes. Some said they may have watched too many non-reality television dramas or cop shows thinking that was the real thing. Daily experiences with the public some in pathetic situations, some high on meth or stronger drugs, some with mental disabilities changes any preconceived ideas and challenges even for well-trained police officer.

Dedicated as our officers are, in the end, they need money to live.

Last year a delegation from the Georgia Sheriff’s Association, including Jasper County Sheriff Donnie Pope, lobbied our state legislators hoping to get a legislative bill that would help struggling counties who are constantly in need of officers and have trouble recruiting and retaining officers. This request was very similar to the one implemented for teachers a number of years ago that sort to even the playing field for salaries from county to county. House Speaker, David Ralston, met with the delegation and told them not to look at the state for help and told them to go back to their county courthouse. Last year, the State of Georgia awarded their officers a 26% pay increase, starting salary now at $46,000.

Terry Norris, executive director of the Georgia Sheriff’s Association, and a delegation of sheriffs from the around the state hoped to get support for a one cent sales tax increase that would be used to increase salaries for non-state officers.

Three legislators offered Senate Bill 254, but this bill did not address the salary equality. In the meantime, county citizens are faced with less police protection as the turnover continues to drain experienced officers.
