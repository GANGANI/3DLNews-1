40 Years of Friday: Where are they now?

Rose Lane
Mary Frates
Mary Frates modeled for Town and Country, now she wears her support for the arts on her sleeve

In 1977, FRIDAY newspaper wasn't the only publication that featured a photo of Mary Frates modeling a beautiful evening gown.
The newspaper was, in fact reporting in its Feb. 18, 1977, that a photo of Mary, as well as those of a number of other local women, were included in the latest issue of Town and Country Magazine. That particular issue of the magazine, which was "well-stocked” on local newsstands, included a 23-page spread on Oklahoma City. Eight color pages were devoted to the women’s photographs.
Mary said she remembers someone calling her and asking her to be a model. Her husband was running for office and she said if you look carefully at the photo of her, you can see a "Forward With Frates” sign through the window.
Mary had been in the modeling business, both here and in Arizona. She said she often walked the catwalk during Oklahoma City’s own Fashion Week when top designers would come in for the event.
1977 was a very memorable year for her. That year she and other organizers launched a three-day pilot program that provided 100 Oklahoma high school students with intensive arts training in five disciplines, the Oklahoma Summer Arts Institute at Camp Egan near Tahlequah. Mary was the director and it was, according to the Oklahoma Arts Institute website, "a resounding success.”
Friday, February 18, 1977: One of the most beautiful of the beautiful models pictured in the current issue of Town and Country is Mrs. Kent (Mary) Frates as she appeared, in full color, in the national magazine.
The tradition of hiring the nation’s top artists to teach the program began that first year, with prima ballerina Maria Tallchief, conductor Judith Somogi and former U.S. Poet Laureate Donald Hall on the faculty.
The next year, Mary and the institute moved to Quartz Mountain and was expanded to a two-week session. Mary spent the next 25 years at the helm of the program.
In 1983, the Oklahoma Arts Institute created the Oklahoma Fall Arts Institute. The series of four-day weekend workshops for adults offers training with nationally renowned artists in the literary, visual and performing arts.
Fall Institute workshops continue to offer intensive arts education not only for teachers, but for artists of all levels from Oklahoma and beyond.
Today, the Oklahoma Arts Institute continues to recruit the nation’s top artists to teach the programs. Actors Jane Alexander and Richard Thomas, Pulitzer Prize winner N. Scott Momaday, Grammy Award winner Leona Mitchell, dancers Maria and Marjorie Tallchief, Academy Award winning director Ross Kauffman, and visual artists Fritz Scholder, Robert Zakanitch and Richard Avedon have all taught at Quartz Mountain.
Mary is an emeritus board member of the Oklahoma Arts Institute.
She is also president of the Oklahoma Chapter of the International Women’s Forum, an organization geared toward the development of young women into leaders.
"That takes me to quite a number of interesting destinations,” she said.
Most of her time, she said is spent in the arts. Mary has worked in film production, including a documentary for PBS and with "Bringing Up Bobby,” for which her son, Cole, was an executive producer. Scenes for the film were shot in Nichols Hills and at Casady School.
She also does some writing and has been involved with the Oklahoma City Ballet from early on.
"I’m real pleased the ballet has become such a great organization,” she said.
Mary said that she is excited that community as whole is embracing the arts.
"My background is the arts and I’m pleased about what’s going on in Oklahoma City,” she said.
