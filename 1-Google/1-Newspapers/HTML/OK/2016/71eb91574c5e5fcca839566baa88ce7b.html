Creative writing spans the ages at Casady School

Dylan Dobson
Senior Aubrey Herman reads to kindergartners Camden Vesta, Nate Simms and Gabi Goiran.
Last week, all of the hard work from Whitney Finley’s senior creative writing class paid off when the seniors read their children’s books to Casady’s kindergartners.
Toward the end of this trimester-long class, seniors explored their imaginations and reminisced in their favorite childhood stories, to ultimately create and share their own stories with the younger students. Given about a month to write, plan, and illustrate their works, the seniors worked tirelessly to create seamless rhyme and meter to accompany vibrant illustrations.
The process to go from mere ideas to bound and displayed books began with brainstorming. With sketches of each page, a process referred to as “storyboarding” showed the layout of the illustrations and text. Illustration mediums ranged from paintings, drawings and mixed media, to digital documents, each taking several hours a day to complete.

Senior Tek Kreidler reads to Richa Hapani, Vivien Anderson, Grant Sullivan and Ethan Evans.

Aside from rhymes and pictures, another important aspect to children’s literature is the underlying lesson. In worlds where salmon and bears work together, dragons are befriended, dinosaurs have abnormally small arms, and sunflowers grow remarkably large, these stories contain much more than just originality. Students explored a broad spectrum of morals, including compassion, perseverance, adventure, team work and friendship.
Inspiration for the stories sprang from all aspects of life. Tek Kreidler ‘16, “was inspired by all the amazing friends [she has] that are always there to support [her].” “Pearl’s Perseverance,” written by Gabby Jones ‘16, was inspired by the “lack of diversity in children’s stories, and by women who orchestrate their own plans.”
With the finished product copied and bound, each book and its author made the journey across the lake from the Upper Division to the Primary Division. Between the three kindergarten classes, each senior read to around four groups of children, each consisting of about three kids.
The kindergarteners were teeming with excitement, unabashedly pointing out their favorite pictures and parts of each story. While some played games guessing how the story would unfold, others were hanging on the edge of their seats, questioning the good intentions of the dragons.
“The kids were very observant, they admired the paintings and were involved in the story," Gabby Jones ‘16 said.
Parker similarly recalls how “they all wanted to hold the book and touch the pages. It was the best feeling in the world!”
Unanimously, the class concluded how, despite the stress, the kindergarteners made it all worthwhile with their interest and appreciation for the seniors’ creations. In the spring, the kindergarteners will take their inspiration from the stories read to them, and create their own stories after going through a similar process.
The fun and excitement surrounding these types of fun projects provide a warmer and welcoming environment for the lessons they introduce. It provides both Upper Division and Primary Division students alike with the opportunity to express their values in creative ways.
“It was amazing to see everyone’s books come to life,” Parker said. “I had spent so much time on my [story], but once I heard the kids squealing out of excitement, I knew it was worth every minute.”
