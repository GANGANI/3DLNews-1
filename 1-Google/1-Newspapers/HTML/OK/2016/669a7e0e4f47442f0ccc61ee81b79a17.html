Lessons from Who-ville

Laurel Hall
The Reluctant Preacher, like many of you, loves to watch the classic Christmas specials on television over the Thanksgiving holiday.
My favorite is “Dr. Suess’s How the Grinch Stole Christmas.” God, the master storyteller, has so much to teach us through stories -- in fact stories are God’s teaching tool of choice. It is necessary, though, to watch and listen to our favorite stories with fresh attention to what God is telling us. Jesus, quoting the prophet Jeremiah, said it this way, “You have eyes -- can’t you see? You have ears -- can’t you hear?” (Mark 8:18). With fresh attention to a familiar classic and faith like that of a little child let’s hear God’s word to us from the Whos down in Who-Ville.
With God, and with the Grinch, it’s all a matter of the heart. Proverbs 4:20-23 (NLT) says, “My child, pay attention to what I say. Listen carefully to my words. Don’t lose sight of them. Let them penetrate deep into your heart for they bring life to those who find them, and healing to their whole body. Guard your heart above all else, for it determines the course of your life.”
The Grinch has a heart that is “two sizes too small” -- that’s the reason given for his loathing for Christmas and his resulting crimes against the Whos. This depravity of heart is something our culture experiences and labels with awful frequency. We lament people’s “heartless” acts against us. These people, like the Grinch, are our enemies. We name-call and ostracize, just like the song “You’re a mean one, Mr. Grinch.” We tell ourselves there’s no hope for the heartless as we lock our doors. We have to keep out the “nasty wasty skunk.”
But this is Advent, friends. It’s the season of hope. The truth is that there is hope, even for the Grinch among us. There is hope because God breaks into our world, “… and they will call him ‘Immanuel’ which means ‘God with us.’” (Matthew 1:23 NLT) God is with us, and by “us” I mean everyone. This is good news!
The angel says that it’s “good news of great joy for all the people” (Luke 2:10). That means that even the Grinch has a reason to rejoice. He may not know it yet, but there’s no stopping God’s love from breaking in. Jesus is coming whether we welcome him or not. And he is God, not just with me, and not just with her or him or them, but with us -- all of us.
But the Grinch has misjudged the Whos. He sees all the hubbub and thinks that the Whos are happy because of the presents. How often are Christians misjudged in this same way by non-believers? Are we lumped into the company of purchase frenzied consumers who only find satisfaction in our stuff? How do we as the Church win over the hearts of confused onlookers who cannot tell us apart from the masses of black Friday shoppers? How do the Whos win over the Grinch? It turns out that the Whos are celebrating presence, not presents.
When the Grinch sees that there is gathering, singing and hand-holding “with no presents at all” he wants to be a part. He realizes that there is a place for him in Who-Ville. Just like the proverb says, his body is strengthened as his heart grows three sizes. The image the cartoonist gives us for the presence that brings the Whos out of their homes and into a praise-singing circle is a star.
“Where is the newborn king of the Jews? We saw his star as it rose, and we have come to worship him” (Matthew 2:2 NLT). This newborn king, this God with us, this presence in our world is what we celebrate -- and it is irresistible. It’s irresistible to even the hearts among us that are “a dead tomato splotched with moldy purple spots.” It’s irresistible when we don’t allow ourselves to be indistinguishable from the present-obsessed masses. It’s irresistible when we follow the star into the hand-holding community that is for all the people. ‘This Advent season, may we so authentically celebrate the presence of Immanuel in the presence of each other that we become irresistible to all the “seasick crocodiles.”
