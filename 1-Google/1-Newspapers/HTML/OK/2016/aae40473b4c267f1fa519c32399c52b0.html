Most Powerful Women: Prosperity, Politics shake up the rankings as Love tops list with Everest

Vicki Clark Gourley
The ballots are counted and not surprisingly there has been a significant shift in OKC FRIDAY’s Most Powerful Women poll this year. Unlike the Forbes list, which is based strictly on verified wealth, or lists where you can buy your way up, OKC FRIDAY mails ballots to 265 business, civic and charitable leaders. We count the votes and rank the candidates.
Major changes this year are attributable to two factors. Energy prices have not recovered. Politics, particularly in the Republican party, is in complete disarray.
#1 Judy Love soared to the top of the 2016 Most Powerful poll. It is not surprising given her continued level of involvement in all things charitable and for the arts. Also, her family’s fortune, which was founded in 1964, when Judy and her husband, Tom, had the confidence and ambition to get into the retail gasoline business with one service station in Watonga, Oklahoma, has continued to grow. Love ranked No. 7 on Forbes’ list of America’s 50 richest self-made women in 2015.
Her enthusiasm for every project rockets her above all others. She makes sure she gives and others do also whether chairing the United Way Drive or The Allied Arts funding.
Like Chickasaw Nation Gov. Bill Anoatubby, who won the 2016 Power Poll by becoming a top sponsor when others pulled back, Love powers forward, refusing to let Oklahoma City slide backward. (The summer Most Powerful poll includes both men and women, and Judy Love ranked 17th in 2016.)
#2 Christy Gaylord Everest moved up to second place this year. She ranked number one for several years when she served as chair and chief executive officer of Oklahoma Publishing Company, which published the city’s daily newspaper, The Oklahoman.
Now running the E.L. and Thelma Gaylord Foundation with sisters Mary Gaylord McClean and Louise Gaylord Bennett, she continues to be one of the most important givers and leaders in the state. The Gaylord Foundation has a broad spectrum of interests including arts, education, medical research and children’s issues.
#3 Meg Salyer, last year’s Most Powerful Woman, is third this year. The Oklahoma City Councilwoman and owner of Accel Financial Staffing concentrates her efforts on civic activities, although she is also a staunch supporter of the arts. She was the first woman President of Oklahoma City Rotary Club 29, the largest Rotary Club in the world.
#4 Tricia Everest is the youngest generation in the Gaylord family taking an active role in the community. She served as an Assistant Attorney General representing the State of Oklahoma as general counsel to state agencies and commissions, as a litigator in tort, procedural, and employment law cases. Tricia serves on the vestry of All Souls’ Church and is a director for Allied Arts. She is currently advisory board chairman of the Inasmuch Foundation, a trustee of Oklahoma City University, a director of YMCA of Greater Oklahoma City, a member of the Board of Visitors for Casady High School and a director for the Committee of 100. Oklahoma City University honored Everest with the 2012 Distinguished Philanthropist Award.
#5 Polly Nichols leads by example and humility. She is a survivor of the Oklahoma City bombing and co-chaired the fundraising drive to build the National Memorial and Museum. She champions the nonprofit world as being an important partner to businesses to accomplish educational, charitable and arts goals in the city. She was a co-founder of the Juliette Low Leadership Society Luncheon for the Girl Scouts and co-chaired major fund drives for United Way and Allied Arts. She serves or has served and chaired the boards of 20 major nonprofits. If there is a worthy organization needing help, Nichols will be there.
There are several newcomers to the 2016 list. Others have returned to the list or moved up or down depending on their visibility during the past year.
Two newcomers are Stacey Pezold, COO of Paycom and Hilary Farrell, Vice-President of Ackerman McQueen Agency. Teresa Rose Crook returned to the list after leaving Chesapeake Energy, getting married and now working for the Oklahoma City Community Foundation.
Fortunately, Oklahoma did not experience any major tornado disasters like Moore in the past year so United Way CEO Debby Hampton moved down a couple of slots, because she has not constantly been on the news this year.
Bankers, lawyers, doctors, judges, nonprofit agency heads, teachers, entrepreneurs, investors philanthropists and volunteers, not only are women moving into the boardroom, women are smart, tough and keep the city running.
