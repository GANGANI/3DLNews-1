By Yvonne Miller 

No injuries or hazardous materials in 17-car train derailment northeast of Hazelton

• BCEM Manager Loreg said railroad anticipated trains to be running again by 4 a.m. Tuesday

 
  Share   Tweet 0 Comments

February 14, 2024

Yvonne Miller

This train derailment just east of Hazelton, Kansas, occurred Monday about 5:30 a.m.

Early Monday morning, Feb. 12, at approximately 5:30, a Burlington Northern Santa Fe (BNSF) train was westbound about two miles northeast of Hazelton, Kansas, when it derailed. Mike Loreg, Barber County emergency manager, said 17 railcars were involved in the derailment along K-2 highway.

"We were fortunate we had no hazardous materials to deal with and no injuries," Loreg said.

Kendall Kirkham Sloan, BNSF director of external communications, confirmed to the Newsgram that the cause of the derailment was still under investigation as of Monday evening. The train was carrying "freight of all kinds," Sloan said. Derailment pictures showed numerous Intermodal transport cars.

Soon after the derailment Loreg told Wichita news stations, "There is no danger to homes in the area, and no hazardous materials are involved. I think anytime people hear a train derailment, they're concerned, especially when they see a lot of train derailments on the news, and there's chemicals in the water and hazardous material in the air." He said this good news "relieves a lot of the peoples' worries and concerns."

Almost directly after the derailment, BNSF Railway released a statement that said that there were no injuries to the crew, and personnel are working to clear the line. Tri City Road, which goes north from Hazelton to Sharon, was still open, according to Loreg.

Yvonne Miller

This is but one of many cranes and other heavy equipment brought in by BNSF railroad to help clean up the mess from this 17-car derailment northeast of Hazelton, Kansas, early Monday morning.

The derailment site was soon filled with BNSF trucks, emergency personnel and literally tons of heavy equipment to clean up the site. When asked about the BNSF emergency crews, Loreg said they come from as far as Texas. Each crew has certain jobs, such as to investigate the cause; survey; cut the rails; pull all debris off; or install prebuilt rails.

Loreg said he was told both the main track and Track 2 should be up and running by 4 a.m. Tuesday. Living in Kiowa, this reporter heard trains again running through town in the middle of the night, well before 4 a.m.

Derailments affecting both tracks, as this one did, stop train traffic throughout this main route that runs from Chicago to New Mexico.

When asked by the Newsgram how many trains were involved with delays and an estimate of costs to BNSF when derailments happen, Sloan said the company does not disclose that information. Loreg said he's been told the cost could be multiple thousands of dollars per minute.

Local law enforcement, fire department and medical personnel, the Barber County Sheriff's Office and other emergency workers responded to the derailment.
