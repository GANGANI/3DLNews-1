The first day -- and every day -- are good days to be a Charger

Luke Williams
Eighth grade girls head to their first class: Blair Brawley, Camryn Fulkerson, Safia Salim, Drue Taylor, Reagan Williams and Mary Kate Magness.
Time played its cruel joke on all of us. Before our very eyes it had raced past us in an indecipherable blur, leaving us just as soon as it had arrived. And once it left, we all began to hear it: the roaring sound of each passing second ticking down as Time sang its song and we creeped ever closer to the instant that the perpetual beating would cease. The first day of school, for good or for ill, had come once again.
And has there ever been a better time to be a Charger? Beginning with the first day of school, Heritage Hall has found itself on the verge of greatness. In the words of Headmaster Guy Bramble, “I think there is an abiding belief that the Upper School student body has never been such fertile ground for creating some transformational leadership... I’ve just got a good feeling that [it] is ready to really break out in terms of helping us be, culturally speaking, the kindest, gentlest, most caring school that I’ve ever been a part of.”
It all began on the 19th, when the doors opened and students came flooding back in, giving the halls of Heritage life once again.
The Division Head of the Lower School, Betsy Horn, said, “The first day of Lower School is my favorite day of the year by far, starting with carpool because I love, love, love seeing the children get out of their cars and the smiles on their faces as they bound into the building! There is so much excitement and joy and energy in the Lower School on that first day.”
Embodying Charger Pride, the young students eagerly rushed to their classrooms wanting to see their new teacher and all their old friends, ready to begin their year of learning and making new friends. Students of the Middle School were filled with a similar type of fervor.
An eighth grader, Reagan Williams, said that “getting to see all of [her] friends and meet the new teachers” was her favorite part of the first day of school.
And eighth grade teacher, Dee Richardson, said her favorite part of the day was “seeing all the old faces and meeting the new students.”
During that first day, all within Heritage Hall were getting their first taste of the wonders this new school year had to offer.
Aneirin Truong, a freshman in the Upper School, remarked that “the upperclassmen welcomed [him] very well.”
This sense of relationships between each and every student, as well as between every student and teacher, is what fueled the fire that made the first day of school so bright. The teachers at the Hall were all excited to finally begin sharing their knowledge with students again, and students were eager to be there and receive it.
Beginning with a convocation in which seniors told embarrassing stories about their freshman year, including sweating through your whole shirt in the middle of class and receiving the locker number 666, the High School set the example and flourished with Charger Pride. Freshmen were welcomed by seniors, promises were made, and goals for the year were set. But the most admirable of all the goals set for this school year was not set at the end of the day, but at the beginning.
Early in the Upper School convocation a speech was given by Bramble. He spoke of a distant land called Camelot, a land where Right is Might, honor is the code that all live by, and the meek walk equally among the strong.
He spoke of a Heritage that could be Camelot, a land where one should never be afraid to speak out against what one believes is wrong.
“Camelot is a dream worth pursuing.”
More than anything else, the first day of school gave us this. The first day of school gave us hope that it was more than a beginning, but hopefully it was an end to what Heritage was, and now we can start dreaming about what can be.
