Murrah building design architect remembers the day

Vicki Clark Gourley
Jim Loftis
On April 19, 1995, Jim Loftis sat in what was then Presbyterian Hospital with his wife, Jan, who was having minor surgery. An explosion, so loud he thought an airplane had crashed into the hospital, shook the building. He ran to a window, saw the column of black smoke, and started trying to pinpoint its location.
The television news reports were saying the explosion was at the Federal Court House, but as the helicopter's camera zoomed in, Jim knew different. It was the Alfred P. Murrah building. He had designed it.

Two large architectural firms, Shaw & Associates and Locke, Wright, & Foster contracted with the federal government to coordinate and do the work. Loftis did the design part.
He alerted the hospital to prepare for a large number of injured people, and continued to watch the television.
“About 11 o’clock, I realized they didn’t know how to get in the building,” Loftis said today, reflecting on 20 years ago. His design of putting the utilities and elevators in the round corners instead of a central core was unique at the time.
He drove toward the building, finally parking at 8th and Hudson.
“I met Gary Marrs for the first time. He put me in a van with a driver and plans for the wrong building,” he remembers.





Loftis felt apprehensive as the van approached the building. He had not served in the military or ever been to a disaster site. He feared a sight similar to an airplane crash.
“It was amazingly clean. The rescue teams prioritized the injured and there we only two dead bodies. There was so much dust; one (dead body) had to be pointed out to me.”
Loftis saw a college friend from the OU School of Architecture, who said he thought a bomb had gone off on the street in front of the building. They looked at the pool of water with ceiling tiles floating in it. It was the bomb crater flooded by water from broken pipes.
It started raining. Authorities found a bomb in the building (it turned out to be a dummy), and everyone had to pull back. Loftis went west down 5th street and saw a truck axle that had been blown off and hit a red car. Later he learned it was from the bomb truck.
As evening fell, Loftis told the authorities that Ed Kirkpatrick, who had been the engineer on the job, still lived in Oklahoma City, and could probably help. Seeing bright lights down the street, he wandered toward them.
“We were in shock. Standing there in the debris and muck watching Connie Chung reporting,” Loftis said. “In fact, I am standing here in the same shoes today. I almost threw them away, then decided to have them cleaned up and they have been re-soled a couple of times since.”
Tom Brokaw set up the NBC cameras on the roof of a nearby, wooden gas station. He wanted to interview Loftis, who then climbed up there with Brokaw. The camera crew asked Loftis where they could place their equipment that the structure would hold the weight.
For six weeks, the media continued to call Loftis. Besides a repeat engagement with Brokaw, he talked to television reporters from as far away as Australia.
“If you have something to say, they will continue to interview you. They asked questions like 'did you design the building to take a bomb?”
He had. It was in the days of the Vietnam protests and pipe bombs were being used.
“The first floors were designed to withstand a pipe bomb, but not a 900-pound bomb like McVeigh built.”
Looking back 20 years later, Loftis recalls two things that surprised him. No one anticipated that people would come and leave personal articles at the site, “like a pair of signed tennis shoes.” Finally, a fence was erected, on which these mementos could be left.
He also regrets that the two people killed in the Water Resources Board across the street were not counted as bombing victims. Loftis designed the renovation of that building. The bomb concussion pushed the slab roof two feet down on the supporting columns.
