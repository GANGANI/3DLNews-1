Oh, the stories we can tell!

Derek and Kimberly Hammond, and Dianne Meacham, at right, let Kay Gustafson borrow new baby Bennett Hammond for a cuddle, during a party to introduce Dianne's new grandson. Photo by Joy Richardson, okcFRIDAY
Department of Human Services will be knocking at our door momentarily. We’re warping our grandchildren, leaving
scars that will last a lifetime.
In a burst of holiday giving, my husband Jerry picked up four of those Hallmark children’s books in which you can record your voice reading the stories. That was task No. 1. Reading the directions was the second. Then we got to the good part – our voices doing interpretations of the teacher’s voice saying, “Why Mary loves the lamb you know!” When Jerry intoned that in a girly-teacher-voice, let’s just say that the term creepy child molester comes to mind.
He made a verbal omelet out of that good egg Humpty Dumpty, and after the Humpt-ster fell, Jerry thought it a good idea the first run through to add, “Oh damn!” I made him re-record it. Then in that Hickory, Dickery, Dock thing, after the “clock struck one and his addition of “and knocked the hell out of the mouse” – I sent Jerry to his misbehaving corner for “time out”. Oh, I’m not blameless – I let out a screech that will set the kids’ ears back for weeks as “the mouse ran up the clock.” We got all musical through the “Itsy Bitsy Spider” – and our combined voices are loud and like, totally tuneless. Another glass of wine and the last line of “Little Boy Blue,” “He’s under the haystack, fast asleep” was accompanied by a wheezing snore that would demand one run to the doctor for one of those sleep apnea breathing machines.
We managed to massacre the Little Piggies and the Cat and the Fiddle, and when “the little dog laughed” – you don’t even want to envision the sound of a crazed chortling canine impression – it would give you nightmares.
Plunging through the “My Little Princess” book, we added the granddaughters’ names instead of the written term princess, and we were GOOD. One Princess book stubbornly refused to record a page that was totally crucial to the mesmerizing storyline, and Jerry had to take it back to Hallmark to exchange. They were wonderful about it, and after turning the pages to prove his point, he left the clerk in hysterics at his “Princess voice.”
One finds, if one is wacky, that there is lots of latitude in the reading of fairy tales, there are sound effects and amusing “asides” and family humor to personalize the stories. We delighted in the fun record of how odd we are which will haunt our progeny for as many generations as there are batteries. Oh, excuse me, there’s the doorbell. Hmmmm, a van with DHS printed on the side – and I don’t think it is Department of Homeland Security.

Judy Samter can create the most subtle and soft coloring with pastels, one color just floats into another. And her watercolors are equally beautiful. Her birds, with their exquisite detail seem ready to lift off the page.
Howell Gallery did a Christmas showing of Judy’s work, and friends came to see the many kinds of art she does. Art always goes better with tasty treats and there were gingerbread wafers to be dipped in pumpkin dip that would change your life!
Those wandering the gallery were Julia Eisner, Betty Crow, Betsy Hyde, Sally Whitten, Janet Riggan, Katie Davis, Linda Rosser, Barbara Kerrick, Linda Barnett, Irma Elliott, Lila Stevens, Ellen Eisner, and Jean Burke. Handing out a synopsis of the many places Judy has placed permanent art, was daughter Suzanne Fletcher. Judy’s exhibitions have included numerous regional and national juried shows, winning prizes in watercolor, pastel, oil and graphics. Judy’s Artist Statement says, “I am inspired by subjects found in nature, seeing a special beauty in ordinary things.”
TRAVEL TIP
Okay, you caught me – this has very little to do with travel, but it is helping organize MY life. Friends told me that they buy greeting cards and then either categorize them in a card file, or group the sentiments and separate them in zip-lock bags. If I’ve run past the same Valentine card and an old get-well card, once, it’s been fifty times. Now I can go straight to the kind of card I want to send. So, by extension, I can travel as I’ll have extra time because I’m not sorting through juvenile birthday or retirement congratulations, cards.
