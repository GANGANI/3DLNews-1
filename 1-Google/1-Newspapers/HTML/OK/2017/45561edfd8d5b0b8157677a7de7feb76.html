The Cowboy wants you

The End of the Trail, the magnificent iconic statue standing in the entry of the National Cowboy & Western Heritage Museum signifies a Native American and his horse, both weary in body and spirit at the end of their journey. Sculpted by American-born artist James Earle Fraser, The End of the Trail statue is one of the most recognizable images in the United States. Many people are familiar with the image, but few know its history. Originally, it was more than just an image on a postcard, tattoo or belt buckle. It is a major sculptural work, and for its creator, it was more than just a piece of art, but a meaningful reminder of a childhood spent on the American frontier.
Docents at The National Cowboy & Western Heritage Museum not only become extremely knowledgeable about western art and the traditions of ‘The old West’ they have a great time sharing their knowledge as they interact with visitors from around the United States and around the world.
The museum is one of the most popular tourtist attractions in the country.
The best news is the museum currently seeks community members who wish to become volunteer docents to participate in its upcoming orientation on August 24, 2017, at 9:00 a.m., and training beginning on Thursday, August 31, 2017.
Docents work in the galleries and classrooms, conduct Museum-based lessons, and integrate tour themes with exhibition content.
This particular group of docents will work closely with children and encourage them to explore and learn more about the American West.
“I love finding out new things every day,” said Jerry O’Hare, Museum docent. “I enjoy the people, the dedication they have, and their curiosity.
This is a chance to share your knowledge with those who are interested in learning more about the heritage, the state’s heritage, and the American West as a whole.”
Training is scheduled every Tuesday and Thursday through September 28, 2017. New recruits must commit to attending all 10 days of orientation and program. Training times vary, but are generally between 8:30 a.m. – 4:30 p.m.
“Museum docents are vital to the Museum’s mission because they help to preserve and interpret the evolving history and cultures of the American West,” said Todd Bridgewater, Manager of Volunteer Engagement. “Volunteering is an excellent way to make a difference in other people’s lives, as well as your own life.”
To become a docent, complete the Volunteer Application online. Once it is received and reviewed, a Museum staff member will reach out to schedule an interview.
For a detailed description of the docent role and its commitments check the website, nationalcowboymuseum.org/.
The National Cowboy & Western Museum is accredited by the American Alliance of Museums (AAM).
The Cowboy Museum is located only six miles northeast of downtown Oklahoma City in the Adventure District at the junction of Interstates 44 and 35, the state’s exciting Adventure Road corridor.
The Museum offers annual memberships beginning at just $40.
For more information, visit nationalcowboymuseum.org.
