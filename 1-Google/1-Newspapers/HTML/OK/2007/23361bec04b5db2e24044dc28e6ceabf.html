The Skirvin
The return of the grandeur

The Skirvin Hotel rises above the cityscape
By Rose Lane
OKC Friday

In less than three weeks, Oklahoma Cityans will have the opportunity to return to the tradition of elegance that was -- and will be once again -- the Skirvin Hotel.
The Skirvin Hilton will open its doors with 225 guest rooms, a fine restaurant and a piano bar on Feb. 26, taking Oklahoma City back to the time when luxury was the standard set by William Balser “Bill” Skirvin when he first opened the ornate hotel on Sept. 26, 1911.
Since then, the building has been expanded from 10 to 14 stories, and bought and sold, placed on the National Registry of Historic Places, and in 1988, closed.
It is the end of one chapter and the beginning of another in the history of the grandiose structure -- and in downtown Oklahoma City, Fridaylander Dannie Bea Hightower said.
“My father would be thrilled,” she said. Hightower is the daughter of the late Dan W. James, who bought the hotel in 1945 and resold it in 1963 to a group of investors from Chicago.
Hightower remembered those as “marvelous days,” when the Skirvin was “the destination.” Hightower and her late husband, Frank, announced their engagement with a party in the Skirvin’s Crystal Room and celebrated their wedding with a reception in the Venetian Room in 1949.
Meetings of the Junior League were always at the Skirvin and the city’s first big charity ball, the Winter Ball, took place in the Persian Room in 1958. The Beaux Arts Debutantes Ball were always staged at the Skirvin.
“I think it’s absolutely just the most wonderful thing,” Hightower said of the resurgence of the Skirvin. “It’s just going to open to a glorious new future.”
She said her family and the Hightower families have always felt the city needed to have a “heart.” As with many metro areas, Oklahoma City has seen a retreat to the suburbs. But with the help of MAPS, downtown has undergone a revitalization that Hightower described as “most miraculous.”
The Skirvin Partners were chosen by Oklahoma City to buy and restore the hotel. The city participated in a portion of the cost of the project and will see a significant return on its investment. The hotel will be managed by Marcus Hotels and Resorts, a Wisconsin-based company with several historic hotels already in its portfolio. John Williams is the general manager.
Hightower said that through the years her family watched as the grand old building sat vacant, but was not destroyed.
“Where there’s life, there’s hope,” she said.
