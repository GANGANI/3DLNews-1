The goal is peace;
the work is hard.

ED FARRELL, National Memorial board member from Tulsa, left, Gene Barth, Barbara Farrell and Ed Barth. Friday Fotos By Vicki Clark
The joy of making a difference in the world was tempered but not muted last week as leaders of the Oklahoma City National Memorial gathered to present the third annual Reflections of Hope award as reports from the Virginia Tech tragedy filled every news show and paper.
Changing the world one person at a time, one day at a time until the killing of innocents to make political or personal statements is stopped and the cancer of terrorism is removed from the face of the world, that’s the goal, the real prize.
Towering vases of maroon amaryllis and orange roses, the colors of Virginia Tech, centered the tables around the edge of the room as Oklahoma Cityans gathered to mark the anniversary of the Oklahoma City bombing with the presentation of the Reflections of Hope Award.
The third presentation of this international award honored Seeds of Peace. The international summer camp in Maine brings together student leaders from warring countries in an effort to change their perceptions of each other. The goal of the program is to reverse the legacy of hatred by nurturing friendships, which become the basis for mutual respect.
Founded in 1993, Seeds of Peace first brought together 46 Israeli, Palestinian and Egyptian teenagers.
Young men and women who had attended the camp told their stories about how Seeds of Peace had changed their lives before co-founder Bobbie Gottschalk accepted the $25,000 award from Linda Lambert, Chair of the Selection Committee and Kari Watkins, Executive Director of the Oklahoma City National Memorial & Museum. The award is made possible through a grant from the Donald W. Reynolds Foundation in honor of Linda Lambert’s work on the Memorial over the past decade.
