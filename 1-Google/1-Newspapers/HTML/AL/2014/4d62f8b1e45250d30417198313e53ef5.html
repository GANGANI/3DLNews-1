Coretta Scott King Award honors outstanding African-American children's authors and illustrators

  • Updated: Jan. 15, 2014, 9:38 a.m.|
  • Published: Jan. 15, 2014, 8:38 a.m.
By
  • Amanda Borden, Hoover Public Library

On this day 85 years ago, our nation's most iconic Civil Rights leader, Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. was born.

Famous for fighting racial inequality through nonviolent measures, King's awards include the Nobel Peace Prize, Presidential Medal of Freedom and the Congressional Gold Medal. In 2011, a national memorial statue was erected in his honor in Washington, D.C.

What you might not know is that the American Library Association also established a children's book award to commemorate the life and work of Dr. King and to honor his wife, Coretta Scott King, for her continued work for peace. Known as the Coretta Scott King Award, this award is given annually to authors and illustrators of children's books that demonstrate an appreciation of African American culture and universal human values.

The very first author to be honored was Lillie Patterson for "Martin Luther King, Jr.: Man of Peace" in 1970.

Last year, Andrea Davis Pinkney was selected as the Author Award Winner for her book "Hand in Hand: Ten Black Men Who Changed America," a collective biography of ten well-known African American men who dramatically changed history.

The 2013 illustrator award went to Bryan Collier for "I, Too, Am America." A picture book rendition of Langston Hughes' famous poem, Collier uses mixed-media collage and visual symbolism to engage readers in this tribute to Pullman porters.

Outreach librarian Katie Jane Morris claims her favorite medal winner for writing is the 2002 pick "The Land" by Mildred Taylor. A prequel to Taylor's award-winning Logan family series, this title follows the story of Paul-Edward Logan, the son of a prosperous landowner and former slave, as he sets out to find his own land. Morris claims that she likes this book even better than Taylor's most famous book from the series "Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry."

Morris' pick for illustrations is "Underground: Finding the Light to Freedom" by Shane Evans. This picture book very simply tells the story of a family's escape to freedom via the Underground Railroad. Told in two and three- word sentences per spread, Morris says this is a great book for very young children.

My choice for best book is Christopher Paul Curtis' "Bud, Not Buddy" which received the award in 2000. Set in 1936 in Flint Michigan, this is the story of a 10-year-old orphan who runs away in search of his father. Not only is the book inspiring and heart-warming, but it's pretty comical, too.

My favorite illustrator by far is Jerry Pinkney, who has won five Coretta Scott King medals for his books "Patchwork Quilt" in 1986, "Half a Moon and One Whole Star" in 1987, "Mirandy and Brother Wind" in 1989, "Minty: A Story of Young Harriet Tubman," in 1997 and "Goin' Someplace Special" in 2002. "Goin' Someplace Special" is one of my all-time favorite picture books as it illustrates the journey of a young girl to the one integrated public building in town during the 1950s...the library.

Consider checking a Coretta Scott King award book this month in honor of Martin Luther King's legacy.
