Adiós, Jeff Sessions

  • Updated: Jul. 14, 2020, 9:31 p.m.|
  • Published: Jul. 14, 2020, 8:26 p.m.
By
  • Kyle Whitmire | kwhitmire@al.com

This is an opinion column.

Four years ago, Jeff Sessions was the most popular politician in Alabama, comfortably in a Senate seat that could have been his for the rest of his life.

Today he’s as unemployed as the former football coach who beat him this week in the Republican primary.

Don’t have pity for him. He brought this on himself.

Rather, have pity on Alabama. Republican voters may have rejected Sessions, but it seems they did so for all the wrong reasons. Or for the wrong person.

There was a good reason to send Sessions packing.

This is the man, after all, who oversaw family separations on America’s southern border. This is a man who ordered the detention of children away from their parents without safeguards in place to ensure they could be reunited.

And, frankly, this is a man who seemed to enjoy the role he played in the fleeting moment he was allowed to play it.

It’s a sad, inescapable fact of our justice system that parents and children are separated and families are broken every day. I’ve been in courtrooms when it’s happened — the moment a verdict is read and a spouse breaks into tears, when mothers wail and children look to the adults near them for some reassurance that won’t be there. I don’t care what the defendant has done — it’s a terrible thing to witness. Every crime has two sets of victims — the victims and the defendants’ families. And I’ve never seen a prosecutor take any pleasure in that moment.

But as attorney general, Sessions didn’t try to mitigate this unavoidable horror. He expanded it and used it as a threat against immigrants trying to get into a country to give their children opportunities for better lives. He took those parents’ promises and turned them into little hells on Earth.

In the midst of doing so, he had the audacity to quote scripture in his own defense.

"I would cite you to the Apostle Paul and his clear and wise command in Romans 13, to obey the laws of the government because God has ordained them for the purpose of order," Sessions said.

Sessions stopped there in his Bible lesson, or we would have seen that Paul said love is the fulfillment of the law. Sessions could quote scripture, but he must have skipped that part of the Bible when Mary and Joseph fled to Egypt as refugees from horrors back home.

Or he just didn’t care.

In May 2018, Sessions traveled to San Diego, Calif., to announce family separations would be U.S. policy. Go back and watch that speech.

“If you are smuggling a child, then we will prosecute you, and that child may be separated from you as required by law,” he said.

But it wasn’t just what he said. It was how he said it. The man spoke with a twinkle in his eye like a department store Santa. Yes, breaking families apart is, to some degree, unavoidable. However, this man enjoyed the very prospect of it. He took pleasure in it.

Like his boss, Jeff Sessions was devoid of empathy. And whatever humiliation Sessions got at the hands of Alabama voters Tuesday, it’s nothing compared to what he doled out to other people he didn’t even know — many who traveled here to be refugees from unspeakable horrors at home.

Four years ago, Jeff Sessions left a safe elected office for the chance to run the U.S. Justice Department and it didn’t all work out the way he hoped.

But this week, he got just a taste of what justice really is.

Kyle Whitmire is the state political columnist for the Alabama Media Group.
