A Life-Saving Mission

Posted Tuesday, January 4, 2022 1:00 pm
Carleen Wild

Out of all of the missions that Maj. Bridget Flannery has been a part of, throughout both her military and civilian lives and careers, the one that she is currently on she believes may have always been her destiny.
“You can see it in the rear view but you don’t see it at the time… the necessity of the people being put in your way. I think back all the way back to my recruiter, or my whole life…my parents that brought me to Montrose. There are so many people that this is built on their shoulders…” Flannery told the Moody County Enterprise, ahead of a move early December from her home in Flandreau, to Little Rock, Arkansas.
Flannery’s latest assignment with the Army National Guard is a critical one. One that, quite frankly, no one else has ever been assigned. Ever. In part, because Flannery is the one, now with a team surrounding her at the Guard’s Professional Education Center, working to create a training program for the entire nation on sexual assault prevention and response. She calls it Buddy Aid.  
The term had been used before — Buddy Aid was what the Armed Forces long ago called the first aid training given to Soldiers and Marines in Basic Training to help heal a fallen comrade. The name, over time, fell somewhat by the wayside. The concept however, to provide first aid to a fallen comrade, is exactly what Flannery realized Buddy Aid was doing after seeing it in practice while on deployment in 2013 in Afghanistan.  
“In 2012, we get sourced for Afghanistan and part of that proceess is we do pre-mobilization briefs to better understand the threat,” she said. “I and a couple of other key officers were at Ft. Bliss learning about what the threats were and this and wasn’t even on a slide. It was just mentioned about the incidence of sexual violence. You know, 2012, it’s a mature theatre, predators had taken hold…there was a lot of violence associated with the rapes. And there were both internal and external threats.”

Flannery, already at that time a victim advocate for sexual assault or harassment in the Guard, wanted to know more. She contacted a few Sexual Assault Response Coordinators (SARCs) both in Afghanistan and here at home to better understand the threat to her unit. And then she created something she thought might work to help with any crisis that would arise.
“We credentialed extra advocates, we did more training because of those reports. You practice your response to incidents and you’re evaluated on them. We failed miserably but at least we practiced,” she said.
But the training wasn’t a failure.
“One night a runner said someone wants to talk with you, and it varied over the course of our deployment...but it was in the neighborhood of about 1,200 soldiers that we were responsible for. I didn’t know them all, I didn’t know their names, and turns out this soldier was not one of ours. But this soldier said to me, I heard it was safe here,” Flannery said, her voice quivering.
Her battalion commander told her upon returning home, that she couldn’t let what she had learned overseas stay there. She used five slides to train others to respond to sexual assault and violence like they would any other tactical threat. Essentially, they were — what do you say, what do you not say, what do you do, what do you not do, and what do you do when no one has told you but you suspect.
She began teaching it wherever she could locally. Others learned about the opportunity and started asking her to come speak to their advocates and units regionally. The curriculum was been credentialed and validated by clinicians in the field. In 2014 she was given orders to take a week and document how she trained Buddy Aid. The program and its reach again grew. In 2019, she was encouraged to submit the training to the Warrior Resilience and Fitness Innovation Incubator which “aims to identify, evaluate, select, and disseminate evidence-based practices across the National Guard to promote resiliency and prevent harmful behaviors including suicide.” Two years into being one of 27 total programs selected as a pilot, the Guard is working to formally implement Buddy Aid into its training across the nation. Just weeks ago, she introduced Buddy Aid to the United States Air Force. It was the first official introduction of the training outside of National Guard Bureau.
“When we launched the Innovation Incubator in 2019, our goal was to identify and support promising, state-level approaches to prevention and share those best practices across the National Guard. Buddy Aid is a prime example. What started out as a pilot program to provide sexual assault prevention and response training in South Dakota, is now part of the National Guard Professional Education Center’s permanent curriculum available throughout the 54 states, territories, and DC,” said Rear Adm. Matt Kleiman, chief, National Guard Bureau’s Warrior Resilience and Fitness Division.
Leaving home wasn’t easy, and she’s unsure of how long this mission will have her gone. Flannery’s husband Randy, now retired from a career in the Guard himself, and the couple’s ten-year-old son, Titus, remain in Randy’s hometown of Flandreau. But they agreed, she said, as a family, that this was something she needed to do. Her goal is to get the program off the ground and see where the mission takes her next.
“It’s important work, there are so many people that are touched by this, it is unbelievable. It’s not a scientific perspective but I think the CDC is wrong. I think it’s low, I think the one in three women and one in four men is low. I do. That’s how it feels when you talk to so many people. It is absolutely unbelievable the walking wounded that we have, that we don’t want to talk about,” said Flannery. She added that victims of sexual assault are four times as likely to suffer lifetime symptoms from their post-traumatic stress as victims where the catalyst is combat. She believes that it’s because so much of the time it’s someone they were in uniform beside them.
“I can’t say enough, every time something happens and I think about all the leaders that paved the way for this…those mission commanders in Afghanistan, they taught me a lot. These young guys and girls that, while we were figuring this all out. The good that comes from this. They do not know the good that they did.”
