Historic winter weather barreling toward Brookings area

High winds, more than 1 foot of snow possible

Posted Monday, February 20, 2023 11:00 pm
Mondell Keck, The Brookings Register

BROOKINGS — Old Man Winter is giving an ultimatum to residents in and around Brookings who need to travel or to stock up on supplies: Do it today at the latest, because you aren’t going anywhere starting Wednesday and into Thursday.

Brookings and the surrounding area is under a winter weather advisory today that will transition into a blizzard warning at 6 a.m. Wednesday and continues until noon Thursday — and it’s going to a monster of a storm if current trends hold, with howling winds and snowfall totals reach a foot or more.

“Needless to say, this will be a very impactful snowstorm,” National Weather Service meteorologist Jeff Chapman said. “In many ways, it’s going to end up being as bad or worse than the system that we had at the very beginning of the year — the bulk of that system went south of (Brookings) … (but) this system is going to have very broad reach — in fact, most of South Dakota, most of central and southern Minnesota are not going to escape the very significant snow and wind.”

Chapman, who is based out of the weather service’s Sioux Falls office, said snow is expected to develop by mid-morning today, with light to moderate snow continuing into the afternoon. The total by tonight is likely to be in the 3-inch range, he said.

That doesn’t sound too bad, right? Ah, but that is just Old Man Winter’s vanguard — following a brief break tonight, the bulk of the winter storm’s firepower will begin arriving Wednesday morning. Chapman said that, by then, the wind will be gusting from the northeast at 40 to 45 mph and snow will redevelop. The storm will begin dealing its heaviest blows Wednesday night with 45 to 55 mph wind gusts and heavy snowfall. Chapman said Brookings can expect anywhere from 6 to 10 additional inches from Wednesday afternoon into Wednesday evening. Near-blizzard to blizzard conditions will make travel very difficult to impossible as well.

“This is the time now to make those plans to not travel during that time,” Chapman noted. “Make alternative plans to either travel earlier or later or to find a way you don’t have to.”

He added that high winds and snow are expected to continue into Thursday morning, with snow likely ending by midday Thursday, accompanied by a gradual decrease in the winds. By the time it’s all said and done, Chapman figures there will be 12 to 16 inches of snow on the ground in Brookings area, with higher amounts farther east — in the 18- to 20-inch range — into portions of southwest Minnesota toward Marshall and Ivanhoe.

If some folks still want to set out on the road regardless of the danger, Chapman had some sobering advice for them. 

“Well, the ultimate recommendation would be not to travel during this event. It’s going to be that hazardous. Travel could be that difficult to impossible,” he reiterated. “If you have to travel, it’s going to be very important to watch the trends in the forecast. When we get the wind coming up late (tonight) into Wednesday and Wednesday night, it is just going to be just horrible out there in terms of extremely low visibility at times of falling snow, whiteout or near-whiteout conditions — it’s just not going to be suitable to be out on the road.” 

Deadly travel conditions aren’t the only threat this storm will bring, though —there’s also the possibility of power outages.

“You definitely want to prepare for the potential power outages in terms of having a source of water, have a way to have ready-to-eat food because if you lose power, you’re not going to be able to throw something in the microwave to eat or perhaps even run a stove,” Chapman said. “In terms of heat, you want to try to move to interior rooms away from windows to keep the warmest possible air (and) have plenty of blankets around.

“The one thing that you should not do is use certain heating sources to try and warm your home, and that would be using your stovetop or bringing in a gas grill from outside and running that,” Chapman continued. “There are propane space heaters that can be used, but, again, you have to have an appropriately ventilated space to use those.”

He also urged folks to exercise caution once the storm leaves the area Thursday afternoon and cleanup efforts begin.

“This is going to be one of those very significant snowfalls in terms of amount and the degree of drifting we’re likely to see,” Chapman said. “Snow removal is going to be a strenuous activity for most — if your health is compromised in anyway, find someone else to help you do this. Make sure that you’re not doing too much to remove the snow yourself. Take breaks.

“The other thing to watch for, again, that we haven’t mentioned, is that temperatures are going to falling the further we get into this event,” Chapman noted. “In fact, by the time we get into Thursday morning as that snow is starting to taper off in some areas, our wind chills are going to be in that 15 to 25 below zero (range) and, in fact, getting down closer to 30 below zero by Friday morning. The cold itself is going to be dangerous in terms of dealing with it during the snow-removal process.”

So, with push coming to shove, this morning and afternoon is the last chance for people in and around Brookings to prepare for what’s to come. Take advantage of it, because, for better or for worse, historic weather is barreling toward the region.

“To put this storm into historical perspective, it will likely be a top three to top five event for snowfall across most of the region,” Chapman said. “It looks like we will probably fail to meet the heavy snowfall that occurred in many areas during the winter of 1962 but, again, this is a historic event in that it will probably be a once-in-50-year type event for February.”
