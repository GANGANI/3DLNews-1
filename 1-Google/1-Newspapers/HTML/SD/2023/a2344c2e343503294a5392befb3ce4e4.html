Farewell, my very good friend

Posted Friday, February 17, 2023 11:00 pm
Roxy Stienblock of the Plainsman

He was the grandpa that I never had.

There are times that simple words cannot express how much somebody means to you. Monday evening was the last time I would hear from my friend Mark Smith.

We met years ago and proceeded to form a great friendship. We both knew someone in common, Kenny Larson, who my mother took care of for many years and who Mark knew for nearly as many years. I had a love for history and he loved to share his extensive knowledge and past.  

A few years ago, Mark donated a Model T Speedster to the Dakotaland Museum, and I was fortunate to go and take a picture of him and his friend and my former science teacher, John McEnelly. Mark would later say it was his brother, Scott Smith, who actually donated the speedster and he had made the donation on his recommendation.

Mark was a great friend and was willing to give anyone the shirt off his back. He loved to send treats or meals to local dentist offices, to close neighbors and friends. Churches would have soup and sandwich drives or cookie drives and Mark enjoyed supporting them all. When my husband was sick with COVID, Mark even brought us soup that he made.

That became our thing. When he or my small family was sick we would bring the other soup, stew, or any other meals.

I enjoyed our daily chats. We would talk about the weather, his past, town gossip, how my daughter Kenzleigh was doing and share funny videos.

Mark attended Kenzleigh’s first birthday; he didn’t have to, but he did. He stepped into the role as a grandfather to me and a great-grandfather to Kenzleigh. He spoiled Kenzleigh, and I know he adored her, maybe not as much as he did his cats, but pretty close.

Kenzleigh was sick a lot the first year of her life. With two air flights to Sioux Falls Children’s Hospital and days of stays, it made for a challenging first year. Mark was always there to comfort me during the high stress times. He would even go as far as to dropping  items off to help Kenzleigh, if he knew I was unable to get out. He was an active cheerleader with Kenzleigh’s physical therapy as well.

Since neither Jayme nor I have any actively involved parents in our life or Kenzleigh’s, Mark was always there to offer words of wisdom and support. He would always say ‘hospital bills and a job can wait…Kenzleigh comes first.’

Kenzleigh grew up without any maternal or paternal grandparents or great-grandparents and Mark filled that role perfectly.

His running joke was that when Kenzleigh was older, she would have to change his Depends some day and take care of him.

Mark took phenomenal care of his yard and plants. Every year during the State Fair he would let fair-goers park and drive over his nicely manicured, weed-free yard for no charge, as that was a family tradition passed down from his father.

He said he had a small deck put on the front of his home where he could put his Adirondack chairs and place some pots. He took out old, nasty trees and planted new ones. He said he spent a lot of time fixing up his home and “taking care of a home never stops.”

In the summer he enjoyed going on walks and taking pictures of “interesting” objects, whether that was an old sock, a penny, or something else. He enjoyed going around the town and finding beauty in even the ugliest things.

Mark loved going on long drives and taking pictures of nature, animals, and whatever else caught his eye. He would talk about the days when he was a kid and delivered papers in Woody’s Trailer Court, as he walked the trailer park now.

We shared a love of NASCAR and our favorite driver, Ryan Blaney. Although I was, Mark wasn’t a Chase Elliott fan. We shared pictures and talked about former Huron driver Kenny Larson and his racing career as well.

He enjoyed writing short stories for the Plainsman, drawing upon the lifetime of memories that also led him to publish two books of those same short stories and one book of poetry. Mark would send the the columns to me to read and ask my thoughts or opinion, then send them on to see if they would find a home on the pages.

Often they did.

We even talked about his future plans, how he had already gone to Kuhler Funeral Home and prepaid his service.

That he would be cremated and would have no service, no notice and be interred in the wall at Sturgis.

I told him time and time again that either way I would sadly share the unfortunate news with the community that one of their own has passed, and that he would have a lengthy obituary. He said fine, you can write my obituary. I told him I would even go as far as going to Sturgis to see him placed into the wall. He always said that his obit should say he loved animals.

A few weeks ago, Mark was admitted to Huron Regional Medical Center with a bad case of bacterial pneumonia.

Mark being his stubborn self, he thought he could self-treat prior to going to the hospital. He recovered, had been discharged and was home when he passed away in his sleep a few days later.

Mark had written a column regarding his impressions of the care he had received during his stay at HRMC and the people who delivered that care. And, in a completely Mark way, offered a bit of advice to HRMC on how to perhaps share some of their talents with those who weren’t patients at the hospital.

We’ve included that column here today.

I know in the weeks, and months, and years to come, I will miss my friend. I expect there will be a time some summer morning, when I will see an interesting flower or snap a picture of something to text to Mark…then remember.

Goodbye my friend.
