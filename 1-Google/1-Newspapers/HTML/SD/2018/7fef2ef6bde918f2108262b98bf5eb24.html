Wolsey dairy takes next first step

Posted Friday, May 25, 2018 11:00 pm
Curt Nettinga of the Plainsman

WOLSEY — After more than 38 years in the dairy business, Russell Jungemann knows one thing beyond a shadow of a doubt.
“I will tell you,” he said with a slight grin, “I have gone through my very last first milking.”
Jungemann, who with his wife Janet, son Lucas and his wife Kari, operate Lazy J Dairy LLC, saw the family operation go through an expansion that has nearly doubled the head of cows they milk over the past couple of years.
“In our old setup,” Russell shared, “the cows came from the free-stall area, and turned right into the milk parlor.” The expansion requires the herd to hang a left instead. And Holstein milk cows are, as are many mammals, creatures of habit. So, there was a learning curve.
For cows and operators as well.
“The first time in the new parlor, we had half of the attachments up and running,” Lucas said. “The plan was for us to go through milking, then get the other 16 attachments set up and running.”
“They told us it would delay us a couple hours,” Russell said with a wry shake of his head. “Several hours later, we did get the next round of milking done. That was when I decided I was done with first milkings.”

Where it started
Lazy J Dairy grew from Jungemann Dairy, on the family farm north of Wolsey. Shortly after Russell and Janet married in 1980, the business kicked into gear.
“We started with 10 milk cows,” Janet shared. “Within a year we had grown to 20 cows and doubled that over the next few years.” The couple purchased the farm where they now live and work in 1993, from Larson Dairy. “With the move from a stanchion barn to a milk parlor, we expanded to 100 cows, and added 1,000 acres of farm ground.”
When Lucas returned from college in 2005 and joined the operation, the decision was made to expand further to accommodate. A new free stall barn was built, with enough room to hold 480 cows, and additional space to expand.
“Five years after that we added two more pens and were milking 540 cows,” Janet added.
While the herd was expanding, the original parlor installed by Greg Larson was still in use. “I always said that Greg got the really good years out of the parlor,” Russell said, “then we used for another 25 years.”

The latest expansion grew from simple need
“We were faced with an ‘upgrade or get out of the business’ situation,” Janet said. “The old parlor when we bought it was a double-four, meaning we could milk eight head at a time. We doubled that to a double-eight, but were still working with older equipment.”
The new parlor is a double-16, or 32 head at a time. At the present, the dairy milks 840 head of cows three times a day, with a goal of increasing production to 950 head in the next month.
The double-16 setup has the cows facing away from those doing the milking. The approach of milking from behind is a more efficient use of space in the parlor, than the old approach of milking from the side, requiring fewer steps from the technicians to take care of the 32 head of cows. The business part of the cow is at eye level, again a more efficient approach that limits the stooping and reaching, an important aspect when there are 900 cows on the “to-do list.” It takes an average of 3.6 minutes for a cow to get milked at Lazy J.
Milk production averages 83 pounds per cow, per day. Milk is generally calculated in pounds, but for a more understandable scale, a gallon of milk weights 8.6 pounds. So, at Lazy J Dairy, the average is just about a quart short of 10 gallons per day. And that is an AVERAGE. On this day, the cows in Pen 11 are leading the other pens of the barn, with an average of slightly over 100 pounds per head. The milk is collected in one of two 8,000 gallon bulk tanks. Tanker trucks currently haul eight 7,000 gallon loads out each week. Lazy J Dairy sells its product to Land-O-Lakes, which resells it to one of a handful of cheese plants in the area.
“Right now,” Lucas said, “most of our milk is going to the Baby Bell plant by Brookings.”
“They have also sold to the AMPI cheese plant at Hoven and the Agri-Pure factory in Lake Norden,” Russell said. “But our check comes from Land-O-Lakes.”
“After we invested in the new parlor, barn and accessories, we had to increase the number of head we milk,” Janet noted, “to create the additional revenue to address the cost of the new equipment. One thing led to another.”
The free stall barn is 320 feet wide and 420 feet long, covering just over three acres. Cows are penned in groups of 128 head - which is four groups of 32 - which increases milking efficiency. The barn was built with the ability to expand. A wall of fans at floor level occupies one wall, to better regulate the temperature in the barn when temperatures increase. Dividers extend down from the high ceilings to keep the circulating air down upon the backs of the cows.
One side of each pen is stalls for the cows to lie down if they choose. The other side is a feeding area. When a pen circulates through the milk parlor, the pens are cleaned and the sand that is spread in the stalls is raked. A short time after the feeder passes down between adjacent pens, a skid steer loader with a blade on the front pushes the feed back within reach for each pen.
The farming side of the operation raises corn, soybeans and alfalfa, a great deal of which goes to feed the increased cow herd. All of the heifer calves are raised and placed into production and all steer calves are fattened and sold for slaughter.
To keep expenses down, members of the Jungemann family did most of the plumbing in the free stall barn. The entire operation has built in redundancies in the event that anything fails.
For the past 15 years, Lazy J Dairy has been a training area for a host of Brazilian exchange students attending school in Minnesota. “We normally have the kids for a year, and over the time we’ve been involved in the exchange program we have had 40 people through the program,” Russell said. “Most of them have realized success in some aspect of the dairy industry in Brazil when they return.”
In addition to learning how a modern dairy farm operates, the students also get a firmer grasp of English, which benefits those who go into administrative positions with their knowledge.

Driven by technology
“The industry is constantly evolving,” Russell said. “Consistency is paramount and staying on top of the overall herd health can help us diagnose problems when they are smaller and deal with them before they get to be big issues.”
To that end he holds up a devise about the size of his thumb. “This is a monitor that uses the same technology of a Wii bowling game. It’s all based on motion.”
He opens a computer program and explains the process.
“The monitor reads motions and determines whether the cow is feeding, chewing her cud, is lying down, walking and other things,” he said. “A cow that is not feeding and is not active gets our interest.”
Cow activity is also a trusted sign of a cow that is in heat. The Lazy J cows are closely watched to ensure overall health and that when the optimum time for breeding arrives, the person doing the artificial insemination is aware and prepared. Cows that are not bred in a timely fashion still eat as much or more, during the time they are ‘dry,’ or not being milked. The goal is to reduce the unproductive time.
“You can see that the production on this cow has been slipping and her activity is down the last day or so,” Russell said. “The herdsman has access to this information as well and will make a decision on treatment.”
The number of dairy farms in the state continues to fall, as the size of the remaining operations increases.
“When we started in 1980,” Russell said, “there were 7,700 dairies in South Dakota and about 7,000 were Class A operations. Now there are 230 dairies and all but one are Class A operations. Just like the small farms have been taken over by operations that are always increasing. The margins on dairy farms is thinner and thinner, so we have to be more and more efficient.”
It has been a nearly-40 year history of continuing growth for the Jungemanns, which begs the question of where the “Lazy” portion of “Lazy J” derives.
“Oh that,” Russell said with a chuckle. “My father and his brother each used a “J” brand - one used the left hip and the other used the right. My uncle had two sons, and I am one of five boys. I and my brothers each received a heifer when we were in school, and we had to tell them apart. I just laid the “J” on its back on the right hip for my heifer - a Lazy J. It was a pretty big deal.
“And I think since then we have branded at least two head!”
