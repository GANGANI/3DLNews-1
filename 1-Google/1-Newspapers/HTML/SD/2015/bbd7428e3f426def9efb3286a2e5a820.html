Lawsuit paints unflattering picture of Wilmington Trust

Maureen Milford   | Delaware News Journal

EDITOR'S NOTE: All claims against Matthew Waschull were dismissed in 2018 because the statute of limitations expired before the claims were brought. The dismissal of these charges was upheld on appeal.

Wilmington Trust was brought to its knees in 2010 after departing from its conservative roots and betting on speculative real estate development in Kent and Sussex counties through its commercial real estate division.

Now, a lawsuit brought by a prosperous real estate executive from Maryland offers an inside glimpse into one of Wilmington Trust's most celebrated business lines – wealth advisory services. And it's not a flattering picture for the institution founded by du Pont family members.

Charles Bresler, whose company owned signature properties from downtown Wilmington to Washington D.C. to Houston, was an alluring target for Wilmington Trust executives peddling wealth advisory services, court documents show. Bresler's publicly traded company, Bresler & Reiner Inc. of Rockville, Maryland, kept a corporate account at Wilmington Trust. At the peak of the real estate market, the business was ringing up $103 million in annual revenue.

Bank executives, eager to expand their relationship with Bresler, approached him with a strategy to preserve his personal wealth, according to a Wednesday ruling by U.S. District Judge Peter J. Messitte. This included a plan to pay his estate-tax bill. The bank proposed a trust through which Charles and his wife, Fleur, would be covered by a minimum $50 million, and possibly as high as $190 million in life insurance.

Here's how the program was designed to work: The bank created the Charles Bresler Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust with Wilmington Trust as trustee. The bank then loaned the money to the tax-sheltered trust to pay the annual multi-million dollar premiums on three life insurance policies, according to the Bresler lawsuit in federal court.

Bresler put up $3.7 million as a one-time collateral pledge, the suit says. At the deaths of both Charles and Fleur, the insurance benefit would pay off the loans and the remainder would be put into the trust for beneficiaries tax free. Charles died at 84 in 2010; Fleur remains very active today.

The deal with Wilmington Trust closed in February 2004. The Breslers were never told the bank received more than $2 million in commissions from the insurance companies for placing the policies, the lawsuit alleges.

Bresler's son, Sid, said his father thought the agreement with Wilmington Trust would take care of his estate tax issues. But the nightmare began in 2005, Sid said, when the bank demanded that the senior Bresler put up another $2 million in collateral – and explained that he would pledge additional collateral every year until both he and his spouse died.

"That was not what he signed on for," Sid Bresler said of his father. "He didn't wish to burden my mother or the family with this. My father spent from 2005 until he took his last breath in October 2010 trying to resolve this with these people. This has been a nightmare. These people knew my dad had been defrauded and for their own reasons chose not be honest with us."

In 2009, Charles Bresler filed suit against Wilmington Trust and some executives, alleging breach of contract, fraud, negligence and breach of fiduciary duty. Last year, a Maryland jury sided with the Breslers and awarded them $23.36 million in the breach of contract aspect of the case. On Wednesday, Judge Messitte denied Wilmington Trust's motion to amend the verdict. The fraud, negligence and breach of fiduciary duty aspect of the case will be tried later.

Wilmington Trust has denied all the allegations in the Breslers' federal lawsuit, saying Bresler was knowledgeable about secured lending and insurance, according to filings. He also had his own legal counsel. The bank performed "fully consistent" with the Bresler agreements, Wilmington Trust says. The way the bank sees it, "the Breslers want their money for nothing."

The recent verdict comes at the same time the bank's new owner, M&T Bank Corp. of Buffalo, N.Y., battles a number of legacy problems related to Wilmington Trust – including an ongoing federal criminal investigation in which the government is believed to be closing in on primary targets.

M&T, which bought Wilmington Trust in 2011 at a fire sale price, declined to comment on the litigation. C. Michael Zabel, group vice president with M&T in Buffalo, said he "can't shed any light on events that took place at Wilmington Trust prior to our merger, nor can I address issues that are pending before the court."

But emails, letters, memos and depositions in the Bresler case and another case involving Charles' lead bank contact reveal a bank culture markedly different from Wilmington Trust's historic image as a highly-principled institution:

• Wilmington Trust's former general counsel and corporate secretary, Michael A. DiGregorio, said in a 2012 deposition he did not think the bank had a policy to deal fairly with people. When asked if he associated the Wilmington Trust brand with putting clients ahead of the interests of Wilmington Trust, DiGregorio responded: "No." When asked if he associated Wilmington Trust's brand with integrity, DiGregorio responded: "No."

• In a deposition for another lawsuit against the bank in 2006, DiGregorio said Matthew Waschull, a former managing director for private wealth services at Wilmington Trust who dealt with the Breslers, was "always trying to dig some more money out of clients." Waschull was a defendant in the fraud aspect of the case, but the charges against him were later dismissed because the statute of limitation expired.

• A September 2006 internal email sent to Waschull by Ralph Wileczek, a former vice president, details revisions to the Bresler insurance. In that note, Wileczek refers to the "bad news" as an insurance commission dropping to $175,000. Wileczek is a defendant in the fraud, negligence and breach of fiduciary duty aspect of the case.

• In a lawsuit filed against Wilmington Trust in July 2006 by Edmond M. Ianni, a former bank vice president and the lead contact with the Breslers, Ianni said he called the Wilmington Police after he was physically threatened by a co-worker who was trying to "illegally" coerce money from him. Ianni alleged in his complaint he was ordered by superiors not to report the threats by the co-worker to legal authorities, senior management or The News Journal. They asked him to sign an acknowledgment promising he would not report the threats by a co-worker and "related wrongdoing at Wilmington Trust," the lawsuit says. When he refused to agree to the "cover-up," he was placed on administrative leave, Ianni alleged in his lawsuit – which was under seal when originally filed.

• Wilmington Trust, in its 2006 answer to the Ianni lawsuit, alleged Ianni "in his overzealous sales efforts...overreached and misrepresented the requirements related to the insurance products that he was seeking to sell."

• In a 2005 intra-company memorandum, carrying the subject line "Incident in the Lobby conference room," Waschull recounts an ugly confrontation with Ianni over how an "incentive payment," or commission, would be split for the sale of a high-end life insurance policy. At the meeting, Ianni "posed the possibility of his going to The News Journal with this story, and how damaging that would be to WT's [Wilmington Trust's] reputation and stock price."

• At a 2004 company sales meeting in Texas designed to motivate people to sell, Ianni was portrayed as Moses parting the Red Sea, according to court documents.

• An intra-company memorandum in 2003 talks of a staff member using the Wilmington Trust system "to gain personal information on another staff member."

"It was Animal House," said Sid Bresler. "Nobody asked: 'If we incentivize people who are making respectable salaries and bonuses and then offer huge incentives payments, will they deal fairly and honestly with people?'

Sidney Bresler and Bresler family attorney Phil Musolino discuss their case against Wilmington Trust.

Philip Musolino, a Washington, D.C. lawyer who is representing the Breslers, said while Wilmington Trust marketed itself to clients as the family's "trusted adviser," the culture was really "buyer beware." In an interview with The News Journal, he added: "When commissions become all that matters, by definition your clients' interest don't matter at all."

DiGregorio did not return calls for comment. Waschull declined to comment and referred calls to his attorney, who also declined to talk. Wileczek refused to discuss the case. Ianni did not return calls for comment.

For 107 years, since its founding in 1903 by du Pont family members, Wilmington Trust was considered a bank of integrity and soundness.

But sometime in the late 1990s or early 2000s, the bank departed from its traditional conservative values, court documents and interviews show. A plan was put in place to build on its traditional long-term banking relationships, including serving as the lender for the state's residential construction boom in Kent and Sussex counties.

Wilmington Trust apparently didn't foresee the Great Recession hitting in 2008, bringing with it the worst housing downturn in southern Delaware's history. By August 2010, the bank's future was in jeopardy. Land prices in southern Delaware had plummeted, affecting the value of the collateral for many of Wilmington Trust's commercial loans. In addition, the financial condition of many borrowers or guarantors on its loans was deteriorating. Federal regulators had tightened control.

Directors began scrambling to save the institution. Finally, in an 11th-hour deal in October 2010, the bank agreed to be sold at a stunning discount to M&T. Without "a strategic transaction" acceptable to regulators, the bank would likely "face significant regulatory actions in the near term, which would likely result in a significant impairment of its business prospects," a 2011 proxy statement reads.

The "merger" with M&T went through in early 2011. The deal resulted in shareholders taking huge losses and nearly 1,000 bank employees being cut from the workforce.

In the four years since Wilmington Trust ceased to be an independent bank, several legal actions have uncovered evidence that shows how it lost its way in its final decade.

Since 2013, the U.S. Justice Department has brought six criminal prosecutions related to Wilmington Trust that focus on accusations of bank fraud or conspiracy to commit bank fraud. All involve schemes related to real estate deals. Three of the cases were brought against former Wilmington Trust officials in the bank's commercial real estate division. All have pleaded guilty and are cooperating with the investigation. They have not yet been sentenced.

Brian Bailey, the highest ranking former executive be have been indicted, served as Delaware market manager and reported directly to the bank's former president, Robert V.A. Harra. As part of a plea agreement, Bailey agreed to cooperate with the government, including testifying before a grand jury or at a trial.

Bailey and others were alleged to have engaged in a scheme that resulted in past-due matured loans not being reported to the Federal Reserve, according to prosecutors. A matured loan is one in which the principal payment is past due and must be repaid in full to the lender unless the bank extends it through a new agreement.

As part of the scheme, Bailey and others at the bank, including Joseph Terranova, who headed Wilmington Trust's Delaware commercial real estate division, would cause matured loans to be waived if the loans were current on the interest payment and considered to be in the process of extension, the government alleges. This led to Wilmington Trust not reporting to the Federal Reserve and others the full extent of past due loans, prosecutors allege.

By the end of December 2009, the total amount of matured loans not reported to the Federal Reserve as past due or nonaccrual status – because they were listed as "waived" – was more than $373 million, according to court documents. Of that amount, approximately $330 million in loans were at least 90 days past due with some up to 1,280 days past due, according to the filing.

At his August plea hearing, Bailey told the court that "everyone" in his area was working to extend the maturity of past due loans, including people working "above" and "below" him.

A separate civil lawsuit filed by shareholders, parallels the criminal cases, with investors alleging the officers and directors conspired to fraudulently conceal hundreds of millions of dollars' worth of past-due and non-performing loans at the bank. Shareholders scored a significant victory last year when a federal judge allowed the case to move forward after ruling that investors have "sufficiently alleged that defendants made false and misleading statements."

That ruling was followed in September, by Wilmington Trust reaching an agreement with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission to pay $18.5 million to settle charges that it made false and misleading disclosures about its past-due loans over multiple quarters in 2009 and 2010. The bank paid the money to settle the accounting and disclosure fraud charges, according to the SEC. The bank neither admitted nor denied guilt.

Charles Bresler wasn't always rich.

As a young man in the 1950s, he worked with his father selling chickens, eggs and butter in the Florida Avenue meat market in Washington, D.C., his son, Sid, said.

Looking toward the future, Charles Bresler saw an opportunity in the real estate industry. With the blessing of his wife, Fleur, Charles Bresler worked for free learning the real estate business.

It paid off. Eventually, he built Bresler & Reiner real estate with Burton Reiner. The company, which owned and operated motels, hotels, apartment buildings and office projects, went public in 1971. One of its best known developments was the Waterside Mall in Washington, D.C., but it owned properties in Virginia, Maryland, Pennsylvania, Texas and Florida.

In Wilmington, it owned two buildings near Rodney Square, the I.M. Pei Building at 1105 N. Market Street and 919 N. Market Street.

A 2003 report in the Washington Post lists the company as having assets of $243 million.

Outside his business life, Charles served in the Maryland House of Delegates in the 1960s. Both he and Fleur were lovers of turned and carved wood artwork. Their collection by some of the country's best-known wood artists was donated to the Smithsonian American Art Museum.

"My father came from humble beginnings and made something of his life," Sid Bresler said. "But what mattered most to him was taking care of the family."

By 2003, Charles was in his late 70s, his estate worth millions.

"Accordingly, he began to consider how, after his death, he might provide for the future of his wife Fleur, his five children and nine grandchildren, looking for a way to transfer his sizable estate to them with the minimum of trouble and expense," reads a federal court opinion handed down Wednesday.

"Eventually, Charlie's concerns came to the attention of WTC [Wilmington Trust Company], which had previously done business with Charlie in connection with some of his corporate interests."

Bresler & Reiner kept a corporate account with Wilmington Trust. The bank also leased space in one of the company's office buildings in Wilmington.

"My father reached a level of success where the family would have, potentially, a fairly substantial tax burden," Sid Bresler said.

Given Wilmington Trust's reputation, Charles Bresler listened when approached by bank executives, his son said. Bresler did ask a du Pont family member who was a friend about the bank and was told they were "great people," according to court testimony.

The bank romanced its high net-worth customers with such things as Harry and David gift towers wrapped in customized Wilmington Trust ribbons, court documents show. A card would be from the private client advisers, one 2003 intra-company memorandum says.

The bank proposed to Charles Bresler a customized "wealth creation and preservation strategy" that involved life insurance held in a trust, according to court documents.

The program was put forth by Ianni, a Princeton-educated lawyer who had been brought on board at Wilmington Trust in 1999 to focus on "prospecting activities on two important targets," according to an internal memorandum.

"The first is prospecting for leads from our Corporate Financial Services officers, where the target prospects are the executives of our major clients," the memorandum says. "The second is prospecting for leads through financial intermediaries in geographic markets where we do not have offices and/or staff."

Ianni outlined a strategy that involved the creation of the Charles Bresler Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT). Unlike traditional life insurance policies, the ILIT consisted of a death benefit and an investment account that would increase in value each year, thereby increasing the total death benefit.

The plan called for Wilmington Trust not only to make loans to cover the annual premiums but also to borrow extra to "overfund" the policies to increase the size of the investment account, according to court documents.

Since the interest rate on the loan was designed to be lower than the rate paid by the insurance company on the investment, a profit was guaranteed, according legal documents. The profit also would enjoy certain tax protections because it was earned by the trust, according to the Bresler lawsuit.

The deal closed in February 2004.

According to court documents, "incentive" paid to the executives could be substantial for the ILITs.

Court documents show that Ianni got approximately $488,000 in commissions for the Bresler insurance and Wileczek got approximately $324,000.

Meanwhile, Charles Bresler, whose health was failing, thought everything was taken care, his son said. A year later, he found out otherwise.

"To Mr. Bresler's great surprise, well after this plan was put into place, he was advised by a WTC lending officer that he would have to make substantial additional contributions to the trust to support future premium payment loans," reads a letter sent by the Breslers' lawyer in 2007. "In the event he refused to make the additional contributions the policies would be canceled and the cash from the cancellation and most, if not all of the funds in the trust would be taken by WTC to pay off the loan."

Ianni was fired in 2005, according to legal documents. The next year, Ianni sued the bank over a commission, alleging Waschull and others retaliated against him after he reported the incident of workplace violence, the complaint says. The bank and Waschull denied the allegations.

Wilmington Trust then countersued Ianni alleging clients asked Ianni about the need to pledge additional capital to cover the loans.

"When asked about the potential need for additional collateral, [Ianni] either misrepresented that there would be no need for additional collateral or provided misleading and unresponsive answers, all of which were designed to induce clients to agree to purchase the insurance so that [Ianni] could receive the incentive compensation."

Ianni denied in legal papers he made any misrepresentations.

The Ianni case against Wilmington Trust ended with a confidential settlement.

The Breslers sued in 2009. Wilmington Trust continued to pay "under protest" the minimum annual premiums to keep the policies in effect.

Wilmington Trust "deprived the Bresler family of tens of millions of dollars of potential death benefits, reduced the 'net in trust' available to the ILIT by tens of millions of dollars, cannibalized the investment accounts for the insurance policies, and cost the plaintiffs millions of dollars in out of pocket expenses," the Breslers say in legal papers.

"The last day of his life he was reviewing one of the amended complaints," Sid Bresler said.

In another ruling in the case on some procedural motions, Judge Messitte made some critical remarks about the Bresler insurance program designed by Wilmington Trust, including the fact the bank wore "three hats in the transaction."

"...An incestuous triad that WTC seems to blithely ignore – created a glaring conflict of interest," Messitte wrote.

Wilmington Trust counters that the Breslers want to impose obligations on the bank that were never part of any agreement, according to court documents.

Wilmington Trust alleges in a 2013 filing that the Breslers lack evidence of any contract that requires the bank to lend annual premiums of up to $5.5 million on the basis of a one-time collateral payment of $3.7 million. Despite this lack of evidence, the Breslers have each refused to post additional collateral, the bank alleges.

"The evidence will establish that Wilmington Trust performed fully consistent with the written agreements between the parties, and that the Breslers, as a result, will receive a windfall of $36 million if Mrs. Bresler were to die this year, without having put up a dime," the bank alleges in 2013.

As of August 2014, Wilmington Trust had extended up to $17.1 million in loans to the Bresler trust through a line of credit to keep the policies from lapsing without any additional collateral, according to Wilmington Trust. The value of the collateral account was approximately $4.9 million, "not even a third of the value of the current obligations of the Bresler Trust to Wilmington Trust," the bank says in legal papers.

"Accordingly, the Bresler loans are currently under-collateralized. In the event that the Bresler policies should lapse or not pay death benefits in excess of the Bresler loans, Wilmington Trust will be damaged since there are insufficient assets to secure the Bresler loans," the bank says in an August 2014 legal filings.

The Breslers have been "unjustly enriched" by this arrangement, the bank maintains in legal papers.

But Judge Messitte in his Wednesday ruling related to the breach of contract aspect was unfavorable to the bank. Wilmington Trust was ordered to continue to pay the premiums on Fleur Bresler's life insurance for the rest of her life. He also ordered that Wilmington Trust return the $4.9 million in the collateral account.

"WTC needs to be reminded – once again – that it lost this case," Messitte wrote.

"This result is what happens when a bank like Wilmington Trust makes decisions based on short-term greed without any regard for the interest of their clients. Eventually, they will pay what they owe whether they like it not" Musolino said.

"The Bresler family recognizes that it took 10 years for Charlie to be vindicated and if more years are ahead the Breslers are willing to do what they have to do to see to it that Wilmington [Trust] finally meets its obligations," Musolino said.
