    Lawyers share more findings with Honeywell suit

    • By Linda Kennedy Planet News Editor news@metropolisplanet.com
    • Oct 11, 2018
    • Oct 11, 2018 Updated Oct 18, 2018
    • 0
    • Facebook
    • Twitter
    • WhatsApp
    • SMS
    • Email

    Residents of Metropolis and Massac County gathered at the Metropolis Community Center to hear Richard Kruger, from Kruger, Henry and Hunter Law Firm of Metropolis, along with Kevin Thompson and Katrina Carroll, representing two of the five other participating law firms, speak about a class action lawsuit filed on May 16 at Benton’s federal courthouse against Honeywell.

    “As I mentioned several months ago, our law firm has participated in the gathering of information regarding the chemical substance of the discharges from Honeywell. These discharges are radioactive, toxic and carcinogenic. The chemicals involved include uranium, thorium and plutonium.”

    The purpose of the meeting on Wednesday was to give details on the findings they had discovered since May following several tests.  

    “After the findings we have uncovered over these past few months, we are asking for three things from Honeywell:  medical monitoring of all plantiffs and class members; if the plant should reopen by 2020, a correction of the emission problems must be made; and we are finding, tragically, a real high number of residents who have been diagnosed with certain types of cancer, which we plan to pursue personal insurance cases against the plant,” Thompson said.

    Thompson explained that certainly types of cancer can be caused due to radiation exposure, such as gallbladder, liver and thyroid cancers and several childhood cancers.  For example, one finding has been the increase number of residents who have had their gallbladders removed.  “We are looking to see if these gallbladder surgeries have any connection to radiation the individual may had been exposed to,” he added.

    Attendees were also informed of a letter which was recently submitted to Dr. Ileana Arias, director of the Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry, Center for Disease Control, in Washington, D.C., to inform her office of the notice of intent to file citizen suit under the Clean Air Act, the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act and the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation and Liability Act.

      A copy of the letter was also sent to Darius Adamczyk, Honeywell CEO; Andrew Wheeler and Cathy Stepp, both with the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA);  Attorney General Jeff Sessions; Alec Messina, director of the Illinois EPA; and Lisa Madigan, Illinois Attorney General.

      The letter explained the plant, which is located on the outskirts of Metropolis, made uranium hexafluoride (UF6) from at least 1963 until 2017 adding, “What the populace did not know was that continuously for decades the plant expelled air laden with radioactive material and other metals through a system of fans and ducts operating around the clock to vent air from within the plant to the atmosphere and that filters routinely failed.

      “For over a half century winds have carried the radioactive materials, fluorinated compounds and heavy metals throughout the area in such concentrations that radioactive materials and metals can still be found deposited in soils and buildings in and around Metropolis.”

    Interviews

      Several interviews have been conducted from Honeywell employees, explaining the day-to-day processes, which went on inside the plant.

      These interviews were included in the 12-page letter.  A portion of these interviews included the following:

      Fifty-five gallon drums, bolted shut and filled with powdered uranium ore from all throughout the world, would come to the UF6 plant where they would be emptied with an automated “drum dumper.” Each time the drum dumper emptied a barrel, employees stated radioactive dust containing metals would be released into the air.

      Matt Carlsen, an Operator 2 Technician at the Honeywell plant from March 2005 to July 2016, described the dumping process and how easily it allowed material to be released into the air — “They would fill up all these filters, have a blow back system that would bump them, and it would […] drum it off, it would come down through rotary valves that I told you would leak all the time, be dusting out, you’d go walk by, you’d see everybody dusting out, and here come there is this white cloud look like baby powder coming out that’s the most radioactive stuff in the plant.”

      After the drums were dumped, they were cleaned. Earlier in the plant’s history workers sandblasted the drums, which also released radioactive and metal-contaminated dust into the air.

      Later, a water cleaning method replaced sandblasting. Six-inch berms around a concrete cleaning pad contained the wastewater that then entered a series of drains leading to the UF6 plant’s wastewater treatment facility where, after moving through a single settling pond, the water was discharged into the Ohio River.

      Darrell Lillie, a plant operator at Honeywell for 25 years beginning in June 1992, recounted how contaminated material would eventually enter the Ohio River, “[…] if it rains contamination can run off of that, so it just goes to the drains, goes through ETF. ETF is environmental protection facility gets cleaned up before it’s discharged into the river.”

      From the drum dumper the radioactive ore went into a dryer known as a “calciner” that would heat the radioactive material containing ore. Although intended to contain the dust within the calciner, vents from the device discharged into the atmosphere outside the UF6 plant, presenting another pathway for radioactive material.

      Clay West, an operator at the Honeywell plant from 2011 through July 2014, noted, “If you have a stream of hot gases and you have a little bit of dust in that stream, then it has potential to go very, very high into the atmosphere and get in the wind stream.”  

      After the calciner, the radioactive material went to “ore processing” where it went through yet another drying process. Water was then injected into the dry, powdered radioactive material from which pellets were made. Workers referred to the pellets as “green salt.”

        In the “fluoronator,” fluorene gas was introduced to the radioactive green salt, thereby converting the radioactive green salt into a gas — UF6 — which was then pumped into canisters and shipped to customers.

      Once radioactive dust from the plant entered the wind stream, it easily contaminated the surrounding community.

      “Well, we could very easily have a pinhole leak or a broken filter in our backup reductor filter and be releasing small amounts of dust into that incinerator off gas stream into the atmosphere and get on the wind stream out here, make it out into the community,” stated West.

        Over the reach of time, on occasion an alarm would sound warning the populace of a “release” from the plant. Rarely would such an alarm be accompanied by an acknowledgement or announcement by plant officials, but even on those rare occasions the message always took the same reassuring tone that any such release did not exceed regulatory limits and did not pose a risk of harm.

        As many as 70 sampling devices, eight which were located around the perimeter fence and 62 inside the plant, were arrayed throughout the UF6 plant’s buildings and grounds from which tests for radiation were conducted on a daily basis by plant personnel working in the on-site health physics lab.

      Marvin Barnhill, a lab analyst in the plant’s health physics department during this time period, testifies that he never observed any non-detect readings for radiation from the eight outside sampling devices.

      Barnhill added, “Additionally, sampling was performed periodically at various locations in the community surrounding the Honeywell plant. These sampling sites included the nearby Maple Grove Elementary School, ponds on private property across the Illinois border in Kentucky, the Brookport Lock and Dam and various private residences in the vicinity of the Honeywell plant.”

      Barnhill testifies as to the fact that he did not recall observing any samples taken from any of these off site sampling areas to read “non detect” for radiation.

      Plant workers themselves actually observed their exposure to radiation first hand. “I just like, man, this stuff is hot. I mean I could feel the HF [Hexafluoride]. If you shaved, you know you shaved that morning, went to work you know, boy, I mean that HF, the fumes, it would eat you up,” said Lillie.

      Never have Honeywell officials or government officials disclosed the daily emissions of low-levels of ionizing radiation into the environment where the populace of Metropolis has lived and worked for years. Honeywell either knew or should have known of the obvious poor conditions and environmental practices at the site and knew or with the exercise of reasonable diligence should have known of the threat of a release of radionuclides, heavy metals and fluorinated compounds.

           

      Russian Plutonium

        Additionally, the letter explains during a public Licensee Performance Review in Metropolis on May 3, 2018, Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) Region II Chief of Fuel Facility Inspection Omar R. Lopez-Santiago admitted to community members that the Honeywell UF6 plant has never had a license to handle plutonium.

      During that review, former Honeywell employee and Metropolis resident Howard Cook informed the NRC representatives that Honeywell had in fact received and processed downgraded fissile material extracted from Russian nuclear warheads beginning at some point in 1993. During the meeting, Cook explained that Honeywell received Russian fissile material in the form of green salt delivered in 15-gallon drums with markings written in Russian.  Union members obtained a sample of the Russian green salt, took it to the plant’s lab and demanded to know what it contained.

      Lillie, described discovering the mysterious 15-gallon drums and testing their contents, only to find after testing that they had disappeared.

      A Honeywell representative stated the during review in May that the UF6 plant was in a “ready idle” state and that it was anticipated that the UF6 plant would go back into production when the price of uranium rose.

      No mention was made by representative or NRC of any planned changes to the UF6 plant’s design that would serve to limit emissions from the facility.

           

    Environmental Investigation

      Before issuing this Notice of Intent to File Citizen’s Suit, a scientific investigation of the environmental impact of the unpermitted releases by Honeywell and the dispersion of toxic material was conducted.

      Sampling of soil, bulk material, construction debris, household dust, sewage sludge, surface water, drinking water and groundwater has been performed by Downstream Strategies, LLC of Morgantown, West Virginia and Boston Chemical Data Corp. of Natick, Massachusetts to investigate impacts from the Honeywell emission on residential properties surrounding the Honeywell Plant. The collected samples have been analyzed for radionuclides and other contaminants.

      Review of the analyses for various radionuclides, metals and fluorinated compounds indicates a linkage to activities at the Honeywell facility. The nature of uranium and decay products detected present a marker establishing an environmental pathway.  

      Eberline Analytical of Oak Ridge, Tennessee performed radioisotope testing. Additional microscopy and X-ray testing was performed by Microvision Laboratories of Chelmsford, Massachusetts.

      Samples taken on and around the plaintiffs’ properties and at other locations throughout Metropolis confirm an elevated presence of radioactivity from uranium and uranium decay-series isotopes, as well as radioactive micro particles containing percent concentrations of uranium and the uranium decay product, thorium. The radioactive micro particles found in homes close to the Honeywell Metropolis facility contain up to 48 percent by weight pure uranium and more than 4 percent radioactive thorium.  The feedstock to the Honeywell Metropolis plant manufacturing process has approximately the same magnitude of uranium and thorium.

      Radiochemistry testing of dusts in surrounding properties has detected activities of uranium-234 and uranium-238 along with radioactive decay products of these isotopes including bismuth-214, thorium-230 and lead-214.  Onsite activities of uranium and thorium are an order of magnitude above these already elevated offsite activities.

      One of the dust samples had a positive detection for plutonium-238, an industrial isotope not normally found in the environment and with no known natural source. A sample of Metropolis municipal sewerage sludge was found to contain actinium-228 at an activity significantly above background.  

      This sludge sample also had the highest detected level of fluoride of all samples collected offsite; indicating that fluoride, which is a major Honeywell Metropolis Plant contaminant, was present in the wastewater from the municipality.

      Honeywell was contacted to see if they wish to make a statement following Wednesday’s meeting.  

      According to a Honeywell spokesperson, the statement read: “Honeywell is committed to the safety of our facilities and the communities in which they reside. The Metropolis plant is highly regulated by both the NRC and the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).  

      “Honeywell has filed a motion to dismiss the complaint under the Price Anderson Act, which governs nuclear facilities. That motion is currently pending before the court. We believe this lawsuit is without merit, and we will defend ourselves vigorously,” the statement concludes.

      Six plaintiffs, who reside close to the plant, are seeking remediation of the radioactive and metal contamination found on their property.

      The addition of 13 more plaintiffs has been added to the lawsuit.  They include:  Roger Steward, Saundra Steward, Verna Welch, Savannah Welch, Wanda Sullivan, John Sullivan, Todd Faulkner, Kim Faulkner, Clyde Schmidt, Tim Beck, Ronald Roundtree, Steve Kruger, Rollo Burnett, Daniel Burnett, Clyde Wills, Ron Mott, Jeff Crippen, Randy Langford and Richard Todd Reames.

      “As of Oct. 10, 519 Massac County residents have signed retainer agreements to be class members in this case,” added Kruger.

      Thompson concluded the meeting by stating, “Metropolis exposure is novel, very unique, because there is not another uranium conversion plant to compare it to, which means a lot of research and scientific discovery will be involved.  But Honeywell made a decision [when not correcting emissions] — a decision to hurt people, to hurt property, to hurt property value.”
