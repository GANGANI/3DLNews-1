Gretchen Johnson, 103, 71-year Beverly resident

by Caroline Connors

Gretchen Johnson, a 71-year resident of Beverly, died at her home on Oct. 6 of natural causes. She was 103 years old.

Born Gretchen Schuessler in 1909, Johnson was the youngest of the three daughters of a prominent Joliet doctor and his wife. She attended Joliet High School and Joliet Junior College before heading to the University of Illinois at Urbana- Champaign with the intention of becoming a doctor, according to her daughter, Gretchen Mc- Dowell, of Beverly.

Johnson’s life changed course soon after enrolling at U. of I. when she came home to care for her ailing mother. At the age of 20, instead of returning to school, she married Howard Johnson, a young man she had met in her chemistry class at junior college, and moved to Chicago to be near her husband’s job at U.S. Steel South Works.

The Johnsons weathered the Great Depression on the southeast side of Chicago and in 1941 moved to Beverly, buying a home where they raised their four children on the 2600 block of 107th Street that was surrounded by prairie, Mc- Dowell said. The family joined St. John the Divine Lutheran Church at 105th Street and Oakley Avenue, and Johnson quickly became involved in the congregation and the Beverly community, serving in leadership roles in a variety of organizations and clubs, including the Morgan Park Junior Women’s Club, the Ruth Society and Lutheran Child and Family Services.

“My mother loved the neighborhood and all that was going on,” McDowell said. “She was a homemaker all her life, and church was the center of her social life. She was not one to sit at home; she liked the social and charitable aspects of the women’s clubs and was always supportive of opportunities to help other people.”

Johnson’s skills as a cook and baker were in high demand at church functions, McDowell said, and her mother happily obliged requests for specialties such as Swedish coffee bread, which her husband often shuttled to church as soon as it came out of the oven.

“My mother was a marvelous baker; her pie dough was exquisite,” Johnson said. “She also baked the Communion bread for 20 years when they belonged to Good Samaritan Lutheran Church and provided the altar flowers every week, using flowers from her garden or ones that she found along the railroad tracks.”

Johnson was also a talented seamstress who made her daughter’s clothes, an avid reader and an enthusiastic outdoorswoman, her daughter said.

Some of the family’s favorite memories were from its annual summer trip to Peninsula State Park in Door County, Wisc., McDowell said, where they camped in a tent, fished for walleye and made homemade jam from berries they picked and cooked over an open fire. After her father retired, Mc- Dowell said, her parents traveled the country with a 12-foot house trailer.

Although she was born and raised in a conservative era, Johnson was “very liberal and forward thinking,” her daughter said, and was open to the changes that occurred during her lifetime.

“Her grandfather was a Lutheran pastor, and when she was growing up, men sat on one side of the church and women on the other. Years later, Mother was the first woman to sign the constitution to allow women the right to vote in the church,” McDowell said. “She lived through two world wars, the Great Depression and saw a man walk on the moon. She was a White Sox fan, voted in the last presidential election and, in the last couple of years, was keeping up with my brother on Skype.”

Johnson’s 100th birthday in 2009 was celebrated with a large party at the Oak Lawn Hilton, McDowell said, where family and friends gathered from all over the country to give thanks for the guest of honor. As she did throughout her life, Johnson thoroughly enjoyed the festivities, her daughter said.

“Mother enjoyed life,” Mc- Dowell said. “She had a great sense of humor and great faith. Every morning when she woke up, she said, ‘This is the day the Lord hath made; let us rejoice and be glad in it.’ She was an awesome lady.”

Johnson is survived by her sons, Carl (Marilyn), Lloyd (Paula) and Guy (Jean); her daughter, Gretchen; her grandchildren, Cathy, Carol, Tisa, Adam, Samuel, Lloyd, Russell, Fred, Lisa, Paul, Andrew, Adel and Tess; and her great-grandchildren Martha, Elizabeth, Charlie, Christine, Sophia, Izabella, Morgan, Philip, Benjamin, William, Taylor, Alex, Ashley, Amelia, Leah, Emily, Madeline and Olivia.

Visitation was held at Beverly Ridge Funeral Home on Oct. 9, followed by a memorial service at Bethlehem Evangelical Lutheran Church on Oct. 10. Interment was private.

Memorials may be made to Bethlehem Evangelical Lutheran Church, 9401 S. Oakley Ave., Chicago, IL 60643.
