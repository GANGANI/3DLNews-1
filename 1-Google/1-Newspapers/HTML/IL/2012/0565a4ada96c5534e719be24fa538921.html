Historian shares stories aboutlocal men who gave their livesfor the cause of freedom in Civil War

Sun, 01/01/2012 - 7:00pm admin

So many men enlisted in the Union Army from Union County that the Chicago Tribune of Sept. 4, 1862, claimed it should be awarded the title of “the banner county in the State in furnishing volunteers in the army.” 

A project sponsored by the John A. Logan Museum in Murphysboro plans to honor the memory of the men from Congressman John A. Logan’s district, including Union County, who gave their lives for the cause of freedom during the Civil War. 

The project includes museum displays, presentations and other programs and will continue through the 150th anniversary commemoration of the Civil War ending in 2015. 

Regional historian Darrel Dexter, of rural Jonesboro, has researched the 1,877 men from Union County who answered President Abraham Lincoln’s call for volunteers during the Civil War (1861-1865). 

Four hundred and twenty-nine of those who enlisted died or were killed during the war. 

This number amounts to nearly 4 percent of the entire population of the county, which was 11,181 in 1860. 

To place these numbers in perspective, imagine nearly 3,000 men from the county’s present population enlisting in the army and the death of 677 of them.

All families were affected by the war, but some more severely than others, Dexter said.  

Ten members of the Dillow family, eight of the Toler family, and six from the Hileman family died in the war. 

Five members of the Casper and Lence families did not return to Union County alive.  

The Cruse, Miles, Wood, Hicks, Treece, Davolt, Goodman, Stokes, Stone, Verble and Norris families each lost three members.

The Civil War regiment which lost the most Union County men was the 109th, which was later transferred to the 11th Illinois Infantry.  Two hundred and eleven of their number died in the war. 

In John A. Logan’s 31st Illinois Volunteer Regiment, 45 Union County men died.  Thirty-five of the county’s dead served in the 60th, 36 in the 81st, 26 in the 22nd Ohio, 19 in the 18th, and 10 in the 6th Illinois Cavalry.

Most of the men who died in the war died of diseases contracted while in the service. 

Dexter’s research revealed that typhoid fever was the most commonly named illness, but chronic diarrhea, pneumonia, measles, small pox, dysentery, and consumption all took their toll.

Sixty-seven men from Union County were killed in action or died later from wounds received in battle. 

The bloodiest day of the war for the county was Feb. 15, 1862, when 16 of their men were killed at the Battle of Fort Donelson in Tennessee. 

One of those who fell on the battlefield was 18-year-old Jerome Provo, of Jonesboro.  The March 8, 1862, Jonesboro Gazette reported that, “Mr. J. J. Provo left home with C. H. Williford to recover the body of his son Jerome Provo who was killed at Ft. Donelson. 

"After considerable trouble owing to orders prohibiting travel up the Cumberland and Tennessee rivers they reached their destination and found the body and returned home on Tuesday. 

"The deceased had been dead nearly four weeks yet the features were very natural and the body in a remarkable state of preservation.  The corpse was visited by nearly all our citizens on Tuesday and Wednesday morning and many silent tears were dropped on the coffin containing the remains of him who left us but a few short weeks before. 

"On Wednesday at 10 o’clock the remains were followed to the grave by a large concourse of citizens and several hundred troops from Camp Dubois. 

"The ball which caused the death entered his right breast, dividing the main artery of the heart, passing on the opposite side some distance below its point of entrance. Jerome belonged to the 48th regiment and possessed every requisite of a true soldier. Of stout and muscular frame, obedient to orders, fearlessly brave and pleasant and of cheerful disposition he was admired by all his associates.” 

One year later, his brother, Warren J. Provo, died of wounds received at the Siege of Vicksburg. 

Two years later, their father, James J. Provo, was shot by a Civil War soldier on the streets of Jonesboro.

Nine men from Union County were killed during the Siege of Vicksburg, which lasted from May to November 1863, and four were killed at the Battle of Belmont on Nov. 7, 1861.

Andrew Jackson Montgomery and Ephraim Dillow were killed at Kennesaw Mountain, Ga., on June 27, 1864. 

Capt. John B. Raymond, of Company E, 31st Illinois Infantry, in which they served, wrote the following letter to Ephraim’s father, Levi Dillow, in Anna:

“Dear Sir, It becomes my painful duty to inform you of the death of your son Ephraim late of my Company. He was killed in an engagement with the enemy today.  He was shot through the left breast just above the nipple.  Ephraim was a good soldier—always ready to do his duty—either in camp or in the battle field.  His final statements will be made out and forwarded to the Adjutant General of the Army at Washington and you can obtain his arrearages with the US Govt.  Please accept the sympathies of a friend of your son’s.”

Others met their death due to accidents, which especially plagued the 109th as they moved south. 

Nathan Simpson, an 18-year-old soldier of Co. C of the 109th, was killed Oct. 20, 1862, by an accidental discharge of a comrade’s gun on the Diadem sternwheeler that took the regiment across the Ohio River from Cairo. 

The bullet passed through the boiler deck and cabin floor and hit him in the right side.  The bullet then ricocheted through the floor and hit another soldier on deck. 

James E. Frost, a drum major in the 109th, deserted in 1863 and returned from the war with a revolver that he proudly exhibited to a crowd in front of Charles Cruse’s store in Jonesboro.  Ben F. Sullivan, 1st lieutenant of the 109th, looked at it then gave it to Frost who handed it to David Miller and while in the hands of Miller the gun discharged. 

The ball hit Sullivan in the center of the abdomen and lodged above his left hip.  He was carried to his house, where he died on Feb. 28, 1863. 

Jacob Durall was a 17-year-old private in Co. C, 109th.   The Feb. 7, 1863, Jonesboro Gazette reported his death by stating, “A serious accident occurred on the 17th.  The night was bitter cold and the men were quartered in their tents.  Jacob Durall, Newton Mackey, Jackson Nipper, and James Stafford of Captain Rich’s company had carried a pair of live coals into the tent and tied the entrance shut to keep the cold air and snow out. Stafford came into the tent half an hour later than the others and found Durall suffocated and others moaning and coughing in bad condition from the smoke from the charcoals.”       

One of the most tragic deaths of a Union County Civil War solder was that of Andrew J. “Andy” Bailey of Co. D, 109th.  The Sept. 27, 1862, Jonesboro Gazette reported, “A soldier of Company D in a desperate state committed suicide by blowing his brains out with a revolver Wednesday night. He borrowed the pistol from a boy in camp and said he intended to shoot chickens. He went home and told his wife (Laura E. Bailey) he was going to kill her and then himself.  She ran from the room, but he shot himself in front of his 10-year-old daughter (Martha E. Bailey).” 

Bailey lived in the house near the Methodist church in Jonesboro, which was occupied in 1909 by Adelia Dougherty. 

One soldier of the 109th even died of delirium tremens. An officer of the regiment reported, “It was not induced by indulgence of liquor, but from the scarcity of it. He had been accustomed to strong drink all his whole life and being deprived of it during his connection in the army resulted in the above state.  He exhibited the first symptom about 8 o’clock Wednesday and died after a most intense suffering of five hours.” 

Over half of the soldiers who died in the Civil War were under age 25 and 45 of them were teenagers, five of the youngest being only 17 years old. The oldest was 49-year-old Ezekiel J. Spaulding, who died at Shiloh.

The final resting places of only 171 of Union County’s Civil War soldiers have been located and photographed by Dexter. 

One hundred and seventeen are buried in marked graves in 18 national cemeteries scattered mainly throughout the South. 

Twenty-five found their final resting place at Memphis, Tenn., 17 at Vicksburg, Miss, 12 at St. Louis, Mo., and nine at the notorious Confederate prison camp at Andersonville, Ga. 

Most of the remaining soldiers are likely among the thousands of “unknown soldiers” buried in national cemeteries or in now unmarked graves in county cemeteries.  

In Union County cemeteries, there are 14 Civil War casualties buried in St. John’s Cemetery, 10 in Jonesboro Cemetery, and six in Casper Cemetery. 

Other Union County cemeteries with soldiers who died in the Civil War are Campground, Landreth, Ebenezer-Hall, Water Valley, Anna, Grammer, Kimmel, Lick Creek, Moscow, Tripp, Wall, Mt. Olive, Hinkle, Kelley, and Mt. Tabor.

Only four photographs of Union County Civil War soldiers have been located. 

Anyone with information or photographs to contribute to this project is asked to contact Darrel Dexter, 4350 Lingle Creek Rd., Jonesboro, Ill. 62952, <a href="mailto:darreldexter@hotmail.com">darreldexter@hotmail.com</a> or call (618) 747-2068.
