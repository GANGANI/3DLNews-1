World War II veteran commemorates Veteran's Day with story: Five Cravatta brothers served during the war

  • By ANGIE FIELD
  • Nov 7, 2012
  • Nov 7, 2012

This Sunday, Nov. 11, people across the United States will celebrate Veterans Day in honor of armed service veterans. In celebration of this important and reverent day, former long-time Savanna resident Mike Cravatta has shared the story of how he and four of his brothers were called to serve in World War II.

“I need to mention my mother who saw five of her sons leave for the war,” said Mike. “She would hang a blue star in the window each time one of her sons left. She had five stars in the window.”

The blue star was a symbol commonly used by mothers with children in the service. It was used to show love, pride, hope and also grave concern for their children.

The use of the blue star started in World War I when mothers with children in the service would proudly display the symbol in windows and on walls. If their son was lost in the war, the blue star would be solemnly replaced with a gold star.

Luckily, the story ended well for Mike Cravatta’s mother who saw her five sons return safely from time served during World War II.

“I was in high school when war broke out,” said Mike. “I remember hearing about Pearl Harbor. It happened on a Sunday, and they told us at wrestling practice. Like any young kid I assumed the war wouldn’t last long. I remember thinking we were so powerful that the war would be over quickly.”

Three years later, Mike Cravatta was drafted, his time to be served in the U.S. Navy.

“I was on an LST,” he said, explaining the naval vessel that was created to support amphibious operations by carrying great quantities of vehicles, cargo and troops and depositing them directly on the shores for their intended missions.

The youngest of the five brothers, Mike Cravatta had seen his siblings leave for war before him. “I saw this and thought, it is something they had to do. I felt the same way when I went. It was something I had to do,” he said.

Mike’s oldest brother Paul Didato, who was a half-brother on his mother’s side since she lost her first husband in a mining accident, had been in the service prior to the war.

“Paul joined the Navy at age 17,” said his proud brother. He had been discharged off the USS Arizona in July 1941, only five months before the attack on Pearl Harbor when the USS Arizona was bombed by Japan, killing 1,177 officers and crewmen. “He was called back in as a chief petty officer and went back to serve on the USS Saratoga aircraft carrier,” added Mike.

John Cravatta was the next brother to be called into the war. According to Mike, John was in the Army stationed in Texas. He was a boxer in the Army and did not make it overseas because his eardrum was ruptured in a boxing incident.

Mike Cravatta explained how the boxing accident might have been a blessing in disguise. “I was on the LST 680 and we were picking up troops. One troop noticed my last name and asked if I knew John. The soldier relayed the story of how John had to be left behind because of his injury.

The LST 680 delivered these troops as assigned. “We left them off at the island. They got off and then we backed the ship out. I found out later that 75 percent of them were killed there,” said Cravatta.

The third son, Dave Cravatta, had the distinction of being a paratrooper during World War II. He was one of the paratroopers involved in the Invasion of Normandy.

Sam Cravatta was a Navy radioman on a PT boat in the South Pacific.

“He was under the command of President Kennedy before (Kennedy) was president,” said Mike, who came close to actually getting to see this brother during the war. Their boats were in the same South Pacific gulf at one time but they missed seeing each other by hours.

“As for me, I can’t say I wasn’t scared,” said Mike, who manned a 20-milllimeter gun, aiming it at enemy aircraft. Sailors were killed on the ship and Mike Cravatta vividly remembers bodies being buried in the sea.

“We had to go, and we had to fight. It was a must deal to save our country,” he said.

Mike safely returned to Savanna following his time in the service. “Of course the first thing I had to do was get a job, and I did,” he said.

Mike’s parents were both from Italy and met in Chicago. The couple moved to Savanna so Mike’s father could take a job on the railroad.

“That is what I wanted to do before I left for the war. I always wanted to work on the railroad like my father,” he said.

The veteran took a position with the Milwaukee Railroad and worked there for 45 years, with his final position being clerical supervisor.

Mike Cravatta, who has many extended family members still in the Savanna area, moved to Lake Ozark, Mo., following his retirement and later moved to Oswego, where he now resides.

He keeps very busy with his hobbies. He goes bowling three days per week, serves as photographer for the church and the bowling alley, is part of the Resurrection Mass choir at his church as well as an usher and can occasionally be found singing some karaoke around town.

“Being in the service, it did change my life to a certain extent. I was older and wiser for having been there,” said the 87-year-old veteran who will proudly commemorate Veteran’s Day this Sunday.

His surviving siblings, Sam and John, will also likely have a Veteran’s Day filled with memories of the time when they proudly served their country.
