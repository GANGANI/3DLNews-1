A 10-year tribute: Flyin'' Illini - A turn for the worse

  • By The News-Gazette
  • Nov 4, 1998
  • Nov 4, 1998
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Print
  • Save

He was in town at the time, but Larry Smith couldn't make it to Lou Henson's big retirement bash at the Assembly Hall two years ago.

They only let convicted drug dealers out so often at the Urbana Work Release Center.

"When you're in the penal system, you have no say," said Smith, who caught the ceremony on TV in his room. "I saw Kenny (Battle) was there. Steve (Bardo) made it."

Oh, how Smith wanted to be there, to say so long to his old coach, get one last applause from the fans and talk with ex-teammates about good times.

There haven't been many of them for Smith since he turned in his Illinois basketball uniform seven years ago.

He's 30, with a prison record and a low-paying job doing something back near his hometown of Alton.

He says he does "grind work" for American Steel in Granite City, but the company has no record of his employment.

He's a good semester and a half away from getting his college degree, and doesn't have the cash or the time to go back and get it.

He has five kids – ages 8, 7, 6, 5 and 5 – from three women, and no ring on his finger.

"You read that story in Sports Illustrated?" Smith said of the piece on '90s athletes and all their paternity problems. "That was me."

High hopes

He was supposed to be making millions after college – not license plates.

"I'm surprised Larry's not in the NBA right now," said former Flyin' Illini teammate Kendall Gill, who is. "Because if he was there right now, he'd be an All-Star. He had freaky talent, man."

Ask any of the recruits he came in with in the fall of 1986 – Bardo, Gill, Nick Anderson, Jeff Finke, Phil Kunz, Ervin Small – and they'll all tell you the same thing.

Smith had more tools than True Value, more talent than any of them.

"Larry had talent coming out of his eyes, ears, nose and everywhere else," former teammate Lowell Hamilton said.

When he came out of Alton High in '86, he was one of the state's most highly touted guards since Isiah Thomas.

"Could be the most exciting Illini to set foot in the Assembly Hall in years," read his freshman bio in the media guide. "Will remind fans of former Illinois great Derek Harper."

He'd averaged close to 25 points a game. He made highlight-film passes. He shared News-Gazette Player of the Year honors with Anderson.

"Larry Smith was the best high school guard I've ever seen," former Illinois assistant Dick Nagy said. "And I don't just say that because I recruited him. He had size, he had strength, he was an unbelievable passer. He just overpowered very good guards in high school. I mean, made 'em look like babies."

Give him the drive of Gill or Bardo or Battle, and he could have been a special someone.

That's all he was missing.

"Larry would let me beat him in practice," said P.J. Bowman, a backup guard from Centennial. "And when I say 'let me,' I beat him and I took pride in the fact that I beat him, but I always would ask myself afterward, 'Am I supposed to be beating Larry Smith 1-on-1 consistently?' "

No way, P.J.

"Larry was as talented as anybody we had, but he wasn't willing to put the time in he needed to be great," former assistant Jimmy Collins said. "He was always satisfied with being just average. Had he really put in the time, he could have gone as far as any of them."

Here comes trouble

You didn't have to be an aspiring psychiatrist like Bowman to see trouble ahead.

The warning signs were all over, starting with the off-court company Smith kept.

"The people he chose to befriend were people who jumped on his back for the ride, and he let 'em ride," Collins said. "I think he should have shook some of those people off him. He had some people that influenced him the wrong way."

His troubles made headlines in the summer of '88, as he was about to enter his junior year and the Flyin' Illini were preparing for their Final Four run.

After Smith's involvement in two scuffles outside local bars, Henson announced he was suspended, saying then, "We won't put up with this sort of thing."

"We were concerned because we looked at him as a person that could really make or break the team," Bowman said. "We all knew it was important for Larry Smith to play well."

Smith's punishment was a seat on the bench for the Marathon Oil exhibition. He was reinstated for the opener against UIC and stayed out of trouble until the following year, when he was declared academically ineligible.

He was an important piece to the Final Four puzzle, leading the Illini in assists, starting nine games and helping out a whole bunch when Gill broke his foot in January.

Hamilton loved playing with the guy. He knew if the two of them were in the game at the same time, Smith would get him a dunk.

"I made the game easy for them," Smith said. "All of 'em. If they were in the game with me, they knew I'd throw them a pass that would be in the perfect spot for them to just jump up and get it and slam it."

Smith wishes every year would have been like that Final Four year. It was run and gun, slam and jam – a change from the past.

"I like to play more up-tempo, and Lou's system was more efficiency, don't turn the ball over," Smith said. "I had to acclimate my game to the system. I felt like the system should have been adjusted to me."

He wasn't around for the 1989-90 season, but returned for for one final fling in the fall of '90 after getting his academics in order.

The Illini were happy to have him. He was named team co-MVP and a second-team All-Big Ten choice after averaging 13.6 points, 5.8 assists and 5.0 rebounds.

"He did a lot of things people didn't think he could do when he came back," former teammate Tom Michael said.

'Oh Larry. Oh Larry'

When Smith's Illini days ended, he didn't have a juicy NBA contract awaiting him like Anderson, Battle and Gill.

He didn't have a degree like Bardo.

He didn't have a dream like Bowman.

He was the 100th pick in the CBA draft, with no diploma, one daughter and more little ones on the way.

"Money. I needed money, man," Smith said. "I think I tended to drift to something that could come easy, like basketball did. I wanted to take the shortcut, make easy money."

So Smith says he started selling drugs in Alton, site of his greatest achievements. He says he did it for a year and a half, maybe two, before he paid the price.

His first arrest, in January 1994, was for unlawful possession of cocaine with intent to deliver. After being released on bond, Smith failed to show up for his jury trial in September and a warrant was placed for his arrest.

Smith had been running from the authorities for nine months when he was busted the second time, in June 1995.

"I sold to an informant who was a crack addict. The police were watching the activity the whole time," Smith said.

The charges: unlawful possession of a controlled substance, unlawful delivery of a controlled substance and unlawful possession of a controlled substance with intent to deliver.

The plea: guilty on all three counts.

The sentence: four years in prison.

The headline in the next day's Alton Telegraph: "Oh Larry, Oh Larry" – Collins' reaction to the news.

He went from No. 23 to No. B29044.

"I went from something really good to something really bad," Smith said.

He only ended up spending seventh months behind bars – three in the Madison County Jail while waiting for his case, four at the Taylorville Correctional Center – before being let off for work release duty in Urbana.

While in town, he did salad prep for 10 months at the Hideaway Restaurant & Lounge, where he was a model employee.

"He always had a smile on his face, even going through what he was going through," Hideaway owner Bobby Slade said. "I mean, that would have to knock anybody down, I would think."

Not the fun-loving Smith, who cracks more smiles more than Miss America.

"He's an awful nice kid," said Stan McAfoos, his high school coach. "I really did like Larry. But he was too nice. He was easily led and got into some problems."

Smith's been a free man for two years, and promises he's put his problems behind him.

"I learned my lesson," he said. "I'm working. I don't make much money, but it's work."

He's talked to Nagy about getting into coaching, but that takes a college degree. Nagy's tried to convince him to go back to Illinois and finishing what he started, but Smith says he's too broke and too busy.

"He just doesn't think like you need to think to be successful in this day and age," Nagy said.
