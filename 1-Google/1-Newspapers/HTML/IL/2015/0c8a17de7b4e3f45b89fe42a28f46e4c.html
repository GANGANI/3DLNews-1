Law Firm Leaders

Schopf & Weiss chairman enjoys his independence

By John Flynn Rooney Law Bulletin staff writer
Posted February 3, 2015 2:36 PM
  • As a CDLB subscriber you have access to Lawyerport News, including access to 30 years of news archives. Click here to find news in your practice area and gain insights into lawyers, judges and the legal community.

Five years ago, when Steven A. Weiss hit the ball off the tee of a par-3 150-yard hole, he hoped to see it land on the green. But a sand trap obscured his view.

When he got to the green, he didn’t see his ball. After a brief search, and against all hope, he walked to the cup and looked down.

There it was.

Despite being a frequent golfer, it has been his only hole-in-one.

But there was a problem.

Weiss was playing alone that early summer morning at Olympia Fields Country Club. No one else was nearby.

U.S. Golf Association rules recommend that a hole-in-one be attested to by another person.

Weiss went to the clubhouse after his round and told others about his accomplishment. That was met mostly by congratulations and a fair amount of “it doesn’t count,” he said.

He saved the golf ball as a keepsake.

“I’m waiting for the next one,” he said.

Weiss has served as chairman of Schopf & Weiss LLP, a litigation boutique, since 1990. It opened three years earlier with four lawyers and now has 17.

Some competitor firms, including Ungaretti & Harris, have merged with larger firms. As of Sunday, Ungaretti & Harris LLP, with more than 100 lawyers, merged with Nixon, Peabody LLP, which has more than 600 lawyers working in 16 offices, including one in Chicago.

Schopf & Weiss has no plans to go that route.

“We have been very successful on our own and enjoy our independence,” Weiss said.

The firm has both a managing partner, Jason M. Rosenthal, and a non-lawyer, Michael T. Hoffman, who serves as executive director. Those two run Schopf & Weiss’ day-to-day operations.

Weiss’ role is to provide strategy and vision. He’s involved in decisions about adding lateral partners and about whether to focus more on a specific practice area.

The firm’s main practice areas are business litigation, intellectual property, insurance recovery and antitrust litigation.

Schopf & Weiss lawyers frequently represent clients in cases involving trade secrets.

“One of the things we’re doing now is looking at how to expand our practice in that area,” Weiss said.

Last month, Weiss represented an employer in a case alleging two former high-level executives left a company and misappropriated trade secrets for their new business.

The trial lasted two weeks in Ohio state court. The case settled just before closing arguments; Weiss declined to identify his client or the settlement amount.

The firm’s clients include Exelon Corp., the Art Institute of Chicago, Sears Brands LLC and Redflex Traffic Systems.

The average hourly billing rates for Schopf & Weiss’ 10 partners are between $400 and $600. Its lawyers also offer alternative-fee arrangements.

Weiss spends about a quarter of his time on firm management issues and the remainder on client matters, including trials.

Brett A. August, a partner at Pattishall, McAuliffe, Newbury, Hilliard & Geraldson LLP, opposed Weiss in a trademark infringement matter about 20 years ago. August represented Gateway Inc., a computer manufacturer, and Weiss represented a small information technology sector company that was accused of infringing on Gateway’s trademark.

The matter settled quickly, inexpensively and involved Weiss’ client having a reasonable amount of time to transition to a new brand, August said.

He called Weiss affable, easy to work with and pragmatic.

“It was clear that he just wanted the best business solution for his client,” August said.

The best part of Weiss’ job as chairman involves deciding on strategies, seeing them through and watching them succeed.

For example, while the firm handled IP litigation, it did not have a licensed patent lawyer and had to team up with outside lawyers with that designation.

“In 2013, we hired a patent lawyer who made it much easier for us to handle patent litigation,” Weiss said referring to partner David L. DeBruin.

Paula E. Litt, another Schopf & Weiss founding partner, called Weiss smart, decisive, likeable and inclusive.

“He’s done a great job of making sure everyone has a sense of ownership from the receptionist to the partners,” Litt said. “He treats people as individuals and with respect.”

Weiss uses current technology options to assist clients in reaching their goals. Now, most of the cases the firm handles involve electronic discovery.

In the past, a case could have involved millions of pages of documents, which lawyers and firm litigation support staff members would review.

“Now with the new technology, you can do searches for your documents to identify those that are privileged, those that are responsive and those that are important to the case,” Weiss said. “It certainly saves a lot of money and time and is often more accurate.”

One of the firm’s main marketing initiatives is an annual program called Movies on Trial. After the screening of the movie, a panel discussion is held. Attendees can receive Continuing Legal Education credits.

Movies screened at the Music Box Theatre in recent years as part of the series include “To Kill a Mockingbird” and “A Civil Action.”

Weiss was raised in Shaker Heights, Ohio. He received his undergraduate degree at Cornell University and law degree at the University of Michigan.

During those years, he worked as a bartender and a house painter.

“The house painting was more profitable, but the bartending was more fun,” Weiss said.

After becoming an Illinois lawyer in 1980, Weiss moved to Chicago and joined Reuben & Proctor, which later merged with Isham, Lincoln & Beale.

Weiss left that firm in 1987 and became a founding member of Schopf & Weiss.

In August, Weiss will become chairman of the 55,000-member American Bar Association Section of Litigation.

“One of the areas I plan to focus on is the relationship between in-house counsel and outside lawyers at firms,” Weiss said. “That would include things like offering alternative-fee arrangements and having those two types of lawyers work on cases together.

“In other words, making the relationship more valuable to the client.”
