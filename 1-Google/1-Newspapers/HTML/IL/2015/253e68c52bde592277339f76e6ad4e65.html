At spot of life-changing injury, Elrod’s life of service honored

By Lauraann Wood Law Bulletin staff writer
Posted June 11, 2015 3:20 PM
  • As a CDLB subscriber you have access to Lawyerport News, including access to 30 years of news archives. Click here to find news in your practice area and gain insights into lawyers, judges and the legal community.

Almost 46 years ago, hundreds of demonstrators gathered on Chicago’s streets to protest the trial of the “Chicago Seven” in the wake of the 1968 Democratic National Convention.

During the “Days of Rage” in October 1969, the demonstrators smashed storefront windows and damaged cars in the Loop area.

It was far less volatile on Wednesday, though, as family, friends and former colleagues crowded a corner of Dearborn and Madison streets to dedicate the intersection to Richard J. Elrod — a former legislator, Cook County sheriff and Cook County circuit judge who was severely injured during the disturbance.

It’s on that block where Elrod, then chief city prosecutor, broke his neck while tackling a protester who tried to escape police, leaving him paralyzed from the neck down.

Elrod died in April 2014.

When his wife, Marilyn Elrod, learned the city wanted to dedicate the corner to her late husband, she didn’t think the location could be any more appropriate.

“I said to my son, ‘How fitting that that’s where it would be, because that’s where his life changed and his life began,’” she said.

Those who knew Elrod professionally or personally spent the afternoon reflecting on a man whose resilience propelled him through a rewarding career as sheriff from 1970 to 1986 and as a circuit judge from 1988 until 2014.

Despite his injury, Marilyn said, Richard never considered himself disabled. Even when he was preparing to transfer to a rehabilitation center after his injury, she said, he wouldn’t let physicians tell him what he likely couldn’t do.

“He told the doctors, ‘Why are you telling me this? You don’t know what I’m going to be able to do. I’m just starting rehab; I’m gonna prove you wrong,’” she said.

And prove them wrong he did.

Through rehab therapy, Elrod regained limited use of his legs and arms to the point where he could move around with canes and later a scooter when long distances became too difficult.

His family said he never missed a day of work unless he was really sick and unable to make it, and he always looked forward to leaving home at 7:30 a.m. for a long day of court at the Daley Center.

Steven M. Elrod, Richard’s son and a partner at Holland & Knight LLP, said court reporters even started to request reassignments from his courtroom because they knew it meant they would be working late.

However, Steven said, his father still always found time for family.

“We went on family vacations, he attended every one of our events and every one of our children’s events,” he said. “He never missed a thing.”

Daniel J. Elrod, one of Richard’s grandchildren and an associate at Katten, Muchin, Rosenman LLP, said his grandfather’s injury never affected the way he taught his grandchildren about morals, the law and public service.

He said he was very proud when he learned the city would dedicate a prominent intersection to the man who taught him to give back when possible and work hard at all times.

“As a Chicago native, you grow up walking around the city and you see all these street signs of famous names — some you recognize and some you don’t,” he said. “You don’t really realize what they have to do to earn that honor.”

Supreme Court Justice Anne M. Burke was one of several state and federal judges who attended Wednesday’s dedication. She and her husband, 14th Ward Ald. Edward M. Burke, were “young couples who grew up” with Richard and Marilyn.

The justice said it was bittersweet to learn of the dedication because of what happened to him as well as the fact that he couldn’t witness it with everyone else.

“But on the other hand, it’s so wonderful to have a man to do what he did in his everyday life (and) be courageous,” she said.

“It just encourages all of us and (lifts) ourselves up when we feel bad about ourselves and say, ‘Wait a minute. Stop. Get a life here. Look who really had a life, and look what he did with it.’”
