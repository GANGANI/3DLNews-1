    Fugitive La Shish owner charged in fraud scheme

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
    • Click to print (Opens in new window)
    By Barbara Ziemba, Press Release and Press and Guide
    PUBLISHED: October 6, 2007 at 10:00 p.m. | UPDATED: June 17, 2021 at 11:36 a.m.

    DEARBORN

    The fugitive owner of a well-known metropolitan Detroit eatery has been indicted by a federal grand jury with conspiracy to commit extortion and fraud.

    The indictment, unsealed Tuesday, alleges Talil Khalil Chahine, 52, owner of La Shish restaurants, conspired to extort money from former employees of the La Shish restaurant. Also indicted is Roy Bailey, 54, of Romulus, a top Detroit immigration official with Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) who allegedly helped pull off the scheme.

    Chahine, along with Mohammad Bazzi, 61, of Detroit, and Mohamad Arzouni, 59, of Dearborn Heights, were charged with conspiring to defraud the federal government by obtaining immigration benefits, including permanent resident status, by arranging false marriages with other La Shish employees who were U.S. citizens.

    Bazzi is related to Chahine and worked at La Shish, the indictment says.

    Another well-known area restaurateur, Samir Leon, owner of the Leon’s Family Dining chain, was named in the indictment but not charged.

    In all, nine charges were filed against Bailey for misusing his position as the assistant district director with ICE and its predecessor, the Immigration and Naturalization Service (INS), by accepting large sums of money and other gifts in return for granting immigration benefits. The alleged bribery also involved the release of several people who were in federal custody, including 39-year-old Antonio Ivezaj of Milford, who also was indicted.

    The latest charges against Chahine are not related to criminal tax evasion charges that were brought against him last year. Since that time, federal officials believe he fled to Lebanon and still lives there.

    In a press release, U.S. Attorney Stephen Murphy described the charges as “very serious.”

    “Corruption on the order and degree alleged in today’s grand jury indictment is very serious, because it amounts to the selling of one’s office,” he said. “While this matter appears to be an isolated incident, prosecution will nevertheless be pursued with the utmost and thorough-going professionalism because no one is above the law.”

    If found guilty of the charges, Chahine could be sentenced to up to 25 years in prison; Bazzy and Arzouni would face up to five years in prison, a spokeswoman for the U.S. Attorney’s Office said Thursday.

    The 17-page indictment says Arzouni and Bazzi entered into false marriages to evade U.S. immigration laws. In January 2000, Arzouni and Bazzi filed for permanent resident status based on their fraudulent marriages.

    Two months later, Bailey gave false information to an INS officer that caused Arzouni’s application to be approved.

    The indictment doesn’t say if Bailey did the same for Bazzi.

    While Leon wasn’t charged, federal officials said Bailey took cash, casino chips, alcohol, clothing, jewelry and “hundreds of free meals” from him, who was identified as “Samir L.” in the indictment.

    Bailey also allowed an associate of Leon’s to enter the U.S. from Syria based on a fraudulent non-immigrant visa application, according to the indictment.

    It’s also alleged that Bailey took a gun from Leon to Windsor, then returned it to Leon.

    The spokeswoman declined to say why Leon was not charged along with the others.

    As of Friday, Robert Forrest, Chahine’s attorney representing him in the tax evasion case, had not returned a telephone call. It wasn’t known if he’s still representing him.

    David Lee, Arzouni’s attorney, disputed the charges, describing his client’s marriage as “valid.

    “Mr. Arzouni has been in this country legally for a number of years; this was a valid marriage and we intend to defend the case,” he said. “He didn’t conspire with anyone to defraud the country or anyone else and we hope that a jury will come to that same conclusion.”

    Bazzi did not appear in court to answer the charges and it was unclear if he had an attorney representing him.

    A fire last month at Leon’s restaurant on Michigan Avenue in Dearborn temporarily closed the facility; no one answered the phone there last week. An attempt to contact him through the Taylor restaurant was unsuccessful.
