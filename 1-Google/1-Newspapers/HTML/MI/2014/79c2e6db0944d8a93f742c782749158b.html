Celebrating Black History: IRA "DEADEYE" WILLIAMS - The Business of Entertainment

Ira Williams
Princella Tobias�
The sounds of the African-American giants of blues, jazz and soul -- reshaped the world of music. Their sound stirred the core of our soul; their words told stories - our stories of blues and jubilee; of being down-and-out and of deliverance; of a love that moved you -- from your fingers, to your toes, and deep into your heart.

Today, we know these African-American musicians as legends of their time. And, rightfully so. But, back then, they were not allowed in all places; simply because of the color of their skin.
So, when we enjoy their great sounds, let us think about the people behind the scenes -- the ones who gave these great musicians a venue to entertain us back in the day. People like Ira �Deadeye� Williams and others provided a venue for them to entertain, African-American style, with soul and swagger.

Williams owned the East End Bar & Grill in Benton Harbor, allowing B.B. King, Al Green and others to give us that �feel good� sound.
Not all of the musicians pictured below played at the East End, but their music and songs entertained us for decades - thanks to our hometown legend, Ira �Deadeye� Williams.

By Ralph Heibutzki
Spirit Correspondent

There's always a dividing line in any local music scene between the people playing onstage, and those who work behind the scene � who often get little or no recognition themselves, but create the framework that makes the shows happen. Ira Williams, known locally as �Deadeye,� is one of those people.

From 1955 through 1990, Williams ran the East End Bar & Grill, which gave African-Americans a place to go � at a time when they couldn't enter most other clubs or feel comfortable in the ones that did. For years, Williams and Arlene (�Mrs. Smokey�) Sisson, owner and operator of Mrs. Smokey's Lounge � were the driving forces in Benton Harbor's live local club scene.

However, Williams stresses that he never saw Miss Smokey as a competitor. �Well, I was in the bar business first � and later on, her husband (Willie) got into the bar business. We never had any problems,� Williams said. �I had my crowd, she had her crowd. We both had a bar, and we both had food. There were enough people for both of us, so people went to both places.�

When he started, Williams inherited a converted small soda fountain space that held 30 people, at the most; when he stopped, he left a successful 400-seat night spot. �The thing that really stands out is how I did it with no education,� Williams said, laughing. �I finished the third grade � I knew how to deal with people, but I didn't have book smarts. There weren�t too many black people that were highly educated, back then. But I did all right.�

�Why Don't You Buy The Bar?�

Williams was 20 when he left Greenville, MS in 1941 � where he could count himself lucky if he earned a dollar for a lengthy day of hard labor. �I was out in the fields, picking cotton for 50 cents an hour � it was rough,� he recalled. However, Williams's mother had already moved to Benton Harbor, so joining her there seemed like the next logical step.

That same year, however, Williams got drafted into the U.S. Army, where he saw plenty of action in France and Germany with General George S. Patton's forces. After World War II concluded in 1945, Williams returned to Benton Harbor and began working at Whirlpool's earlier incarnation, The Nineteen Hundred Corporation. He spent nine years there in all, mostly in the machine shop. (The company adopted its current name in 1949.)

Like many fellow ex-servicemen, Williams was focusing on modest goals � in this case, putting money away for his first house. By 1955, he'd saved up about $6,500, when he stopped at his lawyer's office � and heard a proposal from the secretary that changed his life forever. �Her husband had this little bar out there, on Main and Pine (streets),� Williams said. �Her husband was running it with another guy, and they couldn't get along � so she says, 'Why you don't buy the bar?' I said, 'I'm saving that money to buy a house.'�
Not ready to take no for an answer, the secretary stepped her offer up a notch. �She said, 'I'll tell you what � bring that $6,500 down to the office. I'll put it in a savings escrow (account), and you can take over the bar, start running it, and I'll change it over into your name.' So that's how I got into it,� Williams said, laughing.

�I Never Had Any Problems...�

However, Williams wasn't a newcomer to the live music business � having taken lessons from his mother and stepfather, who were already running the Townline Bar, on Townline Road. �They were in that (business) with another couple of people, so I used to go out there, and help them out on weekends,� Williams said. �I always was a person that could get along with people, so I never had any problems with people.�

The story behind Williams's nickname reflects the relaxed, casual approach that he cultivated with people � particularly those of the opposite sex, as he explains. �I had a friend, Sylvester Lane � him and I ran around together all the time,� Williams said, laughing. �He said, 'You're just like Deadeye Dick' � he was a huntsman, like Daniel Boone. Everything he shot at, he got.�

Blues guitarist B.B. King, Memphis soul legend Al Green and Junior Walker (of �Shotgun� fame) stand out as the biggest names who played at the East End Bar & Grill. �B.B. played for the door � I think he made $800 or $900 that night, close to the '80s,� Williams said. �Al Green was there before B.B. � he had two brothers singing with him. He used to live in Grand Rapids, so him and his brothers used to come down there (East End Bar) and sing. He eventually went back to Memphis, and that's when he became (famous) by himself. Junior Walker was the house band there (at the bar) for about seven or eight years.�

�If People Like You,
You Can Make It In Anything�
The good times and the good vibes lasted for five decades, but by 1990, Williams decided to exit the bar business � citing factors beyond his control that made it impossible to continue in a positive manner, he feels. �Things got rough after the middle of the '80s, when the drugs hit the streets. Things started getting bad in the bar,� Williams said. �My father asked me to get out.� So Williams sold his bar � which closed for good, several years later � and moved on as the manager of a different establishment (D&R), on Fair Avenue, where he stayed on from 1990 through 1995.

As the decade continued, Williams got involved in several other ventures, as well � including the D&R Skating Rink, and the Liberty Theatre � but when he looks back, the social and musical contributions that he made through the East End Bar & Grill tend to stick with him the longest. Asked for the secret of his success, Williams responds: �How to deal with people. If people like you, you can make it in anything you get into. Every year, I'd do something different inside, redecorating and painting. When I first opened the place, it didn't hold more than 20 or 30 people � (to go) from that, to 400 (people), that's quite a jump.�

BACK IN THE DAY:
MEMORIES OF THE EAST END BAR & GRILL

Editor�s Note: No local entrepreneur lasts for long without the support of his customers, and Ira �Deadeye� Williams is no exception. In researching this story, however, we thought it equally important to get additional perspective from those customers, friends and supporters who fondly recall the venue that provides so many pleasurable memories to this day. Their statements follow below.

Kenny Harper

He is a wonderful person and a great businessman. He and my dad were good buddies back in the day. He kept his place open for entertainment for the area. I can remember years ago when Muhammad Ali drove his Rolls Royce to the East End Bar. He looked around and said, �Give everybody a drink; they look like they�re thirsty�. People could come from all over to the East End Bar and Grill. The East End and Smokey�s Lounge were the two places where people, especially black folks, would be entertained.
Not only did he bring in the best musicians, like Willie Clayton, B.B. King and others, he also brought in behind-the-scene celebrities � the DJs � into town, like Tom Joyner, LaDonna Tittle, Ray Cooper, Robin Stone, Mel Devine, Doug Banks, and others.

There are several things that make Deadeye standout. He would always invest back into his business and kept the place nice. We couldn�t wait to see what was new at his club. I remember the double-mirror towers. We all felt like celebrities walking into his place.

Another thing I loved about him is that he made sure everybody made money. He gave our group, The Sportsmen Club, that consisted of myself, (James) Whitelow, Donnie Smith, Ray Shurn, Rollie Titts, Freddie Greer, Tony Richardson, T.H. (Holton), an opportunity to use part of his club to also entertain. We would host Halloween parties, Valentine dances, you name it � to the thanks of Deadeye.

To me, he was �the man� and he always supported others. That is the one thing that sticks out about him the most.

James Whitelow

He was good for the community back in those days. He provided a milestone for the area. He was featured in one of the magazines for being one of the great entrepreneurs in the area. He is a friend of mine and I really appreciate him.

Shunn Harper

It was a pleasure having one of the great pioneers of the bar business to take time out of his busy schedule to come down and school me on the bar business several years ago when I ran Crossroads Restaurant & Entertaiment. He broke it all the way down to how many shots per bottle. He gave me a priceless knowledge. He would also come in, after a golf game, and follow up to see how I was doing. He taught me things you don�t learn in a classroom. He gave me hands-on-101 knowledge.

Archie Davis

He use to come down to the G&W Skating Rink and get down on me about my weight. He would, �Big Daddy, you doing all right? You got to trim down that weight.� He cared. He was always a nice person. He showed me a lot � mentoring me. He expressed how to get into business. How to hang in there. He did so much for our community. During that era, we did not have a lot of black businessmen we could look up to. He was the first that I admired. He brought name acts in to his business, like Junior Walker & the All Stars. He had a variety of artists that came through and enjoyed his place and admired him for what he did in the community. He deserves a standing ovation in our community. He was wise and he provided jobs. People will never forget the East End Bar.

Wilce Cooke,
former Mayor of Benton Harbor

They say music is the universal language of mankind. Ira, my friend �Deadeye�, kept the music scene hopping in Benton Harbor years ago. He was one of only a few places you could go and hear great soul music.

He was also a great businessman and leader in our community. He was on several boards and tried to help people in Benton Harbor. We all should be forever grateful for his service.
