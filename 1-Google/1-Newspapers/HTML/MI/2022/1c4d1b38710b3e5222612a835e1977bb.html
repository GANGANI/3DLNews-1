    Remember 2022: Ten most-read stories of year by News-Herald readers

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
    • Click to print (Opens in new window)
    By Brian Johnston | bjohnston@medianewsgroup.com | Press and Guide, Jason Alley | jalley@medianewsgroup.com | The Oakland Press, Jackie Martin | jmartin@medianewsgroup.com | News-Herald and Jim Kasuba | News-Herald
    December 30, 2022 at 6:30 a.m.

    In a Downriver news year filled with many ups and downs, The News-Herald’s most-read story of 2022 centered around the joy found by a Kroger cashier in distress.

    A number of other “positive” stories also gained readers’ attention, including the return of the infamous Holiday Train and teens who came to the rescue.

    According to online analytics, these stories were read the most in 2022:

    Kroger cashier in distress helped by quick-acting customer in Southgate store

    A woman shopping at Kroger in Southgate was given a first-hand reminder that there are still a lot of good Samaritans willing to rush to someone’s aid at the spur of the moment.

    In March, Nicole Hursey experienced that kind of help from a stranger.

    It not only left a lasting impression on her, but it also did so with readers, who made it the most-read story of the year.

    Hursey was working in the checkout lane when she began to feel ill.

    She suffers from hypoglycemia, a condition in which someone’s blood sugar level gets lower than normal.

    She recognized the symptoms and before she knew it, she was lightheaded and started shaking.

    According to Hursey, things began to get a little fuzzy and just when she thought she was going to lose consciousness, a customer came to her aid.

    She remembers a customer who had already been through the line coming back and asking her if she was alright.

    “Everything started getting really bright and she noticed me shaking,” Hursey said.

    By this time, Hursey was starting to lose her ability to speak.

    Someone asked her if she needed something and she managed to say the words, “orange juice.”

    The customer ran to the back of the store and got some orange juice for her.

    “She was a very sweet woman,” Hursey said. “Without her, I think I would have passed out. My body would have shut down.”

    A few minutes after drinking the orange juice, Hursey, who is not a diabetic, said she was beginning to feel better.

    “I’ve learned my lesson not to do that again,” Hursey said.

    The customer left the store without Hursey getting her name, so she posted a thank you message to her on social media and it went viral.

    Teen who jumped out of moving car in Southgate has died

    A young life was lost last spring when a 14-year-old girl jumped out of a moving car in Southgate and died as a result of her injuries.

    The incident occurred at around 7:30 p.m. April 17, on Fort Street, near Pennsylvania Road.

    Police said they believe that a mother and daughter were arguing when things suddenly took a tragic turn.

    They reported that a 33-year-old Monroe woman was driving north on Fort Street, with her 9- and 14-year-old daughters as passengers, when an argument broke out between the driver and her older daughter.

    “The 14-year-old jumped out of the moving vehicle and sustained injuries,” Public Safety Director Joseph Marsh said.

    During the police investigation, officers arrested the driver for suspicion of operating under the influence.

    The victim was identified as Angelina Jones.

    Chelsea Jones, a relative of the victim, set up a GoFundMe account the proceeds of which were meant to help the family’s financial hardship by assisting with medical and funeral costs.

    “She lit up every room she walked into with her beautiful blue eyes, huge smile and glow,” Chelsea Jones said on the GoFundMe page. “Angelina will truly be missed and has been a blessing to many, especially her family.”

    As for the driver, Marsh said this case has been handed over to the Wayne County Prosecutor's Office to review for possible charges.

    Holiday Train to pass through Downriver after three-year hiatus

    After a long absence, the Canadian Pacific Holiday Train was greeted with much fanfare when it passed through the Downriver area on the night of Dec. 1  — more than most people expected.

    When word got out in early November that the Holiday Train would be returning to the rails this season on its first cross-continent tour in three years, area residents greeted the news with jubilation. Social media was abuzz with people who said they didn’t want to miss it.

    The purpose of the train is to raise money, food and awareness for local food banks in communities across the CP network.

    The CP Holiday Train program actually has two trains, one that travels exclusively in Canada and another that makes stops in both Canada and the United States.

    The latter train had a 30-minute show scheduled for 6 p.m. on the night of Dec. 1 in Windsor, Ontario, after which it entered the United States via Detroit.

    The train had no scheduled stops in Michigan, as its next show after Windsor was in Illinois. However, that didn’t stop Michigan residents from gathering near train tracks along the route to welcome the lit-up, festive Holiday Train as it passed through.

    But because the passing of the train is not an official event  — people simply gather at various locations along the track on their own  — It’s never known exactly how many people will show up.

    One of the biggest gathering places in the Downriver area is a vacant lot next to Thunderbowl Lanes in Allen Park. That proved to be the case again this year. It was such a popular gathering spot that police had to close off the parking lot by late afternoon, as it was filled to capacity.

    Allen Park police estimated that up to 2,000 people gathered in the city to see the train, parking wherever they could find a spot.

    Unfortunately, many of those people were gone by the time the train made its appearance, at around 11:15 p.m. A majority of the people who gathered to see the train had children with them who were waiting in freezing temperatures for hours, so by 10 p.m. the crowd thinned out considerably.

    However, when the train did finally arrive, it pleased those who remained to see it, as it not only slowed down, but came to a complete stop.

    “The train stopped in front of us for several minutes so the conductor could line the switch, which dazzled the crowd,” said Conan Brooks, a loyal train watcher who in past years held food drives at the Allen Park location that coincided with the train’s passing.

    The train then drove through Taylor and Romulus, where there were a number of small Holiday Train watch parties at various locations along the tracks, but the only official one was in Romulus.

    Mike Laskaska, director of Community Services for Romulus, said the city normally holds its Tree Lighting on the first Friday of December. However, this year city officials moved it up by one day, to coincide with the arrival of the Holiday Train.

    After seeing this response, Laskaska said Romulus officials definitely are considering growing the Holiday Train viewing experience in the future.

    Mom stranded in Southgate Kroger lot praises teen employees who helped her

    Readers appreciated good Samaritan stories this year. In May, a second one occurred once again at the Kroger in Southgate.

    A Lincoln Park woman ended up getting a lot more than she bargained for on a late-night Kroger run.

    Teresa Robertson parked in the store lot before going in to grab a couple of items.

    “I was trying to hurry to get the stuff,” Robertson said. “I got there just as the store was closing. When I came out I was like, ‘Oh no, my tire is flat.’ Then, I realized I didn’t have my cellphone.”

    Robertson said she knew she couldn’t go back inside.

    She was alone and vulnerable with a flat tire on her 2005 Mercury Sable — and no way to call for help.

    As she tried to figure out what to do, she noticed a teenager in the parking lot with two other teens collecting baskets.

    The teenager, who she only knew as Gabe, was more than willing to help.

    Not only was he willing to stop what he was doing to help her, so were two other employees working outside at the time. She only knew them by their first names, as well —Israel and Jacob.

    Kroger would not release the names of the teenagers or clear the way for them to be interviewed at the time.

    The boys changed her tire and within a few minutes, she was up and running on all wheels.

    “I am so grateful for what they did and I’m in awe of their kindness,” Robertson said. “You just don’t see that a lot anymore. I have three boys of my own and I can only hope they would do the same thing.

    Another man drove up while the tire was being changed and offered his help.

    Robertson told him the kids had it handled, but the man stuck around and used his car lights to offer better lighting so they could see what they were doing.

    She said she doesn’t know what she would have done without their help.

    “What an impact they had on me,” she said. “They say it takes a village and all of them proved that.

    North Terminal at Metro Airport getting a name change after 14 years

    What’s in a name?

    The answer, in this case, is a major name change at Wayne County Detroit Metropolitan Airport.

    On April 4, what was known as the North Terminal at the airport was renamed the Warren Cleage Evans Terminal in honor of the Wayne County executive.

    It became significant news for the region, as well as those in the industry.

    The North Terminal was simply named after its location on the Romulus airport campus.

    Visitors to the airport began seeing the changes in signage within a few weeks of the announcement throughout the airport, as well as on the roadways.

    Evans expressed his excitement over the decision for the terminal to take on his name.

    “This is truly an honor, and I would like to thank everyone who made this possible,” Evans said. “I have dedicated my life to public service, and I have always believed that you don’t get into this line of work for the awards or recognition. Public service is about serving the public. But, I must say that it does feel good to know that others appreciate what I have been trying to do for all these years.”

    Wayne County Community College launches tuition-free skilled trades program

    The Wayne County Community College District launched its Rocket2Work initiative, which provides tuition-free training for skilled trades programs, in 2022.

    The program is planned to help connect students with jobs that have a minimum starting wage of $15 an hour.

    Programs included in the Rocket2Work initiative involve careers in CDL-licensed professional truck driving, skilled construction, advanced manufacturing and mechatronics, and technology and technical careers, and more. All programs included in the initiative are in high demand with thousands of open positions across the region.

    Mother of teenager killed in two-car crash in Woodhaven struggling to ‘process it all’  

    There was heartache and shock throughout the Downriver area and beyond when a car crash claimed the life of a Lincoln Park teenager.

    Jasmine Alhachami, 18, was killed in a crash at the busy intersection of West and Allen roads in Woodhaven.

    The driver of a black 2020 GMC Terrain sustained minor injuries.

    There were four occupants, including Alhachami, in the other vehicle, a black 2000 Lexus.

    Police noted that there were occupants with serious injuries in that car.

    Shortly after the occupants were transported to a hospital for treatment, Woodhaven police were informed that Alhachami had died.

    Alhachami’s mother, Frances Marchwinski, spoke about her family’s loss and the struggle she was having coming to grips with it all.

    She said losing a child isn’t something parents think about or are prepared for when it happens.

    “She loved soccer and track,” Marchwinski said. “She loved to dance and she loved music. Jasmine was an outgoing and a people person. She was never shy.”

    Alhachami was a big fan of English singer-songwriter Ed Sheeran and was looking forward to getting a prom dress and attending the senior affair.

    At the time, Marchwinski said she was told her daughter and friends were on the way to McDonald’s.

    “It’s just really hard to process all of this,” Marchwinski said.

    Shortly after the crash, the mother was asked what was helping her get through the day. She said, “Nothing.”

    Staff at Anderson High School was notified of Alhachami’s death and grief counselors were in place for students and staff.

    She was a 4.0 student.

    A memorial was put in place near the crash site as a tribute to the teenager’s life.

    Pastor accused of running his multimillion-dollar Taylor church as a ‘cult’

    Most stories lose readers’ interest over time, but the story on Joshua Media Ministries International in Taylor has managed to remain one of the paper’s most-read stories for the fourth consecutive year.

    Published in April 2019, the story of the Rev. David E. Taylor and his church detailed allegations of misconduct, financial misdeeds and more, creating a cloud of controversy over the church.

    Those familiar with the ministry described the multimillion-dollar church as a cult.

    To some members, JMMI represented a path to the kingdom of God and Taylor a full-fledged prophet.

    Former members, on the other hand, had a different perspective, calling the church organization “a slave labor cult” operating on an unholy trinity of intimidation, manipulation and greed.

    One former member made accusations of physical assaults and sleep deprivation against members.

    Taylor also was accused of preying on women more than praying for them.

    “He does not honor women,” said Vicki Yohe, a former member, when questioned last year. “Women are just a vagina.”

    She also said he repeatedly had sex with women in his ministry.

    Police had been called to the church numerous times and Taylor Police Chief John Blair said the department was monitoring the situation.

    New Boston man arrested after allegedly running over, killing Wyandotte woman

    On the night of Nov. 22, a domestic violence dispute turned deadly in Wyandotte, resulting in the death of a 26-year-old woman.

    The incident occurred at approximately 7:30 p.m. that evening in the area of First Street and Poplar.  When police arrived, they found the victim lying in the street.

    Police said she suffered extensive injuries and later was pronounced dead as a result of those injuries.

    Deputy Police Chief Archie Hamilton said a domestic dispute occurred between the victim and the suspect, Troy Szydlowski.

    “During the argument, the suspect ran over the victim with a car, resulting in her death,” Hamilton said. “The man was placed under arrest at the scene.”

    Szydlowski, a 35-year-old New Boston resident, was arraigned on Nov. 25 and charged with homicide-manslaughter, a 15-year felony. He was given a $100,000/10% bond.

    The suspect’s next court date is set for Jan. 5. A probable cause conference will take place before 27th District Judge Elizabeth DiSanto in Wyandotte.

    Lives forever changed after death of motorcyclist at Taylor intersection

    The death of a motorcyclist at a busy Taylor intersection shocked and saddened the many people who witnessed a crash and its aftermath on the afternoon of Sept. 11.

    The fatality occurred at approximately 4 p.m. at the intersection of Pardee and Eureka Road, instantly killing Michael Behnke, a 32-year-old Romulus resident.

    According to Taylor police, witnesses reported that the motorcycle, which was going west on Eureka, was traveling at a high rate of speed when it ran a red light and was struck by a vehicle making a turn.

    Craig Finney of Southgate was one of the many people who witnessed the immediate aftermath of the crash and said he was forever changed by what he saw.

    “Multiple lives were changed today,” he said in a Facebook post following the incident. “Prayers for the gentleman’s family and the woman he hit and all the witnesses.”

    Behnke’s friends set up a GoFundMe fundraiser that set a goal of raising $15,000 to help pay for his funeral and burial costs, as well as for expenses related to his two young children. That goal was easily surpassed by the many generous donations that poured in.

    The 2008 graduate of Lincoln Park High School played baseball and football during his high school years. He went on to earn his associate's degree from Eastern Michigan University and was an assistant supervisor for JP Morgan.

    His obituary stated that he died “doing what he loved — riding his motorcycle.” His other enjoyments included playing pool, going to the casino, as well as cheering for the Detroit Tigers and Lions. But above all, he was remembered as a dedicated father of a 5-year-old son and 3-year-old daughter.

    Billy Shillingburg, a Taylor police officer who investigated the crash, said the Police Department submitted the case to the Wayne County Prosecutor’s Office for review, something that is required after any fatal accident. As of mid-December, the prosecutor had not made a charging decision.

    The Police Department’s recommendation is that no one be charged in this case.
