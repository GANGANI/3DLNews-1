Local Lives - Hollis Rockwell III

  • By Isabelle Pasciolla Herald-Journal Intern

    John Cavanagh

    • Author email
  • Aug 23, 2022
  • Aug 23, 2022 Updated Nov 30, 2022

Fair week in Oceana County brings fun and excitement to many families, but especially for Hollis Rockwell III. Rockwell has lived in Oceana County his whole life, attending Ferry Elementary and graduating from Hart High School in 1995. The county fair has been a part of his life since he was born.

“My sisters had steers so I’ve been going to the fair since I was born basically,” he said.

Rockwell showed his first animal when he was 5 years old and continued showing until he aged out. He started with dairy calves as children under 10 cannot show steers, but once he was old enough he made the switch.

After graduating from high school, Rockwell attended Ferris State University for a while before transferring to Muskegon Community College. While in community college he got a job working for the post office and decided to leave school altogether.

He worked at the post office until 2013 when he quit to become a full-time farmer. In 2016 he got a job at Hart Public Schools as the groundskeeper, a position he retains today.

“I take care of all the grass and the athletic fields,” he said. “It’s kind of like having a second farming job.”

One of Rockwell’s most memorable 4-H experiences was in 2016 when he and his wife, Jill, received the MFB 4-H Excellence in Agriculture award. This award, “recognizes outstanding achievements of Michigan 4-H volunteers or groups that have exhibited excellence in 4-H youth education and leadership development in 4-H agriculture areas.”

Rockwell said he doesn’t know who nominated them for the award, but that it was a great experience.

“I don’t know the details of how all that came about, but I mean the kids are first, and we do everything we can to help the kids out,” he said. “That’s why we’re there.”

Hollis and Jill have four children who are all involved with the fair. Their oldest, Nicole, is 20 years old and in her third year studying pre-veterinary medicine at Ferris State University. She has aged out, but did show all the way through from 5-19.

Their second child, MaKayla is a senior at Hart High School this year. Then there is Hollis IV who is a freshman and Kolton who is in seventh grade at Hart Middle School.

“And if you asked my wife, she’d say she has a fifth child and that would be me,” Rockwell joked.

Hollis is a family name passed down from Rockwell’s grandfather. He isn’t sure where the name really came from or how the tradition started, but he always knew growing up he would name his son, Hollis IV.

“It’s kind of funny, I always talk to my boy about it and he says ‘yup, I’m going to name my son Hollis,’” Rockwell said. “So we’ll see maybe, hopefully there’s a fifth someday.”

Both of the girls enjoyed 4-H very much and even saved some of the money they earned through the program to invest in their own small herd of steer. The boys each bring their own steer as well.

“A couple years ago we had four steers at the fair, so that was a busy time for me and my wife,” he said.

When it comes to his oldest’s showing career, Rockwell said one particular achievement really stands out to him. When Nicole was a junior in high school she won overall grand champion in showmanship.

Grand champion market animals are awarded based on the animal and how it will do in the market. Showmanship awards are given based on “how well the kid shows the animal,” Rockwell said.

“That’s the topnotch of what you can get as far as showmanship at our fair, so that was a pretty high moment for us because that shows a lot of time and effort,” Rockwell said.

Besides this accomplishment, the family also has plenty of memorable experiences with the steers, particularly when they misbehave.

“There’s just so many stories over the years of dealing with steers that have gotten away and stuff like that,” he said. “It always adds excitement.”

Rockwell has other duties that he attends to while his children show at the fair. In 2011 he was made the Beef Barn superintendent, which comes with a multitude of responsibilities.

After he aged out of showing, he said there was a “gap” in which he had no kids to be in 4-H, but he still wanted to be involved.

“I had some nieces and nephews that showed…so I just kind of helped out wherever I could,” he said.

For a few years he helped the previous barn superintendent with paperwork and such on show day. The man was older and eventually retired, so the job was given to Rockwell.

“It works out pretty good because I’m there with my kids anyways,” he said.

As barn superintendent Rockwell is in charge of organizing the beef shows, putting a program together, making show day run smoothly and dealing with any issues that arise in the barn. When it comes to sale day, he is also in charge of the sale order.

He enjoys his position at the fair mainly because it’s for the kids and ensures they have a good experience.

“Like any other job, there’s always times when it gets a little hectic or tough, but definitely the positives outweigh the negatives,” he said.

Rockwell is really proud of the kids at the fair for the amount of time and the level of commitment they put into raising these animals. It’s not just one week, but a whole year that the kids have to work with the animals to train and feed and learn.

“Fair week is just the result of all the time and effort that the kids spend with these projects, so in today’s world I think that’s a huge benefit to teach these kids that hard work does pay off,” he said.

Of course, Rockwell and others get excited about the fair for many reasons. Some adults will take a week off of work just to sit in the barns and hang out with old friends and family members.

“The fair for us is just one big family reunion,” he said. “We love that week.”

Rockwell believes that the animal barns are “a community of their own” as everyone bonds over their common commitments. He said this is great for kids as it broadens their social circles. Instead of just having their school friends, they also have their friends from the fair and 4-H.

“It’s a nice community of people who get together and have fun and teach the kids some things along the way,” he said.

Everyone always gets excited for the fair, but this year has brought its own level of enthusiasm. After 2020, Rockwell said many people learned not to take opportunities like the fair for granted.

“When the rides start showing up and we’re driving by you start to sense a little bit of excitement in the family,” he said.

John Cavanagh
