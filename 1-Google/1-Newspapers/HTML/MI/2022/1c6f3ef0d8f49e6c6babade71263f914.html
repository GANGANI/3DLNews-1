    Michiganders owe $40 billion in student loan debt, but optimistic about forgiveness proposal

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
    • Click to print (Opens in new window)
    By Mark Cavitt | The Oakland Press
    September 11, 2022 at 5:00 a.m.

    April Boucké will leave college with around $60,000 in student loan debt.

    Boucké, a Waterford resident and first generation college student, graduated from Central Michigan University in 2013 with bachelor’s degree in anthropology, music, and museum studies. In 2019, she enrolled in Eastern Michigan University’s music therapy program and plans to graduate with another bachelor’s degree in 2023.

    She is one of over 1.3 million Michiganders who hold around $50 billion in student loan debt, according to the latest data from the U.S. Department of Education. Nationwide, over 40 million Americans carry $1.4 trillion in student loan debt, with around 6 million of those borrowers on some kind of income-based or income-contingent repayment plan.

    She currently wears many hats working part-time to provide music experiences for special needs children, a church, giving private music lessons, and working at a local music store in Waterford.

    “I began to make $500 monthly payments on my undergraduate loans right away,” she said. “As soon as I graduate from EMU and become a board certified music therapist I will look for a job in the field. Sometimes, music therapy jobs are housed within nonprofits, which can qualify for loan forgiveness.”

    Several weeks ago, the federal government announced a plan to pay off $10,000 to $20,000 in student loans for borrowers at certain income levels. A pause on student loan payments and interest, originally put in place at the height of the COVID-19 pandemic and renewed several times, was also extended for a final time through December.

    Boucké, whose loans are currently in deferment because she’s enrolled in classes, plans to apply for that debt relief, viewing it as a targeted stimulus plan.

    “Being a tad closer to not having my student loan payments sounds lovely,” she said. “I have worked hard, made my monthly payments, contributed to society, and have paid my taxes just as I should. This time, I am the one being helped and I’m very grateful.”

    According to that federal data, through March, over 512,000 federal student aid (FAFSA) applications had been processed by Michigan colleges and universities during the 2022-2023 application cycle, including 21,283 at Central Michigan University (CMU) and 17,820 at Oakland University (OU).

    As of April, Michigan colleges and universities had dispersed $1.7 billion in student loans for the 2021-2022 award year, including $102.6 million at CMU and $80.5 million at OU.

    Loans Forgiven

    Under the new plan announced by President Joe Biden, many Americans can have up to $10,000 of debt forgiven if their loan is held by the U.S. Department of Education and the borrower makes less than $125,000 individually or $250,000 as a family.

    That amount increases to $20,000 if they received Pell grants, typically given to the most economically disadvantaged student borrowers.

    For a current borrower and a dependent student, you will be eligible for relief based on your parents’ income, rather than your own.

    Over half of U.S. student loan borrowers owe less than $20,000 on their student loans. In Michigan, over 700,000, or 53.8% of the state’s student loan borrowers owe $20,000 or less.

    Betsy Mayotte, founder and president of The Institute of Student Loan Advisors, a nonprofit that provides student loan borrowers with free advice, said the proposals are a thoughtful start in addressing the student loan debt crisis.

    “It will address the most vulnerable borrowers, but almost more importantly, it has started a robust conversation on the issue that will hopefully lead to more impactful and long-term change,” she said.

    Michelle Pierce, a West Bloomfield resident and local childcare worker, graduated from Oakland Community College and Oakland University. She left both schools with around $40,000 in student loan debt.

    Over 6% of her annual income goes towards paying off her student loans.

    “I have around $9,800 left on my student loans,” she said. “I absolutely feel the pressure of student loan debt. It’s a huge cloud hanging over anyone’s head, but inflation does add another level of stress while having to provide for my family.”

    Katherine Michelmore, associate professor of public policy at the University of Michigan, said this proposal is significant because student loan debt touches a lot of different facets of the economy.

    “Making monthly payments on student loans means there’s less money to spend on other things,” she said. “That’s money not circulating in the economy. Forgiving $10,000 to $20,000 of student debt is huge. This policy could wipe out the debt of millions of borrowers today.”

    It’s expected that sometime in October student loan borrowers will be able to apply for relief online. Borrowers have been directed to a website, https://studentaid.gov/debt-relief-announcement/, to sign-up for updates on the application process. Borrowers have until Dec. 31, to apply, according to the federal education department.

    Those who paid off their student loans can seek refunds on loan payments made since March 2020, when the pandemic began and student loan payments and interest were paused.

    Waterford resident Caitlyn Copenhagen graduated Eastern Michigan University last year and has around $27,000 in student loan debt. She currently works for an educational recruiting company that helps to staff schools with therapists and teachers.

    Each year, over 13% of her income goes towards her student loan debt.

    “I’ll probably end up paying $400 per month for the next 10 years,” she said. “It’s extremely difficult. I’m living paycheck to paycheck. I might have to take another job to just afford living. Student loans are a large share of my paycheck.”

    She plans to apply for the $20,000 in Pell grant relief, having received those grants during her final three years at EMU.

    Tuition Costs

    Critics of the student loan forgiveness program say that while the debt relief is important it does not address the underlying reason that higher education has become so expensive and unaffordable for many.

    Mayotte, of the student loan institute, said despite all the amazing initiatives to help address the student loan crisis, we still have a crisis.

    “Student loans are the symptom,” she said. “The problem is the cost of higher education. We need state governments to re-invest in higher education which would lower the cost of public institutions.”

    Institutions say growing tuition reflects the continued lack of funding from state governments and the increasing personnel and operational costs required to provide relevant and quality educational experiences, resources and faculty for their students.

    In fiscal year 2023, which begins on Oct. 1, Michigan colleges and universities will see an increase in state funding with just over $2 billion going to universities and $530.3 million going to community colleges. Those represent 11.9% and  22.9% increases respectively from the previous year.

    Officials at colleges and universities say it’s a balancing act of trying to limit tuition increases year-to-year as they confront rising costs while still trying to maintain a high-quality educational experience.

    Michelmore, the U-M professor, said at the root of the rising costs is the erosion of public financial support for these institutions.

    “Over the last several years, state funding to public colleges and universities has gone way down, which means that public institutions have had to find other ways to fund education, such as admitting more out-of-state and international students who pay more to attend those schools, and by raising tuition.”

    In Michigan, colleges and universities cannot raise tuition and fees for undergraduate students higher than 4.2%, or $590, whichever is greater. Institutions that exceed that tuition cap are not eligible to receive state funding to support capital improvement projects.

    Lindsay Trevethan, a Waterford resident who owes $13,000 in student loans, graduated from Grand Valley State University in 2013 with majors in history and art history.  Around 14% of her annual income goes towards paying off her student loans.

    Trevethan, who currently works as an administrator at a real estate office, said colleges and universities should figure out where costs can be cut in order to help people better afford their education without having to take out thousands in loans.

    “I understand professors have to be paid and room and board is costly everywhere, but there has to be something done to help people not go into massive debt for the sake of a higher education.”

    Jennifer DeHaemers, vice president of Student Recruitment and Retention at Central Michigan University, said the school has taken steps to reduce costs so that tuition can remain steady.

    One of those changes has been a reduction in grounds crew staff and finding ways to decrease waste on campus, including in the dining halls, by implementing more environmentally-sustainable practices and policies across campus. But those are one-time savings and don’t address the root of the problem.

    “The public has to invest more in higher education if tuition costs are going to stay level or decrease,” said DeHaemers. “Over the past three years, since I’ve been here, we have kept our tuition increases pretty minimal. Every year, our leadership sits down to look at the budget and see if raising tuition is an absolute necessity or if (cutting services) is something we could do for the benefits of our students.”

    Dawn Aubry, associate vice president for Enrollment Management for Oakland University, said the university tries to keep costs low but acknowledges that student loan debt is a problem.

    “We really try to look hard at our expenses every year and we try very much to look at ways to save money and not raise tuition,” she said. “That is definitely not our goal (to raise tuition) every year.”

    Like CMU, Aubry said OU is trying to save money on things that don’t affect student instruction, such as  choosing recyclable materials and more energy efficient LED lighting. The university has cut back on its paper usage and has instead, like many others, opted for more virtual services.
