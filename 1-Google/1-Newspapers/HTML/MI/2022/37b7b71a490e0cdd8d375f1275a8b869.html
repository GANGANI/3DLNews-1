Celebrating Black History Month: Aspiring Inventors’ Solutions Changing How World Sees Water

About one-third of the world’s population, or an estimated 2.2 billion, lacks access to safe water.
Ralph Heibutzki, Spirit Correspondent•
Water is the world’s most basic resource. It’s one that gives life, as an essential part of the human body, but one that also takes it, when people lose access to it. About one-third of the world’s population, or an estimated 2.2 billion, lacks access to safe water. That figure includes 884 million people, who can’t find safe water to drink, and 785 million, who lack access to basic water services, according to figures from the World Health Organization.

These types of statistics have prompted advocates to urge the recognition of water, and access to it, as a basic human right. The Joint Monitoring Program – a project of the WHO, and UNICEF – defines that access as being able to gather safe water in a round trip of 30 minutes or less, including the time it takes to wait in line.

However, those ideas have only gained ground during a global pandemic that’s highlighted the struggle over resources, including water – such as in Pakistan, for example, where 94 percent of its wealthiest inhabitants have access to soap and water for hand washing, compared to only 17 percent of its poorest ones.

Drinking contaminated water often leads to illnesses and death, especially in young children. Spending too much time accessing water can sharply reduce quality by life, by interrupting childrens’ education, and keeping adult parents away from work. At the same time, these challenges have prompted various aspiring engineers, inventors and scientists to develop creative ways of trying to solve them, which are summarized briefly below.

Kiara Nirghin, Drought Buster

For Stanford University senior Kiara Nirghin, food and water security go hand in hand. In 2013, she watched her country of South Africa battling its worst drought in 30 years, one that led to widespread devastation of farmland, deaths of cattle, and constant struggles to find fresh water. Nirghin was just 13 at the time, “but I knew that the solutions being put in place were not groundbreaking,” she said. “So I started looking at the problem and saw how I could break it down into something that I could solve.”

Eventually, after trying out various materials, Nirghin developed a unique mixture of avocado skins and orange peels that can hold large amounts of water. These superabsorbent polymers (SAPs) are common in the agricultural industry, but are expensive to make, and contain many harmful chemicals. Nirghin’s substance releases water slowly, over longer periods of time – allowing farmers to keep crops hydrated, even in dry spells. “It also increases food security because, in theory, farmers can make it themselves,” she said.

Nirghin’s discovery has earned her numerous honors, including the Google Science Prize (2016), and earned places on TIME magazine’s list of 30 Most Influential Teens, and Glamour’s College Women of the Year. She’s also spoken at the United Nations about her work, and written about it in a book, Youth Revolution (2019). “I have definitely seen the potential technology can have in innovation and that’s my main driving force. I want to innovate and solve problems and create products that help people. That’s what I see myself doing,” she said.

Doll Avant: Testing The Waters

Knowledge is power, which is the philosophy behind Doll Avant’s business, Aquagenuity, a company that aims to tell customers how their drinking water stacks up against national standards. Avant formed it n June 2017, just two weeks after her father passed away from kidney issues.

She had already been researching the Flint water crisis, as CEO of Water Transit Solutions, when the discovery of high arsenic levels in local drinking water prompted to start thinking about links between racism, and how it affects environmental policy. Though there wasn’t a clear link to her father’s death, “I wondered: How many people are exposed to chemicals because they’re in their water?” Avant said.

Avant created the AquaScore, an algorithm that analyzes local water quality, based on state and federal data. Once it’s fed the data, the algorithm creates an AquaScore of zero to 100. Customers can use it to see how their supply stacks up against U.S. Environmental Protection Agency limits. Avant hopes that the AquaScore becomes a widespread measurement of water quality, so residents can check how their community’s doing, before it has to face a public health crisis.

“After Flint, people were marching in the streets and treating it like a political problem,” Avant said. “But I’m a data scientist. I saw this as a data problem, as well as a problem of inequity and class. The people who needed information about what was in that water couldn’t get it.”
