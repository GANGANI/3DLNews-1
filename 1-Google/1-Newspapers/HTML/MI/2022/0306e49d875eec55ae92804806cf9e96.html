“It's Deeply Moving”: After 130 Years, Civil Rights Pioneer Homer Plessy Receives Historic Pardon

Louisana Governor John Bel Edwards signs off on the historic pardon of Homer Plessy, joined by descendants of Plessy, Judge John Ferguson, U.S. Supreme Court Justice John Harlan, and numerous academics, civil rights leaders, and elected officials.
Ralph Heibutzki, Spirit Correspondent•
Civil rights pioneer Homer Plessy – whose refusal to leave a whites-only railroad car set the legal precedent for segregation in America – has received a posthumous pardon from the state that denied him full equality 130 years ago. Louisana Governor John Bel Edwards signed off on the historic pardon last week, joined by descendants of Plessy, as well as Judge John Ferguson, U.S. Supreme Court Justice John Harlan, and numerous academics, civil rights leaders, and elected officials.

History would have taken a far different turn if the Supreme Court hadn't upheld Plessy's conviction for violating the Separate Car Act in 1896, as Davis noted, in his pre-signing remarks.

“The fictitious notion of 'separate but equal' remained with us until the United States Supreme Court revisited the issue in 1954 in the context of public education, and implicitly overruled Plessy,” Davis said. “Mr. Plessy’s conviction should never have happened. But, there is no expiration on justice. No matter is ever settled until it is settled right. It is with great joy that today I pardon Homer Plessy and settle this matter. We still have a long way to go when it comes to equality and justice, but this pardon is certainly a step in the right direction.”

Edwards's actions followed a recommendation from the state's Board of Pardons to grant the pardon. The board's authority stemmed from the Avery C. Alexander Act, a Louisiana state law that specifically allows the governor to pardon anyone convicted of violating a state law or local ordinance created to enforce racial discrimination or separation, according to CNN.

“There is no doubt that he was guilty of that act on that date,” Jason Williams, the Orleans Parish district attorney, told the board at its hearing, held last fall. “But there is equally no doubt that such an act should have never been a crime in this country.”

The signing ceremony took place near the site of Plessy's arrest in 1892, which sparked four years of legal battles – started with a trial in which Ferguson, the presiding judge, found him guilty, and fined him $25. The landmark case is known as Plessy v. Ferguson for that reason.

Phoebe Ferguson, the judge's great-great-great granddaughter, acknowledged the pain of a decision that forced Plessy to essentially live his life as a convicted criminal, in the eyes of the courts who first heard his case, and rejected his appeal. “We cannot undo the wrongs of the past, but we can and should acknowledge them and learn from them,” she said.

Plessy Prepares To Fight
The legal confrontations that followed Plessy's arrest were expected, because a citizen's group from New Orleans, the Comite des Citoyens (Committee of Citizens), wanted to challenge the Separate Car Act. Plessy agreed to help test the law, with support from the Eastern Louisiana Railroad, which saw building separate cars for blacks and whites as costly and burdensome, according to oyez.com.

Though Plessy, a shoemaker, was seven-eighths white, he still counted as African-American under state law. On June 7, 1892, Plessy bought a first-class ticket to board an Eastern Louisana Railroad train, and took his seat in the whites-only car. He was arrested after refusing to move into the segregated car.

In the resulting trial, Ferguson rejected Plessy's argument that the arrest violated his Thirteenth and Fourteenth Amendment rights to equal protection under the law. Ferguson had found the Separate Car Act unconstitutional in several other cases, “on trains that traveled through several states,” he said. In Plessy's case, however, Ferguson ruled that Louisiana – and the railroad – could regulate train travel within state boundaries. On that reasoning, he upheld Louisiana's law, and Plessy's conviction.

Plessy promptly appealed to the Louisiana Supreme Court, whose justices proved equally unreceptive. In its decision, the court cited a language from a Pennsylvania ruling that upheld the use of segregated railroad cars: “To assert separateness is not to declare inferiority... It is simply to say that following the order of Divine Providence, human authority ought not to compel these widely separated races to intermix.”


“Separate But Equal” Takes Hold
The Louisana justices' refusal to set aside the law or Plessy's conviction set the stage for Plessy's final appeal, to the U.S. Supreme Court, which heard oral arguments in his case on April 13, 1896. Just over a month later, on May 18, 1896, the nation's highest court ruled against Plessy, 7-1, with only Harlan dissenting.

Writing for the majority, Justice Henry Billings Brown asserted that the Thirteenth Amendment didn't apply in Plessy's case, because it only ensured African-Americans the basic level of equality needed to abolish slavery. As for Plessy's Fourteenth Amendment claim, the court found that, while it was meant to guarantee legal equality for all races in America, it was not intended to prevent discrimination.

The justices also decided that segregation was legal, so long as whites and nonwhites had equal access to the same facilities or opportunities. “We consider the underlying fallacy of the plaintiff's argument to consist in the assumption that the enforced separation of the two races stamps the colored race with a badge of inferiority,” the majority opinion stated. “If this be so, it is not by reason of anything found in the act, but solely because the colored race chooses to put that construction on it.”

The majority opinion drew a sharp rebuke from Harlan, “The Great Dissenter,” who wrote: "The arbitrary separation of citizens on the basis of race, while they are on a public highway, is a badge of servitude wholly inconsistent with the civil freedom and the equality before the law established by the Constitution. It cannot be justified upon any legal grounds."


After The Ruling
Having lost his case, Plessy returned to Ferguson's court, and pleaded guilty to violating the Separate Car Act. The Comite des Citoyens paid the $25 fine on his behalf, and disbanded shortly thereafter, though it voiced no regret for taking up his cause. “We, as freemen, still believe that we were right, and our cause is sacred,” the group said, in a brief statement after the 1896 ruling.

Plessy continued living in New Orleans, and remained active in civil rights causes. When shoemaking declined, as mass production grew, Plessy worked as a clerk, insurance company collection agent, laborer, and warehouse worker. He died somewhere in his sixties on March 1, 1925, because – like many African-Americans of his time – his birthdate remains unclear, nor are there any known photos of him.

However, the negative social effects of the Plessy decision soon became apparent. As historians have noted, Plessy was born a free person of color, in a society where African-American children attended integrated schools, nonwhite men could vote, and interracial marriage was legal – which had happened during the post-Civil War period, known as Reconstruction.

Plessy v. Ferguson wiped out most of these gains, and left “separate but equal” in place for decades as the standard in determining where racial minorities could live, work or attend school, and if they could vote. That standard remained in place until 1954, when the Supreme Court overturned it in its Brown v. Board of Education decision.

For those reasons, Ferguson and Keith Plessy, a cousin of Homer Plessy, ended up creating The Plessy & Ferguson Foundation – to educate and remind people about the impacts of the 1896 decision.

“I once met Rosa Parks, and as I kneeled before her and thanked her for all her hard work, she said to me, ‘Get up, boy! Your name is Plessy. You got work to do,’” Keith Plessy said. “I’ll admit I didn’t know what she meant then, but I’m sure now, and I’m convinced that she was talking about the work we’re doing today.”

For more information about the foundation, visit https://www.plessyandferguson.org/mission.
