    Crime, progress dominated Macomb County news in 2022

    Staff selects top 10 stories of the year

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to print (Opens in new window)
    By The Macomb Daily | macombdaily@medianewsgroup.com
    December 25, 2022 at 10:37 a.m.

    The resolution of a shocking case that ultimately landed a top law enforcer behind bars topped the list of Macomb County biggest news stories of 2022.

    Macomb Daily editors and reporters voted on a list of the county’s top news stories of the year and this was the result:

    1-Mr. Smith goes to … prison

    Two former top elected officials in Macomb County met their fate this year from the feds.

    One former Macomb County elected official went to prison and a second pleaded guilty to a crime and faces prison this year, to conclude two federal investigations into public corruption.

    Former prosecutor Eric Smith reported to Terre Haute Federal Corrections Institute in Indiana on June 30 to begin a 21-month sentence for obstruction of justice for trying to cover up his theft of $75,000 from his campaign fund over several years.

    Smith, 56, of Macomb Township, who held office for over 15 years, resigned in March 2020 days after Michigan Attorney General Dana Nessel charged him with 10 felony counts in a separate case of alleged racketeering and embezzlement for his alleged misuse of hundreds of thousands of public dollars.

    Unknown at the time was that federal officials had pressured him to resign immediately due to new, separate obstruction allegation, of which he was charged in September 2020 and to which he pleaded in January 2021.

    While Smith, the son of a former Clinton Township police chief, easily admitted wrongdoing in his federal case, he has maintained his innocence in his state case and is taking it to trial. That proceeding is scheduled for May 15 in front of visiting Macomb Circuit Judge Nanci Grant of Oakland County.

    In that case, three co-defendants pleaded guilty to misdemeanors after they were charged with felonies and have agreed to testify against Smith. The last of the three to plead was Derek Miller of Warren, Smith’s former chief of operations, who June 1 admitted guilt to “public official refusing or neglecting to account for county money.” Miller, the son of a former state senator, admitted he aided and abetted Smith in his alleged scheme by hiding bank account numbers from county officials and the auditor. He had been charged with misconduct by a public official and conspiracy to commit an illegal act in an illegal manner, both punishable by up to five years in prison.

    Smith’s former chief of staff and close friend, Ben Liston, and Mount Clemens business owner William Weber also pleaded. Weber also was charged this year with felony larceny in a separate incident.

    Meanwhile, as part of a separate, wider investigation, former public works commissioner Anthony Marrocco in September pleaded guilty to one count of attempted extortion in a deal with U.S. Attorneys in which he likely will be sentenced to no more than 16 months behind bars.

    Marrocco’s plea was the end of an eight-year federal investigation into public corruption centered in Macomb County that resulted in over two dozen convictions of mostly public officials, although there is some speculation he still could provide additional information to the FBI before he is sentenced Jan. 26.

    Marrocco, 74 at the time of the plea, of Ray Township, agreed he pressured a developer to purchase tickets to a campaign fundraiser event for each of the developer’s companies or county permits for his projects would be delayed or denied. The value of the tickets was less than $6,500.

    Marrocco was indicted in May 2020 on two counts of extortion, one count of conspiracy and one count of attempted extortion. The original charges included other accusations.

    Marrocco, a Democrat, won six four-year terms at the ballot box before Republican Candice Miller defeated him in 2016.

    The investigation resulted in convictions of Marrocco’s former top deputy, the late Dino Bucci, trash tycoon Chuck Rizzo, towing titan Gasper Fiore, former Clinton Township trustee Dean Reynolds, businessman Fazal Khan and Reynolds’ and Rizzo’s attorney Jay Schwartz, among others.

    2-Tough times for schools

    There once was a time when school closings were related to weather conditions that left everyone feeling jubilant at having the day off. This past year, however, it was a climate of hostility that forced superintendents to close schools across the Macomb Intermediate School District.

    School threats have always been a problem but they seemed to escalate after the Oxford High School shooting in 2021 and while one would’ve thought the pendulum would swing the other way after a summer of fun, the exact opposite seemed to be true.

    A week after most students and teachers returned to school, excited about a year that wasn’t interrupted by the pandemic, a threat on social media forced the closure of Warren Lincoln High School..

    A week later, a Roseville High School closed because of a threat left on a bathroom wall, and it’s been ongoing ever since. No school district or age has been immune to the gun, bomb, knife or disparaging threats. And with every closure students fell another day behind, making the losses in math and reading accrued during the pandemic even harder to recoup.

    It’s been a difficult year for superintendents, teachers and students.

    School life has changed.

    The good news is the changes are working toward something better – from the Macomb County Prosecutor’s Office creating a ‘Hero Award’ to curb to the Macomb County Executive and Board of Commissioners pledging to support a school school-safety expert that will continue developing and coordinating readiness and response plans to school violence incidents. This past year and likely in the months to come, we’ve also seen the addition of new school protocols, security guards, committees and task forces working to recreate a safe learning environment along with support mechanisms including more counselors to help those at risk for violence to themselves and others.

    3- Lakeside Center to replace iconic mall

    Almost every Macomb County resident over the age of 40 has a story about hanging out at Lakeside Mall in Sterling Heights back in the day.

    The property at Hall and Schoenherr roads was once home to sleepy farmlands before the expansive mall moved in and associated development mushroomed into one of the county’s driving economic engines beginning in the mid 1970s.

    Now developers say another transformation will take place in the next two years as the struggling shopping center launches a 12-year, $1 billion redevelopment that will alter the look of the M-59 Golden Corridor.

    Developers envision a livable, walkable community with retail, office, residential assisted living for seniors, restaurants, plenty of green space, and community buildings in a project to be known as Lakeside Town Center. It will be financed through bonds and tax incentive plans that will pay for the infrastructure and likely spark additional development around Hall Road.

    City officials call it the largest private mixed-use investment in Macomb County history.

    Mayor Michael Taylor said the mega-project will deliver much of the community’s wish list including thousands of jobs, more restaurants, entertainment, housing, and a complete new look.

    “City officials and residents have been waiting for this level of redevelopment of this property for years,” Mayor Michael Taylor said.

    Opened in 1976, the regional indoor mall contained more than 1.5 million square feet and two floors of retail space, a movie theater, food court, ice rink and video arcade.

    Over the past decade, retail shopping patterns shifted to more online purchasing and discount retailers assumed more market share causing big box anchors to downsize and/or close. The loss of big box anchors also compromised the viability of the smaller in-line stores in malls across the country and soon thereafter, hundreds of malls began closing.

    Lakeside Mall was once among the city’s top five employers with over 2,500 employees. Today it is estimated the mall has less than 500 workers.

    Enter Out of the Box Ventures, a retail subsidiary of Lionheart Capital, a Miami-based real estate development and capital investment firm.

    Investors created a plan to make massive changes not only to the mall, but property around it as well.

    Lakeside Town Center calls for demolition of some of the existing site and adding 2,800 residential units, 150,000 square feet of new retail space, covered, heated pedestrian shopping and new restaurants and coffee shops among other things.

    Construction is scheduled to start in late 2023 or early 2024.

    4- Election brings historic changes

    Redrawn boundaries for the 2022 election and changing demographics and attitudes led to two Black state lawmakers and a Black member of Congress representing Macomb County for the first time.

    Macomb voters also for the first time elected a Black judge, Teri Lynn Dennings, who had been appointed to the circuit court bench after the first Black judge, Sheila Miller, was defeated in her first electoral effort in 2010 following her appointment in 2006.

    The election saw Macomb somewhat buck the statewide trend of Democrats dominating at the ballot box. In addition to John James winning for Congress, other county wide and county district Republicans won or held on to their seats.

    James, a Black conservative Republican, defeated Democrat and former judge Carl Marlinga in an extremely close race for the 10th District, which includes all of the county south of Hall Road, parts north of Hall Road and two Oakland County towns. James of Farmington Hills, who has promised to move to the district but has refused to give a timeline for his move, is a company CEO and former helicopter pilot in the military.

    While that outcome may not have been a surprise, the election of Democrats Kimberly Edwards and Donavan McKinney for state representative seats may have raised the eyebrows of some political observers. It may have partly resulted from Macomb County state districts for the first time crossing Eight Mile Road into Detroit and other parts of Wayne County. An increasing minority population in Macomb also is creating more diverse electoral politics.

    Edwards, who lives in Eastpointe, upset incumbent Richard Steenland of Roseville in the Democratic primary in the 12th House District, which includes Eastpointe, Roseville and a portion of the east side of Detroit. Steenland won handily in Macomb but lost in Detroit to give Edwards the victory by only 287 votes. Edwards easily defeated Republican Diane Saber in November.

    Democrat Donavan McKinney, who worked as a legislative director in the Michigan Legislature and served on Gov. Gretchen Whitmer’s Environmental Justice Council, won in a landslide over Republican Wendy Jo Watters. He defeated Democrats Kristina Lodovisi and Aaron Delikta in the August primary.

    Dennings, who was appointed last December and took the bench in January, defeated Assistant Macomb Prosecutor Steven Fox by a somewhat surprising 2-1 margin despite Fox’s plethora of eye-catching signs.

    The political parties also created their own news in this gubernatorial election year, particularly a power struggle between two factions of the Macomb County Republican Party, one led by former President Trump backer Mark Forton and the other by  Eric Castiglia. Forton ultimately prevailed.

    Meanwhile, Macomb Democratic Chairman Ed Bruley stepped down after 20 years in the post amid some division in the party. Alysa Diebolt of Eastpointe, over 30 years younger than Bruley, who was an aide for former congressman David Bonior.

    5- Horrific crime scenes in Fraser, Chesterfield

    Macomb County has seen its share of horrific murders over the years. In September, two more were added to the list.

    In Fraser, a bizarre triple stabbing took place Sept. 13 when four teens – two males aged 17 and 14 and two girls aged 17 and 16 — entered a house near Fraser High School and attacked three of four teens in the home.

    Trent Redstrom, 14, one of the alleged intruders, was killed by the intended male victim, and the other alleged male attacker, Collin West, and another male in the home, suffered stab wounds.

    A teenage male resident of the home, a ninth-grader, was detained for possible criminal charges, but within days Macomb prosecutors determined he acted in self defense and defense of others when he fatally stabbed Redstrom and West.

    The mother of the boy whose murder charge was dropped said the attacking teens were “trying to kill our kids.” The woman said her son was able to fight off the attackers and ended up fatally stabbing Redstrom.

    Redstrom stumbled outside where he collapsed on the lawn as the group of hysterical teens gathered around him.

    Leon Gruzwalski, 39, the head of a local motorcycle club, was driving by when he saw the commotion and stopped to provide assistance to the dying youth.

    “I observed him breathing, gurgling a bunch of blood. Another gentleman came by and we flipped him over. I saw he had a neck wound, with a lot of blood coming from his neck… he died in my hands,” Gruzwalski said

    The three alleged intruders are being treated as adults in the justice system. West is charged with first-degree home invasion and attempted murder, while the two girls – Emma Morgan and Isabella Bridges – are charged with first-degree home invasion and assault and battery. They face a March probable-cause conference in front of Judge Alyia Hakim in 39th District Court in Roseville.

    The second high-profile murder took place Sept. 26 when a man killed respected WWJ-AM anchor Jim Matthews and assaulted Matthews’ 35-year-old girlfriend and two young children before attempting to kill himself.

    Prosecutors said Arthur Williamson, 54, of Pontiac, who was a friend of the girlfriend, used a hammer and knife to beat and stab Matthews in Matthew’s home on  Bayview Drive in Chesterfield Township.

    Williamson is charged with one count of first-degree premeditated murder, one count of felony murder, two counts of assault with intent to murder and three counts of unlawful imprisonment. He is awaiting trial.

    When police found him in the basement of the home, he was suffering from self-inflicted wounds and a crack cocaine overdose.

    According to police and prosecutors, Matthew’s girlfriend had invited Williamson over while he was at work on the overnight shift around 4 a.m.  Later, when told him it was getting late and her boyfriend would soon be home, Williamson allegedly “lost it,” records show.

    Matthews was attacked with the hammer and stabbed, prosecutors say.  Williamson also allegedly stabbed the girlfriend in the neck and tied to a chair and assaulted her 10-year-old son, who was found tied up in a closet with closed head trauma. The girlfriend was able to free herself when Williamson passed out.

    The girlfriend told police she and Williamson had talked about dating but were just friends. She said he had come over because it was his birthday weekend.

    Colleagues at WWJ described Matthews, 57, as the “consummate professional” who was dedicated to his industry.

    6-Federal lawsuit targets Eastpointe mayor

    From receiving a poverty exemption on her 2021 property taxes to applying for the city manager job in Harper Woods and stating her intention to remain as Eastpointe mayor if she was hired for that position, Eastpointe Mayor Monique Owens has been the subject of controversy since her election in 2019.

    In November, Eastpointe residents Mary Hall-Rayford, Karen Beltz, Karen Mouradjian, and Cynthia Federle filed a federal lawsuit against Owens alleging that she violated their First Amendment rights when she shouted at them and refused to let them speak at a Sept. 6 council meeting.

    Each attempted to speak during the public comment portion of the agenda but were cut off from speaking by Owens who indicated she would not allow any comments critical of her to be made.

    Less than 20 minutes into that meeting, the four other councilpersons left council chambers saying they would not stand by and watch residents’ freedom of speech be compromised.

    In December, U.S. Eastern District Judge Terrence G. Berg issued a preliminary injunction that states: members of the public may direct comments and express criticism or praise to and regarding public officials including but not limited to remarking about Eastpointe’s mayor and council, disputes between Eastpointe’s mayor and city council members, police matters, and other matters of public concern.

    The injunction is to remain in place during the course of the federal litigation.

     

    7- Road construction, infrastructure work EVERYWHERE

    No matter where you reside in Macomb County – from Armada to Warren – it seemed like traffic was constantly jammed due to road construction.

    Gratiot, Van Dyke, Harper, Hall Road, Quinn Road, I-94, New Haven Road, Schoenherr, Mound and others had detours posted as crews worked to repair or rehabilitate streets.

    Human nature being what it is, motorists grumble over existing potholes and bumps, and then complain while fixes are being made.

    According to the Michigan Department of Transportation, the two biggies – Gratiot Avenue from 11 Mile to 14 Mile roads and Hall Road (M-59) from Romeo Plank to I-94 – are now 80% complete.

    The Macomb County Department of Roads said another 82 lane miles of primary and local roads were reconstructed or resurfaced this year. Or, to put it another way, about 30 football fields of construction were completed.

    Meanwhile, a 9-mile stretch of one of the county’s worst roadways, Mound Road from 11 Mile to M-59 in Warren and Sterling Heights, construction entered its second year on the major hub for automotive, defense, aerospace and manufacturing industries.

    Improvements include the addition of an extra lane on both sides of Mound from 17 Mile to M-59, landscaping, updated traffic signals, and emerging mobility features.

    Road officials say 40% of the $217 million project is completed, with a completion date expected in 2024.

    Another obstacle tying up traffic was incessant work to add new water mains and other infrastructure improvements as Macomb County prepares for the future of transportation.

    8-Fouts fights with council continue

    Litigation between Warren Mayor James Fouts and City Council continued and escalated this year with six different cases being filed in Macomb County Circuit Court involving the two parties.

    Most recently, Fouts was named as the defendant in a civil lawsuit filed by the city council to keep Fouts and his administration from interfering with council president Patrick Green’s ability to serve in that capacity. Former councilperson at-large candidate Gary Boike is challenging Green’s tenure based on term limit language in the city charter.

    Other 2022 litigation between the two parties includes a dispute over who has the final say on the annual city budget and a disagreement over who is responsible for drawing city council districts. Fouts lost the case regarding the budget, but appealed the lower court’s decision and the case is currently pending in the Michigan State Court of Appeals. A settlement regarding council districts is expected soon.

    The two sides have been embattled since Nov. 2019 when seven new city council members were elected. The city budget has gone to litigation the past three years.

    9-Putin’s war ripples felt across the world

    What was Russian President Vladimir Putin thinking when he sent up to 200,000 troops into Ukraine on Feb. 24, 2022?

    A quick victory?

    That he would sweep into the capital Kyiv in a matter of days and depose the government?

    One thing for sure, he underestimated the impact that such an invasion would have on people around the world including 1 million people with Ukrainian ancestry living in the United States and more than 36,000 in Michigan who rallied together in support of their homeland. Macomb County’s Ukrainian population, estimated at around 6,000, joined in the efforts and soon coverage of the war not only included what was happening there but here as well.

    Churches have held candlelight vigils and residents took up sky blue and yellow flags to show their support and raise awareness. Organizations like the Ukrainian National Women’s League of America not only raised money for the defense of Ukraine but partnered with other organizations like the Ukrainian-American Crisis Response Committee of Michigan, a grassroots coalition comprised of representatives from the Ukrainian Cultural Center, Immaculate Conception Ukrainian Church and School, St. Mary’s Orthodox Church and the Ukrainian Selfreliance Michigan Credit Union became a force collecting and shipping military donates to Ukraine and serving as a base for media inquiries and the needs created by the more than 100,000 Ukrainian refugees welcomed by the state. Even the students at Immaculate Conception Ukrainian Catholic School in Warren, an elementary school nestled in a neighborhood populated by many residents who immigrated to the U.S. from Ukraine and still have family there – showed their support by making flags to raise awareness about what was happening. Now many of their classmates are refugees embraced by their school, its faculty and church.

    Putin likely never imagined this to be a local story played out in schools, government offices and national organizations. Nor did he figure Ukrainians who left the country years ago would return to fight in the people’s army or that others like Samaritas director be moved to travel to Ukraine to help distribute the funds and humanitarian aid collected by residents of southeast Michigan.

    Since Russia’s invasion in February, nearly 40,000 civilians have been killed and nearly 5.2 million Ukrainians have fled to countries along Ukraine’s western border including; 2.9 million in Poland, 510,000 in Hungary and close to 900,000 in Romania.

    While many will remain in communities like Warren, which have embraced them, others are hoping the war will end so they can return to Ukraine.

    10 (tie)-‘Auditors’ spar with Utica cop

    Detective Sgt. Greg Morabito retired in June from the Utica Police Department after video footage was released that showed him losing his cool in two separate ugly confrontations with so-called auditors near and at the police station.

    Morabito, who had served as the media spokesperson for the department, had been on paid leave for four months as authorities conducted an investigation into the incidents.

    The blowups were videotaped by two members of the group that goes by the name Frion Media and released to social media.

    The auditors see themselves as activists testing the limits of free speech. They try to provoke a negative reaction that they can post on social media. Public employees who respond calmly to the confrontation “pass” the audit, while those that react negatively “fail” it.

    Morabito was shown making obscene gestures, using aggressive language and slapping a cellphone from the hands of one of the men.

    Steve Jones, 46, of Lansing, one of the auditors, was arrested during the incident at the police department for disorderly conduct.

    Jones said the auditors’ purpose is to ferret out police officers who have poor communication skills in dealing with the public.  He insisted the group was not “anti-cop at all.”

    Equipped with camera phones and aggressive attitudes, Jones encountered Utica officers at a gas station on Hall Road. When Morabito and other officers pulled up on the scene, the confrontation escalated.

    One of the men demands his name and badge number, which Morabito displays on his police vest.

    “You’re a public servant, b—-,” the man says. “You work for the public. You’re paid by tax dollars.”

    A clearly agitated Morabito responded: “No I don’t. I don’t answer your questions.”

    The men continue to make derogatory remarks to the officers, calling them “idiot” “dummy” and vulgar names.

    At one point, Morabito knocks a cell phone being used to record the incident to the ground. He is seen in the video pushing away a man who attempts to look into his police vehicle, and later, arresting Jones, who had gone to the police station to file a complaint against the detective.

    After police leave the scene, Jones and his colleague drove to the police station to file a complaint.

    Morabito is shown in the video first telling the men they have “two minutes” to leave the police station then ordering them out. After a few moments of continued bickering, he announces “you’re done,” and proceeds to arrest and handcuff Jones.

    Jones said he was slammed against a wall by the officers, and suffered a laceration to his shoulder. He was released from custody after about an hour, where he claims police berated him while he was being held

    Mayor Gus Calandrino issued a statement saying Morabito was eligible for retirement and “chose to do so.”

    “The men and women of the Utica Police Department have conducted themselves honorably during this investigation and are anxious to continue doing their job of protecting the residents of Utica,” the mayor.

    10 (tie)-Another grid title for Pilots

    Senior quarterback Brady Drogosh runs for three touchdowns and throws for two as  De La Salle Collegiate wins its second consecutive state Division 2 football championship with a 52-13 victory over Grand Rapids Forest Hills Central at Ford Field. It was the Pilots’ fifth championship since 2014.

    Honorable mention:

    • Macomb County Executive Mark Hackel and Prosecutor Peter Lucido have a very public flap that is played out in court;

    • Clinton Township board members ignite a dust up over Columbus Day;

    • Housing and rental prices continue to skyrocket in Macomb, as do home values;

    • Chippewa Valley sprinter Shamar Heard rules the Division 1 state track and field meet, winning the 100 (10.77 seconds) and 200 (21.32) meter titles;

    • Marijuana industry continues to grow and leads to a pair of dispensaries opening late in the year in Mount Clemens without city approval;

    • Clintondale Community Schools Superintendent Rodriguez Broadnax and one of his elected supporters on the school board resign after an ongoing fight with board members;

    • Detroit Police Department spends months digging through a Lenox Township landfill looking for the remains of Zion Foster, an Eastpointe High School student reported missing in January. The dig was halted in October.
