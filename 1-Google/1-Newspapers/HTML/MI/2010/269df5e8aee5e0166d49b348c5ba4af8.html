‘We do have money to lend’ at 1st Source

Published 10:01 pm Thursday, August 26, 2010

By Staff Report

Carol Styburski, who manages 1st Source Banking Center in Dowagiac, welcomes Angie Eiting of Niles to Dowagiac Rotary Club Aug. 26. Eiting, of Niles, has 25 years of banking experience, including 11 with 1st Source Bank. She is a business banking officer based in Niles. She came to this area from Fort Wayne, Ind. (The Daily News/John Eby)

By JOHN EBY

Dowagiac Daily News

Is there a credit crunch?

“There was and there continues to be,” Angela J. Eiting of 1st Source Bank told Dowagiac Rotary Club Thursday noon at Elks Lodge 889.

She spoke as the guest of Carol Styburski, an assistant vice president who manages the 1st Source Banking Center in Dowagiac.

“Access to credit for small business owners is still pretty tight” because to lenders “cash flow is king,” she said.

Eiting, of Niles, has 25 years of banking experience, including 11 with 1st Source Bank. She is a business banking officer based in Niles.

“The last couple of years have been a very interesting ride,” she said. “If you don’t have access to credit, you’re not hiring people, buying equipment, we’re not going back to work and things continue to operate like they have been the last couple of years. We’d like to see that change. There was a significant drop and we’re starting to see it come back. As I’m out calling on my customers, I’m asking, ‘Are you hiring people back? Are you buying machinery and equipment this year?’ I’m starting to hear they’ve hired one person back or they’re going to temp agencies because they’re still a little concerned about bringing them on fulltime, but at least we are seeing a few people going back to work, which is good.”

According to Campbell R. Harvey, professor of finance at Duke University’s Fuqua School of Business, “Despite the narrowing of credit spreads, a large chunk of the U.S. economy is having greater difficulty borrowing today than during the worst days of the credit crisis.

“A remarkable one-third of small businesses report borrowing conditions are worse today than 2009. Our results show an extraordinary 44 percent of small businesses restricted their capital spending below desired levels because of borrowing difficulties.

“These capital projects create jobs both today and over the longer term. The continued credit problem makes it inevitable that we will see very high levels of unemployment not just in 2010, but well beyond.”

“You’d have thought last year hit bottom,” Eiting said. “Economists are talking about a double dip (recession). I have it in my mind that we probably are going to see it, which is very concerning as a lender — not only because some of my customers who managed to weather the storm last year have used their equity in their businesses. Now we don’t have the same source of funds to keep things going. One third of small businesses say things are worse than in 2009.”

At 1st Source Bank, “We continue to look at loans the same way as we always have,” Eiting said. “We’re very conservative in our lending, which is why we’re still locally owned. To shore up our loans, we’re looking at personal guarantees we may not have had before. We’re asking for additional collateral. We’re talking to people about cash flow. We’re going back to normal underwriting standards” which prevailed before the 2008 Great Recession.

“There were a few years before the housing crisis it was very, very easy to get a consumer mortgage loan. They’ve tightened that up quite a bit in the last 18 to 24 months. A lot of bigger banks who were out there making crazy loans to small businesses lost their shirts.”

Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke said recently, “The challenge ahead for lenders will be to determine how to assess the credit quality of businesses in an uncertain and difficult economic environment,” since “it is in a lender’s interest, after all, to lend to credit-worthy borrowers. Ultimately, that’s how they earn their profits and regulators for their part need to continue to work with lenders to help them do all that they prudently can to meet the needs of credit-worthy small businesses.”

“In difficult economic times,” Eiting said, “It’s still very scary for us as lenders to feel comfortable about making someone a loan, but we’re a for-profit organization and that’s how we make our money. We need to make sure we’re doing everything we can to find a way to finance small business — not look for ways not to.”

Eiting turned to Small Business Administration (SBA) loans. SBA is a federally-managed program and “an important component of how we can start to turn things around.”

The stated SBA mission is to “maintain and strengthen the nation’s economy by aiding, counseling, assisting and protecting the interests of small businesses and by helping families and businesses recover from national disasters.”

SBA offers a variety of loan program to assist small businesses.

The SBA is primarily a guarantor of loans made by private lenders and other institutions rather than a lender itself.

SBA’s business loan programs supplement the ability of certain lenders to provide both long- and short-term financing to small businesses that might not otherwise qualify for loans through traditional lending sources.

First Source has provided assistance to hundreds of Michigan and Indiana businesses.

For fiscal year 2010 (through June), 1st Source ranks fourth against SBA lending peers, having made $9,125,800 in loans to 60 businesses in markets it serves. “The average loan we’re making is about $175,000,” she said.

First Source qualified as a Preferred Lender, or PLP, with SBA, approved through application and audit process meeting SBA guidelines and standards.

Preferred Lender status allows the bank to process and approve loans on its underwriting guidelines and eliminates time to package requests sent to SBA in Sacramento for review “based purely on paper numbers. We have the opportunity as a local lender to come out and meet you and get to really know your business. A lot of times character comes into play that folks in California can’t see.”

“The bottom line,” Eiting said, “is SBA is there to strengthen the economy by helping local families and small business owners. Their goal is to help recover from national disasters. If what happened the last two years isn’t considered a disaster, I don’t know what is.”

SBA loan advantages include longer terms for repaying loan requests, lower loan payments and improved cash flow. Lower down payment requirements (10 percent rather than 20 percent) can preserve capital when a conventional loan would be unaffordable for a small company.

Customized terms and payment options are available, with special consideration given for veterans and military families.

“I’m doing a loan for a military person right now,” Eiting noted. “She’s an Iraq and Afghanistan veteran. Her interest rate topped out at 6 percent instead of 8.25 percent. There are some real advantages to using SBA financing.”

SBA offers a variety of Express Loan programs: traditional, maximum loan amount of $350,000, 50 percent guaranteed and interest rates tied to Wall Street Journal prime — lender may charge 4.5 percent up to 6.5 percent over WSJ; SBA Express Loans, which are used to support working capital lines of credit, term loans and commercial building loans; and Patriot Express loans up to $500,000 with a 75/85 percent guarantee.

Thanks to the SBA guarantor, “If a company went out of business owing us $100,000, we have a 50-percent payback, depending on the program, up to 90 percent payback. We take a little bit of risk, but we get some of our money back, and the SBA backs us up. SBA is self-funded” through fees business owners pay. “It’s not supported by us as taxpayers or the government, but that pool.”

Eiting said the SBA 7(a) loan program offers a 75-percent guarantee.

“It has a higher threshold for dollar amounts,” she said of the maximum $2 million loan amount and SBA maximum exposure of $1.5 million.

“This isn’t necessarily just for the small mom and pop stores downtown,” she said. “These are for manufacturers as well, and we’ve been looking at doing SBA lending for some of our larger clients because it makes sense for them and for us. They can make a smaller down payment and have longer repayment terms. Cash flow is king. When you’re talking to a business owner who’s talking about having a hard time getting financing, it’s probably because their cash flow is not where it needs to be. They can’t afford to pay us back. It’s like when you buy a house, you’ve got to be able to make that mortgage payment. I don’t want your house back and I don’t want your business, we just want to make sure we’re financing something that works for you from a cash-flow standpoint.”

Finally, an SBA 504 loan “is a really nice program because we can do 10 or 20 years where our traditional terms are five or seven,” Eiting said. “It’s a nice program for larger investment, like an $8 million piece of equipment.”

“We do have money to lend,” Eiting said. “I had a guy in my office last week tell me, ‘Angie, I don’t think I’ll qualify.’ I told him, ‘If you don’t bring me your information and let me take a look, you may stymie your business because you’re afraid to ask for financing.’ I think there’s some concern out there in the marketplace right now that if they do come see the bank, we’re not going to give them the loan anyway. It’s a good time to talk to the banker about refinancing. If we can’t do it today, we can put a game plan together for sometime in the future.”

Existing businesses, she would want to review three years of tax returns.

For start-ups, she looks for a detailed business plan, “which really tells me the story of what they’re going to do and how they’re going to accomplish it.”

Her female veteran was a start-up she financed three years ago who is now moving into a building.

With so many business failures attributed to the Great Recession, will they be discredited from consideration?

“Not necessarily,” Eiting answered. “I’ve got manufacturing people who had great businesses until three years ago when they hit the wall. It wasn’t because of anything they did wrong, but they were tied to the Elkhart market. I think we’re going to see that over the next five years and we’re going to have to take into consideration what happened because a lot of folks have been impacted.”

As a banker, she said lawmakers could be most helpful by “getting out of our way.”

“Eighteen months ago they came out with a $35,000 Recovery Act loan. It was meant to help those businesses struggling when everything hit the wall — $35,000 stop-gap, zero-percent interest, the whole nine yards, but they, the Democrats, put so many rules in place that we could not jump all those hurdles, so yeah, of the $35,000, there’s money out there to lend, but because they constrained us so much, it’s very difficult. I think we gave 10 of those loans in the last 18 months. You had to prove you were profitable in two of the last three years. Who’s been profitable? That’s been very challenging.”

State legislators’ priority should be to “take a look at what’s going to happen with our taxes and health insurance. Small business owners are getting hit from all sides. It’s very expensive to offer benefits, as you all know. State legislators need to know boots on the ground from being out talking to business owners. I see Sharon Tyler, but I don’t know how many other of those folks are out there getting to know what’s truly going on in the marketplace.”

Declines in property values also come into play, she said.

“If I have a lien against your house and we’re using that as part of our collateral, what’s happening with the housing market, but especially the commercial side. I did a loan in Niles for a business that two years ago was appraised at $450,000. We had it reappraised to do an SBA loan and it came in at $275,000 in two years. There’s not enough real estate moving in our local market, so when they go out to find the value for collateral, they have to go further outside of Dowagiac or further outside of Niles, which impacts the value. The guy who did the appraisal for me said he couldn’t find a like kind of building that had sold in 2 1/2 years.”
