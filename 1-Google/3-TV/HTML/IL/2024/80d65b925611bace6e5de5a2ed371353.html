Women voters could sway the 2024 presidential election

by AUSTIN DENEAN | The National Desk

Thu, February 1st 2024 at 3:05 PM
Updated Thu, February 1st 2024 at 3:11 PM
President Joe Biden speaks at the "Sunday Lunch" event at the Brookland Baptist Banquet Center, part of the Brookland Baptist Church, in West Columbia, S.C., on Sunday, Jan. 28, 2024. (AP Photo/Jacquelyn Martin)

WASHINGTON (TND) — The gender gap between the two frontrunners for the 2024 presidential election is growing as voters begin to home in on their choices ahead of November.

New polling from Quinnipiac University found President Joe Biden is holding a lead over former President Donald Trump, 50% to 44%, an improvement for the Democratic incumbent compared to December when they were essentially tied.

The boost for Biden comes as more women say they will support Biden over Trump, with 58% going for Biden compared to just 36% for Trump, a five-point swing from December. Figures were mostly unchanged among men, with 53% saying the will support Trump and 42% for Biden, a 1% change from the previous poll.

"The gender demographic tells a story to keep an eye on. Propelled by female voters in just the past few weeks, the head-to-head tie with Trump morphs into a modest lead for Biden," Quinnipiac University polling analyst Tim Malloy said in a release.


The poll comes as Trump was ordered to pay $83.3 million to E. Jean Carroll for attacking her over her claims that he sexually assaulted her in a department store. He has also ramped up his personal attacks against Nikki Haley and mocked her clothing choices, who is his only remaining challenger in the Republican presidential primary.

A majority of women have preferred the Democratic candidate in every presidential election since 1996. In the first matchup between Trump and Biden in 2020, the then-Democratic nominee won the women’s vote by 12%, according to exit polling by the Associated Press VoteCast.

Exit polling from the last several election cycles have indicated Republicans and Trump have struggled with women voters, especially those who live in suburbs and have a college education. Biden won 54% of suburban women in the 2020 election, according to data from Pew Research Center.

What issues women prioritize in November could play a key role in determining who will win the election.

A November 2023 poll by All in Together that focused on priorities for women found abortion remained the top issue, though it was tied with cost of living. Forty-six percent of the women in the poll said a candidate “must share my views on this issue” about abortion, with only 13% saying it was not particularly important.

Biden and other Democratic candidates have sought to make abortion a prime issue in each election since the Supreme Court overturned Roe v. Wade in 2022. Success on energizing voters on the issue was credited by some analysts of why Democrats overperformed polling in the 2022 midterms and avoided a predicted “red wave” amid Biden’s low approval ratings that tend to drag down-ballot candidates down.


That trend has continued in 2024, with Biden and Vice President Kamala Harris marking the 51st anniversary of Roe v. Wade decision and starting a “Fight for Reproductive freedoms Tour.” Biden has also vowed to codify Roe into law if voters reelect him and provide a large enough Democratic majority in Congress.

“The MAGA Republicans are saying there’s going to be a national ban on abortion across every state in America — ban,” Biden said at a campaign fundraiser in Florida this week. “Well, guess what? I made it real clear: If the MAGA Republicans try to pass a national ban to the right to choose, I will veto it.”

Trump has not committed to a national abortion ban during his primary campaign, though he has made several references to the fact that his appointments of Supreme Court justices helped lead to the overturning of Roe v. Wade. He has also called for exceptions to restrictions on abortion.

"For 54 years they were trying to get Roe v. Wade terminated, and I did it and I'm proud to have done it," Trump said in a town hall with Fox News last month.


But there are other issues for women that don’t work as favorably for Democrats in the opinion surveys. Inflation, the state of the economy and immigration have been higher on priority lists among all voters in recent polling, which could be weak spots for the president, who has low approval ratings on his handling of the issues.

The All in Together poll that found cost of living was tied with abortion on women voters’ priorities also indicated a candidate’s approach to dealing with the issue could weigh more heavily on their vote. Nearly half said it was one of many important factors along with the 46% who said a candidate must share their views. Only 2% said it was not particularly important, compared to 13% who said that about abortion.

Biden is still underwater in approval on his handling of the economy but has seen a modest bump recently amid economic reports indicating strong growth and low unemployment that defied predictions of a recession. Among women in the Quinnipiac poll, he had a 46% approval rating on the economy, four percentage points higher than the overall total.

"As inflation fears cool and the bruising campaign heats up, President Biden rides a welcome small uptick in confidence in his handling of the economy," Malloy said.
