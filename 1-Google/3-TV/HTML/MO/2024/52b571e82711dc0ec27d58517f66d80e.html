Senate border deal facing an unlikely path to passage amid resistance from both parties

by AUSTIN DENEAN | The National Desk

Thu, February 1st 2024 at 2:30 PM
5
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
FILE - Sen. James Lankford, R-Okla., the lead GOP negotiator on a border-foreign aid package, speaks with reporters outside the chamber at the Capitol in Washington, Thursday, Jan. 25, 2024. (AP Photo/J. Scott Applewhite)

    TOPICS:

    BorderBillImmigrationRepublicansDemocratsDealNegotiationsSenate

WASHINGTON (TND) — Senators who have been negotiating a deal to deal with the situation at the southern border are nearing the release of the text of the highly-anticipated package, but its ability to make it to President Joe Biden’s desk is in serious doubt before it is even considered for a vote.

Weeks of negotiations seeking to find a deal that could attract the support of both parties may prove too difficult to find as lawmakers in both parties line up to oppose a deal that hasn’t been made public yet.

House Speaker Mike Johnson, R-La., has declared the bill would be “dead on arrival” on multiple occasions throughout the weeks of talks and said House Republicans would settle for nothing less than H.R. 2, an immigration and border security bill that contains many priorities for the GOP but are far too restrictive for Democrats or the president to approve of.

“From what we’ve heard, this so-called deal does not include transformational policy changes needed to actually stop the border catastrophe,” Johnson said during his first floor speech as speaker on Wednesday.

In addition to Republican opposition, some Democrats have lined up against supporting the bill despite limited information about its specific being available prior to the text being released over concerns that it is too harsh and does not address the root of the problems.

The result of the negotiations appears to be hurdling toward nothing being passed despite bipartisan concerns about securing the border amid high levels of crossings and an immigration system that is still struggling to catch up from pandemic-induced backlogs.

It’s a familiar story for Americans when it comes to immigration policy, which has not had a significant overhaul in decades despite numerous attempts by various groups of legislators over the years.

The last extensive law came during the presidency of Ronald Reagan in 1986, and President George H.W. Bush signed a more limited bill four years later in 1990. Both of those came outside of presidential cycles, where the politics of election years were not as prevalent.

“The thing about immigration is in some ways people can kind of grandstand on it, because the people that it affects are not voters. The people that we're talking about don't have a right to vote, so they don't have any political capital,” said Erin Corcoran, a professor at Notre Dame’s Keough School of Global Affairs.

Former President Donald Trump has encouraged Republicans to vote against the deal at multiple points, though he has denied his motive is to deny Biden a legislative win.

“If the bill is not going to be a great bill and really solve the problem, I wouldn’t do it at all. Not for political reasons, just for U.S. reasons,” he said on Wednesday.

Both parties have accused each other of politicizing the border and failing to step up to deal with the issues facing the country, which has resulted in limited compromises. Congress has struggled to find a deal to pass things that there is bipartisan agreement and support for, such as a path to citizenship for people brought to the U.S. illegally as children.

“I don't think you have people in government who see the value in compromise anymore, politically,” Corcoran said. “You've seen it on a variety of different issues, not just immigration.”

The possible failure of the bill comes as states have pleaded with the government for more federal assistance to help the increasing flow of people seeking asylum. Mayors in places like Chicago, New York and Denver have met with the White House and made public calls for further funding and efforts to expand work authorization to help fill job openings and reduce the financial stress on shelters and other services.

The federal government’s 2023 budget includes an additional $800 million for FEMA to help communities dealing with an influx. House Republicans have cited the cost to taxpayers as a piece of the necessity to enact their border bill, releasing a report that estimated illegal immigration costs each taxpayer around $1,000, though researchers have said it is very difficult to pinpoint the exact costs and offsets provided by their economic activity.

Further clouding the bill’s outlook is the military aid for Ukraine that is a part of the package, which Republicans in Congress have been more skeptical of providing as the fighting after Russia’s invasion has continued. Funding for Ukraine, Israel and Taiwan was swept up in a fight over the border, which may make it impossible for the funds to get out the door without a shift in strategy.

Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer, D-N.Y., said in a floor speech on Wednesday that senators will continue working to find an agreement, warning that the “survival of Ukraine is on the line.”

“The only way we’ll rise to the occasion is if both sides are serious about finding a bipartisan compromise,” Schumer said in a floor speech. “We have not concluded negotiations so we will keep going to get this done.”
