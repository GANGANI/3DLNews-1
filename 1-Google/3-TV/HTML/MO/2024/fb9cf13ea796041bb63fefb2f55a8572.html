Republican lawmakers push back against Maine governor's calls for gun reform

by MAL MEYER | WGME Staff

Fri, February 2nd 2024 at 12:41 PM
3
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
Various guns are displayed at a store on July 18, 2022, in Auburn, Maine. (AP Photo/Robert F. Bukaty, File)

    TOPICS:

    MaineGun reformRepublicansGovernor Janet MillsBackground checksLawmakersProposalViolence

AUGUSTA, Maine (WGME) — Republican lawmakers are denouncing Maine Gov. Janet Mills' gun reform proposals put forward this week.

Mills laid out a series of ideas during her State of the State address on Tuesday, but the exact details are still being finalized.

There are policy areas where we can find some common ground,” Rep. Billy Bob Faulkingham, R-Winter Harbor, said.

During a news conference at the State House on Thursday, House and Senate Republicans said there were some parts of the governor's address that they may get behind.

“The House Republicans are committed to working with the administration on storm relief,” Faulkingham said.

They also support help for mental health, but for these lawmakers, with this and other proposals, it comes down to the details.

On October 25, everything changed,” Mills said.

A gunman, later identified as Robert Card, opened fire at Just-in-Time Recreation and Schemengees Bar and Grille in Lewiston, killing 18 and wounding 13 others. Card's body was found on Oct. 27 in Lisbon. He reportedly died from a self-inflicted gunshot wound.

Mills wants to take a three-pronged approach in response to violence and the mass shootings. To keep guns away from dangerous people, she proposes closing a background check loophole by requiring that for private, advertised sales.

“People who can't legally own guns should not have such easy access to them,” Mills said.

“The Democrats' push for a universal background check is nothing more than attempt to create a gun registry,” Rep. Chad Perkins, R-Dover-Foxcroft, said.

It’s a claim other Republicans reiterated, too. WGME asked lawmakers how they could know if there aren’t protections in the bill to prevent a registry if they haven’t seen the exact language.

Sen. Trey Stewart, R-Presque Isle, said there are questions about what will be required and when.

“I think that is the key point here, let's see what the bill actually says,” Stewart said.

In a statement to WGME, the governor's office said she's not proposing universal background checks nor creating gun registry, which she would not support.

“The governor’s proposal requires a check against the National Instant Criminal Background Check System for private, advertised sales. It then incentivizes a check for private, unadvertised sales through changes in law. This thoughtful, measured approach would enhance public safety, protect Constitutional rights and respect Maine’s longstanding outdoor heritage,” her office said in a statement.

Mills' office is still finalizing the bill, which likely means it would be ready next week.

Despite her clear explanation of the proposal, the governor hopes that when the bill is released, Republicans will approach it with an open mind and study it closely to clear up their confusion," her office said. "False characterizations and red herrings should not stand in the way of progress on what are practical, common-sense measures, measures that are not extreme or unusual, that are not a cookie cutter version of another’s state’s laws, but that are Maine-made and true to our culture and our longstanding traditions while meeting today’s needs."

“I think that we have a lot of work in front of us,” Rep. Lori Gramlich, D-Old Orchard Beach, said.

Like those across the aisle, Gramlich wants to see what the governor’s package says. But she said that lawmakers are already working on initiatives to hopefully prevent another tragedy.

“I’m really confident that together we can find bipartisan support to assure that Maine citizens are kept safe,” Gramlich said.
