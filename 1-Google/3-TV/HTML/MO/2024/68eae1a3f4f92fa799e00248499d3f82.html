U.S. military airstrikes struck more than 85 targets in Iraq and Syria

by The National Desk

Fri, February 2nd 2024 at 2:41 PM
Updated Sat, February 3rd 2024 at 9:41 PM
6
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
FILE - A satellite photo from Planet Labs PBC shows a military base known as Tower 22 in northeastern Jordan on Monday, Jan. 29, 2024. (Planet Labs PBC via AP)

WASHINGTON (TND) — The Biden administration is saying that the U.S. does not seek conflict in the Middle East following the retaliatory airstrikes that struck more than 85 targets in the Middle East.

President Joe Biden addressed Friday's airstrikes.

This afternoon, at my direction, U.S. military forces struck targets at facilities in Iraq and Syria that the IRGC and affiliated militia use to attack U.S. forces, Biden stated.

The long-range bombers flew from the United States and struck dozens of sites at 4 p.m. EST Friday that lasted 30 minutes, according to Lt. Gen. Douglas Sims.


Sims said three of the sites struck were in Iraq and four were in Syria.

This comes after the deadly drone attack on a base in Jordan.

U.S. military forces struck more than 85 targets in its retaliatory attack, according to U.S. Central Command.

Airstrikes targeted command and control operations, centers, intelligence centers, rockets, and missiles, and unmanned aerial vehicle storages, and logistics and munition supply chain facilities of militia groups.

Our response began today. It will continue at times and places of our choosing, Biden added in his statement. "The United States does not seek conflict in the Middle East or anywhere else in the world. But let all those who might seek to do us harm know this: If you harm an American, we will respond.

Defense Secretary Lloyd Austin shared Biden's sentiment that the U.S. is not seeking conflict "anywhere."

This is the start of our response. The President has directed additional actions to hold the IRGC and affiliated militias accountable for their attacks on U.S. and Coalition Forces," stated Austin.

Austin added that actions will "unfold at times and places of our choosing."

On Monday, Auston told a room of reporters at the Pentagon, "We will respond when we choose, where we choose, and how we choose."

National Security Council spokesperson John Kirby said a "tiered approach" is expected and not a single strike.

Kirby added that the targets "were carefully selected to avoid civilian casualties and based on clear, irrefutable evidence that they were connected to attacks on U.S. personnel in the region."

Friday marked five days after Sgt. William Jerome Rivers, Sgt. Kennedy Sanders, and Sgt. Breonna Moffett were killed at Tower 22.

All three soldiers were from Georgia.

At least 40 other American troops were hurt in the attack.

Earlier on Friday, President Joe Biden and first lady Jill Biden joined grieving families at Dover Air Force Base to honor the service members.

Biden noted in his statement that he spoke with the fallen soldiers' families at the dignified transfer.

The deaths were the first U.S. fatalities blamed on Iran-backed militia groups, who for months have been intensifying their attacks on American forces in the region following the onset of the Israel-Hamas war on Oct. 7, 2023.

"These service members embodied the very best of our nation: Unwavering in their bravery. Unflinching in their duty. Unbending in their commitment to our country — risking their own safety for the safety of their fellow Americans, and our allies and partners with whom we stand in the fight against terrorism," Biden previously said. "It is a fight we will not cease."

In a separate incident, two Navy SEALs died during a January mission to board an unflagged ship that was carrying illicit Iranian-made weapons to Yemen.

It's not clear, however, whether militia members were killed in Frida's attack.

"We know that there are militants that use these locations, IRGC as well as Iranian-aligned militia group personnel,” Sims said. “We made these strikes tonight with an idea that there would likely be casualties associated with people inside those facilities.”

It was unclear what the next steps will be, or whether the days of U.S. warnings have sent militia members scattering into hiding, making it more difficult to detect and strike them. But it was evident that the recent statement released by Kataeb Hezbollah, one of the main Iran-backed militias, saying it was suspending attacks on American troops had no impact on the administration’s plans.
