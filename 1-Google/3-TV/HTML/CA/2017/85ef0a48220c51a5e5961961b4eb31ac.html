Helena's Incident Command Post preps thousands of firefighters

by Noelle Medina

Thu, September 7th 2017 at 11:52 PM
Updated Thu, September 7th 2017 at 11:52 PM
icpweaverville-1504760921447-8430726-ver1-0.png

WEAVERVILLE, Calif. - Lowden Park in Weaverville has been transformed into the "brains" behind the Helena Fire operation.

The fire, burning in the hills of Trinity County, is being battled by thousands of fire personnel from state, national and international agencies.

Craig Ferguson, an Incident Commander in Melbourne, Australia, was part of the firefighting force on Wednesday, September 6.

"Just watching and observing how fires get managed and seeing what I can take back to Australia...you're world class for the way you manage disasters here, and there's a lot we can learn from," Ferguson said.

The 24/7 Incident Command Post (ICP) is where firefighters and other personnel on the fire effort come together to discuss strategies, eat, rest and rejuvenate before heading back to the front lines.

On average, these firefighters complete 16-hour shifts for 14 days before getting two days off.

The post has it's own "Main Street," which is the center of the operation.

On the park's baseball fields, tents and tables provide a place for firefighters to eat and sleep.

Aaron Campbell, the manager of D.F. Zee's Firefighter Catering Company, explained a few of the menu items his staff has prepared for lunch and dinner.

"Shrimp scampi, we do homemade meatloaf, fresh-pressed Salisbury steak sometimes," Campbell said. "A lot of people I've talked to...don't realize there's a lot more that goes into it than just fighting the fire. It's a little city in itself right here."

In the ICP's "mini-city," Incident Commander for the Forest Service and Unified Commander for the Helena Fire, Clay Templin, said his role is a little bit like being the mayor. He added that the morale at the camp is, "really good."

"We work long days, and so, our firefighters are tired, but that's why we provide them with a good meal and the opportunity to get cleaned up."

Approximately 1,450 firefighters were at the camp on Wednesday.

In regards to advancing the Helena Fire's containment, Commander Templin said they're constructing a lot of containment lines.

"We're going to try to capture that using some of the old containment lines that occurred during the Eagle Fire in 2008."

As of Wednesday, the Helena Fire was estimated to cost around $6 million, and the cost will increase as crews continue their fight for full containment.
