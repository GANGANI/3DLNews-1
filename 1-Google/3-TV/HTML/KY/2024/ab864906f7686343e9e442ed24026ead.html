New bill would largely ban cell phones in Kentucky classrooms

Some teachers think it could do more harm than good.
By Samantha Valentino
Published: Jan. 30, 2024 at 3:37 PM CST

FRANKFORT, Ky. (WKYT) - A new bill could largely ban the use of cell phones in Kentucky classrooms.

The bill’s sponsor says the idea came from a teacher.

“Teachers want to do a really good job, and you can’t, you can’t teach distracted kids,” said Representative Josh Bray, R-Mount Vernon.

Representative Bray says he was at his son’s basketball game when a teacher told him the biggest issue he’s facing in the classroom.

“He’s having to fight the battle of TikTok videos, YouTube, text messaging, while he’s trying to teach kids how to do math,” said Representative Bray. “It’s very frustrating and just felt like it was something that needed to be addressed in education.”

That’s why Bray filed House Bill 383. It would require school districts to create a policy ensuring cell phones aren’t being used in the classroom, with some exceptions, including emergencies.

“God forbid there’s a health emergency or something, a school shooting, you know, we parents want to be able to communicate with their kids, make sure they’re okay,” said Representative Bray.

There are also exceptions for when teachers want to integrate cell phones into their lessons or if a student is authorized to use them under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act.

“I think it probably will do more harm than good. Because we have So many discipline problems, we already have each school, and each district already comes up with their own cell phone policy. And those policies are they’re not working,” said Laura Hartke, an organizer with Kentucky 120 United AFT. “I know of several just of our members and teachers in general, who have been stabbed with pencils over trying to get a cell phone, and so what’s going to happen there?”

Hartke says while teachers struggle with cell phones creating distractions in their classrooms, they also struggle trying to keep them out of students’ hands.

“How does this new bill get those phones out of the hands of students without alienating parents and students even further? What consequences are there? Who’s going to take those consequences? Is it going to be on the teacher’s back? The admin? The district?” said Hartke. “I just saw a TikTok of a teacher who got maced over taking a cell phone. So, what does this bill do for that? What happens to the teachers that are taking the cell phones? And what does that do to the relationships? I’m not really sure, but those are the fears that the teachers have.”

As the bill is written, each school district’s board of education would be required to adopt a policy regarding the possession and use of cell phones in schools. This policy would be required to be included in the district’s written standards of student conduct.

“The policy shall, at a minimum, forbid a student’s use of a personal telecommunications device during instructional time, except during an emergency or if directed to do so by a teacher for an instructional purpose. A student who violates the policy shall be subject to discipline as provided by board policy,” the bill reads.
