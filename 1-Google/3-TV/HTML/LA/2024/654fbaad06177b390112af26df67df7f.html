Crawfish shipper says Central Louisiana may provide 'hope' for projected historically bad season

By Jesse Brooks
Published: Feb. 1, 2024 at 8:42 AM CST|Updated: Feb. 1, 2024 at 9:12 AM CST

NATCHITOCHES, La. (WVUE) - The current projections for the 2024 crawfish season in Louisiana have been troubling for farmers and consumers alike as limited supply are expected to make prices soar on historic levels.

READ MORE ‘Worst season ever;’ Experts predict historically bad crawfish harvest to drive prices up

Some early prices for crawfish are already steep. James Karst sent Fox 8 a photo of a menu from Houma restaurant that offered a 10 lbs. crawfish special with one pound of potatoes for $139.99.

Despite the unfavorable outlook, one crawfish supplier in Natchitoches is now saying that farms in Central Louisiana could provide “hope” for the state industry.

The expected poor season is being attributed to a devastating drought, extreme heat, and late fall weather conditions. Supplier Louisiana Crawfish Company says that the farms they purchase from in the southernmost part of the state have lost a significant portion of their female and juvenile crawfish, which has hurt production in the early months. With that in mind, the company says that the season is experiencing a delayed start, 6-8 weeks behind schedule.

As challenging as the situation has been for the south, the company says they are seeing encouraging signs from farms they purchase from in Central Louisiana. Due to the central part of the state having a heavier clay soil, crawfish there have faired better than in the south as they’re able to burrow deeper and survive harsh conditions.

The company says that the different soil in the central area helps retain moisture, boost survival rates, and retail normal production.

Central Louisiana is expected to be at normal production but since the region experiences colder temperatures than the south, the season there starts later than other areas, according to the company’s report.

The company also says that they’ve found areas in the Atchafalaya Basin and Mississippi River that received more substantial rainfall than other areas of the state, which could result in a strong wild crawfish crop.
