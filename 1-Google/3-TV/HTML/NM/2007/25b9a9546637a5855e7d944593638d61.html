How tornadoes are formed

by Nick Bender

Wed, April 18th 2007 at 5:54 AM
If conditions are favorable, this violent rotation will extend downward towards the surface making contact with the ground as a tornado. / Brian James, KVII Chief Storm Chaser

Wed, 18 Apr 2007 10:54:51 GMT — Tornadoes are arguably one of the most deadly and unpredictable forces in Mother Nature's arsenal. Yet, even after years of field and laboratory research, the exact science behind how these monsters of the sky form is still not fully understood.

At the surface, southeast winds ahead of a developing thunderstorm, and stronger west winds farther above the ground, create horizontal spinning columns of air. When the thunderstorm moves over these horizontal spinning columns, rising air within the thunderstorm's updraft tilts the horizontal rotating air vertically.

This imparts further rotation to the thunderstorm, enabling the updraft to spin violently.

If conditions are favorable, this violent rotation will extend downward towards the surface making contact with the ground as a tornado.

On February 1, 2007, a new rating system called the Enhanced Fujita-Scale went into effect to classify the severity of tornadoes.

You may ask, what is the difference between the old Fujita-Scale, or "F-Scale," and the new Enhanced Fujita-Scale, or "EF-Scale?"

The Enhanced Fujita-Scale estimates the wind speed of tornadoes based on damage. The old Fujita-Scale wind estimates were too high for F2 and stronger tornadoes; structures - especially houses, aren't built that strong.

So, meteorologists and engineers at The Wind Science and Engineering Center at Texas Tech University devised a very detailed table of damage indicators, with each indicator having a table containing numerous degrees of damage to the object or structure.

It is from these tables that wind estimates for tornadoes are derived.

"What is very helpful about the EF-Scale is there's a whole bunch more damage indicators we can use to assess the damage," said Steve Drillette, the Warning Coordination Meteorologist for the National Weather Service in Amarillo.

Drillette went on to say that they can use telephone poles, trees, mobile homes, automobile showrooms, malls, schools and various other structures and objects that now have absolute data that can be used to estimate the wind speed of tornadoes.

This data enables a much higher degree of accuracy for the rating of tornadoes.
