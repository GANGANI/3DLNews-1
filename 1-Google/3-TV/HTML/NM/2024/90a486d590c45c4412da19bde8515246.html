Emergent fentanyl crisis unfolds in Portland following Oregon's drug decriminalization

by JANAE BOWENS | The National Desk

Fri, February 2nd 2024 at 2:52 PM
Updated Fri, February 2nd 2024 at 2:53 PM
7
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
FILE - A man prepares to smoke fentanyl on a park bench in downtown Portland, Ore., on Thursday, May 18, 2023. (Beth Nakamura/The Oregonian via AP, File)

WASHINGTON (TND) — This week Oregon declared a fentanyl state of emergency in Portland for 90 days, just years after officials there decriminalized drug possession.

Governor Tina Kotek made the declaration Tuesday. Officials will have more resources to respond to the crisis and help improve patients' connection to care.

Overdose deaths from synthetic opioids have increased by 533% in that area from 2018 to 2022.

The long term solution is to make sure that anybody in Oregon, not just in Portland, that is ready to seek a pathway to recovery, that they have a place to get the treatment they need, Kotek said during a press conference.

The move comes years after the state decriminalized all drugs back in 2020. Other states and cities have done the same with the rationale being to treat drug use and dependence as a health and social issue, not one of criminal justice.

"If they didn't see this coming the rest of the world did," explained Sgt. Betsy Smith, spokesperson for the National Police Association. "We've got to quit looking at it as a victimless crime, but is this a mental health crisis? Drug addiction? Absolutely."

Smith said decriminalizing drugs does not work, because there is a disconnect with the mental health care system.

"If you arrest somebody for possessing, say heroin and they're a heroin addict when they get convicted of that crime, a part of their sentence should be drug rehabilitation," Smith added.

Eric Johnson from our Seattle station, KOMO, highlighted his loved one, 35-year-old Lexi Evanson, and what he calls her slow suicide. Evanson was addicted to heroin and then fentanyl for years. She died in December of acute bacterial meningitis.

No, I can’t ever give up, then I’ll just be like the rest, and I don’t see myself like them," Evanson said in a previous interview about going after her dreams. "I think I’ve experienced enough life and death for three or four lifetimes.

People like Lexi are why Washington state switched course and increased the punishment for publicly using or possessing drugs after essentially decriminalizing it in 2021.

Other places like the state of California and the city of Philadelphia also decriminalized drugs and are now seeing a spike in overdoses.

In Oregon, Republicans are working to end the drug decriminalization measure while Democrats are pushing for tweaks that would make possession of a small amount of a drug like fentanyl a misdemeanor.

According to the CDC, over 150 people die every day from overdoses related to drugs like fentanyl.
