Weather 101: What's an inversion

By Brad Miller
Published: Dec. 8, 2015 at 11:58 AM MST

Why is it warmer at higher elevations this morning? The answer is an inversion. Normally, air gets cooler the higher you go in elevation. In this case, however, a layer of warm air sits several hundred feet above the valley floor. This stable warm air traps the cooler air at the surface. It also traps any moisture and pollutants in the air as well. It is a dry morning now, but if there were moisture in the air, valley fog would be a very real possibility. Above the warm layer, the air cools in elevation as normal.

It's pretty easy to see where the warm layer is. In Grand Junction, just below 5,000 feet at 8:00 this morning, the temperature was 26°. In Rifle, at 5300 feet, the temperature was 24°. But in Eagle, just above 6500 feet, the temperature was 34°! Aspen was 32°, just below 8000 feet. The top of the milder layer was at the top of the Grand Mesa, with a temperature of 27°, just above 10,000 feet.

The only way for the inversion to go away is for the air at the surface to warm to the same temperature as the warmer layer. This could happen today, but is more likely to happen tomorrow. Until then, if you want to warm up, head to the mountains!
