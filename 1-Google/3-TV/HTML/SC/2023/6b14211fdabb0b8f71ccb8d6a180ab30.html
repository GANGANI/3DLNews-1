Union asks feds to revoke SC’s ability to govern workplace regulations

By Steven Ardary, Jana Cugliari and Melissa Rademaker
Published: Dec. 7, 2023 at 11:30 AM EST|Updated: Dec. 7, 2023 at 11:30 PM EST

CHARLESTON, S.C. (WCSC) - South Carolina workers represented by the Union of Southern Service Workers are asking the U.S. Occupational Safety and Health Administration to take over health and safety enforcement from the state.

The union says the standards and inspections conducted by the state fail to protect workers and hold employers accountable.

Union representatives say the state took an average of four business weeks to investigate complaints compared to an average of 2.6 weeks for investigators in Virginia and 5.7 days in North Carolina.

They also suggest the agency isn’t conducting as thorough an investigation as its federal counterpart as the state found 67% of worksites to be compliant in 2022 versus 44% nationally.

Employers are also paying lower penalties for those violations at an average of $1,402 compared to nearly $2,000 in neighboring states.

“SC OSHA’s ongoing negligence has had enormous ripple effects across the state, leaving low-wage workers effectively fending for themselves in the face of major health and safety violations,” the organization said.

Steffany Blinn, a union member and assistant manager at her job, says those numbers represent real-time issues, like her own danger at the gas station where she works.

“We have no key for our locked spill box, which holds the supplies for a fuel spill, we also have no PPE, personal protective equipment, that would keep us protected from chemicals splashing on us,” Blinn says.

That’s why Blinn submitted an OSHA complaint, but she wasn’t happy with what happened next. She says the department took weeks to respond, did not let her participate in the inspection or talk to her and she is still missing a solution.

“I had no follow-up from them, I had no consequences for what my store were. I still have not been contacted by OSHA to know if any of those things have come to compliance of if they had to pay the fine,” Blinn says.

She’s one of many union workers who are supporting a petition asking the federal OSHA to step in and make the South Carolina OSHA step up, or give the reigns to the feds.

Workplace accident attorney Gary Christmas says while people should have a reasonable expectation of safety at work and many accidents are preventable there will always be some human error.

“Some employers are great actors, and they have good training programs and but even when they do that, they’re still mistakes. Some just don’t really have the programs at all,” Christmas says.

OSHA does not routinely inspect workplace safety, instead only when a complaint is filed. Christmas says the name of the game is prevention, since all his clients would much rather have their health and safety, than all the money in the world.

“People respect what you inspect and if you don’t have the ability to inspect because manpower, resources, whatever it may be, you’re not going to get the full compliance or substantial compliance that you’re looking for when you pass that kind of law. It’s, you know, it’s a tall task,” Christmas says.

He compared OSHA to first responders in terms of the expectation that they monitor all complaints across the state, yet they do not have the agencies patrolling locally the way police or fire do for safety. Christmas believes that the system of payouts to those who need it is working and people are getting their dues, but he has noticed that the boom in jobs and workers is leading to a logical rise in injuries.

Organizers are also warning that the agency doesn’t have enough inspectors saying only 287 inspections were conducted for the more than 150,000 establishments.

“Even as the state’s population grows, SC OSHA’s staff of investigators has declined, and the agency has failed to ramp up its enforcement capacity,” organizers said.

Christmas says his work sometimes crosses paths with OSHA, in fact recently one of his cases collected the background to make a complaint.

“I have had cases - in the past year we had a water tank, I think in Walterboro one time where someone had a brain injury, and we gathered the information of all the violations and then presented the package over to OSHA and in that case, they did prosecute and they did I think have two or three violations against the employer in that case,” Christmas says.

The request for OSHA to take over inspections comes on the heels of a civil rights complaint filed by the group that claimed the state agency discriminated against workplaces with predominantly Black workforces “leaving thousands of low-wage black workers vulnerable to preventable injury and illness.”

In a statement, OSHA says:

South Carolina OSHA is a proud State Plan and is fully committed to its mission of making South Carolina a safe place to work and live for all employees. We are especially proud that we continue to have one of the lowest rates of employee injuries and illnesses in the nation among State Plans and Federal OSHA. It is important to note that SC OSHA follows Federal OSHA procedures in the enforcement of its State Plan. In addition to our enforcement program, we work cooperatively with employers, employees, associations and other groups to provide free safety and health training and consultation. USSW is one of the groups SC OSHA has been meeting and working directly with over the past year.
