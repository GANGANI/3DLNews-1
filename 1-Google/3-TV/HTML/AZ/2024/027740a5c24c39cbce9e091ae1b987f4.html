DEADLY DUI CASE: Pinal County Attorney admits error

By Valerie Cavazos
Published: Jan. 30, 2024 at 7:48 PM MST|Updated: Jan. 30, 2024 at 7:50 PM MST

TUCSON, Ariz. (13 News) - The conflict surrounding a deadly DUI case in Pinal County is intensifying.

The victim’s family is asking the Arizona Attorney General’s Office to review the case.

They also said their rights have been violated.

It’s called the Victim’s Bill of Rights and it’s embedded in the Arizona Constitution.

ADDITIONAL COVERAGE

  • Victim’s family fights for felony charge in Pinal County
  • Pinal County Attorney called DPS evidence ‘irrefutable’

There are statutes designed to not only support victims but to protect their rights to justice and due process.

The family of Marshall and Mary Black said the Pinal County Attorney Office’s violated their constitutional rights.

Marshall was driving with Mary on SR 77 just outside Saddlebrook Ranch when they were involved in an accident.

The force of the crash was enough to trap them in the car and send Mary to the hospital with catastrophic injuries.

“She was on a ventilator fighting for her life,” said her daughter Nandia Black.

New details from an Arizona DPS report show the other driver had been arrested for DUI and taken to jail.

His blood alcohol level was .175, more than twice the legal limit.

He told a trooper he had “a few drinks, cocktail.”

The trooper noticed the driver had “watery eyes”, “slurred speech” and he could smell the “odor of alcohol” on him.

And when asked if the driver remembered what happened, he he told the trooper “I wrecked. Trust me I already know what’s going on. Let’s get it done with and take care of business. I know I’m impaired.”

He also admitted he “fell asleep” at the time.

DPS requested three DUI charges and aggravated assault with serious physical injury, which is classified as a felony.

Nandia said she had no idea and that the family had never heard from anyone in the Pinal County Attorney’s Office.

“No one reached out to my father, who was also seriously injured and hospitalized,” said the couple’s son, Andrew Black. “No one from his office reached out to anyone in my family.”

Mary died from her injuries months later.

“They were dealing with a fatality,” Nandia said. “Our family was never contacted.”

“As a former prosecutor and a former FBI agent for 27 years, the handling and treatment of your victims is a critical function of a prosecutor’s office in a law enforcement office,” Adam said. “And you must reach out to the victims and the victim’s family. And we had none of that here from Volkmer’s office.”

An issue Pima County Attorney Kent Volker just discovered after 13 News reached out to him about the case.

“To my knowledge, I’ve not seen any notations or anything to suggest that we did have a conversation with him,” Volker said. “I don’t believe we’ve independently spoken to him.”

He then pointed to a reason.

“My understanding, I believe, is he (Marshall) has since deceased. He has since passed,” he said.

13 News Investigates told Volker that’s not true, according to the family.

He then responded, “I would trust the family.”

13 News Investigates asked Volkmer if that’s normal to not contact the victims in a deadly DUI case.

“Certainly not, no. In fact, Arizona and my office are very proud we have our victim’s Bill of Rights,” he said. “And quite frankly, if we’ve not had any conversations with him, we’ve fallen short of our obligations and I’ll make no bones about it. I apologize to him. I don’t have an excuse. I don’t have a good reason why that happened.”

Nandia said she was the first to call months later to find out the status of the case and she was referred to victim’s services.

“They didn’t know much about what was going on in the case,” Nandia said, “And they were telling me, they’re reviewing the case. And my question is, what is to review when you have an individual who’s driving intoxicated? He was charged with speeding.”

What the family didn’t know at the time was the prosecuting attorney had dismissed all the charges, all misdemeanors, without prejudice two weeks after the crash.

Volkmer told 13 News Investigates that the decision had been based almost entirely on the DPS crash reconstruction report, which stated the driver didn’t speed, he didn’t cross lanes, and Marshall Black made a left-hand turn at the time of the crash.

Volkmer contends it shifts the blame away from the other driver.

“You have to establish that his behavior, his driving caused that accident. And we can’t do that,” he said.

Volkmer stood firm that the driver had not been speeding in my first interview with him.

“The estimated speed according to the DPS report was 55. If we could establish he was speeding, 100 percent we would charge, we would prosecute,” he said.

But that same DPS report has an inconsistency.

Volkmer pointed that out during the second interview.

“The estimated speed was 55. But if you turn a couple of pages later, there’s an opportunity for a checkbox,” he said. “And on that checkbox, the trooper indicated that the speed was unreasonable under the circumstances. So we’re following up to say -- hey, why did you make that check mark?”

13 News Investigates reported that Volkmer brought in an attorney for an independent review of the case, but has said he doesn’t think the outcome will change.
