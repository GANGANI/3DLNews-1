High risk of anxiety, burnout in ER health care workers during pandemic, study shows

by Muriel Bailey

Tue, March 9th 2021 at 5:20 PM
Updated Wed, March 10th 2021 at 6:07 PM
4
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
UAB emergency department staff (Courtesy: UAB)

A recent study shows a high risk of anxiety and burnout in emergency department health care workers from COVID-19.

ER physicians say the COVID-19 vaccines have brought down their anxiety and stress levels but, during the height of it all, they were trying to do their job and worried about being exposed to the virus.

The emergency department serves as the first stop for the majority of patients.

UAB'S emergency department director Dr. Walter Schrading has worked in UAB’s ER for five years. This last year spent battling the pandemic.

“This is one of the few times in my career that I can remember ever sort of being scared going to work,” Schrading said.

He says the fear of being exposed to covid played a huge role.

"I Could become ill or exposed to the disease and expose my family," he said, "even after just a routine shift. I myself would change clothes come home and shower after every shift wash all my clothes."

"I know of one of my collegues basically lived a part from his wife for almost the entire year during this pandemic because she was a vulnerable person who had cancer and he was afraid of infecting her so he lived a aprt from his wife," Schrading said.

In a recent study involving 1600 medical professionals nationwide nearly half of the workers in the ER reported anxiety associated with COVID-19. Almost 20 percent screened positive for an elevated risk of having PTSD.

"Part of the stress was the fear of catching covid yourself, you're inches away from someone's airways you're putting a tube down their lungs to breathe for them you're high high risk of catching covid yourself," Cullman Regional Medical Center ER Dir. Mark Christensen said.

Not only were health care workers dealing with the stress and anxiety of being exposed--they also experienced burnout from being so busy.

“At times it was overwhelming with the number of covid patients coming in," Christensen said, "it was a broad spectrum some with mild symptoms some were on death's door and trying to bring those people back to life and reassure others they would be fine it was very challenging."

The doctors say they have their own ways of coping with the stress and burnout but counseling is available to them.
