Local business owner loses $172,000 in bank fraud scheme; warns others

by Cynthia Gould

Tue, February 6th 2024 at 7:19 PM
Updated Wed, February 7th 2024 at 7:03 PM
Business owners bank account hacked; loses $172,000. (WBMA)

SHELBY COUNTY, Ala. (WBMA) — Drained bank accounts, thousands of dollars lost. If you see a bank name on your caller ID, experts say assume it's spoofed with a scammer on the other end.

There are other dirty tricks where con artists hack cell phones to get access to banking information. One local business owner shared his story to help warn others. "I had $172,000 boom, vanish," said Joshua Capps who owns a roofing company in North Shelby County. Capps says he is still trying to get at least part of that money back which was stolen last March from his Regions business account.

The FBI tracked the hacker to Arizona with the deposits made in three banks: JP Morgan Chase, Wells Fargo, and Green Dot Bank. How did they get access? "My iphone got hacked and they went into my personal notes where passwords and logins were stored. Never in a million years did I think somebody would get access," explained Capps.

According to Capps, when the bank texted a verification code, that went straight to the scammers. "Hopefully we will gain back $100,000. Definitely a hard hit," said Capps. The fraudulent accounts are now frozen as the FBI works on the case. The process is lengthy.

He advises you not store such information on your cellphone and if you do put a password on your notes files. Business fraud insurance is also available along with security technology to block hackers.

In another type of scam, Bob Trevan's friend Judy Martinez got a call in California from someone claiming to be with the fraud department at Wells Fargo asking for verification of her accounts. It was a spoof; the imposter call appeared to be a trusted bank phone number on caller ID.

"The follow up on this is the bank worked on it but said you willingly gave this information out, therefore they will not be able to get this money back. Turns out $147,000 is what this family lost," explained Trevan. He says it is so frustrating that so many people have hard earned money and they lose it so quickly.

The rampant banking fraud has the attention of Congress. "They are targeting vulnerable Americans to the tune of $9 billion dollars a year. Alabamians lost $65 million dollars," said Senator Katie Britt during a banking committee hearing last week.

Industry leaders are pushing more awareness with campaigns like BanksNeverAsk. "My dad is 86 years old. If it says bank on caller ID, he thinks it's his bank. We need to stop that," said Paul Benda with the American Banking Association. Benda says too often consumers are convinced to willingly hand over their login credentials. "These people are very good at what they do," explained Benda.

He calls on the telecommunication industry to ensure caller ID is accurate. If the authentication drops, it should read unknown.
