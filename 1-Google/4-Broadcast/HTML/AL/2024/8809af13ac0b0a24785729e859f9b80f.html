Oglala Sioux tribe bans South Dakota governor for border remarks

By Dakota News Now staff and Gray News staff
Published: Feb. 5, 2024 at 12:28 PM CST

SIOUX FALLS, S.D. (KSFY/AP/Gray News) - Tensions have flared after between the South Dakota governor and the Oglala Sioux after she spoke on Wednesday about wanting to send razor wire and security personnel to Texas to help deter immigration at the U.S.-Mexico border.

In her speech during a joint session in the state capitol in Pierre, Gov. Kristi Noem also claimed drug cartels were active in the state’s reservations.

“Due to the safety of the Oyate, effective immediately, you are hereby Banished from the homelands of the Oglala Sioux Tribe!” Tribe President Frank Star Comes Out said in a four-page statement Friday addressed to Noem.

“Oyate” is a word for people or nation, the Associated Press reported.

He objected to Noem’s use of the term “invasion,” saying that what is happening at the southern border doesn’t constitute one.

Star Comes Out accused the governor of trying to use the border issue to help get former U.S. President Donald Trump re-elected and boost her chances of becoming his running mate.

Many of those arriving at the U.S.-Mexico border are Indigenous people from places like El Salvador, Guatemala and Mexico who come “in search of jobs and a better life,” the tribal leader added.

“They don’t need to be put in cages, separated from their children like during the Trump Administration, or be cut up by razor wire furnished by, of all places, South Dakota,” he said.

He also accused her of “blatantly disrespect” when she claimed a gang called the Ghost Dancers is killing people on the Pine Ridge Reservation and is affiliated with cartels spreading drugs across the Midwest. The Ghost Dance is one of the Oglala Sioux’s “most sacred ceremonies,” the president said.

Star Comes Out asserted the tribe’s sovereignty, saying it’s not part of South Dakota.

Noem responded by pointed out her actions that she said are helping improve the lives of tribal members.

“It is unfortunate that President Sar Comes Out chose to bring politics into a discussion regarding the effects of our federal government’s failure to enforce federal laws at the southern border and on tribal lands. My focus continues to be on working together to solve those problems,” she said.

“As I told bipartisan Native American legislators earlier this week, ‘I am not the one with a stiff arm, here. You can’t build relationships if you don’t spend time together,’” Noem said. “I stand ready to work with any of our state’s Native American tribes to build such a relationship.”
