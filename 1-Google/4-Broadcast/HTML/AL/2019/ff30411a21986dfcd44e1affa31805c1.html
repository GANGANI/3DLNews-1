Alabama company's diabetes device could be a game-changer
by Ashley Gooden
Friday, Feb 1st 2019
Local company looking to make life easier for diabetics worldwide (Aerbetic Facebook Page)
BIRMINGHAM, Ala. —

Nearly 30 million Americans have diabetes, and around 600,000 thousand of those are Alabamians. ABC 33/40 is taking a look at how hard our communities are being hit by the disease and how a local company is working to help make life easier for diabetics worldwide.

ADVERTISEMENT

Of the nearly 600,000 Alabamians, around 130,000 of those don't even know they have diabetes. As researchers work towards a cure, experts are also coming up with new ideas to help alert diabetics and their caregivers.

Alabama the Beautiful has some ugly truths, and one of them is smack dab in the middle of "The Diabetes Belt."

"If you google the 'diabetes belt,' we are considered the highest in the nation, in the rate of diabetes, incidents of diabetes, and prevalence of diabetes," says Dr. Malek Karassi.

Endocrinology, Diabetes & Metabolism Specialist Affiliated with Shelby and Princeton Baptist Medical Centers.

Karassi says in addition to the foods we eat, there are several other reasons why diabetes is so prevalent: genetics, skipping out on exercise, and driving instead of walking.

"Also, sometimes lack of education, poverty, that would lead to obesity and eventually type 2 diabetes," says Karassi.

For Rose Bryant, it was genetics. She's had diabetes for 20 years, but with diet, exercise, and a lot of water, she's keeping everything under control. She is, however, looking for a non-invasive way to check her blood sugar.

"[I have] black little dots all over my little fingers from pricking so much, so it would be great if they could get something like that where you wouldn't have to prick your fingers oh god, it would be great," says Bryant.

COO and co-founder of Aerbetic, Eric Housh, and CEO Arnar Thors say they have the fix.

"What we're trying to do with Aerbetic is bring a solution to the market that is non-invasive, not annoying, not painful, it doesn't require that. We also are trying to bring something to the market that's affordable," says Housh.

Aerbetic looks like a watch, but detects the gases that indicate a diabetic event and alerts the patient or caregiver. Fun fact: the company will be headquarted in Downtown Birmingham's Historic Long Lewis Hardware building.

"We're gonna make worldwide change and it's gonna start right here in Birmingham, Alabama," says Housh.

The research for a cure for diabetes continues, but in the mean time, Bryant wants others to know it's important if you feel diabetic symptoms not to ignore them.

"Watch your body, your body talks to you, listen to it, that's all I ask," says Bryant.

Aerbetic is not on the market yet, they're looking at launching early 2020, but they are looking for people to join their trial run. If you or a diabetic you know or care for would be interested, click here.
