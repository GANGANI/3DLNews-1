Amid nationwide teacher shortage, could school shootings play a role in staffing problems?

by Erin Wise

Sat, May 28th 2022 at 7:43 PM
Updated Mon, May 30th 2022 at 11:10 AM
Amid nationwide teacher shortage, could school shootings play a role in staffing problems?, ABC 33/40 News

    TOPICS:

    Alabama,United StatesFlorida,United StatesParklandParkland,Florida,United StatesTexas,United Stateseducationsocial issuesMike Howard

Teachers are hard to come by right now amid a nationwide shortage. Some educators told ABC 33/40 that school shootings make that problem worse--while others believed it has little to no impact on recruitment and retention.

According to a report on teacher recruitment and retention in Alabama, “the state’s educator prep graduates have declined nearly 20%. The number of bachelor’s degrees earned in traditional teacher education fields has declined 26% over the last 18 years.” The report did not specify the reason behind the decline.

But, could school shootings be or become a factor in a declining workforce? ABC 33/40 reached out to Alabama educators with this question.

St. Clair County Superintendent, Dr. Mike Howard told ABC 33/40 in a statement:

Right now, education is under the microscope and fewer people are entering the field. Events like this only make the situation worse. No one wants to feel unsafe in a place they work, and many are apprehensive about overseeing the safety of so many children and adults in a school environment. It’s a big responsibility to ensure everyone’s safety. We have over 11,000 staff and students in our system, and I feel that weight, especially after tragedies.

Michael Sibley, the Director of Communications for the Alabama Department of Education doesn't anticipate a negative impact from the Uvalde school shooting.

Unfortunately, we live in a society where violence can erupt anywhere. Being an educator is a calling, a special motivation to impact the minds and lives of young people, and in turn, impact communities. There are more than 740,000 students and nearly 50,000 public school teachers in Alabama. The important work of educating our young and producing the leaders and workforce of tomorrow will not be deterred by any isolated incident. What happened in Texas is a tragedy; however, educators are steadfast and resolute in their responsibilities to nurture young minds and bring out the best that society has to offer. That will not change.

After the school shooting at Marjory Douglas High School in Parkland, Florida killed 14 students and three teachers--the National Education Association surveyed its members. 60 % of its respondents said they "were worried that there could be a mass shooting in their schools."

Dr. Christine Drew, an associate professor of special education at Auburn University, said the department is doing the best it can to prepare future teachers.

"It's really hard to know where to start with how to prepare future teachers. Other than to hold space and listen to them and validate their fears or worries and support them in being the best teachers they can while being honest with the challenges they face," said Dr. Drew. "In general, we have not always addressed tragedies like this head-on. We have a lot of students with differing opinions about changes or modifications to laws or school protocols, so we try to prepare teachers as best we can for a lot of different issues that could come up in classrooms."

Dr. Drew said teachers deal with a lot of stress.

"Aggression from students or dealing with parents who get frustrated--they are really up against a lot in the real world," said Dr. Drew.

There are also the stressors experienced during the last two years during the pandemic, as well as barriers put up by laws and school boards--not to forget pay that doesn't keep up with inflation.

Drew believed an Increase in school shootings, could also be a factor in leaving the field.

"If you're not and teacher and you've never had to hold a student with severe anxiety who now thinks someone is coming to kill them, while they cry--it is really hard for the average person to understand that level of stress. It's not an easy thing," said Dr. Drew. "There are teachers who are probably reconsidering. There are teachers considering retirement which is a big issue in education right now. And they may be reconsidering their choice of major. We know that across the nation special education and education programs, in general, are seeing a decline in enrollment. That is at the bachelor's, master's, and doctorate levels. "

Since the Columbine High School shooting in 1999, conversations in teachers' lounges have changed.

In 1981, it was Susan Gaskin's first year teaching.

"My worst fear was to fail the children and not be able to teach them what I needed them to know," said Gaskin.

She retired in 2019. ABC 33/40, asked what was her worst fear then.

"Violence."

Gaskin was mainly concerned with violence outside of the classroom. However, more and more often, violence is happening inside schools--like in Uvalde.

"Anyone who's been there through Columbine, through Sandy Hook, through Marjory Douglas, and now Robb. I think we all go back and think about where we were when that happened," said Gaskin. "It's always still at the back of your mind."

Gaskin was actually subbing at an East Alabama school during the Uvalde shooting.

Although those moments are memorable, Gaskin believed there are issues more pressing in education that deter teachers. However, she said something has to be done to prevent further violent school shootings.

Better mental health services for school districts is the first improvement she'd like to see.

"Child psychologists. We've got to have mental health professionals who are in these buildings who can say 'this child is a danger to themselves and others and we need to put them somewhere that they can get the help they need," she said.

Gaskin would not be opposed to stricter background checks to buy guns but does not think an outright ban on guns would help.

According to the 2018 survey from NEA, most educators supported stricter gun law proposals.

The survey showed 85% supported bans of assault-style weapons. 84 % supported banning bump stocks that modified semi-automatic weapons to operate as machine guns. 99% approved of universal background checks. 78% agreed with raising the age to buy a gun to 21. 91% wanted measures to prevent mentally-ill individuals from getting guns.
