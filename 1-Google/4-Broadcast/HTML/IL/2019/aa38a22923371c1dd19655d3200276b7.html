Battling addiction: 'I joined a pill gang of soccer moms'
by Lauren Kostiuk
Wednesday, Dec 11th 2019
jaime smith.JPG
ST. JOSEPH, Ill. (WICS/WCCU) —

It's the story of addiction you don't always expect.

ADVERTISEMENT

Jaime Smith grew up in Mahomet, Ill. She is a mom of four and a loving wife. She is also a recovering heroin addict.

"I was sitting in a bathroom of a $200,000 home in St. Joe, snorting heroin with a bunch of other mothers,” Smith said.

Smith's story doesn't start in a dark alley or on some street corner. Instead, it starts in an upper-class neighborhood in Champaign County.

In 2013, Smith and her family moved to St. Joseph - a small town of about 4,000 people.

From the outside, it looked like Smith had it all — a high-paying career, great family and a big house.

But on the inside, she was battling a dark and dangerous secret.

She said she always felt like she suffered from some type of emotional disability or mental illness.

But she was never medically diagnosed.

"I was going through a lot. My personal life was kind of unraveling. It seemed like one thing after another was happening and I was sprawling out of control," she said.

So, Smith went to her local doctor for help. They prescribed her anti-depressants.

But some women in town had something else to offer.

"They knew I was under a lot of stress and they saw me upset one time,” Smith said. “They approached me and before I knew it, I was in a pill gang."

Looking back now, Smith believes the anti-depressants were the start of her addiction. She believes she was stressed and depressed and should’ve been sent to a therapist instead of receiving a prescription.

Smith then started taking Hydrocodone in order to sleep.

"I was like, ‘Okay it is in a prescription bottle. It came from the doctor. It will help me sleep. No big deal,’” Smith said. “I had taken the medication before for surgeries, birth of my children, all of those things. I never had a problem."

But that quickly changed.

"Within two weeks, I had to take it every single day, all day long and within a month I was combining it with Oxycontin, Percocet and anything I could get my hands on,” she said.

Those drugs came from a group of housewives.

"We were sitting in our nice fancy homes, our husbands were at work and we were popping and swapping pills together,” Smith said.

Their trick was "doctor shopping" in the area.

"They would go to the doctor and they would get Percocet and then I would go to the doctor and get hydrocodone,” she said. “We all had these different medications and we would trade."

No one knew what was going on, not even her family.

Smith went from being shy to the life of the party.

"I was talking to everybody. I was outgoing, I was energetic and all the shyness went away,” she said. “I was like, ‘No one will know because I am like the happiest person on Earth.’”

But unlike the others, it became harder for Smith to control her addiction.

"Within two months I was a full-blown addict,” she said. “My mind was on one thing and one thing only, that was when and where I could get my next bottle of pills."

At 35-years-old she began snorting cocaine and discovered heroin.

It was cheap and easy to get.

"I was having heroin delivered right here to my house and put in my mailbox. I would leave a $10 bill, or a $20 bill and I would have enough heroin to get me through that day,” Smith said.

It got to a point where Smith's addiction was unstoppable.

"It's complete torture. I was so sick that I could not get out of bed so even if I wanted to stop, I didn't know how to stop,” she said.

Smith said she was too ashamed to admit she had a problem.

"Here I am, you know 35-years-old, a mother of four kids, an amazing husband and I am in my bathroom snorting heroin,” Smith said. "It was literally like a demon took over my brain and lived inside of my head, but I never had that demon before I picked up a needle."

That demon made her hit rock bottom.

“I took off. I quit my job. I cleared out all of the money and I moved to Chicago."

This is just the beginning of Smith’s journey. In the second part of our series, we will tell what happened next that made her get help and why she is telling her story today.

That will be airing next Wednesday, December 18.
