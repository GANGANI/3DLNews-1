5 Marines confirmed dead after helicopter crash from Nevada to California, military says

by The National Desk

Thu, February 8th 2024 at 7:15 AM
Updated Thu, February 8th 2024 at 7:32 AM
7

FILE - A Marine CH-53E Super Stallion helicopter flies during training at Marine Corps Air Station Miramar in San Diego, Tuesday, Feb. 6, 2024. (K.C. Alfred/The San Diego Union-Tribune via AP)

    TOPICS:

    MarinesHelicopterCrashCaliforniaNevadaCH-53E3rd Marine Aircraft WingInvestigation

LAS VEGAS (TND) — The five Marines who went missing on a helicopter flight from Nevada to California have been confirmed dead.

"Five Marines with Marine Heavy Helicopter Squadron 361, Marine Aircraft Group 16, 3rd Marine Aircraft Wing have been confirmed deceased following a CH-53E helicopter crash on Feb. 6, 2024," according to a release from 3rd Marine Aircraft Wing.

The identity of the Marine will not be released until 24 hours after all next-of-kin have been notified, officials said.

Recovering the remains of the Marines and equipment has begun. An investigation is underway.

"It is with a heavy heart and profound sadness that I share the loss of five outstanding Marines from 3d Marine Aircraft Wing and the “Flying Tigers,” Maj. Gen. Michael J. Borgschulte, commander of 3rd Marine Aircraft Wing, said in a statement. "These pilots and crewmembers were serving a calling greater than self and were proud to do so. We will forever be grateful for their call to duty and selfless service. To the families of our fallen Marines, we send our deepest condolences and commit to ensuring your support and care during this incredibly difficult time."

The CH-53E Super Stallion helicopter left from Creech Air Force Base outside Las Vegas on Tuesday, the U.S. Marine Corps' 3rd Marine Aircraft Wing said in a statement. It was bound for Marine Corps Air Station Miramar in San Diego County.

The craft was found just after 9 a.m. Wednesday morning outside San Diego in Pine Valley, according to a Facebook post from the 3rd Marine Aircraft Wing.

Waves of heavy downpours hit the area throughout the night Tuesday from a historic storm that has drenched California this week.

The National Weather Service in San Diego called for 6 to 10 inches (15 to 25 centimeters) of snow in the mountains above 5,000 feet (1,524 meters) and gusty winds late Wednesday.
