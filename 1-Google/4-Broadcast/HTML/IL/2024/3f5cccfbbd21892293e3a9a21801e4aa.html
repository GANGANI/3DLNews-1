Energy Department official defends liquified natural gas pause facing pushback

by AUSTIN DENEAN | The National Desk

Thu, February 8th 2024 at 12:35 PM
Updated Thu, February 8th 2024 at 1:54 PM
4

WASHINGTON (TND) — A bipartisan group of lawmakers are voicing concerns with the Biden administration’s plan to pause the consideration of new natural gas export terminals in the U.S. in an election year decision that has also drawn fierce blowback from the energy industry.

The Senate Energy Committee held the second hearing this week examining the administration’s planned pause that has been met with stiff resistance from Republicans and some moderate Democrats.

David Turk, Energy Department deputy secretary, defended the administration’s decision in front of senators on Thursday.

The United States’ LNG capacity has doubled in recent years and is set to double once again under projects that have already been approved. It comes after the U.S. took over the top spot as the world’s largest LNG exporter last year and helped fill some gaps left by Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, which created an energy crunch as America and its European allies tried to reconfigure energy supply lines to punish Moscow.

“In fact, I would find it irresponsible if we weren't taking a step back and undertaking this rigorous analysis,” Turk said. “The U.S. Department of Energy remains committed to keeping energy affordable for all Americans, strengthening energy security for us and for our allies and protecting against the worst impacts of climate change.”

Turk said the administration is “very comfortable” about the EU’s energy supply moving forward as the pause does not stop current or approved projects and that the department is projecting demand to decrease over time.

Energy Department officials and the White House have said the pause will allow the administration to study proposed projects for expanded production and what impact they will have on the environment and domestic energy stability. Some supporters of the plan have said that it will make domestic prices more sustainable.

The natural gas industry has been vehemently opposed to the idea of a pause and said it would be a “win for Russia.” They have also voiced concerns that the pause could delay projects from coming online and disincentivize investment into production, which is expensive and takes years to develop into a functioning site.

“After eight years of U.S. LNG exports, the benefits are no longer theoretical. The facts are clear U.S. LNG has been determined to be in the public interest. The decision to pause LNG export approvals could have far-reaching consequences, potentially jeopardizing international relationships, economic stability and progress towards our environmental objectives,” said Charles Riedl, executive director of the Center for LNG, a trade group.

Energy Secretary Jennifer Granholm said when it was announced that the pause wouldn’t have any immediate effect on gas supplies to Europe and Asia, but that has been met with a skeptical response and the pause has concerned European allies about their energy futures even though the European Commission has said it will not have any short- or medium-term effects on its security of its supply.

“We feel that indeed, the U.S. and EU and the UK need to stand together when we're looking at Ukraine and the situation that we face there. And by not being able, perhaps, to honor the commitments that have been made in the United States towards its allies, you are going to indeed force us to continue to do business with Russia,” said Dr. James Watson, secretary general of Eurogas, a trade group for the European gas industry.

Many European countries have had to continue to rely on Russia for energy needs despite sanctions and other penalties resulting from the war in Ukraine, but the region’s leaders are trying to reduce their dependence on Moscow. They have attributed a lot of the early successes to the expanded exports from American suppliers.

Biden had been facing pressure from environmental groups and younger voters to slow the production of fossil fuels due to the connections between production and an increase in the emission of greenhouse gases that fuel global warming. Some industry groups and Republicans have accused Biden of acting for strictly political reasons in pursuing the pause.

“That's what it is Mr. Chairman, a transparent, very transparent election-year payoff to appease radical environmentalist who want to end exports of American natural gas,” said Sen. John Barrasso, R-Wyo., and the committee’s ranking member.

Sen. Joe Manchin, who has pushed for more U.S. production of fossil fuels and is a key swing vote in any Senate legislation, also accused the White House of playing politics with the decision to pause approvals.

“The White House has shown it is so concerned with indulging radical climate activists and is willing to play politics with our energy security and that of our allies. This reminds me all too well of the administration's misguided unlawful pause on oil and gas leasing,” the West Virginia Democrat said.

The pause on natural gas comes amid a back-and-forth relationship between Biden’s administration and the energy industry. While he has made some moves to prioritize the environment in his energy policy, he has also taken some steps to expand production like approving the controversial Willow oil drilling project in Alaska.

While some lawmakers are hoping to act legislatively to prevent the pause, the bill would face a difficult path forward through a Democratic-controlled Senate and may be vetoed by the president.
