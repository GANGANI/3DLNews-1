The origins of KOATKOAT's history dates back well before we were a television station. In fact, KOAT was a radio station. As technology advanced through the years, television was expanding across the country.In the 1950s, the then-owners of KOAT were looking to expand into television, first applying for a license to broadcast on channel 13, but later changing it to channel 7. After months of fighting with other interested broadcasters, KOAT was able to secure its license. Studio facilities were built to prepare for KOAT's first broadcast. Everything was set to go until an equipment setback caused a delay. On Oct. 2, 1953, KOAT went on the air for the very first time. The first shows to hit the airwaves were a western show and a recap of a college football game. KOAT's first newscast was anchored by Dick Lewis and aired for 15 minutes at 11 p.m. Slideshow above: A look at photos of KOAT through the yearsThe 1960sAs the '50s rolled on, so did KOAT. The station continued to grow the news team, and in the 1960s, the team became stronger and focused on resources to inform New Mexicans. But the team remained small, with only five people covering the entire state of New Mexico and each member of the team having multiple talents. The 1960s was a period of growth for KOAT. As the station grew, it became too large for its original location at Central Avenue and Tulane Drive. A new building was constructed on University Boulevard near Lomas Boulevard. It was a place that allowed the staff to move around Albuquerque much easier than the previous location in Nob Hill. As KOAT grew in the '60s, so did its news coverage. The news team covered impactful events, like the election of President John F. Kennedy and his trip to New Mexico. Tierra Amarilla Courthouse RaidThe team also covered the civil rights movement and how that played out in New Mexico. One of the biggest moments of the time was the raid of the Tierra Amarilla Courthouse led by Reies Lopez Tijerina, a controversial figure in New Mexico. Some called him a civil rights activist, others blamed him for instilling violence in the state. On June 5, 1967, the group led by Reies Lopez Tijerina had its goals: Capture the district attorney, free jailed Alianza members and raise awareness for the group's movement. During the chaos of the raid, a jail guard was shot and a sheriff's deputy was injured. KOAT news crews were there that day, one member of the crew filming an interview with the sheriff, giving the film to the airplane pilot to fly back to Albuquerque in time for the 6 p.m. news. The news crew said it was unheard of, at the time, for such a quick turnaround for an interview from 350 miles away. Tijerina escaped, leading to a manhunt. He later surrendered and spent two years in jail. Moon landingNot only did KOAT cover New Mexico's biggest stories of the 1960s, the biggest event of mankind was also shown on KOAT's airwaves.It's a huge moment in history and broadcast television — the 1969 moon landing. It became one of the most-watched events in television history that left many in awe. Television technology advanced to the point of having a camera on a spacecraft that was able to broadcast pictures back to Earth. It marked a great achievement of television's technological advances. The 1970sMuch of the social disorder from the decade before was spilling into the 1970s.Anti-war demonstrations erupted across the country. Many were protesting the war in Vietnam. Many demonstrations took place on college campuses, including at the University of New Mexico. News crews were among those injured in the protests. New Mexico's role in WatergateThe 1970s also brought Watergate, with New Mexico becoming part of the story. U.S. Sen. Joseph Montoya, from New Mexico, was one of the members of the Watergate Committee. Because of that tie to New Mexico, KOAT reporters were sent to Washington to cover part of the hearings. Realizing the importance of stories that involve politics, KOAT developed a team that would cover the state legislature. Crime on the rise and Crime StoppersCrime in Albuquerque was also a problem at the time.At the time, gangs and drugs were two issues getting attention because of the influence of young people. All of the issues regarding crime made for civil unrest during the decade. It led two police officers to take action and go on strike. Those officers eventually went back to work. In 1976, an Albuquerque Police Department detective was investigating a murder and came to KOAT with the idea of reenacting the crime to help solve the crime. Then-KOAT general manager Max Sklower liked the idea because it was voluntary and thought it would work in helping the police. Not long after the story aired, a phone call came in to help solve that murder.The segment later became known as Crime Stoppers. Crime Stoppers has now become an international organization, tracing its early beginnings to Albuquerque and KOAT. The 1980sAs the 1970s came to an end, the 1980s began with one of the most infamous moments in New Mexico's history, the New Mexico State Penitentiary Riot. It was one of the worst prison riots in the country. KOAT was one of the first to cover the riot. Video Above: Watch KOAT's coverage of the 1980 state prison riot The story played out for many years, with KOAT following the story every step of the way. Reporters followed every hearing and every lawsuit and analyzed how the system may have failed. Beverly Shoemaker forced a helicopter pilot to land in the state prison yard to bust her boyfriend and two others out of jail. Sky 7 was in the air when the chase in the helicopter began. The inmates were eventually captured. One of them was the murder of Linda Lee Daniels.Daniels was taking groceries into her house and was abducted from the front lawn of her home. Daniels' body was found a week later in Tijeras Canyon. Two people were convicted and given life sentences, while two others were given deals in exchange for testimony. Another high-profile case of the 1980s was the murder of Cindy Ray. Ray was a pregnant mother who was forced into a car and driven to the east mountains before being strangled. Ray was found to have bled to death after her baby was cut from her body. Pierce was later convicted of murder and sentenced to life in prison. The baby survived. Changes at KOAT in the 1980sAs coverage of New Mexico's stories grew in the 1980s, so did KOAT. In 1979, KOAT began to hit its stride and introduced the first weeknight co-anchor team of Johnny Morris and Mary Lynn Roper. The pair anchored the news alongside Jim Boggio on sports and Howard Morgan with the weather. The team dominated ratings for six years. During that time, KOAT moved to its third and current location at Carlisle Boulevard and Comanche Road in northeast Albuquerque. Slideshow above: A look at Sky 7 through the yearsThe location is a state-of-the-art building that has larger studios and workspace compared to KOAT's first two locations. The building was designed so Sky 7 could have a home outside of the station. Technology advancementsAs KOAT expanded, so did technology. Overall, technology was advancing all around us, leading to so many advancements. Joe Diaz was there in the 1980s, covering all the new technology that influences our everyday life today. New things like digital watches, surround sound systems, and even the home computer were once new and unheard-of concepts. These technological evolutions helped lead all of us into the Internet Age. The 1990s The 1990s will be remembered as the time the world went online, connecting people together and changing the way news was gathered. The internet changed everything. Everything was becoming digitized. It changed the ability to put stories into context and have quick access to information. With that new enhancement of technology came more places to get news across the globe. While that competition for news presented a challenge for local stations, it created an opportunity to strengthen the connection to the community. KOAT in the community To help connect with the community, KOAT created KOATs for Kids, a yearly drive to help children across the state stay warm during the winter.KOAT also developed a series of stories to help give resources to young parents called "New Mexico Children First." It was a way to connect with other families and deal with issues that they were concerned with. TV was a powerful tool to help reach those parents. The series received recognition from the National Association of Broadcasters in 1997.Not only did we connect with New Mexico through a series of stories and charitable events, we traveled the state celebrating the culture, uniqueness and the people of New Mexico. 1992 Christmas Eve fatal DWI crashThe 1990s was not unlike any other decade. While technology advanced and news gathering changed, the horrific stories did not. There were several that would define the decade. On Christmas Eve in 1992, a horrific head-on crash happened. Gordon House admitted to drinking several beers before slamming into a car, killing Melanie Cravens and her three children. Cravens' husband was also injured in the crash. The family was on their way to look at Christmas lights. At the time, New Mexico had the highest DWI rates in the country. After the crash, relatives of the family spent the next several decades trying to harden the rules and laws against drunk driving and make a difference in New Mexico. Hollywood Video murdersAnother horrific tragedy happened on March 3, 1996. Three employees working into the morning hours at a Hollywood Video store were tied up and executed. The bodies of the video store employees were found the next morning. The grandparents of one of the teens were waiting outside in the parking lot. They were kidnapped and taken to the east mountains, where they were also murdered. Their bodies were found several days later, huddled together. Esther Beckley and Shane Harrison initially planned to rob the store to get money and to impress her lover. Beckley took responsibility for her part in the murders but also opened up about other choices she made in life. The 2000sAs New Mexico entered the new millennium, big stories began to impact our community. The Cerro Grande FireIn the summer of 2000, tragedy struck northern New Mexico. The Cerro Grande Fire tore through Los Alamos, burning several homes. Video Above: KOAT's live coverage of the Cerro Grande Fire on May 10, 2000The fire began as a controlled burn in the national forest. Weather conditions caused the prescribed fire to burn out of control. The fire was on the verge of destroying the entire community. The fire burned for several weeks before it was finally extinguished. Four hundred homes, along with 11 structures at Los Alamos National Lab, were destroyed in the fire. Nearly 3,000 were killed on that day alone. Of that number, 343 were America's Bravest — firefighters who risked their lives to save others. The entire nation was heartbroken. The tragedy hit home in New Mexico. Al Marchand of Alamogordo was a flight attendant on one of the flights that crashed into the World Trade Center. Action 7 News spoke with his wife after the tragedy. She wanted people to know who her husband was and share his life with New Mexico. The World Trade Center will always be remembered in New Mexico. The Sacred Heart Church transformed parts of the steel from the collapsed tower into a bell tower for the church. It's a symbol of hope, strength and remembrance. Politics in New MexicoAs the country began to change and heal after Sept. 11, New Mexico also began to change. New leadership was put in place by New Mexico's voters. Bill Richardson was elected governor in 2002. Richardson cut taxes and brought film production companies to the state. Richardson was also a key figure in bringing the space industry to New Mexico. The New Mexico Spaceport broke ground in 2009. It later became home to Virgin Galactic. High-profile cases of the 2000sThe decade also saw some unforgettable crimes. In 2004, Cody Posey confessed to killing his family and tried to hide their bodies in a pile of manure. Former national news anchor Sam Donaldson discovered the scene at his ranch in Hondo, New Mexico. West Mesa MurdersA few years later, in February 2009, a gruesome discovery in southwest Albuquerque. Christine Ross was walking her dog Ruca when the pair found a large femur bone on their route. Police later determined the bone was human. A once-empty desert became a large crime scene. Police found 11 bodies and an unborn child. The 2010s In the 2010s, an increase in gun violence and school shootings nationwide and in New Mexico. In 2014, two students were shot and injured at Berrendo Middle School in Roswell. In 2017, two students were shot and killed in a school shooting at Aztec High School in northern New Mexico. In 2019, a student took a gun to V. Sue Cleveland High School in Rio Rancho and planned on shooting several students. The gun misfired and no one was hurt. Emcore Office ShootingThree school shootings during the decade, but the violence didn't stop there. On July 12, 2010, Robert Reza entered an office building and shot his girlfriend and several others. Two employees died before Reza turned the gun on himself. This shooting served as a reminder to local businesses of potential dangers and inspired police departments to train for the worst. James Boyd ShootingThe decade also saw events that sparked police reform across the country, including Albuquerque. In 2014, James Boyd was shot and killed by two officers of the Albuquerque Police Department while he was camping in the foothills. The decade would also end with a change in leadership once again when Michelle Lujan Grisham was elected governor in 2018. She would soon face a global pandemic more than 1 year after taking office.     The 1960s

    As the '50s rolled on, so did KOAT. The station continued to grow the news team, and in the 1960s, the team became stronger and focused on resources to inform New Mexicans. But the team remained small, with only five people covering the entire state of New Mexico and each member of the team having multiple talents.

    The 1960s was a period of growth for KOAT. As the station grew, it became too large for its original location at Central Avenue and Tulane Drive. A new building was constructed on University Boulevard near Lomas Boulevard. It was a place that allowed the staff to move around Albuquerque much easier than the previous location in Nob Hill.

    As KOAT grew in the '60s, so did its news coverage.

    Hearst Owned

    The news team covered impactful events, like the election of President John F. Kennedy and his trip to New Mexico.

    Tierra Amarilla Courthouse Raid

    The team also covered the civil rights movement and how that played out in New Mexico.

    One of the biggest moments of the time was the raid of the Tierra Amarilla Courthouse led by Reies Lopez Tijerina, a controversial figure in New Mexico. Some called him a civil rights activist, others blamed him for instilling violence in the state. On June 5, 1967, the group led by Reies Lopez Tijerina had its goals: Capture the district attorney, free jailed Alianza members and raise awareness for the group's movement. During the chaos of the raid, a jail guard was shot and a sheriff's deputy was injured.

    KOAT news crews were there that day, one member of the crew filming an interview with the sheriff, giving the film to the airplane pilot to fly back to Albuquerque in time for the 6 p.m. news. The news crew said it was unheard of, at the time, for such a quick turnaround for an interview from 350 miles away.

    Tijerina escaped, leading to a manhunt. He later surrendered and spent two years in jail.

    Moon landing

    Not only did KOAT cover New Mexico's biggest stories of the 1960s, the biggest event of mankind was also shown on KOAT's airwaves.

    It's a huge moment in history and broadcast television — the 1969 moon landing. It became one of the most-watched events in television history that left many in awe.

    Television technology advanced to the point of having a camera on a spacecraft that was able to broadcast pictures back to Earth. It marked a great achievement of television's technological advances.

    The 1970s

    Much of the social disorder from the decade before was spilling into the 1970s.

    Anti-war demonstrations erupted across the country. Many were protesting the war in Vietnam.

    Many demonstrations took place on college campuses, including at the University of New Mexico.

    News crews were among those injured in the protests.

    New Mexico's role in Watergate

    The 1970s also brought Watergate, with New Mexico becoming part of the story.

    U.S. Sen. Joseph Montoya, from New Mexico, was one of the members of the Watergate Committee. Because of that tie to New Mexico, KOAT reporters were sent to Washington to cover part of the hearings.

    Realizing the importance of stories that involve politics, KOAT developed a team that would cover the state legislature.

    Crime on the rise and Crime Stoppers

    Crime in Albuquerque was also a problem at the time.

    At the time, gangs and drugs were two issues getting attention because of the influence of young people.

    All of the issues regarding crime made for civil unrest during the decade. It led two police officers to take action and go on strike. Those officers eventually went back to work.

    In 1976, an Albuquerque Police Department detective was investigating a murder and came to KOAT with the idea of reenacting the crime to help solve the crime. Then-KOAT general manager Max Sklower liked the idea because it was voluntary and thought it would work in helping the police. Not long after the story aired, a phone call came in to help solve that murder.

    The segment later became known as Crime Stoppers. Crime Stoppers has now become an international organization, tracing its early beginnings to Albuquerque and KOAT.

    The 1980s

    As the 1970s came to an end, the 1980s began with one of the most infamous moments in New Mexico's history, the New Mexico State Penitentiary Riot. It was one of the worst prison riots in the country.

    KOAT was one of the first to cover the riot.

    Video Above: Watch KOAT's coverage of the 1980 state prison riot

    The story played out for many years, with KOAT following the story every step of the way.

    Reporters followed every hearing and every lawsuit and analyzed how the system may have failed.

    Video Above: The Action 7 News team covers the aftermath of the 1980 prison riot

    Prison escape

    There were also multiple prison breaks during the 1980s, including a bizarre one involving a helicopter.

    Beverly Shoemaker forced a helicopter pilot to land in the state prison yard to bust her boyfriend and two others out of jail.

    Sky 7 was in the air when the chase in the helicopter began. The inmates were eventually captured.

    Read more: Relive New Mexico's most bizarre prison break

    The inmates who escaped in the helicopter were eventually captured.

    High-profile cases of the 1980s

    There were some very serious stories in the 1980s that KOAT's growing news team covered.

    One of them was the murder of Linda Lee Daniels.

    Daniels was taking groceries into her house and was abducted from the front lawn of her home. Daniels' body was found a week later in Tijeras Canyon.

    Two people were convicted and given life sentences, while two others were given deals in exchange for testimony.

    Another high-profile case of the 1980s was the murder of Cindy Ray. Ray was a pregnant mother who was forced into a car and driven to the east mountains before being strangled. Ray was found to have bled to death after her baby was cut from her body.

    Read more: New Mexico's most notorious female killers | Watch: New Mexico's most notorious female killers

    Darci Pierce took the baby to the hospital, claiming she had given birth, but doctors didn't believe her story and called police. Pierce was later convicted of murder and sentenced to life in prison. The baby survived.

    Changes at KOAT in the 1980s

    As coverage of New Mexico's stories grew in the 1980s, so did KOAT.

    In 1979, KOAT began to hit its stride and introduced the first weeknight co-anchor team of Johnny Morris and Mary Lynn Roper. The pair anchored the news alongside Jim Boggio on sports and Howard Morgan with the weather.

    The team dominated ratings for six years.

    During that time, KOAT moved to its third and current location at Carlisle Boulevard and Comanche Road in northeast Albuquerque.

    Technology advancements

    As KOAT expanded, so did technology.

    Overall, technology was advancing all around us, leading to so many advancements. Joe Diaz was there in the 1980s, covering all the new technology that influences our everyday life today.

    New things like digital watches, surround sound systems, and even the home computer were once new and unheard-of concepts.

    These technological evolutions helped lead all of us into the Internet Age.

    The 1990s

    The 1990s will be remembered as the time the world went online, connecting people together and changing the way news was gathered.

    The internet changed everything. Everything was becoming digitized. It changed the ability to put stories into context and have quick access to information.

    With that new enhancement of technology came more places to get news across the globe. While that competition for news presented a challenge for local stations, it created an opportunity to strengthen the connection to the community.

    KOAT in the community

    To help connect with the community, KOAT created KOATs for Kids, a yearly drive to help children across the state stay warm during the winter.

    KOAT also developed a series of stories to help give resources to young parents called "New Mexico Children First." It was a way to connect with other families and deal with issues that they were concerned with. TV was a powerful tool to help reach those parents. The series received recognition from the National Association of Broadcasters in 1997.

    Not only did we connect with New Mexico through a series of stories and charitable events, we traveled the state celebrating the culture, uniqueness and the people of New Mexico.

    1992 Christmas Eve fatal DWI crash

    The 1990s was not unlike any other decade. While technology advanced and news gathering changed, the horrific stories did not. There were several that would define the decade.

    On Christmas Eve in 1992, a horrific head-on crash happened. Gordon House admitted to drinking several beers before slamming into a car, killing Melanie Cravens and her three children. Cravens' husband was also injured in the crash. The family was on their way to look at Christmas lights.

    At the time, New Mexico had the highest DWI rates in the country. After the crash, relatives of the family spent the next several decades trying to harden the rules and laws against drunk driving and make a difference in New Mexico.

    Hollywood Video murders

    Another horrific tragedy happened on March 3, 1996.

    Three employees working into the morning hours at a Hollywood Video store were tied up and executed. The bodies of the video store employees were found the next morning.

    The grandparents of one of the teens were waiting outside in the parking lot. They were kidnapped and taken to the east mountains, where they were also murdered. Their bodies were found several days later, huddled together.

    Esther Beckley and Shane Harrison initially planned to rob the store to get money and to impress her lover.

    Beckley took responsibility for her part in the murders but also opened up about other choices she made in life.

    Video Above: Action 7 News anchor Dianne Anderson interviews convicted murder Esther Beckley in a multi-part series

    David Parker Ray

    In the final year of the 1990s, another high-profile story rippled across New Mexico.

    A woman who was kidnapped and held as a sex slave managed to escape from her captor. She was discovered by neighbors before getting help from the police.

    Her captor was David Parker Ray. Police searched his property and found a trailer next to it — a torture chamber that Ray called his "toy box."

    Watch More: Download the Very Local app to learn more about David Parker Ray on Hometown Tragedy

    Ray was convicted of kidnapping and sexual assault in 2001. Investigators say he bragged that there were more victims. He agreed to tell police but backed out of the deal and died in prison in 2002. He was only a few months into a 223-year prison sentence.

    The 2000s

    As New Mexico entered the new millennium, big stories began to impact our community.

    The Cerro Grande Fire

    In the summer of 2000, tragedy struck northern New Mexico. The Cerro Grande Fire tore through Los Alamos, burning several homes.

    Video Above: KOAT's live coverage of the Cerro Grande Fire on May 10, 2000

    The fire began as a controlled burn in the national forest. Weather conditions caused the prescribed fire to burn out of control. The fire was on the verge of destroying the entire community.

    The fire burned for several weeks before it was finally extinguished. Four hundred homes, along with 11 structures at Los Alamos National Lab, were destroyed in the fire.

    Video Above: Action 7 News Special - After Cerro Grande

    Sept. 11, 2001

    As residents and the state were recovering from the wildfire, another tragedy struck.

    On Sept. 11, 2001, the United States was attacked. Planes were hijacked and crashed into the World Trade Center in New York, the Pentagon in Washington, D.C., and another crashed into a field in Pennsylvania. Nearly 3,000 were killed on that day alone. Of that number, 343 were America's Bravest — firefighters who risked their lives to save others.

    The entire nation was heartbroken.

    The tragedy hit home in New Mexico. Al Marchand of Alamogordo was a flight attendant on one of the flights that crashed into the World Trade Center.

    Action 7 News spoke with his wife after the tragedy.

    She wanted people to know who her husband was and share his life with New Mexico.

    The World Trade Center will always be remembered in New Mexico. The Sacred Heart Church transformed parts of the steel from the collapsed tower into a bell tower for the church. It's a symbol of hope, strength and remembrance.

    Politics in New Mexico

    As the country began to change and heal after Sept. 11, New Mexico also began to change.

    New leadership was put in place by New Mexico's voters.

    Bill Richardson was elected governor in 2002. Richardson cut taxes and brought film production companies to the state.

    Richardson was also a key figure in bringing the space industry to New Mexico.

    The New Mexico Spaceport broke ground in 2009. It later became home to Virgin Galactic.

    High-profile cases of the 2000s

    The decade also saw some unforgettable crimes.

    In 2004, Cody Posey confessed to killing his family and tried to hide their bodies in a pile of manure. Former national news anchor Sam Donaldson discovered the scene at his ranch in Hondo, New Mexico.

    Read More: Documents detail events leading to murder on ranch near Hondo

    Posey claimed he was abused and was sentenced as a juvenile. He was released on his 21st birthday.

    John Hyde

    Then, on Aug. 18, 2005, five people were killed in 16 hours, two of them being police officers. The person accused of the murders — John Hyde.

    Following the killings, Hyde was committed to a mental institution. Every two years, there's a review of his mental status to determine if he's fit to stand trial for the crime.

    West Mesa Murders

    A few years later, in February 2009, a gruesome discovery in southwest Albuquerque.

    Christine Ross was walking her dog Ruca when the pair found a large femur bone on their route. Police later determined the bone was human.

    A once-empty desert became a large crime scene. Police found 11 bodies and an unborn child.

    Read More: Learn more about the Albuquerque West Mesa Murders

    The case would later be called the West Mesa Murders. While there are suspects in the case, it remains unsolved.

    The 2010s

    In the 2010s, an increase in gun violence and school shootings nationwide and in New Mexico.

    In 2014, two students were shot and injured at Berrendo Middle School in Roswell.

    In 2017, two students were shot and killed in a school shooting at Aztec High School in northern New Mexico.

    In 2019, a student took a gun to V. Sue Cleveland High School in Rio Rancho and planned on shooting several students. The gun misfired and no one was hurt.

    Emcore Office Shooting

    Three school shootings during the decade, but the violence didn't stop there.

    On July 12, 2010, Robert Reza entered an office building and shot his girlfriend and several others. Two employees died before Reza turned the gun on himself. This shooting served as a reminder to local businesses of potential dangers and inspired police departments to train for the worst.

    Video Above: KOAT's coverage of the Emcore office shooting

    Columbus Gun Trafficking

    In 2011, New Mexico made national headlines when city officials in one small New Mexico were arrested for gun smuggling.

    Investigators say the mayor and others were responsible for shipping nearly 200 guns to Mexican drug cartels.

    James Boyd Shooting

    The decade also saw events that sparked police reform across the country, including Albuquerque.

    In 2014, James Boyd was shot and killed by two officers of the Albuquerque Police Department while he was camping in the foothills.

    Body camera video showed officers approaching him, using a flash bang and a police dog. Police say that's when Boyd pulled out two knives and they opened fire.

    The shooting sparked outrage, leading to violent protests that lasted for several hours, with police using tear gas to control the crowd.

    The Department of Justice stepped in and put Albuquerque police on a court-mandated path to reform.

    Las Conchas Fire

    The 2010s also saw another wildfire threaten the community of Los Alamos.

    The Las Conchas Fire burned more than 156,000 acres and started after a tree fell on a powerline in 2011.

    Video Above: KOAT's coverage of the Las Conchas Fire

    At the time, the fire was the largest in state history.

    Same-sex marriage legalized in New Mexico

    The decade also saw progress in society.

    In 2013, same-sex marriage became legal in New Mexico. Hundreds of couples went to clerk's offices to get a license to seal their bond.

    By 2015, the Supreme Court ruled same-sex marriage could not be banned anywhere in the country.

    Space Jump in Roswell

    Another moment put New Mexico in the history books.

    In October 2012, Felix Baumgartner became the first person to break the sound barrier in a freefall near Roswell, traveling at 843 miles per hour.

    He jumped from the world's largest balloon at 128,000 feet in altitude. He set eight world records and still holds some of them.

    Change in politics

    The decade would also see changes in local politics as New Mexico elected Susana Martinez as governor in 2010. She would be the first female Hispanic governor in the history of the United States.

    The decade would also end with a change in leadership once again when Michelle Lujan Grisham was elected governor in 2018.

    She would soon face a global pandemic more than 1 year after taking office.

    Through the challenges of the pandemic, we still were dedicated to keeping you informed and continued to provide coverage in any way we could.

    A worldwide pandemic was not easy to navigate, and many turned to local news for information that was needed.

    Ballooning and Balloon Fiesta

    Ballooning has been a big part of our community.

    KOAT has covered every aspect of ballooning, from the birth of Balloon Fiesta to local pioneers setting records across the world.

    In August 1978, after six days of below-zero temperatures, Ben Abruzzo, Larry Newman, and Maxie Anderson made history by becoming the first to cross the Atlantic Ocean by balloon.

    In 1981, the Double Eagle V balloon made a record-setting 5,208-mile flight across the Pacific Ocean. Our own Joe Diaz even jumped out of a helicopter to get the story.

    As we brought you coverage of those record-breaking balloon flights, we also brought coverage of balloons here at home.

    Balloon Fiesta has been a staple in New Mexico since its inception. From the first fiesta with 13 balloons, growing into a spectacle where hundreds of balloons launch each day.

    Video Above: KOAT's full broadcast from the 1999 Albuquerque International Balloon Fiesta

    As Balloon Fiesta grew, it changed locations several times. With that expansion came the expansion of KOAT's coverage of Balloon Fiesta to include live broadcasts from the park.

    But with coverage of a fun and beautiful spectacle, there is some danger that comes with ballooning.

    In 2004, the Smokey Bear hot air balloon slammed into a radio tower near Balloon Fiesta Park. Two children and pilot Bill Chapel were on board at the time of the crash.

    Video Above: Full live coverage of the Smokey Bear balloon accident in Albuquerque

    Everyone on the balloon climbed down the tower safely, and no injuries were reported.

    Even with the rare dangers of ballooning, thousands come to Balloon Fiesta each year to take in the spectacular views and witness our state's beauty.
