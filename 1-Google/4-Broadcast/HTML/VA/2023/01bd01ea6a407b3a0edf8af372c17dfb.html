New Virginia laws: Rules for swatting, bullying, expanded road safety take effect
by Claire Foley
Sunday, Jul 2nd 2023
FILE PHOTO - The Senate floor of the General Assembly Building in Capitol Square on April 4, 2022. Richmond, Virginia. (Credit: WSET)
(WSET) —

July 1 marks the start of hundreds of new laws that Governor Glenn Youngkin signed into action this year.

ADVERTISEMENT

One of those laws taking effect today has roots here in Lynchburg. Delegate Wendell Walker introduced a bill on Swatting. That's when someone makes a fake emergency call to 911 and police and fire respond.

Walker said that he brought this forward in response to the false threat made on E.C. Glass High School in January.

"What the bill does is it puts more strength in the punishment," said Walker. If they find out who makes a fake report, that person will face a Class I Misdemeanor, which is a $2500 fine and up to 12 months in jail. On top of this, the person responsible would have to foot the bill for what was spent by localities on dispatching emergency crews. If someone is hurt or dies as a result of the fake report, that would result in a felony charge, with a longer prison sentence on the table.

Also on the list is a law that brings adults closer to what's going on with their students at school. With this change, schools must let families know within one day if their student was involved in a bullying incident.

Just in time for a busy holiday travel weekend is a new law that extends safety to all motorists.

AAA brought this forward to lawmakers, suggesting that the move-over law that already exists be expanded to not just protect emergency crews that are stopped on the side of the road. Now, drivers must slow down or move over for all vehicles that are stopped and showing hazard lights, warning signs, or flares. Families who have lost loved ones in this kind of crash are grateful.

Melanie Clark lost her husband, Brad Clark in 2018. He was a Lieutenant with Hanover Fire and EMS. He was struck and killed while responding to a call on I-295.

"We're just grateful and hopeful that no other family has to go through - suffer the tragedy - that we have on a personal level," said Clark.

Alexis Wells lost her sister, Angela Hurley, in 2022. Hurley was waiting in her disabled car for assistance when another driver crashed into her at nearly 90 mph. Wells and her family said that the want something positive to come out of this.

"We want to see change and we want to see positive impact and we want to prevent other people from going through this. This is preventable. This is 100% preventable," she said.
