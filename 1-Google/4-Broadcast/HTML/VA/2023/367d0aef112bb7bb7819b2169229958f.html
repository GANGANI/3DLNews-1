'Horrific crime': Young bald eagle dies on Christmas Eve from gunshot wound in Virginia

by Robert Locklear

Tue, December 26th 2023 at 10:55 AM
Updated Tue, December 26th 2023 at 3:53 PM
4
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
It was a sorrowful Christmas Eve at the Southwest Virginia Wildlife Shelter after a young bald eagle died from a gunshot wound in the wing. (Southwest Virginia Wildlife Center)

ROANOKE, Va. (WSET) — It was a sorrowful Christmas Eve at a wildlife shelter after a young bald eagle died from a gunshot wound in the wing.

The Southwest Virginia Wildlife Center said late on Christmas Eve they admitted a juvenile bald eagle from Wythe County that weighed just under ten pounds. The bird's arrival at the shelter followed more than a week of evading capture, which the shelter said the eagle survived by eating deer carcasses.

"It took a crew of people from the Department of Wildlife Resources, Appalachian Wildlife Management, and some concerned citizens to apprehend the eagle and bring it into our care," SVWC said.

After multiple X-rays, the center confirmed that the bird's injuries were from a gunshot wound, with bullet fragments in the wing.

Tragically, the shattered bone was too severe to be fixed, and SVWC said the beautiful bird died.

"Sadly this magnificent bird was unable to recover from its injuries and did not make it," SVWC said.

Chester Leonard, the executive director of SVWC, said he and the staff at the center are saddened by the death.

"Animals deserve so much better," Leonard said. "We wish we could save all of our patients and are deeply saddened by the loss of this magnificent eagle."

Leonard also said this incident was no accident.

"There is no way that this was, at least in my opinion, this was a hunting accident. This was intentionally someone shot a bald eagle," Leonard said.

Shooting a bald eagle is a serious crime--one that could result in a fine of $100,000, imprisonment for one year, or both, for a first offense.

SVWC said it was a "horrific crime" and that if you have information about the incident you should call the Department of Wildlife resources at 1-800-237-5712.
