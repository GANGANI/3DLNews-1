Virginia State Senate passes 3 controversial gun bills, including ban on 'assault' weapons

by KELSEY CHILDRESS | WSET Staff

Sun, February 11th 2024 at 2:16 PM
FILE - Three variations of the AR-15 assault rifle are displayed at the California Department of Justice in Sacramento, Calif., on Aug. 15, 2012. (AP Photo/Rich Pedroncelli, File)

RICHMOND, Va. (WSET) — The Virginia State Senate recently passed three bills that could bring about some big changes for gun owners in the Commonwealth.

The right to bear arms versus the need to stop gun crimes and mass shootings have been a long-standing, controversial debate in Virginia as well as the nation at large. It's at the center of debate on the Senate floor this 2024 session.

On Wednesday, in a 21-19 vote, lawmakers passed Senate Bill 2, which would create a Class 1 misdemeanor -- which could land a violator in jail for up to 12 months and or face a $2,500 fine -- for anyone who imports, sells, manufactures, purchases, possesses, transports, or transfers an assault firearm.

"This is punishing people who believe they have a second amendment right to own firearms," said state Sen. Mark Peake, R-District 22.

State Sen. Saddam Salim, D-District 37, pushed back against Peake and the GOP's characterization of the legislation, saying, "It is our obligation to make sure we don't have weapons of war on our streets.

Republican senators questioned the bill on the floor after it was passed through the House Monday, questioning how it would prevent criminals from accessing such weapons.

"If this bill is passed, how do we get the gun from the criminals that don't care about laws?," asked Sen. John McGuire, R-Distirct 56.

Sen. Creigh Deeds, D-District 25, the chief patron of SB2, responded, saying: "There will always be criminals that don't care about laws."

What this bill will do is reduce the number of firearms that are introduced to our streets and by doing that we will keep guns, these firearms out of the hands of people that intend to do evil with them.

Senate Bill 99 also deals with assault-style weapons. This bill would make it illegal to open carry semi-automatic firearms in a public place.

"When they see these things in public, they're worried it's going to be the next mass shooting. The only reason to carry one of these things around in public is not for self-defense. It's not to protect your home. It's to scare people," said Sen. Scott Surovell, D-District 36.

It is our God-given right to protect ourselves, our family and our children. It's a constitutional right," Sen. McGuire responded.

SB99 passed on Tuesday, in a 21-19 vote.

Finally, Senate Bill 57 would make it illegal to carry any type of firearm into a restaurant that serves alcohol, regardless of a concealed carry permit. SB57 also passed Tuesday, 20-18.

SB99 and SB57 now move to the Democrat-controlled House of Delegates. If they pass, they move to Gov. Glenn Youngkin's desk.
