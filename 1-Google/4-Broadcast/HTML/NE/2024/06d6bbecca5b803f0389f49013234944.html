Nebraska Supreme Court hears governor’s appeal on nonprofit news organization records request

Request stems from Flatwater Free Press report Pillen said was ‘from Communist China’
By Brian Mastre
Published: Feb. 8, 2024 at 5:48 PM CST

OMAHA, Neb. (WOWT) - The right of citizens to get reasonable access to public records went before the Nebraska Supreme Court on Thursday.

Flatwater Free Press — an independent, nonprofit news organization headquartered in Omaha’s Old Market — raised questions last year regarding Nebraska Gov. Jim Pillen’s family business: hog farming.

Over the past few years, reporter Yanqi Xu has investigated the impact of high nitrate levels in drinking water and its potential link to childhood cancer. She ultimately uncovered 16 of Pillen’s hog farms that had high nitrate levels, and published the findings in the Flatwater Free Press in October.

In an interview on KFAB radio after the story was published, Gov. Pillen said that he’s not interested in reading what she found.

“All you got to do is look at the author,” Pillen said in the interview. “The author is from communist China. What more do you need to know?”

Pillen’s daughter, Sarah, who runs Pillen Family Farms, told the Xu that they’re committed to being “positive stewards of the land.”

During the course of the investigation, Xu requested public records — state emails with certain key words like “nitrates” — to see if there’s more to the story. That’s when they were hit with the bill, as told by Flatwater’s attorney Daniel Gutman.

“When we first asked for 10 years of requests, the fee was $2,000,” Gutman said. “As we narrowed the request and cut down the years, the cost estimate skyrocketed to $44,000.”

Flatwater won in district court, but they still don’t have the public records. The state appealed, which was heard Thursday by the Nebraska Supreme Court.

“Not all emails are public records,” said Eric Hamilton, Nebraska’s Solicitor General.

Hamilton says the high cost can be attributed to all the manpower used to make sure the documents are public and to avoid trading classified information, health records, or attorney-client privilege.

“The legislature anticipated the problem I’ve been talking about,” Hamilton said. “These voluminous and sometimes abusive requests are burdening public bodies.”

Matt Wynn is the executive director of the Flatwater Free Press. He listened to the back-and-forth in the courtroom Thursday, and tried to understand what it all means.

“At the end of the day, the law is on our side,” Wynn said. “It’s written that taxpayers deserve to see this stuff — the expectation of openness and transparency. Is it still transparent if they can charge you $45,000 for the pleasure of looking at what they’re doing in the first place?”

After 20 minutes of testimony, the public records debate is in the hands of Nebraska’s Supreme Court justices. It’s not clear when they’ll issue a ruling.
