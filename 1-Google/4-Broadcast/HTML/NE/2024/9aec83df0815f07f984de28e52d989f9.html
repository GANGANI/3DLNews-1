National debt continues upward climb as other issues take priority ahead of 2024 election

by CHRISTOPHER WHITE | THE NATIONAL DESK

Thu, February 8th 2024 at 5:48 PM
4

HUNT VALLEY, Md. (TND) — The U.S. national debt is expected to grow by an eye-opening $21 trillion over the next nine years, as interest rates continue their upward trajectory, according to a new Congressional Budget Office report. The new numbers come as the election season heats up, and as other issues take priority.

The CBO's report shows the debt held by the public will go from $26.2 trillion at the end of 2023 and hit $48 trillion by the end of 2034. Taxpayers are on the hook for that load, but right now there is a large imbalance between the amount of tax revenue the government is pulling in and the amount it's spending.

While government spending is slated to grow from 22.7% of GDP this year to 24.1% within a decade, tax revenue will only grow from 16.5% of GDP to 17.1% in 2025 before it eventually rises to 17.9% by 2034, the report shows.

Two of the most important trust funds -- Social Security and the Highway Trust Fund -- are edging toward insolvency. The latter will be depleted in four years; Social Security's fund will hang on until 2033. Medicare will be out of cash by the mid-2030s, according to the CBO's outlook.

Once insolvent, beneficiaries of these programs face substantial cuts across the board.

Meanwhile, voters turn their focus to the election in November. The general election is still several months away, but as it stands, President Joe Biden is running for re-election and, if he wins his party's nomination, he could face an old nemesis: former President Donald Trump.

Neither of the candidates has talked much about ways to tackle those debt levels, to say nothing of what's needed to stabilize Social Security, according to Marc Goldwein, senior policy director at the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget.

As Trump seeks a second term, the GOP front-runner is promising to “always protect” Social Security, according to his campaign website. Biden is promising to protect Social Security from cuts, a position that many Republicans in Congress now appear to support.

“We’re not going to hurt the people who’ve been paying into Social Security their whole life and then all of a sudden they’re supposed to get less,” Trump said in a Feb. 2016 debate during his first presidential campaign.

The candidates who are no longer in the race were the ones focused on debt and SS reforms, Goldwein told The National Desk. Along with publicizing the debt level, his group is also tracking whether presidential candidates are taking on the U.S.'s financial standing.

As it stands, the healthcare system is the top issue for a plurality of Americans (43%), while 35% of Americans say terrorism and national security are the most important, a CivicScience survey of voters found in January. Immigration rounded out voters' biggest concern heading into November.

Most of those voters are older Americans.

About 52% of registered voters headed into the 2020 election were ages 50 and older, up from 41% in 1996, according to a Pew Research survey. That's the case for Republicans and Democrats, with 56% of GOP-leaning voters above 50, up from 39% in 1996. The same is true for half of Democratic voters going into that year.

That's creating a feedback loop, Goldwein argues.

One reason why Social Security and Medicare costs have "exploded" is because "more people are on them, which means they have more political support, which means fewer people want to touch them," he said. Social Security and Medicare are two of the largest drivers of the national debt.

And with the debt driving upward, it's prompting even Federal Reserve Chairman Jerome Powell to warn about a potential fiscal calamity. The debt is "growing faster than the economy, so it is unsustainable,” he told CBS News earlier this month.

Before making the comment, Powell said he preferred to stay in his own lane, that being monetary policy, which focuses mainly on moderating interest rate levels; increasing them if inflation is too high and reducing them if the economy needs a jolt.

Budget experts say one of the best ways to tackle high debt loads -- aside from making substantial cuts to social programs -- is to make investments in the agency tasked with generating tax revenue -- the IRS. Improving the agency's ability to collect taxes can go a long way toward bringing down the debt load.

it comes with ancillary benefits as well, Goldwein argues.

"You don't have to raise anyone's taxes," he said, as making the process more efficient acts as its own revenue generator. Goldwein added: "You don't have to cut anyone's tax breaks."

Before the Inflation Reduction Act provided the IRS with more funding, generating revenue was a challenge, as was conducting audits. The audit rate for millionaires fell by more than 70% from 2010 and 2019, according to a new report by the Treasury Department. For corporations, that number was 50%.

To get around that problem, "all you have to say is that we're going to do a better job of collecting the revenue, collecting the taxes that people already owe," Goldwein said.
