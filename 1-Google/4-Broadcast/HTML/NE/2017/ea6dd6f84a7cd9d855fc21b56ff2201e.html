Melanoma impacts more than 1 million in U.S.
by Jessica Stevenson
Monday, May 22nd 2017
Melanoma, a growing epidemic (KHGI)

Melanoma is a skin cancer that's effecting more than a million people in our country, including NTV's Jessica Stevenson. Tens of thousands of people die from melanoma.

ADVERTISEMENT

Local doctors say it's a growing epidemic that's sweeping our country. Since the 1970's, doctors say the number of cases has increased by 230 percent, especially hitting our older teens and young adults.

"One person dies every hour of melanoma in the United States," said Board Certified Dermatologist Dr. Sharon Bond.

On average, hundreds are diagnosed each day.

"According to the American Cancer Society, 87,000 Americans are going to be diagnosed in 2017 with melanoma, and just under 10,000 die this year of melanoma," said CHI Good Samaritan Cancer Center Registered Nurse Carol O'Neill.

So what is melanoma?

"Melanoma is a cancer of the melanocytes, which are the pigmented cells in the skin," Dr. Bond said.

"The genetic make-up of those cells are damaged to the point that those cells begin proliferating they grow out of control, they divide out of control and they don't die when they should and they can develop a tumor," O'Neill said.

O'Neill said having the tumor begin on the surface of a body can lead to dangerous assumptions.

"Many times people look at skin cancer as a minor thing and they'll say, 'you know, if I get skin cancer, I'll just have it cut out or cut off and it's no big deal,'" she said.

But, she added often times this is not the case.

"It can be a very dramatic kind of a treatment and for skin cancer there is minor surgery and sometimes major surgery that's involved, sometimes chemotherapy, whether that's an infusion or whether it's a topical type of a chemotherapy, and even radiation treatments. So, it can be very serious," O'Neill said.

Like other cancers, melanoma is diagnosed by stages.

"For example, a stage zero is an insight to melanoma. It's in that top layer of skin where there's no lymph vessels or blood vessels so it has essentially a 99 percent cure rate with wide excisions. Stage four is when it's spread to other organ systems, and although we have some treatments for them, you try to not be in that situation if you can," Dr. Bond said.

In Jessica's case, she was fortunate enough to be considered cured, but doctors say she may not stay melanoma free forever.

"The recent academy that I went to said it can be up to 20 percent per lifetime risk of getting a second melanoma skin cancer," Dr. Bond said. "If you've had two melanomas diagnosed, then there is a study that showed you had up to 30 percent chance of having a third melanoma."

Like with all cancers, it's important to catch it early.

"It's a critical factor," O'Neill said. "The survivability of melanoma is very good if it's found early and it's treated. If it's found late, or if someone chooses not to treat it, it's going to lead to an early demise for that person."
