Teacher survey latest rift over education in Grand Island
by Steve White
Tuesday, Oct 25th 2022
4
View All Photos
FILE - Grand Island Public Schools (Photo Credit: NTV News)
GRAND ISLAND, Neb. —

An unofficial teacher survey is the latest flashpoint over the future of education in Grand Island. As Election Day nears, there's debate over who's got a better grasp on what teachers are facing – the school board or a political action committee known as Chaperone.

ADVERTISEMENT

“This is a conversation, we weren't having it before, now we are,” said Tom Wald, president of Chaperone.

Leaders of the PAC sent a survey to teachers they had contact with, and they say around 200 responded. They did not collect data that would allow us to understand the makeup of the survey group, however, through answers to an open-ended question, they say the survey reached its intended audience.

“It's obvious they're teachers. They know things about their classroom,” Wald said.

NTV reviewed the comments provided by Chaperone. Many contain references to people and programs specific to Grand Island Public Schools. There were approximately 100 comments provided by Chaperone.

For context, GIPS has nearly 800 teachers and more than 1,500 staff members.

Chaperone did not ask educators to identify their school or teaching role so it's not possible to tell if the survey is a representative sample.

So while up to 200 may have responded to the survey, school board members say it's an example of confirmation bias, reaching only teachers who agree with Chaperone's mission and not reflective of all teachers.

“When you cherry pick who you send it to and it's people who are already supporters of your way of thinking, you're going to get the results you expect,” said Bonnie Hinkle, a 16-year veteran of the school board.

NTV reached out to around two dozen teachers. Many did not respond. Several who did said they did not get the survey or disregarded it. Around ten said it appeared to be designed to skew towards the Chaperone view.

"If it started with teachers Chaperone knew, like they said, it would stand to reason that those are some of the teachers who have the most beef with the district, so to say, and they’re likely to send it to people who they think agree with them," one teacher said.

A few who agree with Chaperone said they would not discuss the survey. One teacher said he was afraid of what a “witch hunt” if he spoke in favor of the survey.

Wald said, “It had to be anonymous because there’s a huge fear of retribution.”

School board members say Grand Island Public Schools conducts surveys that hold up to scrutiny when looking at the methodology.

“It's done by a professional organization, that's what they do, it's anonymous and teachers give us feedback. Plus, we've got others' feedback avenues, and we get good feedback from those, very honest feedback,” Hinkle said.

She said they've acted on that, like more full days for planning.

“These are things we have been addressing for some time, so many examples of things we have done to support teachers,” she said.

Leaders of Chaperone take credit for forcing change.

“There's a dress code that was reimplemented, got stricter. Two, there's a tardiness policy, hall passes; three an attendance policy, none of those things existed before we went to the board,” Wald said.

Hinkle disputes survey results suggesting the district isn't honest about its teacher shortage. According to Chaperone, around 80 percent of those responding gave Superintendent Dr. Tawana Grover and the board poor marks for their communication about that issue.

Hinkle counters that it's a nationwide issue and one Grand Island is addressing. She also says groups that claim to be advocating for teachers may actually be causing stress.

“If anything, that's really wearing on teachers to be made to look like they're not doing their job with kids. All you have to do is look at our classrooms and see how much our teachers care about kids,” she said.

While some may call Chaperone a group advocating for teachers, Hinkle said it’s divisive and said it is reminiscent of legislative efforts to expand school choice in Nebraska so dollars follow students into private schools. She said that would take dollars from public schools, and she argued those dollars are needed to improve teacher pay and address the teacher shortage.

Chaperone was founded by former Grand Island Mayor Jeremy Jensen. He was fired as the high school soccer coach and has been critical of the district on social media, accusing GIPS of not supporting teachers and also saying students are failing to meet expectations.

Leaders of Chaperone say it's bigger than the former mayor who sparked the movement and say they plan to stick around beyond this election.

Wald said, “I'm real interested in dropping the temperature of the debate in this community. I'm all for changes that benefit teachers and students. I'm all for Tawana Grover staying here for 20 years, but she's got to make changes,” he said.

Chaperone is supporting school board candidate Hank McFarland, who petitioned as a replacement on the ballot when another candidate dropped out in the Ward B race.

They are also supporting two write-in candidates.

Candidate Tim Mayfield, who's been on the ballot for Ward B since the initial filing period, said in his view, this is “politicizing” things and a distraction from the work going on to recruit and retain teachers.

Mayfield said Chaperone is a PAC “scoring points using teachers."
