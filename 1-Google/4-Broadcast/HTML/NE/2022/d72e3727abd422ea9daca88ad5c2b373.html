GRAND ISLAND, NEB. — The shutdown of one central Nebraska high school newspaper sparked national attention, most recently with an opinion piece published in the Washington Post.

The former Grand Island Northwest student who wrote that piece spoke to NTV.

“Being teased by your peers is one thing, but this is like the adults at my school were teasing me, they were kind of, you know, it was definitely a weird experience," said Marcus Pennell.

A former Viking, Pennell, describes the pushback he received while writing for the school newspaper at Northwest High School in Grand Island.

GRAND ISLAND NORTHWEST HIGH SCHOOL OFFICIALS SHUT DOWN SCHOOL NEWSPAPER AFTER LGBTQ ISSUE

“We knew they were trying to restrict the LGBTQ content and we were like, we’re going to put more LGBT content,” said Pennell.

He said he was told to use his dead name 'Meghan' in a pro-LGBTQ article for the paper.

“It was the first real pushback from teachers and admin I had faced or from the school I had faced.”

While Pennell believes the newspaper was canceled, Grand Island Northwest Superintendent Jeff Edwards said that’s not the case.

When NTV News reached out to him for comment, Edwards cited a letter sent home to parents, stating in part:

When decisions such as temporary pausing, not canceling a school newspaper is made, there are many variables that go into this decision as a district including staff support, student interest, class scheduling and so forth.

Edwards declined to comment to NTV beyond what was in the letter.

"Anything outside of that will be viewed as slander," Edwards said.

Pennell, now an English major at the University of Nebraska Omaha, said he was not expecting the situation at Northwest to get as much attention as it has.

“We definitely knew people weren’t going to be pleased, but we didn’t expect this kind of reaction. I think a lot of high schoolers think that global issues don't really affect them but I think a school newspaper kind of humanizes a lot of these issues and makes high schoolers see it from a high school perspective."

Pennell said he hopes the school’s newspaper can be brought back, not for his sake, but for those of future journalism students at Northwest High School.
