America doesn't have enough doctors: How it's impacting healthcare & why it's expected to get worse
by ANGIE MORESCHI, MIKE GRIFFITH , & LARRY DEAL | Spotlight on America
Monday, Nov 27th 2023
11
View All Photos
Dr. Heather DeReus holding a picture of herself at age 3.{&nbsp;}(Courtesy: Dr. DeReus)

A doctor shortage in the United States is already a problem, and it's expected to get even worse over the next decade. The Association of American Medical Colleges projects a shortage of 124,000 physicians by 2034. Spotlight on America has been investigating the reasons America doesn’t have enough doctors, the states that are hardest hit, and what's being done to make things better.

Seeing a doctor when you need one is something most of us depend on, but just finding a primary care physician who’s taking new patients can be tough these days. And getting an appointment to see a specialist often takes several months of waiting.

“The demand is going up and up and up, and supply is not keeping pace; and a big part of that is because of limits on the training capacity to produce new physicians,” Michael Dill, Director of Workforce Studies with the Association of American Medical Colleges, told Spotlight on America.

Training to become a doctor requires four years of medical school, followed by a residency of three to seven years, depending on the specialty area. A residency provides additional training for young doctors to work directly with patients and more senior physicians. It's a required step to achieve medical licensure in the U.S. and the ability to work independently as a physician.

The AAMC points to a cap on the number of federally-funded residency positions as a major factor impacting the shortage.

Young doctors willing & able

Dr.%20Heather%20DeReus%20with%20a%20newborn%20baby.%7B %7D(Courtesy:%20Dr.%20DeReus)

Dr. Heather DeReus is one of those young physicians in training.

She wanted to be a doctor for as long as she can remember. A video she shared with Spotlight on America shows DeReus at age 3 getting her very first doctor’s kit at Christmas.

“Look, a doctor book! A stethoscope!” the little girl calls out, clearly very excited.

DeReus is now a second-year resident in pediatrics at the University of Virginia Medical Center in Charlottesville.

“I love taking care of my patients. I love talking to the families. I couldn't imagine doing anything else. It's really just the best job in the world,” De Reus told Spotlight.

Video%20of%203-year%20old%20Dr.%20Heather%20DeReus%20getting%20her%20first%20doctor's%20kit%20for%20Christmas.%20(Courtesy:%20Dr.%20DeReus)

After DeReus completed medical school at the University of Florida College of Medicine, she "matched" at UVA for her residency, but not all med students are as fortunate.

“It's devastating. These people work so hard. We've all worked so hard and want this so much, and there are patients that need to be seen, yet we don't have enough spots for them.”

Cap on doctor training positions

In 1997, Congress passed legislation putting a cap on the number of Graduate Medical Education positions, or residency spots, funded by Medicare.

It was believed the U.S. had a sufficient number of physicians at the time, but that was nearly 30 years ago.

Since then, the U.S. population has been growing and aging. Add to that physician burnout, which worsened during the COVID-19 pandemic. Plus, more doctors are nearing retirement, with one out of four over age 65.

That all adds up to America getting a case of a supply-and-demand crisis we’re facing today.

National%20Investigative%20Correspondent%20Angie%20Moreschi%20interviewing%20Michael%20Dill,%20Director%20of%20Workforce%20Studies%20with%20the%20Association%20of%20American%20Medical%20Colleges.%20(SOA)

“I think we should be very worried. Our health depends on having enough physicians and we don’t have enough,” the AAMC’s Dill said.

Impact of shortage

According to a study by the National Institutes of Health, the five states expected to have the worst doctor shortages by 2030, based on demand versus the number of doctors, are:

  1. California
  2. Florida
  3. Texas
  4. Arizona
  5. Georgia

The impact of the doctor shortage is already being felt by patients. Delays in getting care are already common across the country.

When it comes to urgent care, according to the AAMC, a quarter to a third of people seeking same-day care can’t get it.

Affordability used to be the number one reason, but that has changed.

“Now, the number one reason is they can't find a provider,” Dill said.

Push to lift the GME cap

Sen. John Boozman, R-Arkansas, is pushing to lift the federal cap on residency positions.

“The bottom line is we’ve got a problem, and the problem is going to grow,” Boozman told Spotlight on America.

Sen.%20John%20Boozman,%20R-Arkansas,%20told%20Spotlight%20on%20America's%7B %7DAngie%20Moreschi%20%20about%20the%20Resident%20Physician%20Shortage%20Reduction%20Act%20of%202023%20he's%20sponsoring%20to%20help%20expand%20the%20nation%E2%80%99s%20physician%20workforce.%20(SOA)

He’s co-sponsoring a bipartisan bill, called the Resident Physician Shortage Reduction Act of 2023, to help expand the nation’s physician workforce.

The legislation would add 14,000 Medicare-funded slots over the next 7 years.

A National Institutes of Health review found the cost Medicare pays to train residents varies widely, based on location and specialty, with payments ranging from $63,000 to $155,000 per person.

Medicare pays about $16 billion a year for residency positions nationwide, but hospitals and states also contribute, and they've been paying an increasing share in recent years.

“If you're sick, it doesn't matter if you're a Democrat or Republican,” Boozman said. “I think we're at the point now where people are realizing that we have to do something about this.”

Congress did begin to chip away at the problem in 2021, during the COVID-19 pandemic. It passed legislation to fund 1,200 more positions, which are being added over the next few years. But Boozman says more are needed to “combat the looming health care crisis.”
