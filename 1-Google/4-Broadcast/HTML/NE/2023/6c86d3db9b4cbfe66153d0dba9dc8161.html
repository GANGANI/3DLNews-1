Dressing as a 'furry' may be banned in Florida school district
by KRISTINA WATROBSKI | Crisis in the Classroom
Friday, May 12th 2023
10
View All Photos
FILE: Furries{&nbsp;}(Photo by Sean Gallup/Getty Images)
VIERA, Fla. (CITC) —

Several school board members are looking to prevent students from dressing as "furries" in a Florida school district.

ADVERTISEMENT

The Brevard Public Schools (BPS) school board discussed potential amendments to its districtwide dress code policy Tuesday. The items up for debate were brought to the board's attention by BPS students, and they ranged from tank tops and hats to tails and animal ears.

It was interesting to see some of the same students who said 'we should be able to wear whatever we want,' but then say 'but you can't wear a tail and ears,'" BPS school board member Katye Campbell said. "I'm not a big fan of the furry movement, but at the same time, if ears means a headband with pointed ears on them, it's a hair accessory."

A "furry" is often described as an individual who has an interest in anthropomorphism or the act of attributing human traits to non-human entities. "Furries" often say they identify with animals, which for some results in them wearing items like tails, collars and animal ears.

Some "furries" also choose to occasionally communicate using traditional animal sounds, such as in barks or squeaks.

Campbell continued her remarks by saying that students "meowing and barking" at each other is "not cool," but those actions cannot be considered under the dress code.

However, other board members said they are "all about" finding ways to discourage students from acting as "furries." BPS board chair Matt Susin told his peers that his daughter is "tired of furries" in schools.

Brevard%20Public%20School%20school%20board%20chair%20Matt%20Susin%20led%20the%20efforts%20Tuesday,%20saying%20that%20his%20daughter%20is%20%22tired%20of%20furries%22%20in%20schools.%20(Photo:%20Brevard%20Public%20Schools)
This is something that comes up at our dinner table at least every month," Susin said. "At some point, we've got to be able to curb that behavior."

The BPS school board debated for several minutes about how to best incorporate language discouraging "furry" attire into the dress code policy. While the majority appeared to be in support of the efforts, board member Jennifer Jenkins at one point chastised the board for "overcomplicating" the subject.

This is not rocket science, and it's not an epidemic. If you don't want tails on kids, just say no tails," Jenkins said. "The barking has nothing to do with that. If you guys are keeping up with the trends, it's this weird, ridiculous thing that kids are doing in middle school. All children."
Jennifer%20Jenkins%20told%20her%20fellow%20Brevard%20Public%20Schools%20school%20board%20members%20that%20she%20felt%20they%20were%20%22overcomplicating%22%20the%20topic%20of%20%22furries%22%20in%20schools.%20(Photo:%20Brevard%20Public%20Schools)

The discussion concluded with BPS Student Services Director Christopher Reed, one of the writers of the dress code policy, saying he would weigh BPS's options moving forward. One approach he suggested was adding language prohibiting clothing meant to "emulate a non-human."

It is not immediately when the BPS dress code will be finalized or if language referencing "furries" will ultimately be included.
