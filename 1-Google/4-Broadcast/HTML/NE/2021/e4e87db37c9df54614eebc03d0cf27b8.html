"Just Blaize": Boy with rare condition surprised with even rarer gift

by Steve White

Mon, November 8th 2021 at 5:58 PM
Updated Tue, November 9th 2021 at 7:20 PM
10
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
NOVEMBER 8, 2021: Blaize DeGroff of Ord has a rare genetic condition known as Williams Syndrome. Above all else, he loves irrigation pivots and was surprised by Reinke and Holdrege Irrigation with his own scaled down pivot for his backyard. (Photo Credit: NTV News)

ORD, Neb. — Sometimes the kids with the biggest hearts were born with the weakest hearts.

But just like the crops around Valley County, kids like Blaize DeGroff just need to be planted in good soil and watered.”

“Every summer every we drive around and look at neighboring pivots,” his mom Jessica explains.

“We have to turn on a different road, he's always looking out the window,” said Mike, his dad.

Irrigation systems are what make this boy Blaize.

“Pivots go tch, tch, tch,” he said, mimicking the sound of his favorite farm equipment.

Personality is in his genes.

“Very happy, kind kid,” his dad says.

Those are traits shared by many who have been diagnosed with Williams Syndrome.

Jessica, a nurse, attends conferences to learn more about her son’s condition.

She said, “I've actually heard it called the happy syndrome because he's a happy kid and well loved.”

And when it comes to what Blaize loves better watch out.

“When he likes something he really, really likes it. He's obsessed with pivots and water,” said Mike.

Pivots are all around so a family friend wondered if Blaize could have his own.

“The obvious answer was we'll get it figured out, make sure this happens,” said Chris Roth, CEO of Reinke Irrigation.

While some collect scale replicas of farm machinery, Alan Loschen set about making something a little different than a toy tractor.

“Put everything that a normal size pivot would have on it,” he explained.

He fabricated this one-of-a-kind pivot at Holdrege Irrigation, built not to irrigate corn but water the lawn.

“Put one right in his backyard, one-fifth scale that really operates, puts water down,” Roth said.

The middle child in the DeGroff family is the center of attention with a center pivot all his own.

“Oh my gosh, oh my gosh!”

Wearing a shirt saying “be a good human”, Blaize screamed as he came around the corner of the family house to find his very own miniature pivot in the backyard. And it’s an apt word for a child who made his parents pivot.

“Thinking about the past, future, it's all different now,” said Jessica. “Changed everything and everybody for the better.”

Mike and Jessica learned Williams Syndrome is a one in ten-thousand condition, in their case doctors caught it around 9-months-old.

“Right around when he was diagnosed with heart condition, aortic valve at same time diagnosed around same time,” Jessica said as Blaize sprinted by, leading a pack of kids through the water.

A backyard pivot provides the playground he didn’t know he needed.

“I love it, it's exciting,” said Loschen, the pivot builder.

And it’s just like the real deal, from sprinkler heads he can change to proof of ownership.

“It says Blaize DeGroff on it, it's his pivot, has his name on it,” pointed out Chris Roth.

The Reinke CEO said once they heard the story they had to help.

“This is the Nebraska way, this is something he wanted, you just do it,” Roth said.

The company that says its products are “more right than rain” brings a downpour of support for Williams Syndrome.

“That's another whole added benefit to educate the public,” Roth said.

With a gift that will water the fertile soil of a child’s imagination for many growing seasons to come.

“We’re very overwhelmed and thankful. Never in a million years would I think someone would go out of their way, thank you from the bottom of our hearts,” Jessica said.
