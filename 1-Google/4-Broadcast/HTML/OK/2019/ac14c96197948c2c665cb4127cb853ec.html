Looking back on Channel 8's Charles Ely

by Ashley Ellis

Fri, March 1st 2019 at 10:53 PM
Updated Fri, March 1st 2019 at 11:47 PM
Looking back on Channel 8's Charles Ely (KTUL)

TULSA, Okla. (KTUL) — Tulsa's Channel 8's beloved Charles Ely will be retiring this week after 35 years.

We are sad to see him leave, but we are overjoyed for his next journey. Throughout the week, we showcased moments from his 46-year career as an award-winning journalist and the major stories he's covered, as well as stories from old friends and colleagues.

On Monday, his long-time partner and friend, Carole Lambert, reflected on their nearly 30 years together.

Continuing our week-long tribute to Charles, on Tuesday, we looked back on one of the biggest stories Charles says he's ever covered -- the Oklahoma City bombing. All of his experiences and exposes never compared to what happened on April 19, 1995. He raced to Oklahoma City and was there within two hours of the bombing.

Charles spent weeks anchoring our coverage from the Murrah Federal Building along with the trials and anniversaries that followed. Charles, along with former Channel 8 anchors Carole Lambert and LeAnne Taylor talked about the impact of that experience.

Charles Ely had many memorable moments throughout his 46-year career including things you may not know about like a near-death helicopter ride and a melee at a KKK rally.

Charles got his start in his home state of Ohio in the 1970s in talk news radio. Soon, he found himself in journalistic heaven, barely out of the starting blocks, when chaos erupted between the KKK and anti-clan protesters on the steps of the state capitol, July 4th 1977. It was the lead story on the CBS evening news with Dan Rather, and Charles was the reporting correspondent.

Next stop was Atlanta, and from there, he moved to Texas where he had a harrowing helicopter ride while reporting on a hurricane for KXAS.

From Texas, it was finally on to Tulsa where he challenged the VA in the early days of PTSD when the condition was being ignored. He was also one of the first to shine a spotlight on religious snake oil salesmen.

Over the last 24 years, the name Charles Ely has become a part of the Tulsa landscape just as much as the Golden Driller, the BOK Center, or the Praying Hands.
