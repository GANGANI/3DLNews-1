'Never seen anything as dangerous': Fentanyl fuels record overdose deaths

by STEPHEN LOIACONI, The National Desk

Thu, December 9th 2021 at 3:47 PM
Updated Fri, December 10th 2021 at 3:31 PM
Packets of fentanyl seized by the Indian River County Sheriff's Office. Fentanyl and other synthetic opioids were linked to nearly two-thirds of drug overdose deaths in the U.S. in the 12 months ending in April 2021. (Indian River County Sheriff's Office)

WASHINGTON (TND) — With drug overdose deaths in the United States at record levels, lawmakers are struggling to combat the rise of synthetic opioids like fentanyl as debates persist over how to balance law enforcement and treatment.

“[Fentanyl] is cheaper and it’s much easier to manufacture than heroin,” said Rep. Bob Latta, R-Ohio, at a House Republican news conference Wednesday. “It’s more addicting and fatal than heroin.”

Latta has co-sponsored a bill that would extend enhanced criminal penalties related to fentanyl and related substances, currently set to expire in February. He and other Republicans cast blame on President Joe Biden amid signs that fentanyl trafficking across the southern border has increased.

"The opioid crisis is a direct result of President Biden's open borders,” claimed Rep. Steve Scalise, R-La. “The border crisis has led to many other crises, but especially when we see this infusion of drugs coming across our southern border."

However, drug overdose deaths have been climbing for decades – up 250% in the last 20 years – and synthetic opioid deaths have risen steadily since 2012. Drug policy experts say the causes of those trends are complex, and most of the factors involved predate Biden’s presidency.

Data released last month by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention showed drug overdose deaths climbed 28.5% during the 12 months ending in April 2021 to a record of 100,306 deaths. Synthetic opioids, including fentanyl, caused 64% of those overdoses, up from 49% in the previous year.

“The Biden-Harris administration is using every tool at our disposal to reduce these overdose deaths and to ensure that people with substance use disorder can get the support they need,” Dr. Rahul Gupta, director of the White House Office of National Drug Policy, said when the CDC data was published.

Fentanyl and its analogs have spread rapidly in the last decade as drug companies tightly restricted access to prescription opioids. The substance is cheap and easy for drug dealers to obtain, and it is often laced into other drugs without much concern for the increased risk to users.

“I’ve never seen anything as dangerous as fentanyl,” Louis Milione, deputy administrator of the Drug Enforcement Administration, said at a House hearing last week.

The drug is primarily manufactured in labs in China and Mexico and then smuggled into the U.S. DEA officials say agents have seized enough fentanyl at the southern border in the last year to kill the entire U.S. population.

“Criminal drug networks in Mexico are mass-producing fentanyl, which is driving the increase in overdose deaths,” Frank Tarentino III, special agent in charge of the DEA’s Seattle field office, said at a news conference Wednesday. “The DEA and our law enforcement partners are committed to stemming the tide of this surge of lethal pills on our city streets by targeting the criminal networks who are profiteering while causing death in our communities.”

The Biden administration has endorsed bipartisan calls to permanently classify all fentanyl-related substances as Schedule I drugs. However, many drug abuse experts have warned increasing criminal penalties would not decrease illegal drug use or overdoses and would perpetuate racial disparities in sentencing.

“Fifty years of evidence and a parallel upswing in overdose deaths have disproven enforcement-first solutions,” nearly 100 advocacy groups wrote in a letter to congressional leaders this fall.

Jennifer Carroll, a medical anthropologist who teaches at North Carolina State University and Brown University, stressed fentanyl is not inherently more dangerous than other drugs, and the medical community has understood for decades how it works. However, the supply in the U.S. is poorly regulated, and people buying drugs in illegal markets have no way of knowing how much fentanyl they are getting.

“The driver is not so much fentanyl itself...,” she said. “We’re capable of using it extremely safely.”

The 12 months covered by the CDC data include the period during which Americans were most directly impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic. Business and social activities in many states were severely curtailed for months, and experts say that undoubtedly contributed to the spike in deaths.

According to Carroll, the pandemic both exacerbated risk factors and reduced access to mitigation measures and treatment options. People experienced severe emotional strain, were forced to isolate from others, and faced scaled-back social services in many communities.

“I don’t think anyone enjoyed the first year of the pandemic,” Carroll said. “All of us went back to our coping strategies... It’s also the social isolation. It is more dangerous to use any type of drug by yourself.”

In an interview with Fox News Wednesday, Sen. Rob Portman, R-Ohio, called for the Biden administration to work harder to cut off the supply of fentanyl coming into the country, launch a public awareness campaign about fentanyl-laced drugs, and step up treatment and recovery programs.

“They have got to tighten it up, and that means working with China, working with Mexico much more aggressively and tightening of the border," Portman said.

Despite the Republican criticisms, experts say the Biden administration has taken some positive steps to improve treatment for opioid addiction in the last 11 months. Those efforts include relaxing regulations regarding how many take-home doses methadone clinics can provide and making it easier for clinics to offer mobile services.

The Office of National Drug Policy has released model law templates encouraging states to increase access to naloxone and safe syringe programs. The Department of Health and Human Services has also launched a national overdose prevention strategy focused on evidence-based treatment and harm reduction.

“At a time when overdose deaths have reached an unprecedented number, we must meet people where they are and do everything we can to save lives,” Gupta said Wednesday.

According to Carroll, lowering barriers to access to medication is vital to saving lives, as is ensuring drug users understand the risks posed by fentanyl. The CDC now allows overdose response programs to use federal grant funds to purchase fentanyl test strips, which can determine if drugs have been mixed with the substance and help prevent overdoses.

“People are dying because we deny them the ability to understand what they put in their bodies and make safer choices,” she said.
