Toxic firefighting foam: Exclusive documents show military knew about the risk for years

by JOCE STERMAN, ALEX BRAUER and ANDREA NEJMAN | The National Desk

Mon, November 29th 2021 at 2:37 PM
Updated Mon, January 31st 2022 at 1:43 PM
8
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
PFAS appears in firefighting foam that has been routinely used on military bases nationwide (Photo: Department of Defense)

DAYTON, Ohio (TND) — Spotlight on America is uncovering new evidence about a toxic firefighting foam that may have endangered our service members for decades. A whistleblower is revealing new documents that show the military was aware that toxic foam, which contains a set of cancer-causing chemicals, could threaten human health. He says he’s speaking out to inform veterans and active service members about the potential harms associated with this foam, hoping the military will finally acknowledge the risk to firefighters who’ve long used it.

Kevin Ferrara spent 20 years putting out fires in the Air Force, often using a high-powered, chemical-based foam known as Aqueous Film-Forming Foam or AFFF. Now he’s putting out warnings about potential health problems linked with that toxic product. "We were told it was soap and water. We sprayed it up and down the flight lines, out of the training grounds. We sprayed kids during fire prevention visits. We didn’t think it was toxic. We thought it was safe.”

Now the retired Air Force vet who once served as Command Deputy Fire Chief at Air Combat Command Headquarters knows the truth. As Spotlight on America has reported for years, the firefighting foam used by the military since the 1970s contains PFAS, which studies show are linked to cancer and other health problems. The foam has contaminated drinking and groundwater at hundreds of military bases nationwide and leaked into nearby farms, threatening our food supply. Ferrara fears it may have sickened first responders who had no idea that what they were using to battle fires on behalf of the Department of Defense could impact their health.

In an exclusive interview with Spotlight on America, Ferrara told us that the firefighters he worked alongside had no idea AFFF was a potential threat, saying they used it to wash their trucks, even for bathing in the shower. But during his time in the service, he began to notice a shift that sounded alarms in his mind about potential dangers associated with the foam.

According to unclassified emails Ferrara shared with Spotlight on America, the Air Force was concerned about PFAS in AFFF as far as 2013. That’s when a PowerPoint presentation Ferrara supplied us shows the military acknowledging PFAS chemicals "may pose risks to human health."

But Ferrara’s documents show it wasn't until 2015 that firefighters were told to stop daily testing of the foam systems on their trucks, with its use eventually restricted to emergencies only.

And yet it took until 2016 for the military to take what it calls "aggressive steps" on the issue, with the Air Force fire chief announcing in a memo that year that the military was moving away from AFFF to a more environmentally friendly formula, saying they were "totally committed to protecting human health and the environment."

Though the foam is being phased out, Ferrara is trying to make up lost time, making sure firefighters are aware of the potential hazards of what they’ve been exposed to, accusing the military of failing to be proactive. He now speaks at firefighter conferences and has even launched a podcast in hopes of getting the military to acknowledge potential harms to first responders who were repeatedly exposed to toxic foam.

They're completely ignoring the fact that firefighters for decades have been exposed to this," retired Air Force veteran Kevin Ferrara told us. “Firefighters are sick. They’re developing cancer, other medical illnesses. And firefighters at the end of the day we just want answers.

This year, Congress forced the Department of Defense to start doing blood testing during firefighters' annual physicals to document PFAS exposure levels. According to ATSDR, a federal public health agency that investigates emerging chemicals of concern and conducts research, some studies have already found higher levels of PFAS in firefighters who use PFAScontaining AFFF compared to the general population.

But this past summer, the Inspector General scolded the DOD for not having a plan to track and analyze the results of that blood testing, saying they're missing an opportunity to assess significant long-term health effects related to PFAS. The inspector-general stated, "Without tracking, trending, and analyzing PFAS blood test results, DOD officials will not know the extent or effect of PFAS exposure among the firefighter population across the DOD.”

If the military isn't keeping track, Ferrara says he will. He’s has launched an effort to survey firefighters to get their blood test results, hoping to add his own data to those studies showing correlations between higher PFAS levels and AFFF use. Active service members have anonymously been turning in their results, he says.

But veterans exposed to the toxic foam for decades may not even be able to find out their numbers. That’s because the Veteran’s Administration doesn't recommend blood tests to determine levels of PFAS, according to its website.

Ferrara says it's time for the military to face this issue head-on. He's fighting to push along testing, research and a reckoning, so firefighters who've risked their lives for their country don't have to lose them because of the military's inaction.

"Firefighting is a brotherhood, and we protect each others' backs," whistleblower Kevin Ferrara told us, fighting back tears. "I'll go to bat for anybody, especially my fellow firefighters, my fellow veterans. They deserve it."

Spotlight on America has repeatedly reached out to the DOD for interviews with leaders on this issue. They have not agreed to speak with us, and did not answer specific questions for this story related to potential harms to military firefighters as a result of PFAS exposure, how the DOD is handling blood testing for PFAS, or requests from people like Ferrara to be more forthcoming with information about PFAS-related health concerns that could impact firefighters. The DOD routinely updates its efforts on PFAS related issues on its website, which can be found here. There do not appear to be any specific reports or articles related to the impacts of PFAS use on first responders.

"I wish the DOD would care as much as I do," said Ferrara. "But it seems like they don't and that’s how our veterans feel. That’s how our current firefighters feel. It’s like they don't care. They keep kicking that can down the road."

Put simply, he told us what he wants the military to do.

"Get off your butt and do the right thing."

Spotlight on America is continuing to push for answers when it comes to PFAS exposure in toxic firefighting foam. Look for our extended coverage on Spotlight on America Presents, later this month.

Outside of the military, some local and airport fire departments use AFFF to this day. Its use is banned in several states including California, Colorado, Connecticut, Illinois, Maine, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New York and Washington, but not every state has passed a ban. In fact, the House recently passed the "Build Back Better Bill," which would include $95 million for local fire departments to purchase firefighting foam and gear made without PFAS.
