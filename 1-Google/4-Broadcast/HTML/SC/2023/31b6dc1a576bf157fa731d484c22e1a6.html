Traditional news consumption on decline as digital platforms, streaming services ascend

by CORY SMITH | The National Desk

Thu, December 7th 2023 at 5:31 PM
FILE - Zach Stafford adjusts the volume as he and his mother watch television inside their home on April 28, 2020, in Belleville, Ill. (AP Photo/Jeff Roberson)

    TOPICS:

    News consumptionDigital subscriptionsPew Research CenterStreaming servicesTraditional news outletsYounger audiencesNewspaper circulationMedia landscape

(TND) — Audiences continue to shrink for traditional news outlets, including newspapers and network news, according to a Pew Research Center analysis of data from Comscore, the Alliance for Audited Media and others.

A smaller share of American adults say they closely follow the news, down to 38% in 2022 from 51% in 2016.

The declines have been felt across all age groups, though older people still consume more news than younger folks.

Nearly two-thirds of people 65 and older told the Pew Research Center that they closely follow the news, while just 19% of those 18-29 said the same.

Even among the 65-plus group, however, dedicated news consumption is down 11 percentage points from 2016.

Weekday newspaper circulation, counting digital subscriptions, has dropped 32% over five years.

Behemoths in the newspaper industry, The New York Times and The Wall Street Journal, have mitigated some of their print declines with increases in digital subscriptions.

But that comes with the caveat that some digital subscriptions to The New York Times are limited to cooking and games, according to the Pew Research Center.

And, perhaps surprisingly, traffic to the most visited news websites, which aren’t limited to traditional newspapers, has declined over the past couple of years.

The Pew Research Center found that the average number of monthly unique visitors to those news sites was 3% lower in the last quarter of 2022 compared to the previous year. And that was on top of a 13% drop the year before.

Around 3 million TVs tuned into ABC, CBS, NBC and Fox evening news in 2022, down from over 4 million in 2016.

Old-fashioned AM/FM radio, which of course is not all news content, still holds a large audience. But it’s fallen while podcasting has gained much larger audiences.

The Pew Research Center found that about 82% of Americans listen to traditional radio in a given week, down from 92% a decade earlier.

Meanwhile, 42% are monthly podcast listeners – up from 12% a decade ago.

Streaming is also taking over for traditional TV.

Variety this summer reported that broadcast and cable TV dropped under 50% for total TV usage for the first time, while streaming shares reached a new high.

Robert Thompson, the director of the Bleier Center for Television and Popular Culture at Syracuse University, said viewers and listeners have an overwhelming number of options these days.

“Every time a new streaming service comes online, they do not add two hours of the day,” he said.

The big outlets did a better job transitioning way back from radio to television than they have in recent years transitioning from broadcast TV to streaming, he said.

Legacy outlets were “sluggish” in their eventual embrace of streaming, Thompson said.

The Pew Research Center analysis certainly paints a picture of declining news consumption, he said.

And that may be true.

But he also thinks it may just be harder to track news consumption in this new media landscape.

People are getting news from late-night talk shows and TikTok videos.

News that might originate with a legacy outfit gets aggregated across countless nontraditional channels.

“It's not like your influencer in the apartment next door to you has a bureau in Baghdad,” he said.

Until 1963, network nightly news was 15 minutes a night, he said.

Then it went to a half hour.

Then, in the 1980s and ‘90s, CNN and other 24-hour cable news channels started vying for our attention.

And then the choices became “infinite” with the internet.

Is linear television – TV from broadcasters and cable outlets – living on borrowed time?

Thompson said the answer depends on how far out we’re looking.

“But, in the grand scheme of things, yes,” he said.
