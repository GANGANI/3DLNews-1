News to me: How Gen Z is changing news media and what it might look like in the future

by Connor Ingalls

Wed, September 21st 2022 at 11:40 AM
Updated Thu, September 22nd 2022 at 8:47 PM
Social Media is playing a big role in changing how the news industry operates(WPDE)

    TOPICS:

    Colorado,United StatesSouth Carolina,United StatesTikTokAugie GrantMadison GervasiRussell DowleySydney HenryColin Faherty

HORRY COUNTY, S.C. (WPDE) — Newspapers, then radio. TV, then the internet.

As technology changes, so does how we get our news.

Now there's a seemingly infinite amount of it and ways to consume it.

"The biggest change is technology has brought us so many new ways to communicate," University of South Carolina professor of journalism Dr. Augie Grant said.

Dr. Grant's got nearly 40 years of experience teaching at the college level, so he's seen these changes happen in real-time.

"It used to be the only way you were exposed to news was seeking out a newscast or picking up and reading the newspaper," Dr. Grant said. "Now if you're just following your own social media feed, you have news constantly being delivered to you because it's being shared by others."

That explosion of information is having a big impact on those that will dictate how news media changes going forward, Generation Z.

Coastal Carolina University students spoke with ABC15 about how their generation is getting news, how they feel about it, and what they want to see going forward.

"I definitely would consume news on a daily basis," student Dorian Veliky said.

I try to use like your typical new outlets, your legacy media outlets, but also a lot of it comes from just what friends post on social media," student Sydney Henry said.

"I think this generation is more aware of things and they're more vocal about it," student Russell Dowley said.

He's right. A recent report by the American Press Institute and Associated Press shows that 79% of Americans ages 16 to 40 get news daily.

Maybe not all too surprising, most of the time they're getting it through social media.

"TikTok influencers talking about things," student Madison Gervasi said about her preferred method. "There's a lot of like things that happen in the White House, I follow certain influencers that talk about those issues."

"I follow a lot of social media accounts on Instagram, and they'll post current events or things that have been just happening in the world," student Robynn Wilson said.

While social media has allowed us to stay constantly connected with what's happening, it's also led to the rise of "fake news."

They are more informed than any generation," Dr. Grant said. "They're getting more information. Not all of it is true, but they have more information.

"You're not finding you know just a page of news stories," Wilson said. "You're finding people posting the stuff that they want to post about it."

That skepticism is widespread across Gen Z.

The same API & AP study shows that 9 in 10 young people are worried about misinformation.

"With the fake news and everything, some people just aren't sure about it, and so they try to look at multiple sources," Henry said.

"I feel like I always check things," Gervasi said. "Because people just share things to share it at this point."

Another issue...the constant overflow can take its toll.

I'm sick of seeing like the bad stuff that's happening in the world," student Sophia Kern said. "There's so much bad happening.

"Like when I see something cool I'm like 'oh that's awesome,' and then the next one is like war or something," student Colin Faherty said. "I'm like OK that was cool, that was a nice five seconds of happiness."

The API & AP found just 33% of young people enjoy getting the news, down from 53% seven years ago.

"You're just getting all of these headlines that are trying to grab your attention, and they just end up being depressing," Henry said.

Particularly when those social media sites they follow are using algorithms that continue to populate similar posts.

"It's so bad on the mental health," Kern said.

So how do we keep Gen Z from turning away completely? Dr. Grant believes it starts outside of media, and with the generation themselves learning how to trust in news again.

"One of the biggest emphases we have in academia is on media literacy," Dr. Grant said. "Trying to help people learn how do you evaluate the value of information."

After that, he says the delivery itself will change, to stand out from the rest of the overflow in an engaging way.

They're going to look for a lot more experience," Dr. Grant said. "So whereas I want to read about something, you might want to see something, they want to put on the goggles and they want to be in the experience. So I think that's one of the waves we're going to have coming in the next 10 to 15 years.

"Get us to do a certain thing, or get us to get us a certain place," Kern said.

"It's like we want to see something less serious," Dowley said. "Something new, something fresh, colorful, instead of like stale and played out for like the millionth time."

While no one can say exactly how the future will play out, one thing's for sure - news isn't going anywhere.

"The thing that will not change is people will have news consumption habits and journalists have to follow those habits," Dr. Grant said. "The medium will change."

We'll just have to keep up.
