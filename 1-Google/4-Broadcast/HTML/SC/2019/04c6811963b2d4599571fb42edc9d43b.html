Myrtle Beach Safari owner said 'Lions are a part of a multi-state investigation'

by Annette Montgomery

Thu, December 12th 2019 at 10:00 PM
Updated Thu, December 12th 2019 at 8:45 AM
Law enforcement agencies respond to Myrtle Beach Safari (WPDE File)

MYRLTE BEACH, S.C. (WPDE) — According to its website, Myrtle Beach Safari is a place where you can feed elephants and watch tigers swim, but on Wednesday Dec.12 the safari had visitors for a different reason.

Several South Carolina law enforcement agencies were at the safari Wednesday to speak with Director Bhagavan "Doc" Antle.

South Carolina Law Enforcement Division (SLED) agents served warrants at the safari, according to Tommy Crosby, with SLED.

The agencies at the Safari were SLED, the South Carolina Department of Natural Resources (SCDNR) and Horry County police.

Antle said the agencies were there because of a multi-state investigation dealing with three of the lions at the safari.

“We have the three lions they’re fabulous little babies that are here with us. Many of our guests this summer all hugged them and met them and those lions are permanent residents here at the preserve. The lions are part of an ongoing investigation the state is working on and they wanted us to try and help them look at the trail of where the lions had come from and any ideas or evidence we had about what the lion's lives had been before they moved here to Myrtle Beach Safari," said Antle.

Antle told ABC 15 that the lions at his safari are descendants of lions from Wilson's Wild Animal Park in Virginia. According to our sister station WHSV, that zoo was closed after the owner was indicted on forty-six counts of animal cruelty and, in August of 2019, more than 100 animals were seized from the zoo.

Antle said swabs were taken from inside the three lions' mouths to make sure they were related to the lions in the investigation in Georgia. He said state law enforcement officials are helping the state attorney's office in Virginia with their investigation.

“The lions were DNA tested. The process was looked over so that they could see where the lions had been and how they had been brought from Virginia to South Carolina and just looking at all of the pieces to go back to another investigation. Everyone here today is actually just assisting the state’s attorney office in Virginia. The state attorney's office in Virginia was looking for the information and they can't just come to South Carolina themselves so they asked SLED to come and go through the process," said Antle.

Antle said the information law enforcement asked him for has already been shared with federal entities.

"To me, it's a giant exercise in futility and that the fed has records of all of this already but the state, somehow, not the state of South Carolina, but the state of Virginia, kind of came at it from another direction. There's a process that happens where government veterinarians come to our facility every 90 to 120 days, approximately. They watch every animal that comes and goes, whether it's born or it's brought in to the property as a donation or some other means that we acquire it. They check all of those," said Antle.

Antle said he wants to let all of his concerned neighbors know he is sorry about the traffic jam this caused and law enforcement agencies wanted to make sure the area was secure and everyone that lives and works for Myrtle Beach Safari was on the same page. He said, for now, the lions will stay at the safari and he does not know the next steps regarding the ongoing investigation.
