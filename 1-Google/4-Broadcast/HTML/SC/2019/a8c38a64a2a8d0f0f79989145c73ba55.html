The article I wish I'd had: Diagnosed with POTS and still anchoring the news

by Summer Dashe

Wed, November 13th 2019 at 6:28 PM
Updated Sat, December 21st 2019 at 9:16 PM
8

I have been visiting Vanderbilt University since I was 7-years-old. My older siblings both attended undergrad there. Little did I know 20 years later, I would end up in that very university hospital for answers. I now see a cardiologist and geneticist at VUMC. (Summer Dashe/WPDE)

CONWAY, S.C. (WPDE) — Editor's Note: If you're reading this on a mobile device, click here for the best experience.

For the last three years, I’ve started my day on the floor and ended it on the news. When I was first diagnosed with Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome or POTS, I researched. I read articles and journal entries and blog posts. I stayed up until the early morning hours Googling and searching the internet until I couldn’t find anything new to read anymore. I research. It’s what I do. I’m a journalist. My job is to find answers, but when I started feeling sick in 2017, I couldn’t find any answers, so I’m writing for you the article I wish I’d had.

I can’t pinpoint the day I woke up and my body stopped working as it should. For me, it was gradual, but sudden all at the same time. Some patients with POTS can tell you the exact date their autonomic nervous system malfunctioned.

The autonomic nervous system controls everything you don’t think about. Think “auto,” like “automatic.” Some of those tasks include breathing, digestion, pupillary response, temperature control, heart rate, bladder function and blood pressure. That means the effects are widespread and symptoms are many. Vanderbilt University in Nashville, Tennessee, has an autonomic dysfunction clinic. Their criteria for diagnosing someone with POTS is an increase in heart rate of at least 30 beats per minute upon standing.

“What that means is that when you stand up your heart starts racing,” Dr. James Muldowney, a cardiologist in the clinic, explained.

One of my favorite pieces of information I found in my research was this video by Dysautonomia International, a nonprofit organization for autonomic nervous system disorders. It explains what POTS means and its symptoms. This journal entry by the American Heart Association explains the different subsets of POTS very well. I have hyperadrenergic POTS. My blood pressure also goes up when I stand and my body produces high amounts of norepinephrine, the fight or flight hormone.

I know I spent my last day feeling normal somewhere around May of 2017. I had just lost about 30 pounds and was working out with a trainer. My best friend and former coworker, Sydney Glenn, and I had formed a consistent workout routine. My fiancé and I went kayaking and biking on weekends. I was 26-years-old and at my healthiest. That’s why when I began waking up feeling depleted of energy, I couldn’t understand why.

Next, I stopped sleeping. I was exhausted all the time, but I couldn’t sleep. It was like my body had forgotten how to turn itself off. It just wouldn’t do it. I had recently been promoted to evening anchor at WPDE, so I thought perhaps the drastic change in schedule had thrown off my rhythm. My doctor prescribed me an anti-anxiety medication. It would be the first of about a dozen given to me over the next three years. The tests he ran all came back normal.

One morning, I stood up from my bed and instantly sat down on the floor. My head felt floaty and I started sweating profusely. I was shaking. I couldn’t figure out if I was about to pass out or throw up, so I crawled to my bathroom. That began happening more and more until it turned into my normal routine. Sit up. Feet dangle over the side of the bed. Hand on the wall. Slide down to the floor. Big deep breath. Crawl to the bathroom. Shaking. Sweating. Sick. All over again.

The symptoms seemed to slowly wear off throughout the day. It was like a bad hangover. By the time I’d leave for work in the afternoon I would be coming out of it. When 5 p.m. rolled around for our first newscast of the night, I felt okay. At 11p.m. I was normal again.

Doctor after doctor told me these were panic attacks. They told me I had anxiety. They suggested therapy and a slew of medications. At first, I refused to take them. How could I be doing this to myself, I thought? I’m making myself this sick? They blamed the job. News is stressful, but I’ve lived for this since I was little. Why now? It didn’t make any sense.

One day I emptied the dishwasher and was so out of breath and exhausted from doing so that I slumped down on the floor and scooted to the couch. I laid there for hours. Another day, I had a doctor’s appointment and after walking the short hall to the exam room I felt so sick I curled up on the table while I waited. On a Sunday, I called my mom and told her I thought I was dying. I debated going to the hospital and asked her if she thought I should. I didn’t. How embarrassing would it be if I went to the emergency room for anxiety?

I’ll never forget the trip to Nashville in 2018 to visit my sister when I spent almost the entire weekend in bed. My insomnia was so bad I would go days without any sleep at all, but my body was begging for rest. The day my fiancé and I were supposed to leave for that trip, he found me resting my head on the toilet seat in the first-floor bathroom. I had gone to get in the car but didn’t make it. I couldn’t see properly and it wasn’t because of the tears streaming down my face. I felt like I couldn’t get enough air, so I kept taking big, heaving, breaths. I was out of it. Throw up or pass out? We almost missed our flight. I dragged myself to the airport with a plastic bag in my purse in case I got sick.

Then there was Hurricane Florence. I had my bags packed and ready to move into the news station. I was nervous but so excited to anchor our wall-to-wall coverage. I called my boss from an ambulance at 5 a.m. the day before the storm. I couldn’t keep food down. I could barely stand up. I had a fever. I was a wreck. I wanted nothing more than to be sitting at that anchor desk, riding out the storm with my team, while viewers got the information they needed from us. I was furious. I had been clawing my way through these symptoms. How dare this thing, whatever was wrong, impact my career.

That afternoon at the hospital Sydney, my fiancé, and I watched the governor’s press conference. He issued mandatory evacuations of all coastal hospitals. Discharge non-critical patients. Transfer all others. The nurse told me if I could eat a chicken nugget without puking, I could go home. Sydney ate the chicken nugget when the nurse wasn’t looking. What are friends for, right? I spent the next four days at the station, just like I’d planned. I anchored our wall-to-wall coverage. I drank chicken broth and popped nausea pills and occasionally threw up during reporter live shots. No, I can’t tell you exactly when I developed POTS, but I can tell you this was the moment I decided this was absolutely not anxiety.

“Your mind may be calm and you may say I have no reason to be nervous, I may have no reason to be anxious, but your body’s anxious,” Dr. Muldowney explained. “A lot of doctors, because they examine their patients seated, or the patient will get their vital sign seated or lying down, may have a normal heart rate in clinic and their doctor will think that they’re anxious.”

Writing this out it seems insane that I could have thought those symptoms were all in my head, but I did. That’s what doctors kept telling me. My tests were all normal. Before all of this, the only time I’d ever needed a doctor was for the common cold, strep or flu. It never occurred to me that one day I would go to doctors and they wouldn’t know what was wrong with me.

According to Dysautonomia International, 85% of POTS patients are given a psychiatric diagnosis prior to receiving the proper one. Additionally, the average time to diagnose is more than four years. I love this CNN article I found titled, “A doctor said it was all in her head, but it was the most common condition you've never heard of.”

I went to 11 doctors over this two-year span. Dysautonomia International numbers show roughly 90% of people with POTS are women and many become ill in their childbearing years. For this reason, it's not surprising that my OBGYN was the first doctor who believed me. I'll never forget telling her my symptoms and she looked at me and said, "Oh, Summer, this is not anxiety." (I love you, Dr. Alo!!!) She referred me to a neurologist. POTS was only first officially characterized in 1993, but Dr. Muldowney said it was first observed long before during the Civil War.

“The reasons why were that women, unfortunately, weren’t taken seriously back then and, unfortunately, some of that with health concerns persists to this day,” he explained.

That is unacceptable. I’ve witnessed it. I went to the neurologist and explained my vision had become blurry and I started noticing some memory loss and forgetfulness. I now know it’s called brain fog and this article explains it well. At the time, after my MRI came back normal the neurologist told me I had anxiety and maybe a virus. When I tried to ask more questions, like why I was shaking so badly, he seemed offended. My coworkers had begun to notice the tremors and I had trouble writing with pen and paper. He offered me an anti-psychotic drug for sleep, an anti-viral for the fatigue, and a seizure medication for the shaking. He said I was experiencing “benign tremors” and if they bothered me, I could try the drug. I didn’t take any of it.

I left that appointment, went to my car, called my parents and cried. It took everything in me just to get myself to these doctor’s appointments. Leaving with no answers was defeating. I was exhausted and only getting worse. The internist I saw next decided to do an EKG. It came back slightly abnormal. My pulse was high. The EKG read, “inappropriate sinus tachycardia.” He ordered more tests but said he expected them to be normal. He suggested I had chronic fatigue syndrome. I didn’t believe that, so I took the only abnormal test result I’d had and ran with it.

Two years after that miserable trip to Nashville to visit my sister, I went back. We had a family vacation planned in April of 2019. Just days before my trip, and partially wondering if I was losing my mind, I called Vanderbilt’s cardiology department. With no referral and only desperation in my voice, I asked if there were any appointments the following week. They had one. Off I went. Shaking. Sweating. Sick. Plastic bag in my purse-- just in case. That’s how, after three years of misdiagnoses, I ended up right where someone with this condition could only dream of landing. It took a cardiologist at Vanderbilt about two minutes to suspect I had POTS. (Thank you, Dr. Jennings!!!)

I spent the next 30 days on a heart monitor. A little light-up device sat on my chest linked to a cell phone I carried with me to report any symptoms. I asked my producers to keep an eye out and make sure my chest wasn’t blinking on air. They kindly kept watch. I was thrilled. Sounds wrong, I know. I wasn’t wishing for a heart problem, but I was wishing for an answer.

In May, I got on a plane back to Nashville. By now you know the drill. Shaking. Sweating. Sick. Only this time, feeling much less defeated. I went back to Vanderbilt for more tests and to meet with a doctor from the autonomics clinic. They performed what’s called a tilt table test. I was strapped down and hooked up to monitors. They measured my blood pressure and pulse laying flat and then at a 75-degree tilt. The nurse who performed my test asked me my symptoms throughout. Out of breath. Dizzy. Shaking. Nauseous. Light headed. Blurred vision. Headache. Foggy. I knew my pulse had to go up 30 beats per minute and stay there for 10 minutes during the tilt to qualify for POTS. After the nurse lowered me back down, I asked her if it had stayed high. When she said yes, I responded excitedly with, “nailed it!”

“It’s been a real lot of fun taking care of patients with POTS and other autonomic diseases,” Dr. Muldowney said.

That’s my doctor. During my first appointment with Dr. Muldowney, he said, “Your body is anxious, your mind is not.”

This guy gets me.

“We’re an interesting bunch, aren’t we?” I responded.

“You’re absolutely an interesting bunch,” he laughed.

I interviewed him for this story when I went back for a follow-up appointment in September. I wandered into Vanderbilt Heart followed by a photojournalist carrying a TV camera.

“You didn’t expect to treat a news anchor and not end up on the news, did you?” I joked when I saw Dr. Muldowney again.

“Actually, I did,” he quipped.

For months after that initial diagnosis, I didn’t want anyone to know. I was terrified that if people found out I would be considered “un-hirable” in the news industry. Most of my coworkers probably didn’t know about my illness until this story. If you check out this blog post about the seven stages you’ll go through after a POTS diagnosis, it’s a lot like grief. You’ll see the final stages include acceptance. I’m there.

I suddenly realized what a slap in the face it would be to all the other people with POTS if I didn’t do something with this. The doctors in the autonomics clinic at Vanderbilt are overwhelmed with patients. POTS is more common than diseases like Multiple Sclerosis and Parkinson’s, yet most doctors have never heard of it.

“Their primary care providers want us, as the specialists, to completely manage this disease,” Dr. Muldowney said. “Unfortunately, I’m one doctor in Nashville and I have patients coming from all across the country to see our group. We’re comprised of four physicians.”

This has to change. Despite the impact sharing this story has on my career, if it helps one other patient find answers or one more doctor recognize the signs, it was worth it. As my dad pointed out, “Summer, you were promoted and nominated for your first Emmy while sick with POTS. Look at what you’ve done.” Yes, over the last three years, I started my day on the floor, but I always ended it on the news. I’m proud that no matter how I felt, my viewers, coworkers and sources never would have known. I have no plans of stopping now, and thanks to Dr. Muldowney and the team at Vanderbilt’s Autonomic Dysfunction Clinic, I’ve still got a shot at this career I love so much.

That being said, I know I’m lucky to be able to write that last part. Publications on the Dysautonomia International website show the quality of life seen in people with POTS is equivalent to that of people with COPD, congestive heart failure, and those on dialysis in end-stage kidney failure. It is not deadly, but it can certainly ruin your life. Approximately 25% of people with POTS are so disabled they are unable to work or attend school. There is a wide range of severity. Some people have mild symptoms, while others are bedridden.

The three most common treatments for POTS are medication, high salt/high water diet and exercise. Unfortunately, not all treatments work for every patient. For me, salt and water haven’t helped at all. Medication and exercise have. The way I understand it, you essentially have to re-train your body to tolerate standing up. Mind you, fatigue is a debilitating symptom. I went from working out daily to scooting around my house on my butt. I knew I couldn’t do this alone or unsupervised. Now, imagine trying to find a physical therapist who knows POTS. After two years, I feel I earned this next part of the story.

“That’s not something that was tested on my board examines,” Dr. Zach Daniels, with Professional Rehabilitation Services in Myrtle Beach, recalled.

It was, however, something he happened to come across in college. He had a professor who specialized in this area of research, so he studied it. I coincidentally ended up in his office. I walked in with an exercise program about 15 pages deep that the Vanderbilt team had sent me home with and I handed it to Zach. I fully expected him to turn me away.

“I was honest with you when we first started. I don’t know a ton about this, but we’re going to work through it together and try to figure out what we can do,” he said.

According to the doctors at Vanderbilt, the cause of POTS is unknown. Some patients developed autonomic dysfunction after physical deconditioning, for instance, being bedridden after surgery. Others notice their symptoms begin after a viral illness. For me, Dr. Muldowney recognized signs of a genetic disorder called Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome. I never knew I had it. POTS is very common in people with EDS. That’s a connective tissue disorder that causes overly lax joints, skin and blood vessels. Guess what? My physical therapist studied that in school, too. He has helped me with symptoms for both diseases since that very first day I walked in with a packet.

“To be honest, I went home and watched the news [that day],” he laughed. “I think it was 5 o’clock you were on, and I was just hoping that you wouldn’t be passing out.”

If you’re a regular viewer you’ll know, I was always just fine on-air even after my workouts. I’ve also found that I love swimming. Dr. Muldowney once said, “you’re basically allergic to gravity.” I may belong on Mars, but my swimming pool is closer. I currently take a calcium channel blocker and an antihypertensive drug. Combined, the two have knocked out the insomnia, calmed the shaking and given me back some energy. Between the swimming, physical therapy and medication, I am doing so much better. This time last year, I couldn’t stand up before 1 p.m. These days, I’m in the pool or at physical therapy by noon. I speak to medical students any chance I get. It's my pleasure to help spread the word and inform more people. If you'd like me to come speak to your group, please reach out!

I may never feel like I did before May of 2017, but I have a new motto, “I’m not dying, so I better figure out a way to live.” Every few mornings it’s the same old routine from bed to floor to bathroom. It is just flat out hard to do life some days. When my vision gets blurry, my wonderful co-anchor, Trey, will drive me to work. He makes me laugh so I forget I want to cry and often brings an extra water to the studio for me. If you see me with sunglasses on, I promise I’m no diva, my eyes just aren’t working in the sun. I carry a rag with me to wipe away the sweat. Embrace chivalry! Why, yes, I would love a seat! Standing is the enemy. I noticed my incredible neighbors take up my trash bin on Mondays and it saves me energy. When I wander into my coffee shop they already know it must be decaf. My nail technician is always kind about my sweaty hands and offers me water. I'll be visiting Sydney in Salt Lake City in January and she is already planning things for the afternoon knowing mornings can be tough. My boss never doubted my ability to do my job when I told her I had POTS. My fiancé proposed three days after I got the diagnosis. He has adjusted his whole world to help mine. How thankful I am for the people helping me figure out a way to live.

“There are some people who, unfortunately, despite having the greatest will power and greatest personalities, their bodies just don’t want to cooperate,” Dr. Muldowney told me.

That quote really hurts my heart to write. It’s not what I want to publish. In those early days of my diagnosis, I researched in hopes of finding a cure. I thought, surely, if I kept digging through journal entries and doctors’ research and news articles, I would find someone or something to fix this. I spend my life finding answers. If you’ve seen any of my investigative reports, you know I don’t stop. No is not an acceptable answer to me. My skills have never failed me, but this time, I simply couldn’t find the answer.

During our interview, our chief photographer asked my physical therapist what my future looks like.

“That’s a good question and I wish I had an answer,” Dr. Daniels said. “Nobody can really give you a definite answer and I think that’s part of your frustration is you just don’t know. Nobody can give you a perfect answer and I can’t either.”

Of course, I already knew this from my own research, but my heart sank when he said it. It sank again when I went back to listen to that interview while I was putting this story together. This isn’t what I want to write. It still stings every time I am reminded that I might spend the rest of my life waking up feeling sick.

The doctors at Vanderbilt’s Autonomic Dysfunction Center are studying two areas. Dr. Muldowney told me they’re looking into a vagal nerve stimulator in hopes of creating an implantable device to alleviate some of the symptoms. It’s very early in development. It’s still in the external stage and is not implantable yet.

Additionally, he mentioned research into an autoimmune factor in some patients with POTS. He said one theory is that some instances of POTS could be caused by antibodies that attack your body and activate the sympathetic nervous system. He pointed me to testing being done at The University of Texas Southwestern. I had already come across this in my own 3 a.m. reading, no doubt. The UT Southwestern team is studying the use of Intravenous Immunoglobulin, or IVIG, on patients who may show an autoimmune cause of POTS.

“There are a lot of folks out there that really want to feel better and they’re desperate enough to want to try things before they’re ready,” Dr. Muldowney warned. “My biggest advice is to go with what we know and go with what we think is safe.”

It has been six months since I was diagnosed. I now realize that I may not have found the answer, but I did find the truth. My job is not to find an answer. That’s for my doctors at Vanderbilt. My job is to find the truth. That’s what journalists do. I did that. I just didn’t want to believe it. I asked my cardiologist at Vanderbilt where we are with a cure.

“Ultimately, it’s about management rather than cure and that’s unfortunate,” Dr. Muldowney said.

Yes, my heart sank when he said it. It felt heavy as I typed out the quote. This isn’t the article I wish I’d had.

If you’re reading this because you were recently diagnosed, and you’re in that research phase, I want to make you a promise. I know you’re tired. I know you’re overwhelmed. Let this be your last article for the night. Go to sleep or at least try. Trust me, you need it. Rest assured that if anything changes and our doctors come up with a cure tomorrow, I’ll write about it and publish the article I’ve been so hoping to find. I’ll end my day reporting it on the news.
