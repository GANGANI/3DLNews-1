Informing a community that has grown used to not having local news

by Simon Williams

Wed, April 15th 2020 at 7:02 PM
Updated Wed, April 15th 2020 at 7:44 PM
ABC15's Simon Williams launched a digital segment, “Trabajando Por Ti,” or “Working for You,” in English. It’s a weekly news report, entirely in Spanish, for counties across North and South Carolina. For the last month, it's bringing coronavirus coverage to these communities. (Simon Williams, WPDE)

HORRY COUNTY, S.C. (WPDE) — A total of 4,300 people. That might not seem like a lot, but it’s a number that blew me away; a number I never expected.

It’s how many views are on my first digital segment, “Trabajando Por Ti,” or “Working for You,” in English. It’s how many people watched a news report, entirely in Spanish, for counties across North and South Carolina.

For some, it’s the first time they’ve heard local news reported in Spanish.

Before launching the segment, I was visiting a friend that lives in Conway. She knows I work for ABC15 News, but it’s not something we talk about often. Her English is limited, so most of our conversations are in Spanish.

“¿Me puedes decir lo que paso la semana pasada en 501?” she asked, which translates to “Can you tell me what happened on Highway 501 last week?”

It was one of the rare occurrences she had asked me something related to news, so out of pure curiosity, I asked how she gets her local news.

“Mis hijos,” or, “My kids,” she responded.

I thought about that for a while after our conversation. Days after our conversation, actually. I mean, this is a woman who has lived in the area for 16 years. This is a woman with three kids making their way through Horry County Schools. This is a woman who works hard, seven days a week for her community. “And she can’t even get local news,” I thought.

No news about her kids’ schools. No news about her local governments. No news about the community she drives through, daily. Imagine that.

Those thoughts plagued my mind for a week. So, I brought a proposed solution to my news director: a weekly news segment, entirely in Spanish, with the week’s biggest headlines. Informing a community that lives, works, shops, and raises a family in a city that they’re left in the dark about.

“I love it,” my news director said. “I love it!” she said again. “Please let me know if there is anything you need from me.”

Fast-forward a couple of weeks, and it was ready to launch.

I produced a segment that was exactly 4 minutes and 19 seconds. Translating the stories, building the graphics, writing the transitions, and choosing the video, all I could think about was my friend in Conway.

The segment launched in mid-March, so of course, it was taken over with local, coronavirus coverage. I imagined what it would be like to only see national news on the coronavirus, but never at the local level. “What would I want to see in this segment?” I thought.

I finished producing the segment and walked into the studio to record.

I was so nervous.

Like, bad nervous. Sweaty palms, a shaky leg, and trembling fingers. I wanted to do right by my friend, and that was a lot of pressure.

A couple of takes later, I was satisfied. I finished editing the video and posted it to my Facebook page.

I would’ve been happy with my friend as the only view, but no. A total of 4,300 people. The comments poured in.

A Myrtle Beach man wrote, “Gracias por traer noticias en espanol,” or, “Thanks for bringing news in Spanish.”

“A lot of the Hispanic community really would like to understand what goes on in everyday life,” wrote Nancy Hernandez, “Especially local news, because most Hispanics base their news on Univision not knowing what goes on locally but only nationally.”

Hernandez later told me they were born and raised in Socastee, but their parents are from Mexico. They own a store in Conway.

"When I was young, I would have to interpret everything a news reporter was saying about a hurricane, about a tornado," said Hernandez. Aside from kids, others rely on Facebook, which could just be pure gossip.

I was crying. A stream of tears so fast, that didn’t feel like they’d ever quit. Not because of sympathy, or pity, but because of overwhelming emotion. I realized, that all those thoughts that plagued my mind for the week after talking with my Conway friend, were the reality for so many people.

All the people that live here. All the people that work here. All the people that have kids here. All the people who share these streets with me every single day, that are completely in the dark.

It was like every single person’s own reality hit me at once.

Another resource is offered through the South Carolina Department of Health and Environmental Control (DEHC). On their website, you can click on a small globe near the top of the screen to open a "language" menu. Most of all information is then translated to that language.

The comments, shares, and likes poured in days, and even weeks after I posted the video.

“We have a growing Hispanic community not only just in Myrtle Beach but throughout Horry County,” Erica Vasquez wrote a few days later. “We need to have more bilingual reporters in this area to keep the Spanish speaking population informed of very important news... especially in times like these,” she wrote.

That last line still rings in my head: “Especially in times like these.” It’s a big reason why I’m sharing my journey right now.

In times like these, we see the importance of information, and that’s something we take for granted, often. In this case, it’s information an entire community was used to not getting.

I hope to see a day where 4,300 turns into 5,000. Where 5,000 turns into 8,000, and maybe even higher as time goes on.

For me, though, I know I’ll value the information I have access to, and continue “trabajando” or “working,” for those who don’t have the easiest access.

If you know someone who can benefit from information in Spanish, the reports will be posted every Saturday on my Facebook page.
