A husband's message: 'If you're listening to this, it's because I'm dead'

by Denise Whitaker | KOMO News

Sun, May 31st 2020 at 11:07 AM
Updated Sun, May 31st 2020 at 11:28 AM

We’ve all heard the phrase “absence makes the heart grow fonder.” But more and more couples are being forced into rather close quarters, working from home, social settings nonexistent, during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Rachel and Harold Earls are survivors who have been in both scenarios, and they have observations they want to share.

Their love affair is like a made-for-TV movie:

Harold proposes to Rachel and they marry. But then right away, Harold leaves Rachel, to climb Mount Everest. It's a dream he started planning as a single man, while a cadet at West Point.

Given the 6.5% fatality rate on Everest, he thought to leave his young bride something before he left.

“Hey, honey, I guess if you’re listening to this, it’s because I’m dead,” he said in that recorded message.

“I left her a farewell message in case I didn't come back. And so, going back and listening to that and reading that honestly was incredibly touching to me and made me really put everything in perspective right now,” said Harold.

There was no immediate perspective for Rachel. Left behind for more than two months, she faced many struggles alone, but was there when her husband needed her. Harold reached her in his hardest hour.

“I'd gotten a stomach bug and I called her on the (satellite) phone. The empathy that she had, to just wrap her arms around me on the phone was huge and will stick with me forever,” he said.

Harold made it to the top of Mount Everest with a group of veterans, and now in these times of uncertainty he and Rachel share their struggles to help others.

“When it comes to communication you have to remember you're a team so whatever trial that you're facing, you're facing that together. It's not me against my husband - it's us against whatever we're facing, we face together,” said Rachel.

“I think that I’ve learned so much from my wife, just having compassion for the other person. I had only worried about myself. I had been single. I had just pursued my own dreams even with Everest so what I failed to understand, honestly until I got back from Everest until I got back from Ranger school, of the toll that took on my wife,” said Harold.

“I think in those situations you try to be strong and carry all that weight and almost pretend. But I think is a disservice to each other, because then at the end I came a little bit crumbling down and so I wish what I would have done is said, 'Honey I’m supporting you and I’m glad we're doing this together but here’s, I need your help as well,'" said Rachel.

Today, when there's tension or stress, this young couple has a coping mechanism, a safe word, so to speak.

“It's always that step back and check how am I treating my spouse? And so, we kind of have a phrase that we say. We call it recess, but it's our reset word so it's when we notice, hey, we're not being very kind to each other, hey let's reset. That's our way of saying we're on the same team,” said Rachel.

“The unexpected moments in life, and the unexpected moment in life we're in right now, turn out to be the big moments, the life-changing moments, the moments you remember,” said Harold.

Just about this time, in the middle of the interview, Harold had to stop. Their 2-year-old was knocking on the door of the room, they had closed themselves in, for the interview.

Leo has a younger brother, 1- year-old Wyatt. With little kids, the Earls also make sure their strategy includes spending time together with no distractions. Once they get the boys to bed, they spend time together. It gives them time to reflect and better connect.

Harold, a captain in the U.S. Army and Rachel, a video blogger, don’t believe they are experts or the be-all for relationship advice, but given the struggles they’ve already survived together, they decided to write a book to help others. It’s titled “A Higher Calling.”
