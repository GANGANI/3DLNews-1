Ever wonder how lightning forms? News 10 meteorologist breaks it down

by Ali Van Fleet

Mon, May 11th 2020 at 2:17 PM
4
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
How hair clings to a latex balloon (Ali Van Fleet)

Have you ever wondered what causes lightning?

Lightning is caused by a buildup of static electricity inside a storm cloud. Moving around inside the cloud are tiny water molecules called hydrometeors. These hydrometeors are colliding and bumping into each other—creating a static electric charge.

There are two kinds of electrical charges, positive (+) and negative (-) and they are attracted to each other. When two objects collide and rub against each other (like water molecules) there is a transfer of these electrons.

You’ve probably created your own static electric charge too. You know how when you rub your feet against the carpet and you “accidentally” touch your siblings and they feel that shock? That’s essentially a tiny lightning bolt you created.


Inside our storm cloud, the water molecules are bumping around and creating that electrical charge. The positive charges (+) gather at the top of the cloud while the negative charges (-) travel to the bottom of the cloud.

Keep in mind that while all this is happening, the storm itself is moving. As the storm system travels it is gathering a pool of positively charged particles along the ground.

Remember what I said about positive charges? Opposites attract. The positive particles on the ground are attracted to the negative particles at the bottom of the cloud. These positive particles will take the path of least resistance to get to the negatively charged particles. The positive charges will build upon tall objects such as trees, buildings, and sometimes even people!

Once enough of a charge is built up--- BOOM! Lightning is formed.

According to NOAA, the energy from one lightning flash could light a 100-watt bulb for more than 3 months.

Lightning can be very beautiful but also very dangerous. Which is why whenever you hear thunder head indoors!

According to the National Weather Service, the safest place from lightning is in your home or another building. Be sure to stay away from tall objects standing alone like flagpoles, trees, and telephone poles. If you’re swimming and you hear thunder, get out of the water immediately. Water is a conductor for electricity.

Now onto the fun stuff

I’m going to show you how to create your own charge and harness electricity to use for fun!

Materials: Balloon, empty soda can, and a head of hair from a willing participant.

Blow up your balloon because you’re going to use it to transfer some electrons. Take the blown-up balloon and vigorously rub it in your hair until the hair begins to stick to the balloon. Congrats! You have successfully transferred negative electrons.

Place the soda can on the ground sideways and without touching the can, use your balloon to attract the positive electrons on the can! If the can doesn’t move right away try rubbing the balloon on your head again. You can build an obstacle course or race someone in your house.
