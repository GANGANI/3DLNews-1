News of the Asian Giant Hornet is not reason to kill every bug you see

by Alex Hasenstab and KVAL.com Staff

Sat, May 9th 2020 at 5:12 PM
Updated Sat, May 9th 2020 at 7:14 PM
3

LANE COUNTY, Ore. — Beekeepers are asking you to pay attention.

Don't mistake important pollinators for a pest, especially with many flying insects emerging this spring. And with news of Asian Giant Hornets discovered in Washington, we’re reminded not to panic.

Work is buzzing for beekeeper Brent Hefley.

When a bee hive's population is abundant, a new queen takes over and the old Queen will take off with about a third to half of the workers and they will fly out of the hive

These swarms are looking for a new place to live, and are quite a sight.

“They're pretty loud, some people compared them to the sound of a train going by,” Hefley said.

But there's no cause for alarm, the bees are actually very docile at this time. The concern is getting them to a safe space.

In urban areas bees can sometimes attempt to move into the walls or roofs of structures.

“Really it's destructive for both the bees and a lot of work for beekeepers and not an expense for the homeowner,” Hefley said.

But bee's aren't the only critters stretching out their wings.

“This is the time of year where most insects start to emerge,” said Erica Chernoh.

Another flying insect that's not so welcome has been discovered in the pacific northwest. The Asian giant hornet - an invasive species - was discovered in Washington.

Erica Chernoh, a horticulture expert for OSU's Extension Program, wants to remind the public to keep a level head. And not start killing flying insects out of fear.

“There's a lot of fear put out in the public about that and that kind of leads to hysteria and then people go out and do irrational things that are really unnecessary,” Chernoh said.

Asian giant hornets are a threat to Oregon honey bees. Thankfully, the large 1 to 2-inch yellow wasps haven't been spotted in the state. Regardless, she says before you try to kill a pest, make sure it's actually a pest.

“Because sometimes what you think is a pest is actually a beneficial insect and we want to make sure that we aren't causing unnecessary harm to them,” Chernoh said.

Protecting pollinators by pinpointing pests.

You can call OSU extension's master garden program for help identifying insects in your yard.

You can also call the Oregon Department of Agriculture's invasive species hotline if you think you've seen an Asian giant hornet.

If you see a swarm, you can go to the Lane County Beekeepers' Association website for a list of local beekeepers who will safely remove it.
