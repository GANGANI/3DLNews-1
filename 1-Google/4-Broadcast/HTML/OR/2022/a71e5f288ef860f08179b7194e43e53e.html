2 Black Hawk helicopters crash near Snowbird; crew escapes serious injuries
by Matthew Jacobson & Daniel Woodruff, KUTV
Tuesday, Feb 22nd 2022
18
View All Photos
Two Black Hawk helicopters crashed in the Mineral Basin area of Snowbird on Feb. 22, 2022 (Photo: @liftermike93 via Twitter)
SNOWBIRD, Utah (KUTV) —

Two Utah National Guard Black Hawk helicopters crashed in the Mineral Basin area near Snowbird Tuesday morning, authorities said.

ADVERTISEMENT

The crash happened at approximately 9:25 a.m. in American Fork Canyon. The service members involved were part of the 2nd General Support Aviation Battalion, 211th Aviation Regiment.

According to the Chief Warrant Officer 5 Jared Jones, Utah National Guard aviation public affairs officer, the UH-60 helicopters were involved in mountainous winter training when the incident happened. One helicopter lost its blade which hit the tail rotor of the second helicopter, causing them to crash. One helicopter landed upside down, while the other landed upright.

Both UH-60s were damaged, Jones said, but the crew of eight people escaped major injury.

"It was a blessing that everyone was okay," Jones said.

"We are grateful that no one was seriously injured thanks to the quick reaction and training of both command pilots," added Maj. Matthew Green, commander, 2nd GSAB. "Right now, our top priority is taking care of both crews."

The crash happened on U.S. Forest Service land, about 150 yards away from the ski resort property, Jones said.

The incident is still under investigation. The Utah National Guard has canceled all training flights until further review so safety protocols and regulations can be reviewed.

Multiple people took to social media following the crash to share photos and videos of the crash.

Twitter user @LifterMike93 posted a video of the aftermath. He also capture a video prior to the crash that appears to show the two helicopters flying into the Mineral Basin area.

Another skier, Jani Radebaugh, witnessed the crash happen.

"We saw a backcountry helicopter crash, so sad," she tweeted. "Hopefully everyone is ok - it flipped on landing maybe because of blowing snow obscuring landing area. Blade flew quite a ways into the trees."

Later, Radebaugh told KUTV 2News it was surreal to see it happen.

"At first, you’re kind of like, is this real? Did this actually happen?" she said. "That rotor flew really far, and it flew in the direction away from the ski lift, thank goodness."

Noah Sikorski, a University of Utah student who also witnessed the crash, said it was "crazy" to see the helicopters going down so close to people.

"That is an area that backcountry skiers ski back through right where that second helicopter crashed," Sikorski said, "so people definitely could have gotten hurt, and we’re very lucky that they didn’t."

Jones said the landing zone has been used many times for training flights.

"They are surveyed to make sure they are large enough, factoring in margins for error, and I would assume that this one was selected knowing that it was far enough away from civilian populous that it was of minimal risk," Jones told reporters.

Asked by KUTV 2News if he could say for sure that was the case with this area, Jones said, "I haven’t been to this particular [landing zone] myself, so I’m not going to state that with certainty."

Snowbird Ski Resort closed Mineral Basin to skiing following the crash, and it was unclear when the area would reopen. The Mineral Basin Express and Baldy Express lifts were closed.
