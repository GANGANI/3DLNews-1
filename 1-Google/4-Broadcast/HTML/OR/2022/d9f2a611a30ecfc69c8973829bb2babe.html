Oregon mom challenging law requiring menstrual products in boys' bathrooms in schools
by News Staff
Tuesday, Jul 12th 2022
File SBG image.

Under a new law, menstrual products will soon be in all public school restrooms in Oregon, including the boys', girls' and all-gender restrooms. However, a school board member is challenging the state’s new law.

ADVERTISEMENT

The Menstrual Dignity Act, signed into law by Gov. Kate Brown, requires elementary, middle, and high schools to place the products in bathrooms along with instructions on how to use them by the end of the 2022-23 school year.

The program helps students participate actively in classes and school activities by alleviating some of the economic strain and experiences of shame that are often barriers for menstruating students accessing their education," says the Oregon Dept. of Education in its Menstrual Dignity for Students Toolkit.

Cherylene Stritenberg, an Eagle Point School District board member and part of the district's Diversity, Equity and Inclusion Team--acting on her own accord as a private citizen--is petitioning the Oregon Department of Education (ODE) to have that provision removed from the bill. She said her reasoning is financial.

"The added cost that we are going to put into our school districts to support this is over the top," Stritenberg said. "There’s no need for us to divert funds to go above and beyond what is actually needed. Boys do not menstruate, so to have them in boys' bathrooms is unnecessary."

Stritenberg wants the ODE to roll back that portion of the bill; instead, replacing it with a version that would only require they be placed at any two bathrooms throughout the school.

She filed a petition under an Oregon law that allows enacted legislation to be challenged within 90 days of it being signed into effect. In this case, because the Oregon Department of Education is the agency charged with implementing the new rules, the department was tasked with either denying the petition or initiating rulemaking processes within 90 days. Before making that call, the department is required to “invite public comment on the rule.”

A spokesperson for ODE would not schedule an interview about the petition and declined to discuss the reasons why an interview was not granted. In a written statement he said the invitation to comment "was sent to members of the public who have requested to receive updates related to ODE’s rulemaking process."

To submit a comment, interested residents can email ODE.RuleTestimony@state.or.us by July 20.

The bill received bipartisan support with only five no votes. One of its sponsors, Sen Chris Gorsek, D-Troutdale, said he stands by his yes vote and feels that having menstrual products in both boys' and girls' bathroom is essential.

"I think supporting both young women and transgendered people is an extremely important issue and one that we need to address," he said.

The bill dips into the biannually established statewide education of $9,296,000,000 for 2021-23 to fund distribution of the free products. The measure only sets aside $137,000 from the state's general fund for implementation.

When asked why lawmakers did not indicate their support for having the products in all bathrooms by allocating general funds to do so, Gorsek noted that he was not a member of the Ways and Means Committee.

When pushed on the fact that while he did not establish the fiscal impact, he did vote yes knowing that the funds would come from the already established education fund, Gorsek pointed to the $137,000 saying, "There was some money allocated."

"If there is not enough (money) we can always go back and adjust that," he said. "There are all sorts of needs across the state."

Kim Thatcher, who was one of the five senators who voted no on the measure, says she feels the products would be misused.

"I just know what my sons have done with mine and they play torpedoes with them, so if they are free and free access for little boys, then I think that we are inviting trouble," she said noting that she would rather let local districts decide how to allocate resources like free menstrual products.

"When people vote against something like that they, get called heartless people for not supporting everything and having government do everything for everyone. But we can't," she said.

The Eagle Point School District issued the following statement to our affiliate station in Medford, KTVL:

"Eagle Point School District 9 is fully complying with the law and associated administrative rules of the Menstrual Dignity Act. Mrs. Stritenberg is a valued and respected member of the EPSD9 Board of Directors. With regard to Mrs. Stritenberg's petition to the ODE regarding the Menstrual Dignity Act, she is acting as a private citizen, as is her right, and not as a representative or spokesperson of the EPSD9 Board of Directors."
