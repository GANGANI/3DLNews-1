Seattle woman grapples with long COVID as 20M in US also cope with chronic illness
by Molly Shen, KOMO News anchor/reporter
Tuesday, Feb 15th 2022
Katie Schaber talks to KOMO News about her experience with long COVID.
SEATTLE —

As optimism grows that the U.S. is finally emerging from the throes of the pandemic, frustration is also mounting for an estimated 20 million Americans who are still struggling with symptoms after they contracted COVID-19.

That has placed new scrutiny on nearly one-third of sufferers who have developed what's known as long COVID, including a Western Washington woman who is still struggling with her symptoms.

When you first meet Seattle's Katie Schaber, most people wouldn't think she requires regular appointments with a speech therapist.

But Schaber can go from eloquent to wordless in a matter of minutes.

"My husband asked me a question and I couldn’t answer it," she said. "I started having trouble speaking. I had words in my head and I couldn’t say them out loud."

That first bout with speechlessness came when Schaber returned to work, shortly after testing positive for COVID-19 in November 2020. Her daughter had it first, then her husband, who suffered from flu-like symptoms.

"For me it was the same," Schaber said. "Until it wasn't."

Before she contracted coronavirus, Schaber was an avid runner with no underlying health conditions.

With four half-marathons and many other races under her belt, Schaber suddenly couldn't run at all.

She also experienced brain fog and a racing heart.

Her cough came back and she experienced blood clotting issues before being diagnosed with pulmonary embolisms. Months passed and multiple doctors sent her home before she was finally able to put a name to it: long COVID.

There’s a lot of us out there that survived," Schaber said. "But surviving doesn’t mean thriving.

UW Medicine now has a clinic that specializes in treating patients who suffer from long COVID, seeing 150 new patients every month. The American Academy of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation is tracking it across the country.

"They've estimated there are 20 million Americans that have long COVID symptoms," said Dr. Janna Friedly. "So we’re really just seeing the tip of the iceberg here, and we know there’s many many patients out there that have not yet accessed services who are still struggling."

Friedly shares that she developed long COVID herself. She says it took nine months for her symptoms to clear up.

It's normal for a patient to have a dozen lingering symptoms, including heart palpitations, poor memory, anxiety, depression, and difficulty with daily activities. It's possible that underlying diabetes plays a role in some patients, but there is no known cause or cure.

Friedly says while some long COVID patients are vaccinated, the majority are unvaccinated, either because they fell ill before vaccines were available, they are unable to get vaccinated, or they chose not to get vaccinated. "The single most predictor of getting long COVID is getting COVID," Friedly said. "The more strategies you can use to prevent getting COVID, the more protected you are from long COVID." She encourages vaccination, saying it makes you much less likely to get infected.

"We’ve had a lot of success in managing people’s symptoms but we don’t yet have that one single treatment that will help prevent or treat it for all patients," Friedly said, adding that she is optimistic that new therapies are coming, based on new research coming out, including some from colleagues at UW Medicine.

Schaber is optimistic, too.

She hopes to return to running, but she can embrace a life with a slower pace, too.

"Every time I don’t hit a milestone or don’t’ get better in a certain area, it’s a let down," Schaber said. "So to me, I’m happy I’m here. I’m happy I’m here for my daughter, husband, my family."
