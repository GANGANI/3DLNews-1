WEATHERWATCH
Lead shot possibly to blame for deaths of nearly 60 swans
by Michelle Esteban | KOMO News
Tuesday, Apr 2nd 2019
3
View All Photos
Lead shot possibly to blame for deaths of nearly 60 swans (KOMO Photo)

MONROE, Wash. - Lead shot may be linked to the death of dozens of beautiful trumpeter swans in our state.

It will take lab work to determine the culprit, but Washington Fish and Wildlife Biologist Daniel Zimmerman and Martha Jordan, Executive Director for the Northwest Swan Conservation Association (NWSWANS) have a hunch it's left over lead shot that the birds ate while foraging for food.

"I haven't seen this before in the wild and I’ve been at this for 40 years and it does tug at your heart strings," said Jordan.

Lead shot is banned for waterfowl hunting, but it is legal in some cases. "It still can be used for upland bird hunting for pheasant, dove and clay target shooting, I would just say use non-lead or nontoxic ammo," said Jordan.

Zimmerman describes the 'die-off' as 'not normal' and said typically only a couple trumpeter swans die, 10 at the most in a season.

The Swan conservation group, a nonprofit, is committed to the conservation of both trumpeter and tundra swans in our state and considers itself a leader in the 'Get the Lead Out' campaign to keep swans and other birds from eating lead shot.

The group collaborates with federal agencies with an interest in saving swans and is working with Washington Fish and Wildlife to collect evidence and catalog data to help determine what sickened the swans.

Martha Jordan is the "Swan Lady," with binoculars in hand and her dog Moss by her side, she scoured Crescent Lake this morning for Trumpeters. She found plenty of feathers caught in cattails on Crescent Lake, but no sign of swans on the lake.

"Monday, I found 13," said Jordan.

Lead%20shot%20possibly%20to%20blame%20for%20deaths%20of%20nearly%2060%20swans%20(KOMO%20Photo)

The next day with the help of Zimmerman they counted a dozen more dead swans - some carcasses, some just feather piles.

"They are from a bird that died and likely was eaten by a coyote," said Jordan describing a handful of trumpeter swan feathers found together near the lake's bank.

Zimmerman said in all, 59 trumpeter swans died since mid-March, from Snohomish County to North King County. Fifty in Snohomish County with the bulk found near Crescent Lake, a popular roosting spot south of downtown Monroe.

Nine others were found in Carnation, another popular roosting spot said Zimmerman.

For Jordan it's heart wrenching to witness. She said she watched as one swan clearly wanted his mate to come into the water, but the swan never budged and let out a honk she'd described as alarming.

"Suddenly this noise comes out of this ill bird like a scream, I've never heard anything like that. It was in massive distress," said Jordan.

Zimmerman said tissue samples will be sent to a lab in Canada for analysis and results likely won't be available until mid to late Summer.

Zimmerman and Jordan both think our harsh winter and heavy snowfall may have forced some of the swans into new territories and feeding grounds where they possibly ingested lead shot.
