FCC's AI-generated robocall ban a first step in a long fight to deal with misinformation

by AUSTIN DENEAN | The National Desk

Fri, February 9th 2024 at 12:18 PM
4
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
FILE - The seal of the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) is seen before an FCC meeting to vote on net neutrality, Dec. 14, 2017, in Washington. (AP Photo/Jacquelyn Martin, File)

WASHINGTON (TND) — The Federal Communications Commission outlawed robocalls using voices that are generated using artificial intelligence as the rapidly evolving technology raises alarm bells during a presidential election year that has already seen it used for nefarious purposes to spread misinformation.

The FCC’s decision comes after a robocall imitating President Joe Biden’s voice discouraged people from voting in last month’s New Hampshire primary election. Investigators have linked the fake Biden robocall to two companies in Texas that have been sent a cease-and-desist order demanding they immediately stop supporting illegal robocall traffic on its networks, though no charges have been filed yet.

“Bad actors are using AI-generated voices in unsolicited robocalls to extort vulnerable family members, imitate celebrities, and misinform voters. We’re putting the fraudsters behind these robocalls on notice,” FCC chairwoman Jessica Rosenworcel said in a statement. “State Attorneys General will now have new tools to crack down on these scams and ensure the public is protected from fraud and misinformation.”

The rise of AI has already concerned election officials and misinformation experts as billions of people around the world will cast a ballot in elections, including in the U.S. where the race is shaping up to be a close election that could come down to just thousands of votes in a handful of swing states.

The FCC regulation is among the first steps the government has taken to address AI’s ability to trick voters. It will allow the agency to fine companies that use AI voices in their calls and block service providers that carry them. People who receive the calls will also be able to file lawsuits and state attorneys general will have more authority to act on violations.

Anyone who violates the new law can also face expensive fines from the FCC with a maximum of over $23,000 per call.

Tech companies that are developing AI tools have started to take steps to try to limit the potential damage their products could have on election integrity, but experts say that they are facing a difficult task of stopping it entirely.

“To a degree it could make a difference, if anyone was intending to actually use this for business purposes, now they will not be. The challenge is for things like the robocall that we saw going around New Hampshire voice-cloning Joe Biden, that's the type of thing that this isn't necessarily going to stop,” said Matt Mittelsteadt, a research fellow at George Mason University’s Mercatus Center. “Perhaps there'll be a slight deterrent effect. But as we've already seen with spam phone calls, which have been just for years now impossible to crack down on in any sort of significant way, this isn't going to stop that.”

Companies have just started to roll out procedures for labeling photos as being artificially generated, with Meta announcing labels that would be coming to its platforms this week. OpenAI and others are also working on developing more advanced tools that can automatically identify AI-generated video and audio but have not been developed at the same scale so far as the problem is only beginning to pop up with the explosion in AI’s popularity coming within the last two years.

Addressing robocalls is also only a small piece of the battle for government officials trying to maintain faith in elections, with a lot more dangers lingering online.

Deepfake videos using AI to generate voices and images of celebrities and politicians have also already made rounds online. Some prominent examples during this election cycle include the Republican National Committee creating an AI-generated ad painting a grim future for the U.S. if Biden were to be reelected. Another ad from Florida Gov. Ron DeSantis’ presidential campaign showed images of former President Donald Trump hugging Dr. Anthony Fauci.

The Federal Elections Commission has taken steps to regulate the use of deepfakes and lawmakers in Congress have called for regulating deepfakes, but no bills have gained enough traction to become law amid a whirlwind of hearings and proposals of how to create initial guardrails for the quickly developing technology.

Multiple states have passed laws regarding deepfakes and AI in political advertising, but without federal guidelines the strictness of using the tech varies on a state-by-state basis.

Experts are hoping the FCC’s new regulation is a helpful first step in what is shaping up to be a decadeslong fight in ensuring the worst potential outcomes that come along with the vast promises of AI.

The attention brought on by the Biden robocall and resulting action from the FCC could help spur further attention to developing tools to minimize the problem such as those under development to identify and label AI-generated video and voices.

“Often solutions are a function of the right genius at the right time considering the right problem,” Mittlesteadt said. “Money can help, but talent is the key.”
