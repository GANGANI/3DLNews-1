'Can't afford to wait': WA Senate candidate wants state to declare a fentanyl emergency
by JEREMY HARRIS | KOMO Staff
Friday, Feb 9th 2024
4

A fentanyl user shows the powder before smoking it. (KOMO file photo)
SEATTLE (KOMO) —

Senate candidate Dr. Raul Garcia is calling on Washington state leaders to issue an emergency proclamation to deal with the fentanyl crisis.

ADVERTISEMENT

Garcia, who is an emergency physician, says the current resources for treatment and law enforcement are insufficient to stop fentanyl distribution and the trend of overdose deaths which have increased exponentially in recent years.

“We cannot afford to wait another day. I want a complete commitment from the state help our counties and our cities to succeed," Garcia said. “We want the legislature and the governor to free up emergency funding so we can enhance and increase the quality and number of rehabilitation facilities we have in our state.”

The state of Oregon recently declared a 90-day state of emergency for fentanyl, where Gov. Tina Kotek said a lack of rehab treatment beds is limiting options to help users.

“I will be very honest. I don’t think it’s realistic to say in 90 days we'll have more treatment. The long-term solution is to make sure that anyone in Oregon, not just in Portland, that is seeking a pathway to recovery has a way to get the treatment they need," Kotek said.

Statewide, fentanyl was responsible for 1,795 deaths statewide in Washington in 2022, according to health department data.

In King County, health officials recorded 1,081 deaths that were attributed to fentanyl in 2023, a 51% increase over the 714 fentanyl overdose deaths in 2022.

“It’s a shame to see the stench of death in our streets. We’re losing two to three people a day here, that’s insane," Garcia said.

Gov. Jay Inslee has a $68-million budget proposal to address fentanyl, which would include investments in treatment, prevention, and law enforcement.

The legislature is currently considering several proposals to address the fentanyl crisis, including SB 5010, which would update the statute for endangerment with a controlled substance to include fentanyl and synthetic opioids.

Prosecutors from King County and the city of Seattle sent a letter to the legislature encouraging them to update the law.

"We are writing with urgency to call for the closure of a statutory gap that does not allow felony prosecution when a child is endangered or seriously injured after a parent or guardian knowingly or intentionally exposes a child to fentanyl. This gaping hole in our child endangerment laws must be closed during this legislative session," a joint letter from Seattle City Attorney Ann Davison and King County Prosecuting Attorney Leesa Manion said.

Garcia believes a state of emergency and increased enforcement against dealers will slow down the flow of synthetic fentanyl.

In a news release Friday, Garcia said he wants three immediate steps from Washington leadership.

  1. Prohibiting the sale and distribution of non-medical fentanyl in our state
  2. The complete ability for law enforcement to carry out our laws and arrest fentanyl dealers with class C Felony as it is written in existing Washington Law.
  3. Before the close of session our legislature to include emergency funding to include Mental health, Drug Rehabilitation, and business agreements with universities, trade schools and business’ to accept people back into society from rehabilitation.

"As long as fentanyl dealers feel there is no consequence to them dealing on our streets, we have a big problem. We need to eliminate the supplies, we need to get these dealers out of a job, we need to put them in jail," he said.
