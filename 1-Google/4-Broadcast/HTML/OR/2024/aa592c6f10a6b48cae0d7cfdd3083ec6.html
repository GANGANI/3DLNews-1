Questions about Biden's memory overshadow being cleared of charges in documents case

by AUSTIN DENEAN | The National Desk

Fri, February 9th 2024 at 9:08 AM
Updated Mon, February 12th 2024 at 9:00 AM
11

WASHINGTON (TND) — Questions surrounding President Joe Biden’s age and mental fitness have ramped up once again after a special counsel report clearing him of facing criminal charges for his handling of classified documents also accused him of having poor memory and being unable to remember when he was vice president or when his son passed away.

Special counsel Robert Hurt released his 388-page report on his investigation into the president on Thursday, which cleared him of charges but also painted an unflattering picture of Biden’s memory.

While most of Hur’s report focused on why the evidence did not meet the threshold to pursue criminal charges against Biden, he also said it would be difficult to convince a jury that Biden knowingly committed a crime.

“We have also considered that, at trial, Mr. Biden would likely present himself to a jury, as he did during our interview of him, as a sympathetic, well-meaning, elderly man with a poor memory,” investigators wrote.

Among the most prominent of the accusations made against Biden’s memory were that he couldn’t place what years he served as vice president and couldn’t recall the date of his son Beau Biden’s death.

Biden addressed the report and questions about his mental state in a speech and answered questions from reporters, where he criticized Hur’s depiction of him while trying to highlight the distinctions between his case and the one against former President Donald Trump.

“There’s even (a) reference that I don’t remember when my son died,” Biden said. “How in the hell dare he raise that?”

Biden was emotional when speaking about Beau Biden’s death, noting that he still wears his son’s rosary beads and honors him with a service every Memorial Day.

“Frankly, when I was asked the question, I thought to myself it wasn’t any of their damn business,” he said. “I don’t need anyone to remind me of when he passed away.”

But the effort to counter the charges made in Hur’s report may have also been blunted with another mix-up. When speaking about aid to Palestinians in Gaza, Biden referred to Egyptian President Abdel Fatah El-Sisi as the president of Mexico.

“As you know, the president of Mexico, Sisi, did not want to open the gate to allow humanitarian material to get in,” Biden said. “I talked to him. I convinced him to open the gate.”

It was more evidence for critics of Biden’s mental standing to point to and question how he would handle another four years in office.

“You don't want to have the very thing that you're saying doesn't exist a sentence away from what you just said. The proximity of making that mistake and claiming that his memory is fine was glaring,” said David Paleologos, director of the Suffolk University Political Research Center.

The mistake wrapped up a week with multiple slip-ups for the president while speaking.

Earlier this week, Biden mixed up the names of European leaders with past officials who had died years earlier. On Wednesday, Biden twice appeared to mix up late German Chancellor Helmut Kohl with former Chancellor Angela Merkel while talking about a conversation he had in 2021. Merkel was Germany’s leader during the event he was describing, while Kohl died in 2017.

That came after another error on Sunday, when he confused French President Emmanuel Macron for Francois Mitterand, a former French president who died in 1996.

Both the report and recent polling have ramped up questions about Biden’s age, which was already one of the primary issues facing his reelection campaign.

An NBC poll released Wednesday found 76% of voters had major or moderate concerns about whether Biden has “the necessary mental and physical health to be president for a second term.” Eleven percent said they had no real concerns.

“He does need to try to reassure people that he is up to the job and really the only way of doing that is just by being more public. Now, the problem is that he’s going to be making mistakes and it’s not because he’s older—although that probably is part of it—Biden has never been known as a particularly polished public speaker," said Kyle Kondik, a political analyst at the University of Virginia's Center for Politics.

Biden has acknowledged his age in the past, but asked voters to consider everything he has accomplished since taking office and said, “just watch me.” He continued to insist his memory was fine when taking questions from reporters after addressing the report Thursday night.

“Look, my memory has not gotten — my memory is fine,” he said. “Take a look at what I’ve done since I’ve become president. None of you thought I could pass any of the things I got passed. How’d that happen? You know, I guess I just forgot what was going on.”

Biden’s defense team and the White House criticized Hur’s report for the characterizations of the president and his memory.

Bob Bauer, Biden’s personal attorney, accused Hur of violating “well-established” norms and trashing the president amid political pressure from Republicans who have accused Biden of weaponizing the justice system and unfair treatment of Trump.

“The special counsel could not refrain from investigative excess, perhaps unsurprising given the intense pressures of the current political environment. Whatever the impact of those pressures on the final report, it flouts department regulations and norms,” Bauer said in a statement.

Republicans were quick to point out the passages dedicated to Biden’s memory, along with accusing the Department of Justice of giving the Democratic president preferential treatment.

“Among the most disturbing parts of this report is the Special Counsel’s justification for not recommending charges: namely that the President’s memory had such ‘significant limitations’ that he could not convince a jury that the President held a ‘mental state of willfulness’ that a serious felony requires,” House GOP leadership said in a joint statement. “A man too incapable of being held accountable for mishandling classified information is certainly unfit for the Oval Office.”

The Republican National Committee said Hur’s depiction of Biden was disqualifying for his reelection.

“Joe Biden’s mental decline is both sad and alarming, and putting a ‘sympathetic,’ ‘elderly’ man who can’t remember major events from his own life back in the Oval Office would undoubtedly make America less safe,” the RNC said in a statement.

But former President Donald Trump has also had his own struggles with recalling names and dates in recent months. He recently confused former United Nations ambassador Nikki Haley, his only remaining competition in the GOP presidential primary, for former House Speaker Nancy Pelosi, D-Calif., while talking about the Jan. 6 insurrection. Trump has also said he ran against former President Barack Obama and confused Hungary’s head of state with Turkey.

Biden and Democrats have tried to highlight Trump’s missteps to blunt the impact of the president’s but have been risking drawing further attention to Biden’s age.

The White House and Biden have also sought to contrast Biden’s classified documents case with Trump’s, which resulted in criminal charges being filed against the former president. Trump is accused of refusing to cooperate with investigators and to return the classified materials despite repeated requests to do so.

Biden quoted passages from Hur’s report directly contrasting the two cases during his speech. The report noted “several material distinctions” between the cases, including that Trump is accused of refusing to turn documents over and obstructed the investigation while Biden willfully cooperated.

It’s not immediately clear how effective painting a distinction between the documents case will be for Biden’s reelection campaign and if it will do much to blunt the impact about the questions about the president’s age.

“On balance, nothing really changes,” Paleologos said. “You get what you get in 2024. Trump is Trump, whether you vote for him or not, he is who he is. Same with Biden, you get what you get.”

Along with the classified documents case, Trump is also facing legal peril over his alleged role in trying to overturn the 2020 election that culminated in the Jan. 6 riot and a racketeering case in Georgia over attempts to influence the state’s election officials to alter the results in his favor. Overall, he is facing 91 felony counts and is also dealing with civil cases against him and his businesses.

Trump, who became the first former president to be charged with a crime, had his primary bid bolstered as voters appeared to rally around him after the first charges were announced. But a conviction in any of the cases appears likely to damage his chances in a general election against Biden.

An NPR/PBS NewsHour/Marist national poll released this week found that a conviction takes Trump from being effectively tied with Biden to trailing by 6%. Without a conviction, Biden holds a 48-47 percentage point lead over Trump, within the margin of error.
