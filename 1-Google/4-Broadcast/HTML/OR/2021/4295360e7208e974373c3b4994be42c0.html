Oregon's child care deserts grow more barren as parents try to return to work

by Christina Giardinelli

Wed, August 11th 2021 at 7:56 PM
Updated Wed, August 11th 2021 at 9:40 PM
5
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
Nicole Marcus' two sons dressed up in spiderman costumes. The Medford mother of four has been searching for daycare to no avail. (Nicole Marcus)

OREGON — Nicole Marcus has been on the phone for weeks calling daycare after daycare in an attempt to find a slot for her two children age five and four as she attempts to return back to work after the pandemic.

"It's been insane! I actually almost put in my 2 weeks notice yesterday because I just haven't been able to find anybody," she said during an interview on Tuesday. "I've called upwards of 49 different daycare centers and home care providers. That is not including the (Spanish) speaking people that I didn't write down because I don't speak Spanish."

Her 5-year-old is autistic and she was hoping to place him in the same daycare as his 4-year-old brother.

"He's five, not potty trained and there is an issue with that for preschools and daycares you know nobody wants to change a five-year-old and then there is just no availability," she said noting that the price has also increased since the pandemic.

"One person wanted $1100 just for my disabled son a month and then it was $700 for my 4-year-old and there was another one that wanted $1600 a month," she said. "I was (prior to the pandemic) paying $1200 which was a lot but reasonable and so finding out that it is not available and the price increase is just kind of caused a lot of problems for me in getting the help I need."



She is not alone, according to the latest bi-annually published data analysis commissioned by the state's Early Learning Department and conducted by Oregon State University, all 36 of Oregon's counties are considered "daycare deserts." This means that there are at least three children under the age of 2 for every available child care slot in the county.

The report is based on data collected prior to COVID-19 but local providers say since then the situation has gotten even direr.

"Prior to the pandemic, our region had about enough (daycare spots) for 20% of the 0-5 population. Since the pandemic began about 50% of the providers had to either reduce services or have closed," said Sunny Spicer, Executive Director of Kid Times, a Medford daycare program.

She said running a daycare operation became more difficult with the increased regulations during COVID as well as staffing issues.

"The increased costs around mitigating the virus, the staffing challenges, there are a lot of people who themselves had children in childcare and then weren't able to access it so we lost a lot of staffing across the board," she said. "Regaining staffing has been a challenge because there is not a lot of childcare open. It creates this sort of vicious cycle where we can't find a lot of staffing, we can't expand on it."

She said the housing market has also impacted the daycare situation by adding extra burden both on educators working in the valley and on those running home daycare centers.

"There used to be quite a number of home care providers and it was easier to provide for children in a home care environment," she said. "If you look at the housing and rental costs now, those have gone way up. It's not as feasible as it used to be to run a program."

The OSU study looked at data from March 2020, right before the pandemic hit, and found a continued decline in the number of small, home-based child care provider slots.

One such provider in Grants Pass said the odds were already slanted against the smaller businesses in the industry before the pandemic but COVID-19 regulations coupled with having to take care of their own kids at home made it almost impossible for home care providers to stay afloat.

"To start a business is expensive, to run a daycare is very expensive," said Amber Oldridge. "To pay a teacher a reasonable amount it takes an average of four to five kids and then you add payroll taxes and other expenses."

She pointed out that many of her client base is paid for by the Department of Human Services and noted that the pay it offers to home daycare providers versus larger child care centers is minimal.

"98% of my families are DHS clients and so my rates don't go over what DHS is willing to pay because I don't want to discourage the parents of needy kids," she said. "DHS will pay center-based programs a lot more then they would pay a home however we have to go through the same training and educational background, the same rules and regulations."

She noted that other contributing issues are unfair building code regulations such as a 2020 requirement that all new homecare facilities be equipped with sprinklers. She said not only is that prohibitive for new home care centers but noted that if she were to move homes the code would also apply.

"That is expensive to do let alone finding somebody that would be willing to put sprinklers in my home so that I can remain open," she said.

Those difficulties only skyrocketed during the pandemic with ever-changing rules on capacity limits that imposed more limits on smaller facilities. What is more, she said she had to pay out of pocket for many of the increased cleaning expenses while dealing with supply chain interruptions and price gouging on cleaning supplies, toilette paper, and food items.

"I ran a 24-hour daycare program, my first child came at 5 in the morning my last child left at 1 am in the morning so whenever I would hit the grocery store on the rare day that I didn't have kids, the grocery stores were bare," she said. "I used all of my savings to overbuy."

She said she was able to secure some pandemic grants which went exclusively towards paying staff members that had to be laid off when capacity decreased during the pandemic.

Melanie Mesaros a spokesperson for the state's Early Learning Division (ELD) noted in an email that "4000 Oregon providers operating through the pandemic have been awarded over $80 million in grant funding and supplies by the Early Learning Division (ELD) to support their critical role in serving children and families."

She pointed out that an additional $10 million will become available soon for centers that closed during the pandemic and are now wanting to re-open.

Spicer said the grants will help but said it's going to take time and increased public funding to meet the state's childcare demands.

"There is going to have to be some additional funding just for the infrastructure itself and making it competitive," she said. "Our lead teachers need to have a bachelor's degree. A bachelor's degree is on average, $100,000. You can't then turn around and pay someone $16 or $17 an hour when they've invested $100,000 into their education."

She said investing in early childhood education would also help the state cope with other societal problems like homelessness.

"We know that when children are in quality programs, the societal impact is huge. Parents get back to work, they get into stable housing, the kids do better in school long term," she said. " This is something that I think we as a community need to be fairly focused on if we want to see (improvement on) a lot of the issues that we have been grappling with."

Mesaros noted that the Department is listening to feedback from providers small and large when it comes to the way grants are distributed.

"ELD has convened workgroups that include home-based and center-based child care providers for input on the grant process," she wrote. "We are working to develop a funding formula that’s responsive to the operating costs, as well as financial hardships different provider types are experiencing."
