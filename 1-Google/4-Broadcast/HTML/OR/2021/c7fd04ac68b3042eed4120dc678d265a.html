Portland's new FBI leader Ramsey breaks down fentanyl fight

by Evan Schreiber

Fri, March 12th 2021 at 4:00 PM
Updated Wed, March 17th 2021 at 8:04 AM

PORTLAND, Ore. — He's the new leader with a new set of priorities, though the scourge remains the same. We're learning more about the top issues for the FBI Portland Office's new agent in charge and that includes an opioid crisis that's claiming more lives now than ever before.

In an introductory briefing Monday, Kieran Ramsey, the special agent in charge of the FBI's Portland Field Office, told members of the media that fentanyl is still furiously flooding the streets and it's a priority as he takes on the leadership role.

PAST COVERAGE | Is fentanyl behind rise in fatal drug overdoses?

“I look at our efforts to combat transnational organized crime, if we think of cartels, the drug cartels. They’re still coming up this way," Ramsey said. “We look at the Interstate 5 corridor and we see the increase in fentanyl deaths and fentanyl overdoses in the state of Oregon – huge concern for us, as well – partnering with our colleagues at Homeland Security, the DEA, the state, local partners there, as well.”

It only takes a small amount of the illicit substance to do deadly damage. Both public health and law enforcement experts say the amount it takes to overdose on fentanyl is about the size of two grains of salt.

The opioid crisis is rising amid the coronavirus pandemic. The Pacific Northwest is being flooded with fentanyl and the pandemic is only making the problem worse, according to a new report released by the Drug Enforcement Administration.

Data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimates that 81,230 people died from drug overdoses in the months before, leading up to, and at the beginning of the coronavirus pandemic, and the death toll is startling to even the most knowledgeable of experts.

Oregon saw its own troubling rise, trending with the national data.

RELATED | CDC: Record overdose deaths driven by synthetic opioids before, during COVID-19 pandemic

Oregon’s 580 deaths from drug overdoses are among the more than 81,000 drug overdose deaths that occurred in the United States in the 12 months ending in May 2020, the Oregon Health Authority stated in a news release.

From January to June 2020, at least 339 people died of a drug overdose in Oregon. This figure is nearly 40% higher than the number of overdose deaths from the same period in 2019 and represents an increase of nearly 35% from the previous six-month period, July to December 2019.

During the peak months of the economic shutdown brought on by the coronavirus pandemic, analysts found that Oregon saw a nearly 70% increase in the number of overdose deaths during April and May 2020 compared to the same time in 2019.

The report from the DEA names the CJNG cartelas the main group circulating fentanyl-laced drugs in our region. The DEA says drug traffickers are lacing all types of drugs-- not just counterfeit prescription pills.

"The fentanyl that is coming out of Mexico, in some areas of the Pacific Northwest, is replacing the heroin market," said Special Agent in Charge Frank Tarentino with the DEA's field office in Seattle.

But not all drug enforcement is the same, as Oregon voters broke from decades of drug laws.

Voters passed Measure 110in the November 2020 election which decriminalizes possession of small amounts of hard drugs in the state. It went into effect on February 1, 2021. Instead, those found in possession would face a $100 fine or a health assessment that could lead to addiction counseling. Backers of the ballot measure, which Oregon voters passed by a wide margin, hailed it as a revolutionary move for the United States.

KATU asked Ramsey how the landmark drug decriminalization law will impact his team's drug investigations.

The short answer: Not at all.

“Our concerns when it comes to the drug threat is those criminal organizations, those drug cartels, and individuals that are having serious violent activities surrounding their drug activity or are bringing this poison into the state," Ramsey said. "I don't know what the answer is to it from a public health crisis perspective, but right now I’m not worried about the Measure 110.”

He says the focus will not be on punishing individual Oregonians and individual users for small amounts. But he warned those users to be aware.

“I look at it from the criminality of the larger criminal organization with serious illegal transport of illegal narcotics – poison – into the communities. Not certainly concerned about personal use consumption," Ramsey said. "Although, the risk to anybody these days of consuming something bought on the street that’s not prescribed by a doctor is clearly the extreme of the overdose deaths with fentanyl problems.”
