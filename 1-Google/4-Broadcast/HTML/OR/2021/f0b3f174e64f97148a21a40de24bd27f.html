Yelp forced to turn off local bar reviews after 'China Virus' sign causes uproar

by Ambar Rodriguez

Tue, February 2nd 2021 at 6:02 PM
Updated Wed, February 3rd 2021 at 5:03 PM
3
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
A Marquee sign on the Trophy Club Bar and Grill in Medford reads 'China Virus Hours.' The sign was removed after a post about it went viral causing major backlash. (Courtesy: Zach Sutton)

MEDFORD, Ore. — Yelp has disabled comments and reviews for the Trophy Club Bar and Grill in Medford after a marquee sign that read "China Virus Hours," caused major backlash when a picture of it went viral.

"This business recently received increased public attention resulting in an influx of people posting their views to this page, so we have temporarily disabled the ability to post here as we work to investigate the content," Yelp said in an alert that shows up on the business' Yelp page. "While racism has no place on Yelp and we unequivocally reject racism in any form, all reviews on Yelp must reflect an actual first-hand consumer experience."


After Ken Narasaki, a fourth-generation Japanese American playwright and actor posted a picture of the sign to Facebook, Twitter and Reddit the message went viral causing a flurry of reviews that prompted Yelp to shut down comments on the page.

In a written statement, Trophy Club said it has been getting death threats since the post went viral and noted that it never meant to hurt anyone.

“We are not and never have been racist, nor have our employees. The sign was not intended to be racist, " the statement read. "It was to make light out of the situation we all have been going through for the past year. We apologize to whoever we offended. That was not our intention."

Though the marquee sign has been taken down, the business still has signs posted on its front door and inside referring to state COVID-19 regulations as “China Virus” rules. It states, customers are required to wear a mask, no backpacks are allowed inside, and only outdoor dining is allowed.



Former US president, Donald J Trump has been criticized after his infamous use of the words “Chinese Virus” to refer to the Coronavirus. Since the pandemic began Asian Americans across the nation have shared their experiences dealing with a rise in xenophobic incidents.

Charles Garwood, a Korean American, and a 26-year Rogue Valley resident said he was not surprised by the sign as he has experienced racist incidents before because of how he looks.

“I’ve dealt with this stuff for a long time now, but that one caught me off guard, it makes me feel uncomfortable to live in a town like that, it’s hard to think about people being so openly disrespectful, and putting that type of title on the coronavirus, putting it all on a certain race or area, it’s very unfair,” Garwood said.

Naraski said he was looking to relocate to the Rogue Valley before seeing the sign. He said he contacted the business to discuss how the term on the sign could be perceived as racist.

“I called to explain why that would be considered a racist sign, and the fact that its inspired a lot of anti-Asian hate violence, which spiked all through 2020, people have been beaten, a Japanese violinist was beaten so badly he might never be able to play the violin ever again, it’s no joke,” he said.

He said when he attempted to explain the harm the sign caused, the woman who answered the phone hung up on him. He said when he called back, a male employee answered the phone with a fake accent.

Naraski said the employee spoke in a "sort of chop suey racist accent," and said, "Hong Kong chopstick factory, why don’t you suck my fat white rod."

"It’s bad for Medford, Medford needs more diversity, it would benefit from more diversity if they wanted to attract a large corporation, for instance, to have their headquarters here what company would move to a place that permits signs like that," he said. "It is something that needs to be nipped in the bud.”

Both Naraski and Garwood said they would like to see the business use its marquee to apologize to the community and change company policy to be more inclusive.
