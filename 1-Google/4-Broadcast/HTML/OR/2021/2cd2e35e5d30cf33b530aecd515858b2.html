Remembering Almeda: Watch News 10 Special

by Trish Glose

Wed, September 8th 2021 at 11:58 AM
Updated Thu, September 9th 2021 at 8:02 PM
The Almeda Fire rages through the Rogue Valley, destroying thousands of homes and businesses (Trish Glose/News10)

    TOPICS:

    Austin FlorezJackson County SheriffOregon,United StatesJason JohnSarah LeonardJeremy WhippleKate FlorezMike Haughey

Some say it was the windiest day they had ever witnessed living in Oregon and waking up on September 8, 2020, they just had a feeling something was wrong.

"I woke up that morning, shut off a fan that I had and the hum, the noise continued. I looked outside and it was crystal clear what potentially could be in store for us," says Battalion Chief Jason John, who worked for hours on the Almeda fire.

"I felt bad that morning. I live in the rural so driving in that morning, something felt off. It felt dry. The power went out at my house, my generator was going when I left for work. Everything had this feel, like super dry, you could tell it was going to be super hot, it felt like orange in the air already," says Capt. Mike Haughey with the Medford Fire Department. He and his crew got the call for the Almeda fire minutes after the fire broke out.

Local meteorologists knew the weather that day was coming.

"It was a holiday weekend but even before we had been talking about these extreme fire conditions probably five to six days ahead of time in our forecast discussions, and we issued a fire weather watch four days ahead of time which was almost unheard of we don’t usually issue them more than a couple days ahead of time," says Meteorologist Ryan Sandler with the National Weather Service, "and we issued a red flag warning almost 3 days ahead of time which is also unheard of so we were really talking about these extreme fire conditions."

News10 Chief Meteorologist Milt Radford says the weather played such a huge role that day in pushing the fire as fast and as hard as it did. He says so many things came together to create a "perfect storm" of fire conditions, like high winds, hot temperatures, an ongoing drought, and incredibly dry condtions.

"Normally in the Rogue Valley 25-30 miles per hour, that’s about the tops for breezy wind but we were getting 40 mile per hour winds for hours on end that day, like eight or ten hours we have 40 mph gusts or higher. That is unusual in of itself any time of year," Radford says.

Once the smoke started growing in Ashland and moving north along the Bear Creek Greenway and Interstate Five, the calls started flooding the Southern Oregon Emergency Communications center.

"On the day of Almada we took over 6,000 calls and over 1,000 of those we 911 calls," says Sarah Leonard, training supervisor at dispatch.

"The radio traffic from the firefighters about what they were seeing and hearing and what houses were burning and how they were having to mobilize themselves around the fire and knowing the area and trying to track it and knowing my guys were safe they were going to go home to their families that night and that they had all the resources they needed to still continue to fight this fire. It was a lot," says Sarah Hills, lead dispatcher.

Firefighters were desperately trying to put the fire out and feeling like it was a losing battle for hours on end, "it was sickening because you could see this monster in front of you and it was just getting bigger. Knowing that we didn’t have any back up resources and we were just getting spread to the wind, it was a really sickening feeling," says John.

Meanwhile, law enforcement officers were doing their best to get ahead of the firefighters, working to evacuate the area.

"There were a lot of people with electric wheelchairs. Some of them weren’t able to get out fast enough and we would stop in trucks," says Lt. Jeremy Whipple with Jackson County Sheriff's office, "we would throw them into the back of it and throw their chair into the back. We had citizens even stop I remember a gentleman with a flat bed truck pulls right up to us and he knew I was having a hard time getting this person in my vehicle and I had no way of getting his chair. He pulled up, he could have just left, he was trying to get out himself. He pulled up, stopped middle of the highway and lifted her and the chair into the back of his truck."

There were also countless stories of survival that day. Residents running from the flames, others grabbing what they could and getting out of harm's way. Austin and Kate Florez just closed on their Talent home that day. The two were desperately trying to reach the other, to make sure they were both okay. Kate made it back to her home just in time to grab her cat, walking over unpacked boxes and totes of all of their stuff. The two finally reunited hours later.

"One of the more beautiful moments in my life was, we finally got to the condo, we sat in the living room at this little coffee table. I remember, with the cat and dog around us, we emptied our pockets, and I went to my dash in my car and emptied that out too and we put all of our belongings around this small table and that was it. That was all we had was each other and a few things from our pockets we were able to keep from the fire. And I remember feeling so grateful in that moment that nobody got hurt," says Austin.
