WEATHERWATCH
Mayor calls for public's help after fourth person dies from exposure
by KATU.com Staff
Tuesday, Jan 10th 2017
PORTLAND, Ore. —

Acknowledging the city has limited resources, Portland Mayor Ted Wheeler on Tuesday pleaded with the public to help alert officials to homeless people on the streets whose lives might be in danger because of the biting cold.

ADVERTISEMENT

“This is a community moment. This is an all hands on deck situation, and that’s why we’re asking the public to help us out,” Wheeler said surrounded by other government officials and leaders during a hastily called news conference at City Hall. “Be the eyes for us in the community, help us identify people who might need some help. If you do that we can do the rest.”

Wheeler said he called for the news conference after the city’s police chief informed him that a fourth person had died from exposure.

"That's four people too many," he said.

Police said a man was found dead in Southwest Portland Tuesday morning, marking the city’s fourth death from hypothermia since New Year’s Day. A 68-year-old man died of cold weather exposure on Jan. 1, the Multnomah County Medical Examiner said. Officials said David Guyot was found unresponsive at a downtown Portland bus stop New Year’s Eve. He died nearly 12 hours later in a hospital.

On Jan. 2, another man was found unresponsive, lying under a blanket on East Burnside Street. The state medical examiner ruled that the man, identified as 51-year-old Mark Elliot Johnson, died of hypothermia.

A woman was found dead of hypothermia on Saturday, Jan. 7 in a downtown parking garage. Portland Police identified her as 52-year-old Karen Batts.

The deaths happened even after the city added 600 more shelter beds to its already available 1,100. It also opened the Portland Building. Wheeler said that building will be available again for this weather event as needed.

How to Help

During the news conference Wheeler asked the public if it sees someone in distress because of the cold, call 911.

“You are not inconveniencing anybody by calling 911. You might be saving somebody’s life,” said Wheeler.

If you see someone who could be in danger of exposure to the elements, call the police non-emergency number at 503-823-3333 so an officer can conduct a welfare check.

Several local shelters are open as warming centers. You can call 2-1-1 or go online to find a shelter near your location.

Wheeler said if you call 211, the city will help transport the person in need of help to a shelter.

Multnomah County Chair Deborah Kafoury joined Wheeler at the news conference and echoed his sentiments. She added that donations were needed and if you needed information on where to donate, to call 211.

“While I can’t promise you that we will save every life, I can promise you that we will try,” she said.

Wheeler said the city of Portland was deploying its own resources in the effort and that he was going to do his part and hit the streets with an eye toward getting help for those suffering in the cold and into shelter.

“This is very important, and it’s personal to me," Wheeler said. "As the mayor of this city, I know that a value that we have is that we look out for each other.”

The Portland metro area was facing the coldest start to a year on record since 1979, with overnight temperatures dropping well below freezing-level for almost a week. More cold weather is expected tomorrow.
