A year of Las Vegas sex trafficking: 'What the study shows is just appalling'

by Nathan O'Neal

Tue, February 14th 2017 at 5:17 PM
Updated Wed, June 28th 2017 at 8:52 AM
4
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
An Arizona State University report provides a one-year snapshot of sex trafficking cases developed by the Las Vegas Metropolitan Police Department’s Vice & Sex Trafficking Investigations Section. (KSNV)

LAS VEGAS (KSNV News3LV) — A newly released study paints a picture of a violent and thriving sex trafficking industry in Las Vegas, with a majority of victims identified as children.

In a first-of-its kind study conducted in partnership with Metro Police and Arizona State University, researchers unveil a snapshot into one year of sex trafficking cases in Las Vegas.

A team of researchers identified 247 known victims in 2014, most of them younger than 18 years old.

In some circles, they’re called “throw-away children” — a harsh term for those caught in the underground world of sex trafficking.

“I never told anyone about it because I didn't want people making fun of me,” recalled Randy Carter, who is a child sex trafficking survivor. He became a victim at the hands of family friends at 8 years old.

“There were four pimps that were molesting me, and they were selling me off to other pimps,” said Carter.

The case study led by Arizona State University’s Dr. Dominique Roe-Sepowitz highlighted a single year of cases on file for 2014. The most shocking takeaway for researchers was the sheer amount of violence victims face.

“There was a person who cut the cord off a hair straightener and he whipped the victim until she was in a coma. We saw a lot of sexual assaults knocking out teeth, being real violent breaking bones,” said Roe-Sepowitz, who works at ASU’s Office of Sex Trafficking Intervention Research.

Cindy McCain — wife of U.S. Sen. John McCain (R-Ariz.) – is helping lead the charge for change.

“There's never been a study with data before on this issue like this,” said McCain.

The study showed that more than 100 victims were brought to Nevada from other states in 2014. Most of the victims were children and many of them were never reported missing.

"What the study shows is just appalling. How do we have juveniles that are away from their homes for weeks and months at a time and they're not even being reported missing?” said Capt. Sasha Larkin, who heads up Metro Vice.

Larkin says it comes down to educating everyone in the community how to spot victims of the sex trade, from taxi drivers to bartenders to every school employee.

“We have to be angry ... how can we not as a community as a society that we have people making other people have sex and make them give them the money?” said Larkin.

Over the next three years, a $1.5 million grant will infuse local agencies with much-needed funds to combat the problem.

Researchers plan to conduct follow-up studies focused on Las Vegas.
