Investigators stress notifying them ASAP in missing persons cases

By Alma McCarty
Published: Jun. 15, 2016 at 6:02 PM EDT

News 12 First at Five / June 15, 2016

AUGUSTA, Ga. (WRDW/WAGT) -- Two missing teens have found safe, all within the last two days.

While they were missing, families and friends were worried sick, and along with law enforcement, they took some necessary steps.

When someone, a child or an adult, goes missing or runs away, law enforcement says the clock begins ticking. Every minute without action, or without reporting the person missing, means it's a little bit harder for deputies to track down that person.

Rusted staples on an electrical pole serve as reminds of flyers they once held up over the years. Those may have been flyers for rental homes, or a missing pet, even some for missing people.

Just down the road, Aaron Walker, 15, returned home Monday. He had been missing for months.

"I can't imagine a missing child...and not knowing," said Ellen McGahee, a mother and grandmother just a few blocks from where Walker returned to on Kissingbower Road.

Another runaway, Paul Johnson, 17, was also returned to family after police found him outside Atlanta.

While the two boys are now safe, incidents like these still cause McGahee to worry.

"Worst of any mother's worst thoughts - to not to know where your child is," she said.

"When somebody gets so concerned about somebody not being there and they notify us, we pretty much start [the search] right then," said Lt. Allan Rollins with the Richmond County Sheriff's Office.

Investigators say any and all information they can get right away makes the search easier - as does notifying them as quickly as possible.

"Facebook all your friends and say so and so is missing...have you seen him?," Rollins suggested, "And have them call somebody, and them call somebody. This is one of those things where social media is a good thing."

Lt. Jones Nalley with Grovetown Public Safety says phones and credit cards can help, but often people will leave without those things, taking detective work back to square one. That's when he says consulting friends, and people close by is key.

"We asked the family if they've noticed, if they packed up anything, if they know they're going away for a long time," he said.

While both agencies say this happens year round, kids do tend to leave home more during warmer months.

Investigators say some things to remember when reporting a missing person: provide information, a description and pictures as soon as possible. Richmond County officials say they can get flyers out in a matter of minutes if they're provided with everything up front.
