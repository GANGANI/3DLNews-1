The Oregon DMV took in $11.5 million last year by selling your information

PORTLAND, Ore. (KATU) - Your information is being sold by the Oregon DMV and you might not even know it.

ADVERTISEMENT

Between July 2018 and July 2019, the Oregon DMV brought in $11.5 million from selling driver information to companies, according to department records.

KATU News took to the streets to ask Oregonians if they knew their personal information was for sale.

“No, I didn't,” said Andrew Young.

Most people told a KATU News reporter they were unaware their information was being sold, but they weren’t surprised.

“I'm pretty much expecting it to always happen,” said Eric Marte. “I don't expect a single thing that I have decided to put out there to actually be private.”

“We are probably past the point of having any secure information at this point,” said Young.

The DMV maintains a webpage to explain what personal information is part of the public record. But you won’t find a disclaimer on common forms like a driver’s license application or vehicle registration form.

“The DMV lets drivers know about that. It's in the DMV privacy act. How many people are actually looking at that, I don't know. But it's there,” said Don Hamilton, a spokesperson for the Oregon Department of Transportation.

Your name, address, phone number, and driving record are all part of the public record. The Oregon state legislature defines who can get your information. Courts and law enforcement get access. Insurance companies, databases, private investigators and other groups can also purchase your data.

“Many people understand that their information is part of public records. A lot of people depend on these things being public,” said Hamilton.

A DMV spokesperson, David House, told KATU News the agency does not use record sales as a revenue source. The money, he said, helps pay for things like processing the request, staff, the DMV’s use of the state computer system, data storage, and more. House said the fees they charge ensure the DMV doesn't use any funds dedicated to state highways.

In 1994, federal lawmakers passed the Driver Privacy Protection Act to clarify who could get records from the state DMVs. Oregon has its own version of the law. The federal has come under fire from critics recently, including Oregon Sen. Ron Wyden, D-Oregon, who said people need more control over their information.

“We are going to be examining some loopholes there that need to be tightened to make sure people's privacy is being protected,” said Wyden in an interview with KATU News.

House said the DMV will suspend a company’s access to DMV information if they find it was used improperly. He said the DMV will contact the Department of Justice if driver information was used for criminal purposes.
