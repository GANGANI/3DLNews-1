Dangerous street races, takeovers a growing headache for police amid pandemic

by Cole Miller | KOMO News

Thu, May 13th 2021 at 5:25 AM
Updated Thu, May 13th 2021 at 7:31 AM
3

Cars do drifting stunts outside the Space Needle. (KOMO Photo)

    Cole MillerSeattleSeattle,Washington,United StatesSnohomish County,Washington,United StatesVan CleavePete FisherChase Van CleaveKOMO

SEATTLE – Full-throttle, tire-shredding sideshows and street races are revving up amid the pandemic with law enforcement agencies up and down the Puget Sound now coming together to put the brakes on the dangerous driving.

“It’s bad,” said Trooper Chase Van Cleave with the Washington State Patrol. “It’s hard to quantify but it’s a regular occurrence on a lot of our city streets.”

In Seattle, hundreds of people took over the intersection just outside the Space Needle and KOMO Plaza last September where drivers took turn spinning donuts for nearly two hours.

Various groups have also shut down I-5, SR-167 near Renton, and streets throughout Pierce County. In some instances, they’ve taken a turn for the worse with cars slamming into onlookers.

“It is growing and it’s growing fast,” said Van Cleave.

The State Patrol is actively tracking the groups through social media, saying they sometimes communicate through Instagram to post their plans as to where the want to go on any given night. And once the calls start coming in about the activity, it can sometimes be tricky for troopers and officers to respond.

Dash camera video shared by WSP shows multiple interactions where people crowded troopers and in one call, someone starts slamming on the cruiser’s windshield.

“When you think about one, two or even 10 officers responding to a group that big, it’s dangerous,” added Van Cleave.

In Seattle’s University District just a few weeks ago, racers shut down an intersection and flipped a car, later setting off fireworks.

“It’s fireworks, it’s lasers, it’s gunshots, it’s crashes,” Van Cleave explained. “People are getting stuck in the middle of it. There’s a huge outcry from law enforcement to do something about it.

Tracking the data as to just how many events take place is easier said than done. Often times, the calls are logged as reckless driving reports or road blockages. Each jurisdiction may have a different way of inputting those calls.

In Tacoma so far this year, there have been 27 arrests for reckless driving. On top of that, 19 vehicles have been impounded. All of that happening at just a handful of takeovers.

In Fife, the city recently adopted designated “no racing zones” where even spectators can face a fine if busted.

“The street racing is what we’ve labeled it but it’s all kinds of conduct,” Chief Pete Fisher told KOMO News. “Fife is really serious about having this activity not occur in our boundaries. This is clearly an illegal activity. People have been seriously injured or killed with this activity.”

With a little more than 30 officers, Chief Fisher is often times responding to these calls, too. He says the department has now streamlined the way it tracks the calls with two confirmed street racing events on record since its implementation.

He says he’s never seen it this bad since he started policing in 1994.

“We have a large area of warehouses, parking lots that really suits itself to this activity. It’s not acceptable to be reactive, we need to be proactive.”

WSP says on any given night, the groups can start in south Pierce County and work their way through Seattle and King County, all the way to the northern edge of Snohomish County.

“If you’re an organizer, a participant or an attendee, we’re going to hold you accountable to being there and blocking these roadways and putting people at risk,” said Van Cleave, hinting at a newly-formed task force made up of local, state, and federal partners.

Last year, KOMO’s Cole Miller spoke with someone who sometimes frequents the sideshows about the thrill and the adrenaline rush that comes along with it – and why it’s become such a popular thing to do.

“It’s not only Washington state, it happens everywhere,” he said. “It’s kind of hard to stop all of this. You saw the cops that came to the Space Needle and it took forever to break up.”

On Thursday, KOMO News will dive into the details with that task force and explain the tactics police and troopers are now using. Plus, hundreds of charges either have been filed or will be shortly when it comes to those involved.
