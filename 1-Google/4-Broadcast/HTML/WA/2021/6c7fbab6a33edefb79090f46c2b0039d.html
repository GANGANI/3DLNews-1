'Why aren't we coming together?' Seattle still reeling from year of violent protests

by Jonathan Choe, KOMO News Reporter

Thu, February 25th 2021 at 11:46 PM
Updated Thu, February 25th 2021 at 5:25 AM
11
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
Photo: KOMO News

(KOMO) — Day after day. Night after night. Weeks turning into months of protests. We’ve seen various leaders and organizers come and go, including City Council member Kshama Sawant directing hundreds of people into City Hall.

Rallying cries are constantly changing. One moment it’s Black Lives Matter and calls for homeless reform, then its’ defund the police and raise the minimum wage.

Public parks and city blocks, occupied and over run. The homes of elected officials like Mayor Jenny Durkan, and Amazon’s CEO Jeff Bezos all targeted.

Various groups continue to peacefully march, while a mob dressed in black now remains a constant threat representing a violent and volatile mix of activism.

Nearly one year after George Floyd’s death sparked social justice demonstrations and emboldened others to mobilize, Seattle is exhausted and wounded. Many city leaders remain silent and on the sidelines. From the business community to neighbors living in hot zones like Capitol Hill, some of these stakeholders are planning and preparing, for what could be ahead, even without support from their elected officials.

As more large scale and grassroots protests seem inevitable, Victoria Beach said she’s trying to create a united front in her neighborhood.

“We are absolutely not taking it anymore,” Beach said.

But the chair of the Seattle police department’s African American Community Advisory Council is struggling to find others willing to courageously stand by her side.

“ We have the power to stop it, so why aren’t we coming together?" Beach said.

Jon Scholes, CEO and President of the Downtown Seattle Association, said every public official in Seattle needs to condemn the violent and destructive protesters. Scholes said it’s hard enough for businesses recovering from COVID-19 profit losses, but when protesters continue to target the downtown core that will keep many people away.

“That is going to hold back recovery, small businesses reopening, employers bringing back office jobs,” Scholes said.

During last month’s press conference, interim Seattle Police Chief Adrian Diaz once again made a clear distinction between peaceful marches and the ones led by groups dressed in black.

“It’s time to stop, if not you will go to jail and we will prosecute you,” Diaz said.

Diaz said officers will continue to monitor evolving demonstrations, adapt, and be prepared for all scenarios.

“So we do have a sufficient amount of officers,” Diaz said.

Diaz personally asked City Attorney Pete Holmes to get tougher and more consistent with protest related prosecutions.

“Police are doing what they can and are told to do, what the courts are telling them to do,” said Jim Fuda, Executive Director of Crime Stoppers of Pudget Sound.

Which means no more tear gas for crowd control. But Fuda believes the city will remain in a vicious cycle.

“Until these people are prosecuted and held accountable I don’t see anything changing,” Fuda said.

After all the social unrest and on-going protests, progress or success remains undefined and elusive, especially with so many different agendas, factions, and expectations.

One thing is clear, it’s difficult to predict the future of demonstrations in Seattle. But history has a tendency to repeat itself.
