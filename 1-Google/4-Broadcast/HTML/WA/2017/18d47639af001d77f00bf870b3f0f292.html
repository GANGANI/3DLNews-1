Obama cashes out, accepts $400k speaking fee from Wall Street

by Leandra Bernstein

Mon, May 1st 2017 at 1:57 PM
Updated Mon, May 1st 2017 at 2:10 PM
President Barack Obama laughs during a news conference in the briefing room of the White House in Washington, Friday, Dec. 16, 2016. (AP Photo/Pablo Martinez Monsivais)

WASHINGTON (Sinclair Broadcast Group) — Barack Obama will not be the first ex-president to take a big payout after leaving office, but reports that he will be accepting $400,000 for a single Wall Street speaking engagement is raising concerns among members of his own party.

The Wall Street investment firm Cantor Fitzgerald LP confirmed to news outlets that Obama will be keynoting the company's health care conference coming up in September and receiving a fee just shy of half a million dollars.

According to a statement from Obama's spokesperson Eric Schultz, Obama decided to accept the invitation from Cantor Fitzgerald "because, as a President who successfully passed health insurance reform, it's an issue of great importance to him." The spokesman went on to suggest that the Wall Street venue was irrelevant, noting that Obama implemented tough new regulations on the financial class despite raising "more money from Wall Street than any candidate in history."

In addition to the upcoming conference on Wall Street, the New York Post reported that Obama may have netted an additional $400,000 for a Thursday appearance in front of A&E Networks advertisers. The Post reported that he received the fee for a 90 minute interview with presidential historian Doris Kearns at a Midtown Manhattan event.

One of the strongest opponents of Wall Street and advocate for getting big money out of politics, Independent Senator Bernie Sanders of Vermont, criticized Obama's decision to accept the Wall Street speaking fee calling it, "not a good idea."

"I think at a time when we have so much income and wealth inequality, when Wall Street has so much power ... I think it just does not look good," Sanders said in an interview with CNN.

In a later interview on CBS This Morning, Sanders described the president's decision as "unfortunate," saying that in the unlikely event Wall Street offered him the payout, he would not accept.

Throughout the 2016 primary campaign, Bernie Sanders was relentless in attacking his Democratic opponent Hillary Clinton who accepted millions of dollars for private speaking engagements at Wall Street firms like Goldman Sachs, fueling the perception that she was too close to Wall Street.

To date, former President Bill Clinton and former First Lady Hillary Clinton have received the largest amount in speaking fees after leaving office, earning more than $153 million since President Clinton left office. Additionally, the two netted at least $7.7 million for 39 speeches to big banks between 2001 and 2015 averaging upwards of $200,000 per speech, which now looks like a discount compared to President Obama's rates.

Another leading progressive voice in the Democratic Party, Sen. Elizabeth Warren of Massachusetts also objected to President Obama taking the pay-off after leaving office, saying she was "troubled" by the decision.

In an the SiriusXM radio show "Alter Family Politics," Warren described the influence of money in politics as "a snake that slithers through Washington and ... shows up in so many different ways." Warren, along with Sanders and a small handful of other progressives, attempted to push even tougher Wall Street reforms, like breaking up "too big to fail" banks, but ultimately met with little success.

President Obama would hardly be the first ex-Commander in Chief to take a big pay-out after leaving office. Ronald Reagan was one of the first ex-presidents to cause a stir when he earned $2 million for two speeches for Japanese business interests after leaving office in 1989. Former President George H.W. Bush was earning upwards of $80,000 per speech after leaving office, and his son, George W. Bush started making between $100,000 to $175,000 per appearance starting in 2009.

The optics around President Obama's speaking fees are unique in part because he took such strong positions on reforming Wall Street after the 2008 crash and even began his first presidential campaign by refusing to accept contributions from corporate donors.

"The sad thing is, Obama seemed to speak so eloquently about ethics and morality in politics when he was running," said Peter Mathews, political science professor and author of the book 'Dollar Democracy.'

The American people, especially Obama's supporters, saw the president as "a real reformer" and someone who "was not going to be bought by huge amounts of formal, organizational money, like PAC [political action committee] money," he said, so taking the money after leaving office "is a real problem."

As early as the 2008 election, it became clear that Obama was willing to take money from Wall Street to the tune of $44.3 million. That number declined in 2012, after the Dodd-Frank Wall Street reform bill passed, down to $21 million, but he still raised a total of $739 million during that cycle.

This practice of talking like a reformer but cozying up to special interests is corrosive to the American electorate, Mathews argued.

"It's a huge problem for the American people because they're already alienated and feeling the system doesn't serve them," he explained. "And here's the most visible person in the system, the president, going and, some would say cashing in on on his presidency."

Given this potential to earn millions at the end of a four or eight-year term in office, Mathews is concerned that the lure of cash will increasingly impact the way a president conducts himself in office.

"This is setting a precedent for other presidents who might think, what decision should I not make while I'm in office so I can get a good sweetheart deal when I get out of office," he said.

Obama's speaking fees are also taking place at a time when the amount of money in politics is soaring, especially from special interest groups, and the American public is increasingly upset at the notion that their politicians are being bought and paid for.

Some argue that former president should be free to accept special interest money after retiring from public office because they no longer have a conflict of interest between the public good and their personal enrichment. Others see the post-presidential windfall as an extension of the pervasive problem of money buying influence in politics and worry that a president's time in office could increasingly be shaped by the promise of a big pay-off at the end.

Both Barack and Michelle Obama have been offered their own separate book deals reportedly worth as much as $65 million. Michelle Obama is also expected to go on a speaking tour, with more modest rates of $200,000.
