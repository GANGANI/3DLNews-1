Trump dominates latest Iowa poll, GOP rivals struggle to stay competitive

by Skylar Tallal

Tue, December 12th 2023 at 9:37 PM
Updated Tue, December 12th 2023 at 8:15 AM

DES MOINES, Iowa — We're nearly a month away from the Iowa GOP caucuses. There's another 35 days until Iowa Republicans decide who they want to see on the ballot for president.

We're getting an early look at what the results could look like.

A new Des Moines Register/NBC News/Mediacom Iowa poll shows Donald Trump with a massive lead, getting 51% support from likely GOP caucus-goers.

The GOP presidential field is shrinking ahead of the caucuses, and it seems to be boosting the former president's support.

"That increase in support for Trump, in part, probably comes from some of the candidates who had dropped out [like] Scott and Burgum and some others," Tim Hagle, a political science professor at the University of Iowa, said. "And is maybe, Trump got some of that, their votes, but also in terms of people starting to make up their minds."

The coveted endorsements from Gov. Kim Reynolds and Christian leader Bob Vander Plaats may have helped Ron DeSantis slightly. He jumped from 16% in October to 19%, and it barely moved him to second place.

The more recent endorsement from Americans for Prosperity didn't seem to help Nikki Haley much. It may be a little too soon to tell if the endorsement will have an impact, but she’s now stalled at 16%.

"It's gonna be very difficult I think, at this point, for any of the challengers to beat Trump," Hagle said. "Maybe still a strong second but strong is going to probably [be] pretty relative at this point."

Republican voters are starting to make up their minds, but 46% said they could still be persuaded. However, only 30% among those whose first choice is the former president, said they could jump ship. The other 70% said they plan on sticking with him.

Well of course if things change there's still a high percentage of people, according to the poll that are persuadable meaning that they could change their minds, but that number is shrinking as we get closer to the caucuses," Hagle said. "We do see some movement especially even right up until the caucuses but that's a big gap to overcome.

While history has shown anything can happen on caucus night, non-Trump candidates aren't seeing much momentum as Jan. 15 nears.
