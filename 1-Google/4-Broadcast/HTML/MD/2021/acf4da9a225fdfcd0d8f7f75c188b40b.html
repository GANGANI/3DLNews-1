FDA not acting fast enough to protect babies from toxic metals in food, congressman claims

by LISA FLETCHER, ANDREA NEJMAN and SCOTT SMITH

Tue, November 2nd 2021 at 11:39 AM
Updated Tue, November 16th 2021 at 12:18 PM
6

A new Congressional report finds alarming levels of heavy metals in popular brands of baby foods (Photo: Scotty Smith)

WASHINGTON (SBG) — It's been nine months since the first of two blistering congressional reports on contamination in baby food and lawmakers say they're tired of waiting on the Food and Drug Administration to take action.

Twenty-three attorneys General have joined the chorus, demanding the agency charged with protecting the health of Americans set standards for heavy metals in baby food in months, rather than years.

In February, a congressional report detailed the presence of hazardous heavy metals in baby food. Parents assumed that industry and government health agencies would act. Seven months later, a second scathing report found baby food manufacturers and the FDA are till failing to take substantive action. For Rep. Raja Krishnamoorthi, D-Ill., chair of the House Oversight Committee’s Subcommittee on Economic and Consumer Policy, enough is enough.

What I was hoping after we released the initial report was that some of them would actually step forward and do the right thing," Rep. Raja Krishnamoorthi D-Ill. told Spotlight on America. "But unfortunately, some of them have doubled down and done the opposite.

We sat down with Krishnamoorthi in his congressional office adorned with photos of his family. He fondly reminisced about raising his three young children, jokingly describing his house as a "baby food profit center."

When his congressional committee found out some of the most popular brands of baby food, and items like rice cereal, contain toxic metals like cadmium, arsenic and lead, cleaning up the industry went to the top of his to-do list.

An investigation and bombshell report from Krishnamoorthi's committee detailed the presence of neurotoxic heavy metals, known to pose a danger to developing brains, in baby food.

It gained widespread attention, putting a spotlight on major baby food manufacturers and the agency charged with overseeing them: The Food and Drug Administration.

But after the February report, headlines faded and so did any evidence of real change.

In September, Krishnamoorthi and his investigators discovered the industry was still failing to test its finished products, and taking no steps to reduce contaminants.

According to the new report:

  • Sprout Organics was found to be the 'most reckless' with oversight, asking suppliers to test their ingredients only once a year.
  • Gerber failed to recall toxic products.
  • Newly obtained documents revealed that Walmart actually increased its allowable limit for inorganic arsenic in baby food in 2018.

To try to enact change, Krishnamoorthi wrote the Baby Food Safety Act of 2021, which would set concrete limits for heavy metals and go into effect no later than one year after passage. It would also require companies to test their final products, not just their ingredients, and would force them to disclose the results.

As that bill awaits action on Capitol Hill, families are relying on the FDA to move on the issue through its Closer to Zero campaign, which is their step-by-step plan to "reduce exposure to toxic elements from foods eaten by babies."

But the agency won't finalize limits for all heavy metals until after the Spring of 2024.

I think they [the FDA] need to hustle," said Rep. Krishnamoorthi. "I don't think that they are devoting enough attention to it. I'm glad that they started in response to our report, but we're not close to any guidelines from them about what to do with baby food.

Earlier this year, an exclusive Spotlight on America investigation uncovered evidence that FDA officials were made aware of toxic heavy metals in baby food as far back as 2017, and failed to take any meaningful action.

Scientists and food safety advocates who brought the data to the FDA nearly five years ago told Spotlight on America that the FDA did not act on their findings and instead, attempted to impugn the quality of the science.

That data, confirmation tested by multiple analytical labs, would ultimately become evidence in the congressional investigations. We asked Krishnamoorthi why Americans should trust the FDA to fix the problem now, given the agency's lack of response in 2017. He told us he spoke with the acting commissioner and believes that they're now recognizing the problem. Still, he says, they've dealt with it too slowly, and need to get to work.

The path forward, says Krishnamoorthi, is to keep up the pressure so the FDA and industry are forced to act.

He's encouraging parents to speak with their congressional representatives and their senators and support the Baby Food Safety Act of 2021.

Spotlight on America reached out to Sprout, Gerber and Walmart for their response to several questions related to toxic metals in their baby food. None responded to our inquiry.

In response to the latest Congressional report, the FDA sent this statement:

"The FDA has been actively working to lower the levels of toxic elements that enter the U.S. food supply through air, water, and soil for decades, and as a result, we have already reduced exposure from many foods. Since laying out its Closer to Zero plan in April, the FDA continues to make steady progress towards developing action levels for lead in foods and evaluating the science to establish reference levels for arsenic and cadmium. We have continued our sampling to help inform our process to set levels, and we are working to strengthen our relationships with federal partners, industry and advocates to ensure that we make meaningful and lasting reductions in exposure to toxic elements from foods. We are pleased with the support we have received from these stakeholders to help us come up with solutions to help ensure these foods continue to remain safe for infants and children. And recognizing how critical that engagement will continue to be, we will soon be announcing the date for our public meeting that will specifically address questions around the scope of the Closer to Zero plan.

"As parents and caregivers ourselves, we recognize and understand concerns about toxic elements and how they could impact the health of children. In addition to the launch of our Closer to Zero plan, the FDA has also made progress towards reducing levels of toxic elements from entering the food supply:

"In 2020, we finalized an action level for inorganic arsenic in infant rice cereal and we are actively working on finalizing the action level for arsenic in apple juice and developing action levels for lead in juices and other foods.

"The actions outlined in Closer to Zero are part of our ongoing effort to ensure that food manufactures are meeting their legal responsibility to produce, pack, and hold safe foods. As outlined in our letter to industry earlier this year, all food manufacturers and processors covered by the food safety Preventive Control Provisions of the PC Rule are responsible for considering chemical hazards that may be present in foods. Product testing is a step that a firm may take to meet its obligation to assure preventative controls are effective in mitigating and preventing foreseeable hazards when the hazards require a control.

"The FDA also tests for metals and other elements through the Total Diet Study and Toxic Elements in Food and Foodware, and Radionuclides in Food Compliance Program.

"The FDA has the authority to take enforcement action on a case-by-case basis where foods are found through routine or targeted testing to have levels of a toxic element that may pose health concerns.

"While we understand that people may want rapid changes, it is crucial that measures to limit toxic elements in foods do not have unintended consequences—like limiting access to foods that have significant nutritional benefits by making them unavailable for many families. We look forward to providing additional updates on our efforts as new data, information, progress updates and additional material are made available."
