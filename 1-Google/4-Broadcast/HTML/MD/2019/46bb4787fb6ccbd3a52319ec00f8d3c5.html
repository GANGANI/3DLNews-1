Project Baltimore Investigates: Which Schools Could Be Most Dangerous

An ongoing Project Baltimore investigation examining safety in our public schools is raising concerns among parents and lawmakers.

Newly released data from the Maryland Department of Education shows which schools had the most suspensions or expulsions for major offenses during the 2018-2019 school year. Project Baltimore combed through that data and found which schools in our area could be considered the most dangerous.

“That’s very disheartening to hear due to the fact that we have children who go there,” says Reginald Adams, the Bishop of Life Bridge Christian Center in Owings Mills and the father of an 11-year-old who attends Franklin Middle in Baltimore County.

Like any parent, Adams is worried about his daughter’s safety in the classroom. Two years ago, according to state data, his daughter’s school recorded 440 suspensions and expulsions for things like fights, assaults, weapons confiscations and bullying. That was the most in our region.

“I had a concern. I did. I had a concern,” Adams says. “We’re trying to change that. It’s our desire to change all of that.”

And they did. Baltimore County Schools declined to do an interview but told Project Baltimore in a statement that “parents should not be concerned.” The statement said Franklin Middle added mentor programs, behavioral supports and new models to address discipline.

Numbers just released by the state show it appears to have worked. Last school year, despite an overall increase in suspensions and expulsions across the state, at Franklin Middle, they dropped from 440 to 236.

Project Baltimore sat down with State Senator Chris West, who represents Maryland’s 42nd District in Baltimore County. He says there’s no questions schools have become less safe. West believes part of the problem is a change in Maryland’s Juvenile Justice System. To reduce the so-called “school to prison pipeline,” the Department of Juvenile Justice has drastically cut down on the number of juveniles who are sentenced by a judge after being arrested by police.

Scott Beal, with Maryland’s Department of Juvenile Services, says that’s a good thing.

“Research will tell you that the best treatment is always done in the home,” Beal tells Project Baltimore. “It's always done with the families. It's always done where the kids can continue to go to school and continue to thrive in their communities.”

But Senator West says, that’s the problem.

“Putting them right back in the same classroom where they caused the problem to begin with is not the answer. Because they’re going to cause the problem all over again,” West says. “It does make sense. You see it on the news, on your news program in the evenings. Of course, it makes sense, yes. It’s a problem.”

That problem affects all schools differently. A Project Baltimore investigation looked at state data from the 2018-2019 school year and found which schools had the most suspensions or expulsions for major offenses. In Anne Arundel County, North County High school had the most at 465. Long Reach High topped Howard County with 143. In Baltimore County, Dundalk High School lead the way with 521. But what could be considered the most dangerous school in our region with 588, is KIPP Harmony Academy in Baltimore City.

Project Baltimore reached out to KIPP, which gave us this statement:

“One of the greatest strengths about KIPP is our willingness to tackle big questions and make changes when necessary. When we saw the increase of suspensions in the middle grades last school year we focused on our students’ social-emotional learning and classroom management. As of this fall, we have decreased the number of suspensions in the middle grades during the same time by 75 percent from 81 last year to 20 this year. We can attribute the improvement to building strong and consistent positive behavior systems and taking a more restorative approach to student discipline. We will continue to create a nurturing environment in our schools and maintain the well-being of our students front and center.” - Marsha Reeves, Executive Director of KIPP Baltimore

As for Adams, he’s happy his school no longer tops the list.

“The only way we are going to do it and change is to work along with everybody and help turn these lives around,” says Adams.

Earlier this year, Senator West helped create a commission to study and improve Maryland’s Juvenile Justice System, which he says lags behind many other states. He hopes the commission will issue recommendations to the General Assembly by January 2021. Project Baltimore will be following the commission’s progress.
