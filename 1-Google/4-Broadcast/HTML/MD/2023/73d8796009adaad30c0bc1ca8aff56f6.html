    BALTIMORE —

    As with any year, 2023 swung from spectacular highs to tragic lows, but Baltimoreans never lost hope.

    Maryland mourned those killed and uplifted those injured or impacted by multiple mass shootings in Annapolis, Baltimore, Bowie and Salisbury, among others.

    Our hearts were broken after the deaths of firefighters who bravely put their lives on the line for the city they loved, and made the ultimate sacrifice.

    Despite tragedy, Baltimore wouldn't be scarce for good news. People across our community are doing good deeds, helping make Maryland a great place to live. See Maryland's good news stories of the year in the video above.

    In the Baltimore sports world, Orioles Magic prevailed into the postseason. The Birds clinched a playoff berth for the first time since 2016 and a division title for the first time since 2014. All the while, hearts were heavy across Birdland after the death of our beloved Brooks Robinson, whose memory would serve as a guiding force to keep Orioles Magic alive through October.

    Ravens fans also had reason to celebrate in 2023 with a contract extension for franchise quarterback Lamar Jackson, keeping the MVP in Baltimore for the next five years, and a playoff berth clinched in December. WBALTV.com gave viewers a behind-the-scenes look at Ravens Training Camp and posted live updates from games at The Bank.

    Baltimore is rich in the arts and entertainment, and is proud to honor its homegrown stars. Filmmaker John Waters got his due with a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame, and the city dedicated a portion of North Division Street to award-winning actor André De Shields.

    And 11 News was there to bring you coverage of Maryland's biggest stories of the year, from the Maryland attorney general's 456-page report into clergy child sexual abuse in the Archdiocese of Baltimore to big medical news as recreational cannabis became legal in Maryland.

    The city would hold onto hope as Gov. Wes Moore revived the Red Line east-west transit project that officials said would bring transportation equity, economic justice and freedom. Some residents remain hopeful for constructive change when it comes to public safety in the city, as a new police commissioner and a new fire chief took office.

    SkyTeam 11, Baltimore's only news helicopter, was there for breaking news, alerting viewers to emergencies, like massive fires, and delivering the best view of the more than 11,000 people who took part in the Baltimore Running Festival across our beautiful city.

    And, stories by 11 News Investigates uncovered issues and presented solutions-based reporting from parasite contaminants in the water supply to a lack of athletic trainers at Maryland schools, and revealing the collateral damage of youth violence in Baltimore to uncovering thousands of dollars worth of fraud involving unwanted COVID-19 tests sent and billed to Maryland Medicare account holders. When the truth matters, 11 News Investigates gets answers.

    Even we at WBAL-TV celebrated an extraordinary milestone in 2023, marking 75 years of broadcasting excellence in Maryland. It all began on March 11, 1948, when WBAL-TV hit the airwaves from its North Charles Street studios, and the rest, as they say, is history -- and what a history it has been.

    January 2023

    • MOORE MAKES HISTORY: Maryland Gov. Wes Moore was sworn in as the state's first Black governor, punctuating his inauguration with references to Black history that included an acknowledgement of the enslaved people who once arrived by ship near the State House.
    • MASS SHOOTING: A 16-year-old boy was killed in a quintuple shooting at the Edmondson Village Shopping Center. Another 16-year-old boy was arrested in the shooting that devastated the Edmondson Westside High School community.
    • SCHOOL FLOODED: A sudden shift in temperatures caused pipes to burst and flood Frederick Douglass and Digital Harbor high schools with water. Virtual learning continued for weeks thereafter.
    • SQUABBLE NEARLY CANCELS PARADE: The city's annual Martin Luther King Jr. Day parade stepped off just in time for the holiday after nearly being canceled in lieu of a "day of service." The Baltimore Office of Promotion and the Arts put blame for the parade's cancellation on the mayor's office, which led to the mayor calling for the ouster of BOPA's CEO. A shooting at Morgan State University left five people injured and shut down the campus for hours. After releasing several photos of persons of interest, police have since made arrests as they searched for as many as two shooters. Another suspect was arrested in November in Washington, D.C. In an exclusive 11 News Investigates report, university police union members demanded change. In the week following, two people were shot on the Bowie State University campus during homecoming weekend.
    • PARENTS CHARGED IN SCHOOL SHOOTING: Two parents were charged after police said they were caught on camera assaulting a juvenile victim, which led to a shooting outside Carver Vocational Technical High School.
    • JUDGE ASSASSINATED: A Maryland judge was found dead outside of his home in Hagerstown. Investigators said they believe the shooting was targeted because the victim, Washington County Circuit Court Judge Andrew Wilkinson, presided over a divorce hearing involving the suspect. After a dayslong manhunt, the suspect was found dead in a wooded area of Williamsport.
    • A NEW HARBORPLACE: In one of October's most-popular articles on WBALTV.com and in the WBAL-TV app, the developer in charge of shaping a new Inner Harbor announced the Harborplace pavilions will be torn down. Developer David Bramble shared his vision with 11 News, saying the new plan will transform Harborplace into a place where people want to work, live and play, as well as increase access to the water. At the end of the month, drawings for the initial plans were released showing mixed-use development of retail and commercial space along with a residential tower and amphitheater. In December, the Baltimore City Planning Commission voted in favor of three bills to allow revitalization plans to proceed.
    • COMMISH CONFIRMED: After a series of community meetings and a demonstration inside City Hall, the Baltimore City Council confirmed Richard Worley as the next police commissioner as well as James Wallace as the next fire chief.
    • FIRE DESTROYS MANSION: A massive fire burned through the abandoned Uplands Mansion in west Baltimore. Video from SkyTeam 11, Baltimore's only news helicopter, showed the three-alarm fire burning through the structure off Old Frederick Road between Edmondson and Athol avenues.
    • BALTIMORE 'PRETTY TOUGH': Through on-and-off light rain and temperatures only in the 50s, as many as 11,000 runners from all 50 states and as many as 15 countries competed in the 2023 Baltimore Running Festival.

    After the race, the men's winner, Zachary Ripley, 35, of Rockford, Michigan, told 11 News: "That was pretty tough ... it's really exciting, these kinds of things, there's so much energy with all the people out."

    The winner of the women's race, Emily Royston, 25, of Madison, Wisconsin, told 11 News: "It's tough, like, you get to (Mile) 20, and you're just like, all the miles just feel slower and slower. You see (Mile) 25 and you're still running."

    • ORIOLES SWEPT IN OCTOBER: While September was a big month for the Orioles, October wouldn't last long, as the Texas Rangers held off the league's best team to remain perfect in the postseason through the American League Division Series. Baltimore hadn't been swept in a series since May 2022. The Rangers would go on to beat the Houston Astros in the American League Championship Series, and then the Arizona Diamondbacks to win the World Series.
    • PARASITE IN THE WATER: The discovery of low levels of a parasite in the water at Baltimore's Druid Lake Reservoir came as no surprise, 11 News Investigates learned. Investigative reporter Tolly Taylor obtained documents showing how the Environmental Protection Agency warned Baltimore City officials about water parasite risks at the city's uncovered water reservoirs.
    • OMINOUS RECORD SET: Transportation Security Administration officers at Baltimore-Washington International Thurgood Marshall Airport have caught 36 guns so far, with more than two months to go in the year. The previous record of 35 guns caught in a year was set in 2022.
    WBAL

    November 2023

    • HOPE AMID WAR: Jewish congregations in Baltimore are hopeful amid the release of hostages and a ceasefire. Here's why.
    • SMOKE FROM VIRGINIA WILDFIRES: It wasn't fog: The haze Marylanders saw in the sky came from wildfires in Virginia, and it lingered for days. The fire danger was worsened by drought conditions.
    • POLICE SHOOTING: As many as five Baltimore police officers fired 36 rounds at an armed man who investigators said pointed a handgun at an officer. Police released bodycam video and shared what they believe led to the shooting.
    • 'IT'S BEEN HARD': A retired Baltimore police sergeant who survived being shot in 2019 is still waiting on promises he said city officials made to him. Hear from Sgt. Ike Carrington here.
    • NEW FBI HQ: The federal government selected Greenbelt in Prince George's County for the FBI's new headquarters, but there might be a snag. See why here and why leaders in Maryland say it's a done deal.
    • FUNDING CLIFF: Maryland schools face a funding cliff as they are running out of time to spend federal dollars, which will also create a hole in their upcoming budgets -- 11 News Investigates explains how in this special two-part report.
    • EV FIRES: As fires involving electric vehicles can be especially dangerous, 11 News Investigates looks into the prevalence of such fires in Maryland and whether firefighters have the tools they need to fight the fires.
    • MOSBY VERDICT: Marilyn Mosby's trial finally got underway after several delays, and after a three-day trial, the jury reached a verdict.
    • TOP HONORS FOR THE BIRDS: Two Baltimore Orioles sluggers received postseason honors in the form of 2023 American League Silver Slugger Awards, and the skipper got a well-deserved high honor himself!
    • OUT FOR THE SEASON: The Baltimore Ravens secured a huge win over the Cincinnati Bengals, but it came at a price with a serious injury.
    • DO YOU HAVE LEAD PIPES? More than 275,000 public and private water pipes in Baltimore City and Baltimore County need to be inspected to see if they contain lead. Here's how they're going to get it done and how officials are responding. Then, what's the plan for property owners who don't complete the check? After months of declining to comment, the EPA weighed in on Baltimore's daunting effort to find lead pipes.
    • TIFFIN MATS: 11 News Investigates first reported on Tiffin Mats in 2015 over allegations from customers who said their orders were never fulfilled. This year, 11 News Investigates returned to find a new owner, but the same complaints. In December, the company would be destroyed in a fire that remains under investigation.
    • NO MIGRANTS HERE: Leaders in a Maryland county rejected a request to consider allowing 220 migrants currently living in New York City to relocate to here. See why.
    • MARYLAND GUN LAW OVERTURNED: A ruling handed up by a federal appeals court will make it easier for Maryland residents to get a handgun. See why and what led to the appeals court's ruling. Gun violence experts at Johns Hopkins said they are optimistic that the ruling can be appealed.
    • HAPPY THANKSGIVING! Baltimore is rich with Thanksgiving traditions that help those in need, from the Bea Gaddy Thanksgiving Dinner overcoming obstacles at a new location this year to a Baltimore bar celebrating 14 years of free holiday meals, and the Ravens taking part in numerous food giveaways and more than 15,000 people trotting for charity.
    WBAL

    December 2023

    • LIGHT RAIL SHUT DOWN: The Maryland Transit Administration shut down Baltimore's entire Light Rail line to inspect rail cars after smoke issues were discovered due to punctured high-voltage cables. The MTA released video from inside a train that showed an explosion and fire. Those taking the shuttle buses implemented in place of Light Rail said the service is "terrible" and "a mess." A day shy of two weeks after the shutdown, there was still no idea when service would resume. The next day, the MTA announced Light Rail service would resume the Saturday before Christmas -- for free until Jan. 2.
    • STADIUM LEASE DEAL DOWN TO THE WIRE: With just under two weeks before it was due, state officials approved a stadium lease agreement with the Orioles that extends the existing ballpark lease to include time for feasibility studies and government approval to create a development plan. "Baltimore, the deal is done," Gov. Wes Moore said at a news conference.
    • A BALTIMORE LEGEND RETIRES: Baltimore's only education reporter, Dr. Tim Tooten, signed off for the last time from WBAL-TV 11, marking an extraordinary and accomplished decadeslong career.
    • POWER UP: The Maryland Public Service Commission unanimously authorized Baltimore Gas and Electric's multi-year rate plan that includes a rate increase over three years worth almost $408 million. The rate increases would be offset for the first year, and the average electricity customer could see a $4 monthly increase, and an average $10 monthly increase for gas.
    • DISPLACED DAYS BEFORE CHRISTMAS: Dozens of residents were displaced by an early morning fire at a Windsor Mill apartment complex.
    • FREE SOFTWARE UPDATE: As part of an effort to thwart an increase of carjackings and car thefts, Hyundai hosted a free three-day clinic to upgrade car software.
    • 54-YEAR-OLD COLD CASE: More than a half-century after the killing of 20-year-old Joyce Malecki, the family received a spark of hope when FBI investigators exhumed her body as they continue working to identify her killer.
    • 'NOT A VICTORY LAP': Baltimore City is on pace to break a nine-year streak. According to Baltimore police, there have been 256 homicides in 2023 compared to 320 this time last year. It's the first time in nine years the city is on track to have less than 300 homicides. Here's why the mayor says this is no victory lap.
    • TRAIN GARDENS: Tis the season for the holiday train gardens, it's a tradition we look forward to every year! WBAL-TV photographer Tommy Culp took us to Ellicott City and Lutherville for a peek at the intricate displays there.
    • CONGRATULATIONS, DAD!: Baltimore Mayor Brandon Scott welcomed his first child with his new fiancee Hanna Pugh. The mayor announced the arrival of Charm Jamie Scott.
    • SEASON TO CELEBRATE: Marylanders are enjoying the yuletide season with the sixth year of WBAL-TV 11's Emmy Award-winning special, "Season to Celebrate." This year, timeless holiday classics were performed by some of Maryland's brightest stars, including Country artist Emma White, R&B artist Wordsmith, a cappella group The Trills, and the U.S. Naval Academy Gospel Choir. Plus, a special performance from award-winning talk show host Kelly Clarkson!
