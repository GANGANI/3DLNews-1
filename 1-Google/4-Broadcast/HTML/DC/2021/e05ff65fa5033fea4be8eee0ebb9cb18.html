    Winsome Sears projected Virginia's next lieutenant governor, first woman to hold position

    by 7News Staff, John Gonzalez

    Wed, November 3rd 2021 at 12:52 AM
    Updated Thu, December 16th 2021 at 4:24 PM
    6
    VIEW ALL PHOTOS
    Either Republican Winsome Sears and Democrat Hala Ayala will become the Commonwealth's first female lieutenant governor, and also the first woman of color to hold a statewide office.(Image Courtesy: Winsome Sears)

    Republican Winsome Sears will make history as Virginia’s next lieutenant governor after defeating Democratic challenger Hala Ayala in a heated race, the Associated Press projects. She makes history as the first woman of color to hold a statewide post.

    Sears is the first woman and the first woman of color to serve in a job that frequently serves as a launching pad to the governor’s mansion. Half of the past 10 lieutenant governors went on to become governor.

    Lieutenant governor race in Virginia: Meet Republican candidate Winsome Sears

    Wednesday morning, 7News Reporter John Gonzalez spoke to Sears regarding her win:

    Gonzalez: Congratulations Lt. Governor-elect Sears. What a night, into the morning hours. What does this mean for Virginia?

    Sears: It means change, it means that when you have someone in office especially at this high-level who looks like me, it's gonna be change, we're going to stop the divisive attitudes." We're going to stop the racial constant black against white, you know, it's not conducive to good public policy and it's not conducive to good public nation, public relations, and certainly not good for friendship, which I've heard have been so disseminated because of it.

    Gonzalez: You are a Marine veteran, you have vowed to protect the Second Amendment, you are anti-red flag laws, tell me about that, you're passionate about that.

    Sears: Well, here.s the thing. Harriet Tubman carried a gun and if it was good enough for her, then it's good enough for me. But here's the other thing that most people don't know, the gun confiscation laws were first against Black people, you see, and so, we are entitled to have guns. It's our Second Amendment right and we're not giving them up.

    Gonzalez: You also have an educational background. You said that Critical Race Theory is racist, what do you mean by that?

    Sears: Because in my mind, if a child is uneducated or undereducated, there is no future. And when we see that, if you're rich, you're able to move your child from the school that is failing them to another school then that fine, but then most of the people, who are not able to do so, they look like me, or you know, they're Latino, etcetera. We've got to have that ability for the children to have a good education so that they can have a hope and a future and create generational wealth.

    Gonzalez: My last question, Lt. Governor-elect, how special was it being on stage last night with your husband Terrance and your two daughters?

    Sears: Well, you know, when you have your family you with when you achieve something, it feels so good. I just wish my mother and father could've been here, but especially my grandmother, who is now in heaven, who, you know, politics was her bailwick, so it's become mine now.

    Sears, a former Marine who immigrated to the United States from Jamaica as a girl and vaulted to victory in a crowded primary on the enthusiasm generated by a campaign photo of her posing with a military rifle.

    You can read more about the historical significance of Sears' win here.

    Sears is a conservative who already broke barriers as the first Black Republican woman to be elected to Virginia's General Assembly.

    Her political career started as the vice president of the Virginia Board of Education. She has described critical race theory as racist but believes that the good and the bad of American history should be taught in the class.

    Sears has vowed to protect gun rights and opposes red flag laws. She is a wife and mother of three daughters. In 2012, she experienced tremendous tragedy firsthand when her 27-year-old daughter and two grandchildren were killed in a car accident.

    Her other two daughters and husband Terrance stood by her side on stage in Marriott Washington Dulles in Chantilly Tuesday night during her victory speech.

    She had a brief stint in electoral politics 20 years ago as a one-term delegate in the General Assembly, representing parts of Hampton Roads. Her return to politics after a two-decade absence began when she served as national chairperson for Black Americans to Re-Elect President Trump.

    Ayala, on the other hand, was launched into public office by her organizing role in the resistance to President Donald Trump when he was inaugurated in 2017.

    Ayala won election to the House in November 2017 and quickly rose through the Democratic ranks to serve as chief deputy whip, helping to shepherd a raft of legislation into law after Democrats took control of the legislature in 2020, including the abolition of the death penalty, the legalization of marijuana, and election reforms.
