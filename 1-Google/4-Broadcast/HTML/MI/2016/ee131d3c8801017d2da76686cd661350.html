Pugsley Correctional Facility Families “Blindsided” By Closure Announcement

9and10news Site Staff
06/01/2016 5:49 PM EDT

A sudden decision to close a Northern Michigan prison has left many wondering what is in store for the future.

Yesterday the Michigan Department of Corrections announced they will close Pugsley Correctional Facility.

The MDOC has slated the prison near Kingsley to close in September.

The decision will save the state $22 million.

One reason for the decision is the declining prison population.

But now 230 employees are left wondering what happens next.

9&10’s Caroline Powers and photojournalist Harrison Light caught up with a family directly impacted by the prison closure for our continuing coverage.         

“Surreal. I felt sort of numb. Like it’s not really happening.”

That’s how Betty Clark felt after her husband called her saying Pugsley Correctional Facility, where he’s worked for 15 years, is closing.

He found out from his daughter who saw the news on social media.

“He got no information about it. Blindsided me at school too and we had no idea that this was coming. Nothing from the department. Nothing from Pugsley. Nothing,” says Betty Clark.

230 employees found out yesterday that the prison would be closing. Something many say makes no sense.

“To close it for what they’re saying is not true,” Clark says. “Judge Rodgers has made no bones about that he’s not happy how that prison is run by the warden. We feel that the MDOC, rather than deal with an issue it’s having, would rather shove it under the rug.”

The MDOC says they stand by their decision, and they looked at all possible changes before deciding to close Pugsley.

As a facility that brings hundreds of families to the area, losing it will leave an economic hit on the region.

“I don’t want to say it’s going to be a permanent hit but I think it’ll take some adjusting to,” says Kent Wood, Director of Government Relations for the Traverse City Area Chamber of Commerce. “We would be big proponents of looking for what are the grants or investment opportunities that might be out there for training for those employees and their families to help keep them in the area.”

And other are left wondering where they go from here.

“There’s still uncertainty,” Clark says. “We’ve made roots. We will fight to stay here.”
