Rep. Mike Gallagher, will not seek re-election following Mayorkas impeachment 'no' vote

by JACKSON SINNENBERG | The National Desk

Sat, February 10th 2024 at 5:10 PM
4
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
Chairman Mike Gallagher, R-Wis., leads a special House committee dedicated to countering China holds a hearing at the Capitol in Washington, Tuesday, Feb. 28, 2023. (AP Photo/J. Scott Applewhite)

    TOPICS:

    Mike GallagherRe-electionImpeachmentCongressBipartisanRepublicanMayorkasChina

WASHINGTON (TND) — Rep. Mike Gallagher, R-Wisc., who became the final of three Republican votes to defeat his party's attempt to impeach Homeland Security Secretary Alejandro Mayorkas, announced Saturday he would not seek re-election.

"The framers intended citizens to serve in Congress for a season and then return to their private lives," Gallagher wrote in a statement. "Electoral politics was never supposed to be a career and, trust me, Congress is no place to grow old."

The Wisconsin Republican, who is serving his fourth term, gained a reputation as a firm conservative that was also willing to reach across the aisles. His membership in the conservative Republican study committee -- and his strong voting rate with Republican initiatives during the Trump presidency -- reflected the first half of his reputation while his membership in the bipartisan Problem Solvers Caucus reflected the other side.

As a freshman lawmaker, Gallagher pushed for reforms in the House to decentralize power from party leadership when it came to selecting the heads of committees and to restructure the Congressional calendar to force members to be more regularly in the office all five weekdays. While both were rejected, Gallagher came into his own gavel power last year when he was appointed the chair of the House Select Committee on the CCP, formally leading, on a largely bipartisan basis, the Congressional investigation into the economic, technological and hegemonic competition between the U.S. and China.

A former Marine Intelligence officer who served two deployments in Iraq, Gallagher said in his announcement that he viewed his time in Congress through a similar lens and that it was time to pack up.

Eight years ago, when I first ran for Congress, I promised to treat my time in office as a high-intensity deployment," he said.

"Through my bipartisan work on the Armed Services and Intelligence Committees, chairing the Cyberspace Solarium Commission, and chairing the Select Committee on the Chinese Communist Party, we've accomplished more on this deployment than I could have ever imagined."

Gallagher rose quickly to even greater national prominence, and infamy, Tuesday when he became the third Republican to help vote down their party's long-dreamed attempt to impeach Secy. Mayorkas. Like his fellow rebels, Reps. Ken Buck, R-Colo., and Tom McClintock, R-Calif., Gallagher argued that his colleagues did not establish that Mayorkas committed an impeachable offense and that pursuing such would set a dangerous precedent for future impeachments.

"Creating a new, lower standard for impeachment, one without any clear limiting principle, won't secure the border or hold Mr. Biden accountable and will set a dangerous new precedent that will be weaponized against future Republican administrations," Gallagher wrote in a statement released Tuesday.

It will only pry open the pandora's box of perpetual impeachment.

Gallagher's departure does continue a trend of the last half-decade in Congress of members retiring or deciding to not seek re-election early as hyper-partisanship continues to drive Representatives from both parties out of the halls of power. A number of those helped reduce the House Republicans' already slim majority in 2023 to a razor-thin one this year, with the conference only capable of losing two votes on most major legislative initiatives.
