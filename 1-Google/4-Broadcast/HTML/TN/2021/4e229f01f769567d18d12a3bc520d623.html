DCS, police investigating report of abuse at Chattanooga facility housing migrant children

by WTVC, Latricia Thomas, Sam Britten

Wed, June 16th 2021 at 4:43 PM
Updated Wed, June 30th 2021 at 6:44 PM
3
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
Former Tennessee Temple dorms where some migrant children are currently living. (Image: WTVC)

CHATTANOOGA, Tenn. — UPDATE (Wednesday, June 30):

Chattanooga Police have charged a woman with sexual battery by an authority figure after a report of abuse at a facility housing unaccompanied minor children. See more here.

PREVIOUS UPDATE (Thursday, June 17th):

U.S. Senators Marsha Blackburn and Bill Hagerty, both Republicans from Tennessee, have released statements after a report of abuse at the old Tennessee Temple dorms in Highland Park.

"Hearing about child abuse at a Chattanooga migrant facility is a heartbreaking reminder of the reality of Biden’s border crisis," said Sen. Blackburn in a tweet, "This incident must be thoroughly investigated. Until we secure our southern border, every town is a border town and every state is a border state."

"Any & all reports of child abuse should be fully investigated," said Sen. Hagerty on Twitter, "This situation is upsetting & only corroborates the dangerous effects of Biden’s border crisis that’s putting the safety & security of countless individuals at risk. We must secure our border now."

Earlier today, Chattanooga Police also confirmed that on Monday, June 14th, a 16-year-old boy was reported missing from the Chattanooga facility where the migrant children are staying. See more on that here.

ORIGINAL STORY (Wednesday, June 16th):

Federal, state, and local authorities are investigating after a child reported abuse at a Chattanooga facility housing migrant children.

Tennessee Department of Children's Services Commissioner Jennifer Nichols told state lawmakers about the report during a Tennessee General Assembly joint hearing on Wednesday.

Nichols says the received the report during an unannounced visit the week of May 31 to the old Tennessee Temple dorms in Highland Park. The facility is being operated by the Baptiste Group, which has a contract with the Federal Government Office of Refugee Resettlement (ORR) to temporarily house migrant kids there.

She says DCS officials and an interpreter interviewed six children there, and that one of the children said they had witnessed the incident.

"The sixth youth that was interviewed disclosed that he had witnessed an act that in our policy would substantiate and require that an investigation into that act take place," said Nichols.

DCS Chief of Staff Jennifer Donnals told us the alleged incident is reported to have happened at the facility, not before the child got there. She said state privacy protection laws prohibit them from making further comments.

Nichols says there were 62 children at the facility when Mark Anderson, Tennessee DCS' director of licensing, interviewed the child. There were 41 children there as of Tuesday, June 15. Most were boys between the ages of 12 and 17.

When asked if the facility had not reported the alleged incident until the child told him about it, Anderson said he believed the facility was unaware of it.

Nichols says DCS is investigating, and local law enforcement along with federal agencies have been notified and have gone back to the dorms to interview more children.

"In other words, a referral was made to our own hotline," said Nichols, "Local law enforcement was immediately notified, the DA's office was notified, and since the day after that investigation by our unannounced site visit, there has been an ongoing investigation involving local law enforcement, FBI was notified, the Office of Inspector General for ORR - they call that the Office of Refugee Resettlement - and the federal Homeland Security have all been notified and are working in collaboration with local law enforcement and our department to investigate what this youth disclosed to Mr. Anderson that he witnessed."

Chattanooga Police spokesperson Elisa Myzal says their Special Victims Unit is investigating after they "received complaints involving juveniles that were housed at that location." We asked CPD to clarify their use of the word 'complaints,' as DCS only mentioned the one report on Wednesday, but Myzal said she could not go into the details of the complaints at this time.

CPD says their investigation began on June 7, and that they have contacted the Office of the Inspector General for support and resources.

First Christian Church Pastor Brandan Gilvin hopes it's carried out in an appropriate manner.

"It needs to focus on the health and safety of those children who are leaving behind violence that we can't imagine for the most part," Gilvin told us.

Anderson said that their licensing requires all of the Chattanooga facility's staff to be trained in how to report sexual abuse. He said there are also posters throughout the facility telling children of their rights and how to report abuse, and a dedicated phone for reporting sexual abuse.

Immigration lawyer Brittany Faith was surprised by the DCS going public with this.

"Most of the time, these things would be kept confidential with the Department of Children's Services," Faith said.

During Wednesday's hearing, one Tennessee lawmaker argued that these children should never have been allowed in this facility in the first place.

"We’re not talking about refugees who are coming from war-torn Somalia or North Africa where people are being persecuted for just being a certain religion," said Rep. Scott Cepicky, "These are people coming across the border, illegally, into our country that are being deemed refugees — which is not even close to being who they are — sent to our communities here in Chattanooga, which is two and a half hours down the road, to being dropped off to other places of Tennessee where we don’t know where they’re going."

But the White House has fought back against that very claim, saying the children have been flown into the city for the sole and specific purpose of reuniting them with their families.

"Children were traveling through, have been traveling through Tennessee," said White House press secretary Jen Psaki during a May 21st news conference, "They are simply on the way to unite with relatives and sponsors, to meet sponsors in the state, or just traveling through Tennessee until they reach another destination to unite with family members or legal sponsors. It's right in a place where you know there's a lot of states around, so it's a place where some flights have gone through as children are moving to other destinations.

Rep. Cepicky further asked what documentation is being used to identify the undocumented 12- to 17-year-old children, saying that "it seems to me it sets up the perfect scenario for trafficking," and suggested they interview all the children in the facility.

The situation of migrant children brought to the Chattanooga dorms and other areas across Tennessee has been a source of contention along party lines.

The new 'Joint Study Committee on Refugee Issues' has said it plans to investigate the number of migrant children coming in and impact on community, as they call for transparency from the Federal Government.
