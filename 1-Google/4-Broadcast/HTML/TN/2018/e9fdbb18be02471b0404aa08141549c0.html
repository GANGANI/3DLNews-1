Former EMT, reserve deputy convicted of causing 2018 fatal crash seeking new trial

    Justin Whaley appeared in court on Tuesday for hearing as he seeks a new trial.

    • Facebook
    • Twitter
    • WhatsApp
    • SMS
    • Email
    • Print

    UPDATE: A former Hamilton County EMT who killed a man in a 2018 crash is back in court on Wednesday seeking a new trial.

    Justin Whaley was convicted of vehicular homicide by intoxication and sentenced to nine years for causing the crash that killed James Brumlow.

    During a hearing on April 30, Captain Eric Jenkins and Lieutenant Jake Elrod with the Soddy-Daisy Police Department (SDPD) testified in court that Sgt. Jeremy Wright, a key witness in Whaley's trial, lacked credibility.

    Sgt. Wright was the center of an internal affairs investigation at the SDPD and later admitted to Captain Jenkins that he was untruthful in his statement to Elrod, according to court testimony.

    We have a crew inside the courtroom to bring you the latest.

    Stay with the Local 3 News app for continuing coverage of this story.

    PREVIOUS STORY: A former Hamilton County EMT and reserve deputy convicted of vehicular homicide was back in court on Tuesday asking for a new trial.

    Justin Whaley was originally sentenced to nine years for causing the crash that killed James Brumlow in 2018.

    Whaley's first request for a retrial was denied.

    Two weeks ago a judge denied his request for bond while he and his lawyer waited on a decision on the second appeal.

    Defense attorney Lee Davis filed the motion to get a new trial for Justin Whaley, claiming witness Soddy-Daisy Police Officer Jeremy Wright, who testified that he smelled a strong odor of alcohol on Whaley in his patrol car, was not a credible witness.

    The Hamilton County District Attorney's Office was notified by Soddy-Daisy Police Captain Eric Jenkins that Wright is at the center of an internal affairs investigation alleging he showed a provocative image to a woman and minor.

    Although former Soddy-Daisy Police Chief Mike Sneed testified that both victims would not provide a formal statement, with differing accounts of what exactly was shown in question.

    This is where Wright's credibility is being questioned but prosecuting Assistant District Attorney Parker Garrett says Wright's testimony still stands.

    "Assuming that the untruthfulness allegation is true, our position is that it does not impact Sgt. Wright's credibility to such an extent that it may have resulted in a new trial,” Parker said. "Largely what he testified to is undisputed.”

    The DA's office notified the defense when they were told about the internal affairs investigation.

    The question is whether the jury would have come to a different conclusion knowing this information.

    "It's not that it definitely would have made a different result, but that it may have,” Davis said. "You've got to be convinced that if all this came out, that it would not or may have not resulted in a different result.”

    Judge Boyd Patterson says this would have been discussed in a pretrial hearing and not at the actual trial.

    Attorney Lee Davis says the defense is not questioning the prosecution, but they are questioning the Soddy-Daisy Police Department.

    The next court appearance is scheduled for May 21.

    Stay with the Local 3 News app for continuing coverage of this story.

    PREVIOUS STORY: Former Hamilton County EMT and reserve deputy, Justin Whaley, was back in court on Monday after being sentenced to nine years for causing the crash that killed James Brumlow in 2018.

    Whaley has since made two appeals for a retrial, with the first being denied and his second now being under reviewed.

    On Monday morning, Whaley requested the courts to consider an appeal bond which would allow him to be free while waiting for the next appeal decision.

    Whaley testified that he is not a flight risk or a danger to the community if it was ruled that he could post bond before his next court appearance, but ultimately Judge Boyd Patterson denied the appeal bond.

    "Would you ever flee your obligations to this court?" asked Whaley's attorney. 

    "No sir, my honor means everything to me, and this is my home, this is where I built my business, this is my life, I'm not fleeing that," responded Whaley.

    Whaley answered questions from his attorney and the state. The state argued while Whaley may not be considered a danger to his community, he is a flight risk.

    "He has the ability, he has the means, he has connections outside of the state, he did forfeit his passport but that doesn't mean he can't leave this jurisdiction and as the court has noted he's got the ability to do so if he makes that decision," said the state attorney.

    Whaley's attorney claimed his client has proven that he is committed to serving his time through previous hearings and while being in prison for six months.

    "He does have the resource that if he were to make the easy decision. I do find that an appeal bond is not appropriate in this case, the defense motion for an appeal bond is overruled," said Judge Patterson.

    Whaley was taken back to prison, where he is waiting for the decision on his second appeal which claims the District Attorney's Office disclosed that the arresting officer and key state witness, Jeremy Wright, was the subject of an internal affairs investigation in 2023 and was found to be untruthful.

    Whaley is asking for a new trial.

    Stay with the Local 3 News app for continuing coverage of this story.

    PREVIOUS STORY: The former Hamilton County EMT found guilty of vehicular homicide has filed a petition in light of newly discovered evidence.

    Justin Whaley and his attorney have filed a "Petition for Writ of Error Coram Nobis."

    The Cornell Law School explains the motion as a way to call the court's attention to facts that would have "changed the judgment but were outside the record and unknown to the court at the time of judgment."

    Whaley was denied an appeal for a retrial in March after being sentenced to 9 years in prison for the death of James Brumlow. Whaley was found guilty of drunk driving and vehicular homicide.

    The motion says that last month the District Attorney's office disclosed that the arresting officer and key state witness, Jeremy Wright, was the subject of an internal affairs investigation in 2023 and was found to be untruthful.

    Whaley is asking again for a new trial.

    PREVIOUS STORY: Former Hamilton County EMT Justin Whaley was denied an appeal for a retrial after being sentenced to 9 years in the killing of father and husband, James Brumlow. Whaley was found guilty of drunk driving and vehicular homicide, last year.

    Monday morning, Justin Whaley's attorney argued there were several issues in the initial trial which is why they want to appeal. Judge Patterson said it's a required part of the procedure for the defense to lay out the grounds for review.

    One of the issues brought up was that Whaley's blood alcohol content report should have been suppressed.

    "The blood alcohol report and any expert extrapolation should have been suppressed as unreliable under Tennessee rule of evidence 403 and 703 given the four hour delay between the accident and the blood draw and the low reports of .020," said his attorney.

    "The nutshell is the judge found that based on the testimony that the range was something that could be testified to by a specific BAC conclusion and I think that's what the jury got," said Judge Boyd Patterson.

    Whaley's attorney also brought up the 13th juror rule which would require the judge to act as the 13th juror and independently determine if the evidence is in favor of the jury's verdict.

    "The evidence does not support a verdict and the court should have granted an acquittal in favor of Mr. Whaley," said Whaley's representation.

    "Respectfully, I think it did," Judge Patterson said.

    Family members of James Brumlow were in court for the hearing. Judge Patterson denied Whaley's request for an appeal which Brumlow's family said will bring them some much needed closure.

    "Every time they would drag us back into court it was like James had passed all over again, but now we can kind of, James you can go rest," said his parents-in-law, Jim and April Hinson.

    They said they've been fighting for five and a half years and finally feel like justice has been served. They tell Local 3 that Brumlow's legacy will live on through his two sons.

    "We celebrate every year and we will continue to do that. Not that he's gone, but we celebrate his life," said the Hinsons.

    PREVIOUS STORY: Former Hamilton County reserve deputy and EMT Justin Whaley was sentenced to nine years Monday in a Hamilton County courtroom.

    "I was the one who messed up. I know that. I knew it from the beginning. It was all my fault, and I should've been the one to die," said Whaley.

    Whaley was involved in a deadly, wrong-way crash near Highway 111 in the Soddy-Daisy in 2018. That head-on collision claimed the life of James Brumlow.

    "He was always my protector. In ways that I hated when we were kids, but actually understood when I became an adult," said Brumlow's sister Kisha Gorbski.

    Whaley was convicted of driving drunk and vehicular homicide in the crash.

    Brumlow's son, Andrew Anderson took the stand before the sentencing, telling Whaley "You have showed no remorse for over five and a half years. I don't care about your apology. I don't want to hear it. Because if you were, you would have showed it a long time ago."

    Whaley's wife was the only person to speak for the defendant besides himself.

    "He knows that he made a mistake, and he has been absolutely remorseful ever since he made that mistake from the moment that it happened," said Heather Whaley.

    Heather Whaley says her husband wrote the Brumlow family apologizing to them for what happened, but the victim's family says they have not gotten an apology.

    "I want to spare anyone any chance of an apology, because me nor my family could care less for it. Over the last five years there were ample opportunities. Now is not the time now that you've had time to sit by yourself and thank about it," said Brumlow's son, Ryan Brumlow.

    Judge Boyd Patterson talked through his decision with the courtroom, which is not common.

    Whaley's defense attorney, Lee Davis says he will file a motion for a retrial later this weekend. A court appearance on the matter is expected March 11.

    PREVIOUS STORY: A jury has found former Hamilton County first responder Justin Whaley guilty of vehicular homicide and other charges in the 2018 death of James Brumlow.

    Whaley is scheduled to appear in court for sentencing on January 22, 2024.

    PREVIOUS STORY: Jury members began deliberation right after 7:30pm Thursday night.

    Just before 10:00pm, jury members were sent back to their hotel, leaving no decision for the night. 

    Court will resume at 8:30am Friday with jury deliberation.

    Earlier in the day, Christopher Walker, a Hamilton County Sheriffs Office Corporal said on the stand “Working patrol for Sequatchie County and Hamilton County, it’s not uncommon to have vehicles go the wrong way on 111. We’ve have numerous BOLOs, be on the lookout for people driving the wrong way on 111 and SR-27.”

    More testimony came from Greg Allen, a retired Hamilton County EMS EMT who said (referring to Whaley) “He was speaking normally. I didn’t see any type of impairment that would have been an immediate red flag.”

    The timing of drawing Whaley's Blood Alcohol Content (BAC) and the methods used for its collection also came into question.

    Dr. Jimmie Valentine, a Forensic Toxicology expert witness said “to try to do retrograde extrapolation you’ve got to make some other assumptions. You’ve got to assume what the elimination rate constant for the person is, and generally a range is used for that. Well, that range becomes inexplicable when you’re down into these very low levels where it’s no longer linear.”

    PREVIOUS STORY: Closing arguments are expected on day three of the trial against a former Hamilton County reserve deputy and EMT who is charged in a deadly 2018 crash.

    The suspect, Justin Whaley, is accused of killing James Brumlow in a head-on crash on SR-111 in July of 2018.

    Prosecutors rested their case and Whaley’s team brought four witnesses to the stand.

    These included a Hamilton County Sheriff’s Office corporal familiar with the crash site area, the EMT that checked defendant Whaley out at the crash scene, an expert testifying on forensic toxicology and then a crash reconstructionist.

    The corporal testified to seeing people getting confused and accidentally driving on the wrong side of the road in that area.

    The EMT testified Whaley did not appear drunk when he was checking him for injuries.

    The toxicology expert’s testimony was aimed at breaking down the TBI’s “average elimination rate” findings.

    A special calculation called Widmark’s Formula is used to determine the 0.056 to 0.12 BAC range determined for the defendant.

    The expert testified that results are unreliable at or under the 0.02 BAC level Whaley had four hours after the wreck.

    On Tuesday, Whaley pleaded not guilty to the 10 charges against him.

    In addition to Whaley's plea, a Tennessee Highway Patrol trooper and another first responder who responded to the crash both testified to smelling alcohol on on the defendant while at the scene.

    On Wednesday, two toxicology experts testified about the process of determining a person's blood alcohol content (BAC) and how alcohol impacts the body.

    Responding Hamilton County EMT Steven Scott says he smelled alcohol on the defendant, but did not believe he intoxicated. 

    Dr. Kenneth Cutshall says that the stress of the incident could have contributed to the defendant's reaction.

    "Your flight or fight. That adrenaline is going to produce an opposite effect. You'll seem more aware or in focus. You'll speak faster, quicker, maybe a little bit more precise than if we're just under the influence of alcohol without adrenaline," said Dr. Cutshall.

    The jury visited the crash site to see the route between the home of Whaley's friend, David Taylor, and the location of the crash.

    Local 3 will have a crew in the courtroom covering the trial from start to finish.

    PREVIOUS STORY: Tuesday morning, Justin Whaley appeared in a Hamilton County courtroom where he pleaded not guilty to all 10 charges related to the deadly 2018 crash on SR-111 near US 27.

    In the crash, James Brumlow was killed. 

    James Brumlow's family all wore green, with green flowers, in the courtroom Tuesday.

    Whaley, who was a reserve deputy for the Hamilton County Sheriff’s Office and EMT, was charged with DUI in the head-on crash.

    On Tuesday, a Hamilton County jury heard six witnesses and the 911 call along with watching the body camera footage and surveillance video. 

    Both the state and defense are trying to determine is whether or not Whaley was still under the influence of alcohol when the head-on crash happened. 

    Whaley admitted to drinking the night before with a friend, David Taylor, who was also the first witness called to the stand.

    Taylor testified to buying six bottles of bourbon and that they were taking between 1 and 2 shots of each. He also said they did not eat a full meal, but were snacking.

    Tennessee Highway Patrol Trooper Martin Penny testified saying “As Mister Whaley approached me, the first thing I noticed as he got closer was a very strong odor of alcohol coming off of his person.”

    Another first responder at the crash scene says they smelled alcohol on the defendant when he transported him to the Soddy-Daisy Police Department.

    There were roughly 4 hours between the wreck and Whaley’s blood draw to determine his blood alcohol level, which was 0.02; the state's legal limit is 0.08.

    Wednesday is expected to be another lengthy day, with plans to take the jurors out to the crash site.

    PREVIOUS STORY: Justin Whaley appeared in court on Tuesday morning.

    The former reserve deputy for the Hamilton County Sheriff’s Office and EMT is charged with driving drunk and killing James Brumlow in a head-on crash on SR-111 in July of 2018.

    New court documents show Judge Barry Steelman recused himself from the case last month and transferred it to division three.

    Local 3 News spoke to the victim's family in July of 2022, when they learned about a potential plea deal for Whaley.

    Brumlow's family says they made it clear they are against it.

    Whaley is due back in court on October 18 for another status hearing.
