BREAKING STORY

Losing full-time nurses hits nearly all schools

  • By JACKIE LEATHERMAN
  • Aug 16, 2010
  • Aug 16, 2010
  • 41
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email

  • Facebook
  • Twitter

  • Save

When Maureen Welch heard nurses were being cut from schools, she changed her work schedule.

She knew that her 9-year-old adoptive son, who needs daily medical care, would not be able to go to school without a full-time nurse present, and she was expecting to have to be by his side.

“He either wouldn’t be here, or I would have to be here with him,” she said.

Even her son Noah Welch knows he needs a nurse, too. He was born with fetal-alcohol syndrome and was placed into Welch’s foster care soon after birth. He has a feeding tube and needs to check his blood sugar regularly and receives medication for leg cramps.

“If there wasn’t a nurse here, I wouldn’t get to go … because I need help,” said the Smoketree Elementary School third-grader.

Last school year, each of the nine schools had a nurse.

But because of budget cuts this year, only the high school, Thunderbolt Middle School and Smoketree Elementary School will have full-time nurses. The remaining five elementary schools only will have part-time nurses either in the mornings or afternoons.

Aggie Wolter, the Lake Havasu Unified School District director of special services and emergency planning, predicts 911 will be dialed more frequently this year as a result of the losses.

“It’s being felt,” Wolter said. “911, I assume, will be called more often.”

She said they had to do “some real creativity with schedules” to ensure students receive their regular medication. Office staff will now become the front lines for medical care when the part-time nurses are absent from those campuses.

School staff is working with parents to develop medical reaction plans in the event of an emergency, she said, stressing that office staff will not make medical judgments but rather they will call parents and 911.

“We are not going to be able to provide the same level of care,” she said. “Having less support is not a good place to be, obviously.”

The district governing board eliminated two nursing positions in December 2009, among other budget cuts, after local voters failed to renew a secondary property tax during an election the month prior, top school district officials said. District officials estimate the loss of the revenue to be about $1.7 million and also closed one of its two middle schools.

Federal law gives special needs students, who can have serious medical problems, the right to a public education, but there is no law requiring school nurses.

Smoketree is the designated student development center among the elementary schools and receives the district’s most “severe and fragile” young students, Wolter said.

At Lake Havasu High School, 270 students receive daily medication, the highest number in the district. Daily administrations at the elementary school range from 129 to 185, according to figures provided by the district.

Linda Galloway, the nurse at Smoketree, estimates she sees between 50 to 90 students daily. On Friday, students filtered in and out of her nursing office to check their blood sugar levels.

“You just have to wait and see what comes tomorrow,” she said.
