Kellis 'Unify' model for nation

  • By DARRELL JACKSON, Staff Writer
  • Oct 18, 2013
  • Oct 18, 2013
  • Facebook

High school can be a tough time for any student, but for those with special needs and mildly and moderately delayed students, it can be even worse.

But at Kellis High School, not only have they have shown that all kids can interact together and excel, they have perfected a program that is getting attention nationwide.

Kellis is a model school for a program called Unified Sports, in which regular education students “mentor” and assist developmentally delayed students in sports as well as everyday high school life.

“This program is huge here and I have been at other schools,” said Jeff Wooten, principal at Kellis. “Parents of special-needs students are afraid that when they take them to school, they will not get the same educational opportunity. Here, they go through a normal high school day.”

Students in the Kellis program attend five different classes and interact with all students on campus on a daily basis.

“I have had parents say to me, ‘what do you mean they go to five different classes,’ they always stayed in one classroom before,” Wooten said. “Not at Kellis. We look at all the students the same.”

Instructors at Kellis High School have been working with a program that was the early beginnings to the Unified Sports Program.

Eight years ago, teacher Deborah Taylor started working with the special-education students at Kellis. She is credited with getting started on implementing a program that would have regular education students work with and mentor young special-education students.

“(Taylor) really got the ball rolling and really started working with our mildly and moderately delayed children,” said Steve Savoy, Peoria Unified School District (PUSD) Administrator for K-12. “All the teachers saw this great opportunity to partner those students with general-education students.”

Taylor, who is now working at Liberty High School, was honored to hear the praise she received, but feels the program has come a long way in years since she left.

She said, “That is awesome that they would say that, but it was not just me; there were a lot of people helped. They have gone so much further in the past few years than I could have imagined.”

She got the original idea from her time working with Special Olympics.

“I have been involved with Special Olympics for 35 years and when I was at Kellis we had an adaptive physical education program,” Taylor said during a phone interview recently. “That is where the program started because we had regular-education kids come in and help with the special-education kids.”

That program was so successful, the interaction between the kids, that Special Olympics heard and wanted to be a part of it.

"They took a program of ours and have taken it to another level,” said Tim Martin, president of Special Olympics Arizona. “They took it to the pinnacle and it is one of the most looked-at programs in the United States.”

After working with the program for two years, special-education teacher Mike Wakeford saw the future when he and fellow teacher, Amy Kubel, took a soccer team to the Special Needs National in Nebraska.

“We took our unified soccer team to the national event in Nebraska and that was the catapult that flew us in the right direction,” Wakeford said.

In 2010, Kellis got an inquiry from Special Olympics Arizona about what kind of interest they had in using the unified sports style.

Unified sports is a program designed to provide year-round sports training and athletic competition in a variety of Olympic-type sports for children and adults with intellectual disabilities, giving them continuing opportunities to develop physical fitness, demonstrate courage, experience joy, and participate in a sharing of gifts, skills and friendship with their families, other Special Olympics athletes and the community.

“There was an outreach from Special Olympics Arizona and they contacted our district to see if we had interest,” Savoy said. 

In 2011, Kellis went to the PUSD governing board to get approval to add the course into their curriculum for high school kids, which they did, and after months of training their teachers and physical education coaches, the program began teaching acceptance as part of the class.

“Arizona has always been the leader in Project Unify, a program that teaches acceptance and unity, and Kellis is always the example we use,” Martin said. “They have been just amazing in the way they have implemented the program and how it works.”

The mentoring program for the athletes is considered an elective credit class and counts towards graduation for the students.

“The kids get credit for the class like a normal elective,” Wakeford said. “But, it is more than that to the parents, kids and staff here at Kellis.”

The biggest aspect of the program is the interaction and reaction from parents.

“The parents have been amazing and are shocked to see the program and how it works,” Wakeford said. “The parents can’t believe that their kids are having a real high school experience. All this time they have been told their kids can’t do anything and they get here and are excelling.”

They told a story about a parent of an athlete who has received a varsity letter Kellis had produced for the unified sports program athletes. When the parent found out, they purchased a varsity jacket for their athlete and presented it during an assembly.

“It was presented during an assembly and it was the most amazing thing I had seen,” Wakeford said. “I don’t think there was a dry eye in the gym that day.”

As the program grows and expands, the goal continues to be the students, and always will.

“I am just trying to continue to lead this program in the direction that everyone before me has started,” Wakeford said. “This program means everything to me and I kind of feel like I am just continuing to make it the best program in the state and nation.”

Kellis continues to expand and stretch the reach of the program as students interact in everyday situations.

“I love watching the kids interact in between classes,” Wakeford said. “You see the star basketball or football player high-fiving our unified sports athletes and it just warms your heart.”

Asked what continued to keep him coming to work, Wakeford didn’t hesitate when answering.

“I come to work because of these kids,” he said. “They keep me motivated, and this is what I love to do. When you are involved in a program like the one here, you can’t help but love coming to work.”
