Library equipment to aid disabled

  • By Blake Herzog, @BlakeHerzog
  • Jun 20, 2017
  • Jun 20, 2017 Updated Jun 20, 2017
  • Comments

The Yuma County Library District recently won a $26,750 federal grant for investment into technological equipment intended to help patrons with physical and developmental disabilities to develop their abilities and skills.

Hannah Stewart, the district's youth services manager, said the district is moving forward with buying the equipment and should have it available at the Yuma Main Library this fall through the LEADingTechnology Project, for use by up to 23,000 people of all ages during the first year.

"Not only will people with disabilities have access to this technology, we will be offering workshops for parents, caregivers, and special needs educators as well," she said, working with support groups such as the new Autism Parents Network.

Items being bought with the funding include specialized equipment to support verbal, social and mental learning for people with developmental disabilities, through interactive activities.

Examples include an electronic pen for learners with dyslexia, oversized and durable computer screens that can be used by one person with disabilities, and even larger ones which form a tabletop to be used by multiple students.

Stewart says that depending on participation at the Main Branch, the program could be expanded to some of the library district's other seven locations.

The grant award is part of $800,000 from the federal Institute of Library and Museum Services given to the Arizona State Library, Archives and Public Records for distributions to libraries throughout the state.

Stewart said the library has already been successful with another grant-funded initiative, the Touch 'n Learn Project, which has brought iPad Science Stations to all its locations to support early childhood literacy and education.

With the LEADing (Libraries Enabling Assistive Discovery) Technology equipment, the library will have "specialized, assistive technologies that are not currently available to the public anywhere in Southwest Arizona," Stewart said.
