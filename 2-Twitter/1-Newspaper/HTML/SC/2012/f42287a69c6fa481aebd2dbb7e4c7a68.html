Darlington County Humane Society asks for spay/neuter ordinance

  • JIM FAILE
  • Dec 6, 2012
  • Dec 6, 2012 Updated Jul 30, 2020
  • 0
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Print
  • Save

DARLINGTON, S.C. - The Darlington County Humane Society wants Darlington County Council to enact a spay/neuter ordinance to help control stray dog and cat populations in the county and promote responsible pet ownership.

Council members seemed open to the idea and suggested a work session with the humane society possibly later this month or in January.

Kathy McDonald of the Humane Society told council members Monday that such an ordinance is needed to curb the proliferation of unwanted dogs and cats, many of which wind up in the Darlington County Animal Shelter. And despite the best efforts of the shelter volunteers to find new homes for the animals, many still have to be euthanized, she said.

The humane society operates the county-owned shelter under a contract with council, while the county provides a portion of the shelter’s operational funding.

In addition to arranging for adoptions of animals locally, the group also participates in a rescue effort that operates along the East Coast to help save animals from being euthanized.

The group is asking council to work with them to develop an ordinance, McDonald said. “I’m open to anything I can do to work with y’all to develop this,” she said.

Unneutered dogs and cats that are allowed to roam free breed more animals, and pose safety and health risks, McDonald said. They can also damage property, she said.

McDonald presented a model ordinance developed by the American Veterinary Medical Association she said offers a framework for a county ordinance. McDonald said the model ordinance includes three core components necessary for such an ordinance to work: A containment provision, a spay/neuter provision and a registration provision. The county’s existing ordinance already has a containment provision, McDonald said.

She presented figures to support the group’s request. The number of animals taken into the shelter each year has risen steadily each year from around 1,200 in 1998 to 4,331 as of Nov. 30, 2012, she said.

In 2011, 4,040 dogs and cats came through the shelter’s gates. Of those, 1,358 went to rescues, 77 were reclaimed, 616 were adopted and 1,529 were euthanized, McDonald said.

Those numbers put a strain on the shelter’s operating expenses and on the county’s costs, she said.

She said that jurisdictions that have spay/neuter ordinances are seeing their costs come down and pointed to Richland County as one example. There, the number of animals coming into the shelter annually dropped from 13,218 in 2010 to 11,770 in 2012, an 11 percent decline, while the number of dogs and cats that had to be put down dropped by 20 percent, McDonald said. The cost of operating a facility also declined, saving that county $104,256, she said.

A spay/neuter ordinance save on the operating cost of the shelter and save on the cost for an animal control officer, McDonald said. It would also improve Darlington County’s image and improve the quality of life for residents.

County Administrator Dale Surrett said the county’s existing animal shelter committee made up of council members and Humane Society volunteers could begin working on an ordinance now for council to review during the work session.

Surrett also suggested reviewing any existing animal control ordinances of all four of the county’s municipalities – Hartsville, Darlington, Lamar and Society Hill – to ensure that they are not in conflict with any ordinance the county may adopt.
