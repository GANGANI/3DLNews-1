More evidence of hacking threat

  • Dec 24, 2016
  • Dec 24, 2016 Updated Sep 14, 2020
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Print
  • Save

Most of us probably don’t have much WikiLeaks-worthy correspondence in our personal email accounts. But that doesn’t mean those accounts are safe from hacking. Far from it.

Last week was already swimming with headlines about apparent Russian involvement in hacks during the 2016 U.S. election, an egregious attack that deserves a full investigation. Then on Wednesday, Yahoo announced that 1 billion user accounts had been hacked, leaking email addresses, personal information, answers to security questions and other data.

It’s the biggest such hack in history. The previous holder of that dubious record is also Yahoo, which announced in September that info from 500 million accounts had been stolen in 2014.

Even more troubling, both hacks occurred years ago — the 1 billion-account hack reportedly took place in 2013. Three years is just short of eternity in internet-time, meaning millions of Yahoo users were left potentially vulnerable for an entirely unacceptable amount of time.

And while Yahoo ought to be taken to task for its utter failure to protect user data, it’s hardly alone in falling victim to cybercrime.

The U.S. Department of Justice, Internal Revenue Service, Snapchat, LinkedIn, Dropbox and hundreds of other, smaller agencies, organizations and businesses were attacked successfully in 2016 alone.

Just last week the Mount Pleasant Police Department was attacked with ransomware — software that encrypts files until a ransom is paid. Department officials said damage was minimal and no evidence was lost.

Essentially, anything and everything connected to the internet is vulnerable.

Federal and state governments can certainly do more to go after cybercriminals. And regulations on cybersecurity and government technology should be overhauled and streamlined to help keep up with hackers, whose techniques can change much faster than federal bureaucracy.

Expanding the nation’s preparedness for cyberattacks should also be a top priority for the incoming Trump administration.

But ultimately, defending one’s personal information requires personal responsibility.

The most effective defense is the most obvious one. Use strong passwords, vary them from website to website and change them often. Some site and apps also allow phone authentication, which is even more secure.

If you have trouble remembering your passwords, keep them written down with pen and paper since storing passwords online — i.e. in an email — makes them vulnerable to hacking.

Use extreme caution in responding to emails asking for personal information, even those that appear to come from an official source. Avoid downloading anything sent from an email unless you know and trust the sender.

And keep an eye on credit reports to monitor for any attempts to open unauthorized credit cards or other instances of identity fraud.

Unfortunately, as people live more of their lives online and as more and more companies rely on technology to store and organize consumer data, cybercrime is going to pose an increasingly serious problem.

Already the question is not “if” but “when” an individual will become a victim of hacking. For now, the best advice is to be careful and be prepared.
