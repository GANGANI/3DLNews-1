Woodland business recognized for veteran hiring efforts

Tribeca Transport mechanic Chris DeWitt works on a semi truck at the business Nov. 17.

Posted Tuesday, November 22, 2016 10:00 am
By Rick Bannan/rick@thereflector.com

WOODLAND — Tribeca Transport, a business based in Woodland, was recently recognized by the Washington State Employment Security Department (ESD) for its commitment to hiring military veterans, providing jobs for both inactive and active servicemen and women.

A Nov. 9 release from the ESD listed the transportation company among 11 other Washington employers for their work in employing veterans on their staff.

Tribeca Transport was started in 1947 by Don Behrman, a World War II veteran, according to information from the business’ website. Of the 26 full time-equivalent employees, seven are vets, company Chief Operations Officer Eric Thwaites explained. Employees have served in conflicts as far back as Vietnam, with two individuals still active in reserve branches.

Owner Mike Behrman said that when his father started the business virtually all of the employees were veterans. Thwaites said that starting in the 1980s the number of veterans working at the company declined, though now Tribeca is making a push for more vets again, making a point to hit a 40 percent-veteran workforce as a goal.

Thwaites explained that in simplest terms the business’s operation involves taking waste products then converting them to energy through a secondary process, also creating a soil amendment from what is left after the waste has generated the methane fuel. He said that about 42,000 acres of farmland is managed by the resultant material.

Thwaites himself is a veteran having served in the U.S. Army, mentioning that he served specifically during the fall of the Berlin Wall as well as Operation Desert Shield.

Thwaites explained some of the ways that Tribeca caters to veteran employees. He said one aspect was a “transitional phase” where individuals coming off of active duty can get their commercial driver’s license and work at the company while attending school to figure out where they wanted their career path to go. Unlike some transport or trucking companies that hold employees to a contractual obligation to stay with that company, Tribeca allows for veterans who take up a job with their CDL while seeing where their post-military career path will take them.

Thwaites explained that Tribeca recognizes that some of the military skill set doesn’t immediately translate to civilian life, so having a flexible time period for veterans to be working while deciding on a more permanent career path allows for that decision-making. Tuition reimbursement for full-time workers as well as flexibility for active reserve and National Guard members were some of the other ways Tribeca works with vets so they can work with them.

Basic leadership skills, being able to work in diversified situations and problem solving were some of the characteristics that Thwaites said made veterans attractive to business. In his experience veterans are able to adapt to new working environments better than the average employee given their past experience in a structured environment.

“I just think that we get a stand-up guy that is very honest, disciplined,” Behrman remarked about veteran employees.

One of those employees is Chris DeWitt, a mechanic with Tribeca. Previously to taking on the job this year he had served 10 years in the U.S. Marines. DeWitt is still active in the National Guard, and he explained that what he does with that work could potentially be difficult with other employers, but at Tribeca they have been good at working with his schedule.

“Here, with (Thwaites) being a veteran, everyone is just very understanding about it and very willing to work with my National Guard schedule,” DeWitt remarked.

Thwaites said that although the recognition for Tribeca’s push to hire vets has been validation, the company isn’t necessarily doing it for just the accolades.

“This is all nice that we are getting this recognition but it’s something that we have been doing for many years,” Thwaites said. 

“They’re the ones that deserve the recognition more than anybody,” Behrman remarked about the company’s veteran staff.
