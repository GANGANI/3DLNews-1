Lawmakers talk priorities at legislative breakfast

Washington State Rep. Brandon Vick, center, addresses the crowd alongside his colleagues Sen. Ann Rivers, R-Battle Ground, left, and Rep. Liz Pike, R-Ca

Washington State Sen. Lynda Wilson, left, addresses the crowd alongside colleague Vicki Kraft, a fellow Republican from Vancouver, during a legislative breakfast at Warehouse ‘23 on Nov. 8.

Posted Tuesday, December 12, 2017 8:04 am
Rick Bannan/rick@thereflector

Southwest Washington legislators are gearing up for the 2018 session, answering questions in a panel during a legislative breakfast Nov. 8.

The event featured all the legislators from Washington’s 17th, 18th and 49th legislative districts except for Rep. Sharon Wylie, D-Vancouver. The legislative breakfast, a joint effort between the Greater Vancouver Chamber of Commerce, Identity Clark County and the Columbia River Economic Development Council, featured questions from a business perspective, though issues such as the opioid epidemic and enthusiasm on an Interstate bridge replacement also colored the conversation.

Greater Vancouver Chamber of Commerce CEO Jim McDonagh said that the first four questions that he received from the audience all had to do with the 2017 capital budget, a roughly $4 billion spending package that was held up and could not pass before last year’s lengthy session ended

Sen. Annette Cleveland, D-Vancouver, brought up the sticking point in the budget — a permanent fix over legislation regarding water use rights (known as the “Hirst” decision). She said negotiations were ongoing regarding that issue, and expected to see both the Hirst issue and the overall capital budget dealt with in the first week or two of the session.

Rep. Brandon Vick, R-Felida, mentioned a hearing on the issue in the legislature Dec. 12 involving a possible “Hirst fix” bill, though he was not optimistic the particular bill would be the one to get the governor’s signature.

“At least it shows we have a path for discussion going on,” Vick added.

Currently, Clark County isn’t affected by the decision “yet” Vick said, though he implied that the decision could affect the county down the line.

Last year’s House Bill 1509 was also mentioned. It would change graduation credit requirements to allow flexibility for students to take career and technical education classes. Primarily sponsored by Rep. Monica Stonier, D-Vancouver, the bill also had support of Rep. Paul Harris, R-Vancouver, and though it passed out of the House education committee, the bill failed to pass through appropriations by the end of the session.

Harris described required credit reduction more as a way to give students some reprieve, saying that “We make education too difficult for kids.”

“If you struggle in one class and you are on a six-period day, your life will change as a student because you don’t have the opportunity to fill the credits, it becomes very difficult,” Harris said.

Harris was also a proponent of “de-linking” tests to graduation requirements. This session there was a push to do such de-linking, and one bill, Engrossed Substitute House Bill 2224, managed to postpone using a biology end-of-course assessment as a graduation requirement until 2021.

Harris said he would be working toward de-linking all assessments in the next session, highlighting his focus on helping smooth out the complexity of education.

Lynda Wilson, R-Vancouver, served as chair of the higher education committee in 2017. Regarding education, she touched on House Bill 1404, a bill she sponsored that would study the agriculture, environmental and natural resource business sectors to see what educators should focus on to help build those industries’ workforces.

That bill passed into law, with Wilson speaking positively of the work already undertaken.

When asked how higher education can help the workforce, Wilson turned the attention to career and technical education, mentioning she had gone out to see what some of the career and certification-focused institutions were doing. She touted the avenue as a way to have a good-paying job while not going into as much debt as more traditional four-year schools.

For Vick’s response, he brought attention to several people at tables representing construction, utility and other trades.

“These are the folks that are building the things that we need,” Vick said, echoing Wilson’s look at CTE as a major focus for the Legislature regarding higher education.

Stonier also had legislation around students, specifically postsecondary students taking on loan debt. She sponsored House Bill 1440, which would allow for a “student loan bill of rights” to protect borrowers, though the bill did not pass.

Stonier remarked on how AARP, usually associated with retired persons, was backing the bill that did not pass this session. She said that due to mounting student loan debt, it has now started to burden more than just the students.

“We are talking about a generational problem for … lack of access to higher education (that) became a priority bill for AARP,” Stonier said. “We dug ourselves into a real deep hole and we have to start digging (out) faster.”

A question was raised regarding use of cannabis tax revenue to help combat the opioid crisis, mentioning specifically that most cannabis revenues went to the state general fund and not the counties where they could do more with those funds.

“The opioid emergency is not just about marijuana, and marijuana money is not just about taking care of the opioid issue,” Ann Rivers, R-La Center, said, explaining that the percentage of funding was dictated by lawmakers and goes to a variety of sources.

Rivers mentioned recent research regarding the link between cannabis and opioid addiction, explaining that from what she has seen areas with access to cannabis have weaker black markets for heroin, where black markets for both drugs are more prevalent in places with marijuana moratoriums.

“The largest-growing segment of heroin users in our state are shockingly soccer moms that got addicted to opiates,” Rivers said, “but we’ve been shutting down that market so they no longer have ready access. They do, however, have access to heroin.”

Rivers assured she was not trying to push for proliferation of drugs, but said that a solution to addiction epidemics was more nuanced than a single quick fix.

“We as human beings, we want that ‘silver bullet,’ we want to say no illicit drugs anywhere ever,” Rivers said. “But the reality is it’s like a balloon, and when you squeeze one part of that balloon you are going to see a bulging in other places.”

Rivers said that reprioritization of funding for more education on the opioid emergency likely wouldn’t happen in a supplemental budget year like the one coming up.

Legislative Christmas presents

A final question had a holiday spin as the lawmakers were asked what their Christmas wish for the upcoming session was.

Wilson: Bring back the B&O tax decrease on businesses (vetoed in budget this year).

Kraft: “No new taxes” though “realistically” wanted her legislation which would raise the threshold for B&O filers’ tax liability, allowing more small businesses to only file annually.

Harris: Better mental health funding/support.

Cleveland: “I would like a bridge in my stocking.” 

Stonier: Passing of student credit requirement change legislation.

Rivers: Passing of bill regarding pharmaceuticals that “captured” $144 million in savings (2017 bill was vetoed by governor).

Vick: Places like Clark County get more support, not just Seattle metro.

Pike: Get rid of burdensome Washington Administrative Code items.
