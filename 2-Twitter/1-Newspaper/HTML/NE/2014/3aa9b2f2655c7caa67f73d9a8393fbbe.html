Ne Cattlemen - Rural Futures Institute

  • Dec 12, 2014

by LORI POTTER

Kearney Hub

KEARNEY — Chuck Schroeder’s conversations years ago with ranchers who were mentors and community leaders laid the foundation for his work the past year to launch the University of Nebraska-Lincoln’s Rural Futures Institute.

“They taught me the importance of going beyond the borders of your property to make a difference in the world,” Schroeder said Wednesday at the Nebraska Cattlemen annual conference in Kearney.

Schroeder has raised cattle on his family’s ranch near Palisade and served as state ag director, University of Nebraska Foundation executive vice president and director of development, Nebraska Cattlemen’s Beef Association chief executive officer, and president and executive director of the National Cowboy & Western Heritage Museum in Oklahoma City.

Now, as RFI executive director, he’s pulling together resources from UNL and other higher education centers, nonprofit organizations, and government entities to help rural communities.

“UNL should and could, perhaps, be the best place on the planet to influence the trajectory for rural places in the world,” Schroeder said, noting that 95 percent of Nebraska’s 530 towns and villages have populations of 5,000 or less.

The six Great Plains states have 3,000 communities with populations of 3,000 or less.

Rural matters

“We believe that rural matters economically,” he said, not only for the communities, but also for the state and nation. “Capitalism was not built on the back of General Motors. It was built by small businesses, entrepreneurs.”

Rural also matters socially, in the spirit of neighborliness and working together to get things done, and culturally, because people understand their heritage. “People came out here when it still was listed as ‘inhospitable’ on a map,” Schroeder said.

Environmentally, the United States cannot be “green” without rural people who know the importance of sustainable management of natural resources, he added.

Schroeder also noted rural residents’ history of innovation and entrepreneurship. They identify needs and find solutions.

“We can’t save every rural community,” he said, so the RFI staff looks for towns that have “the will” reflected in core leadership.

Legitimate choice

One goal is to ensure rural communities are “a legitimate best choice for young families.”

Schroeder said that in surveys, young people in Nebraska and across the country say they are interested in raising their families in smaller communities if there are good schools, health care and humanities. They also are interested in making a difference through a local school, church, chamber of commerce or other organization.

“I call it a reverse ‘Grapes of Wrath,’” Schroeder said, referring to John Steinbeck’s novel about Great Plains residents fleeing their Dust Bowl-decimated farms and small towns in the 1930s.

Schroeder has spent about half of his RFI year driving his truck around Nebraska and talking to rural people. Their top concern is to have genuine economic opportunities in their communities, he said. In other words, jobs.

“Leadership trumps everything else in a rural community” for innovation, Schroeder said, adding that broadband access is a must. Rural residents also talked to him about immigration, housing, health care and other quality of life issues.

Help offered

“Everything begins with someone in a rural community saying, ‘Here’s an issue. Can you help us?” Schroeder said, explaining that it doesn’t work to rain down programs on people or treat communities as if they are all alike.

RFI projects can include entrepreneurship, leadership and civic engagement training; health-care assistance, such as helping to recruit doctors; work on criminal justice issues; and community planning and development.

The staff is working in Pilger, where 75 percent of the town was destroyed by June tornadoes, to help residents “rethink, reimagine and reinvent” their town, Schroeder said. He has been amazed to see 100-150 people — the pre-storm population was 350 — ages 18 to 80 at every community meeting.

The Rural Community Serviceship project sent teams of two UNL students trained to work with people and start projects to seven Nebraska towns for 10 weeks last summer.

Schroeder described how the Red Cloud pair who worked on a marketing plan for author Willa Cather’s hometown also helped solve what the mayor said was the town’s biggest problem. Locals had tried and failed for years to get a landowner to clean his littered, run-down property at the end of the main street into town.

The students decided to talk to the man and successfully negotiated access to the property. They organized evening work crews to clean the site and paint the house. They sold old tires that littered the place and gave the money to the owner.

Schroeder said leaders in all seven towns had similar stories, and they all asked if their students could return next summer.

The project is funded with a two-year grant, so the goal now is find a way to sustain it.

Schroeder said RFI’s two biggest challenges are building the capacities of rural communities and their leaders to make things better, and building confidence that they can do it.
