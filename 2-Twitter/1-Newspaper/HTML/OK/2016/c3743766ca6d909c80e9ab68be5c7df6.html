UPDATED: Contract revoked on land linked to casino in Enid

Secretary of State document shows manager is Olive Jackson

  • Jessica Miller | Enid News & Eagle

    Jessica Miller | City Reporter

  • Dec 1, 2016
  • Dec 1, 2016
  • 5 min to read

ENID, Okla. — The city of Enid’s attorney says a contract to purchase property near Government Springs South Park is being revoked, but the person claiming to control the property says she never entered into a contract with the city and has it listed with a local real estate agency.

Enid City Commission approved purchasing the property near 8th and Garriott during a Nov. 15 meeting.

City Attorney Andrea Chism said an initial offer on the property was declined by the owner.

“I was later contacted by the president of HD Manning Development Enterprises, who said he wanted to get the property sold and lowered their asking price. While working with him, I was never informed that the company had a real estate agent. The president of HD Manning Development Enterprises signed the agreement on Nov. 2,” she said Wednesday. “Just this afternoon, I received a letter purporting to ‘revoke’ the agreement. Because I only received that today, I’ve not had the opportunity to discuss with the full commission to determine whether they will want to file an action in district court to enforce the contract.”

Broker: Property actively listed

Olive Jackson, who says she is the president of HD Manning Development Enterprises LLC, said she listed the property for sale with Coldwell Banker Realty III in September or October. She also said she manages the checking account and handles all of the LLC’s paperwork in the state. She said she declined an offer on the property from the city of Enid in May.

Jackson, of Henderson, Nev., said she has two living sisters and a brother. She said her brother, John H. Manning Sr., whom City Manager Jerald Gilbert said signed the sales contract, does not have any right to sell the property.

Manning is an inactive partner in that corporation, and owns 15 percent of the property, Jackson said. He has no right to sell the property, and he was told by his attorney he does not have the right to do it. Manning has tried to rescind the contract, in a letter he sent after Nov. 16.

Jackson said the information on the LLC, available from the state, details Manning is not the managing partner and does not have authority to sell the property. An Application for Reinstatement obtained from the Oklahoma Secretary of State shows the manager is Jackson.

Gilbert said Manning, who was understood to be president of HD Manning Development Enterprises, gave a copy of the operating agreement for the LLC, which states Manning had authority to legally transact business on its behalf.

“That’s what we relied upon in moving forward with that contract,” Gilbert said.

The city received an emailed letter from someone named Henry Manning discussing revoking the contract, Gilbert said. Jackson said Henry Manning is her deceased father.

Jackson said if the city wishes to purchase the property, it needs to go to Coldwell Banker.

The property is listed for sale with Coldwell Banker, which has not received a contract from the city of Enid on the property, broker Justin Helterbrand said. He confirmed Jackson is president of HD Manning Development Enterprises.

There is an active listing agreement on the property, Helterbrand said.

“We were not aware of any agreement with the Realtor being involved in that,” Gilbert said.

Chism did not respond to a request to comment.

‘Greed has gotten involved’

Meanwhile, in an emailed statement Wednesday, Ward 6 Commissioner David Vanhooser said Chism had received notification the sellers of the property intend to de fault on the contract to sell the properties to the city of Enid.

“This contract was at a fair full market value. Now greed has gotten involved,” Vanhooser said. “This is exactly why confidentiality in our executive sessions is both necessary and proper. Commissioner (Ben) Ezzell’s inappropriate divulgence of confidential matters discussed, have now led to potentially losing the ability to control what happens in the Government Springs South Park area.

“The primary purpose of purchasing the remaining property in the park was to expand the park itself. However, equally as important, it strategically would have given the city of Enid complete control as to whether any potential development, casino or otherwise, could occur at that location. By the city of Enid owning that additional property, it strategically landlocked the small piece of property purchased by the casino developers, with no room for expansion. The property which the casino developers have purchased is not large enough to place a casino and parking without the acquisition of additional property. By the city purchasing the only remaining park property, it would have given complete control to ‘block’ placement of the casino at that location, should we have decided that was in the best interest of our community.

“As a direct result of Commissioner Ezzell’s actions, we potentially will not be able to block the casino from going on that location, or in another scenario will put us into a bidding war for that property, which could run many hundreds of thousands of dollars over the current contract. That is completely inappropriate, and I place the responsibility for this directly on his violation of the confidentiality of executive session. The honor and trust which developers place in our commission when they divulge confidential details in executive session is part of the routine course of business and economic development, and must be honored. It would appear that the inappropriate actions of one commissioner, have now had exactly the opposite effect he individually intended, due in part to his lack of disclosing fully the facts, and now has possibly created a situation where a casino may in fact occur at that location without any benefit to the city. So once again I say: Shame on you Commissioner Ezzell.”

Ezzell declined to comment.

‘Technically accurate’

In the Nov. 15 public meeting, it was stated the property was being purchased for the expansion of Government Springs South Park.

“All of that property will be added to Government Springs. It’s also commercially zoned, so if the city had the opportunity to do something for economic development purposes, it can do that as well,” Chism said.

Since then, Ezzell, the Ward 3 commissioner, disclosed plans for the property may include a casino.

A discussion on possibly developing the property occurred during an executive session later in the Nov. 15 meeting, he said.

“The bulk of that executive session was spent meeting with a developer, Richard Grellner, affiliated with the (United Keetoowah Band of Cherokee Indians) tribe who is interested in developing a casino on or adjacent to that site,” Ezzell said. “(Commissioners) Rodney Timm and Aaron Brownlee explained during the executive session that they had been working with this developer for several months under a nondisclosure agreement, and that they wanted Mr. Grellner to sit down and explain everything to the entire commission before it went further. Mr. Grellner repeatedly thanked the commission for the purchase of the property, and explained that it would simplify the execution of his development in the future.

“Although there is no proposal in writing, the outline of a deal was discussed by which the city would swap Government Springs South for some amount of money to be invested in a ballpark somewhere else in Enid. The city engineering department, at the request of Aaron Brownlee, had gone so far as to mock up a very rough design showing several soccer, football and softball fields in a future park south of Oakwood Mall/Lowes. In addition, there was discussion of the casino paying the city an offset for sales tax that they are not required to collect. Again, there was no written agreement, but lots of details were discussed, and Mr. Grellner suggested that there may be something concrete to be considered as early as spring 2017.”

Both Brownlee and Timm denied there were plans for economic development involving the property. In a guest column published on the enidnews.com website on Nov. 28, Vanhooser defended Brownlee and Timm’s “technically accurate” statements, stating they were honoring non-disclosure agreements they had signed at the request of the developer.

Ezzell said Grellner ex­­plained “they already have some interest” in an old laundry facility, but he did not explain the nature of that interest. He said Vanhooser also mentioned he had been approached by the developer about potentially selling a nearby medical complex he owns at 9th and Garriott, and Grellner discussed potentially purchasing other privately owned surrounding properties.

Grellner did not return a message seeking comment.

Contacting the UKB

A United Keetoowah Band of Cherokee Indians official, who contacted the Tahlequah Daily Press about an update on an Enid tribal casino project, decided not to make any statements.

Principal Chief Joe Bunch contacted the Tahlequah newspaper Tuesday evening, and though he cited a crowded schedule before the Dec. 5 tribal runoff election, he arranged to speak Wednesday morning. But upon arrival at the publication’s office 25 minutes late, Bunch said he was pressed for time.

“I just wanted to put a name to a face,” Bunch told a Tahlequah reporter.

He left his cellphone number on his way out the door.
