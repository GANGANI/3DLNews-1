Sampling History: Anthropologist, others work toward 2014 opening of La Pointe Krebs museum

  • Updated: Dec. 24, 2013, 4:37 p.m.|
  • Published: Dec. 24, 2013, 3:37 p.m.
By
  • Joanne Anderson

PASCAGOULA, Mississippi --- The gates are locked most of the time at the La Pointe Krebs House and museum, but progress is being made toward outfitting and opening the museum and eventually getting the house itself restored.

Closed since Hurricane Katrina, the museum building has been restored and stands ready to house and display Jackson County's historical artifacts and provide learning opportunities on the area's early history and cultures.

The LPK Foundation has employed a professional who is sorting and identifying hundreds of artifacts owned by the Jackson County Historical and Genealogical Society. These will be on loan to the LPK museum.

Anthropologist Chelsey Landry of Mobile, Ala., has been working on the collection since July.

An avid history buff, she loves her work. "This is my passion and what I always wanted to do," she said. "This is my life. I dream about it. I read about it. I live it."

Melanie Moore, who directs the LPK Foundation museum committee, said, "Chelsey is indispensable."

Landry, who graduated from the University of South Alabama with a degree in anthropology, brings some impressive experience to her work here. She assisted in cataloging and organizing the archival collection of the Poarch Band of Creek Indians and completed an internship at the USS Alabama battleship museum. While a student at USA she worked for the Center for Archaeological Studies.

Her vision for the LPK museum is to have a timeline of Pascagoula's and Jackson County's history focusing on the prehistoric and Colonial eras. "We will have interpretive displays," she said.

To date, Landry said she had selected 350 of 1,000 artifacts that have value and will be kept and cataloged. "The artifacts were in deplorable condition from Hurricane Katrina," she said.

She and Moore have traveled to other museums in the region to get ideas on museum development and exhibit design.

Chelsey wants to get a master's degree in museum study. The daughter of a historian and artist father and a school teacher mother, she grew up looking forward to trips to battlefields, museums and historic sites. "We did not go to Walt Disney World," she said. "My parents instilled in me a love for other cultures and ways of life."

Financial support for her work comes from proceeds from the annual Fete La Pointe Galas sponsored by the JCHGS and LPKHF. The City of Pascagoula and the Jackson County Board of Supervisors give some financial support annually to the Foundation.

The recent announcement of a $266,150 grant from the Mississippi Department of Archives and History for stabilization and restoration of the building envelope of the LPK House has further energized restoration efforts. A match for the grant came from Gala proceeds.

"This means we can begin some much needed repairs," Moore said. "Saving this oldest Colonial building in the Mississippi Valley is very important. It will be an asset to our community and tourism once it is up and going again."

Rose Wittersheim who serves on the Museum Committee and is working on the textile artifact collection, talked of the house's importance.

"I grew up in Jackson County," she said. "The LPK house is irreplaceable. I feel strongly it should be saved. I remember going on field trips there as a child."

Walter Mansfield, a member of the Pascagoula Historic Preservation Commission and the JCHGS, is assisting with the identification of early pottery pieces and other artifacts unearthed during scientific excavation projects over recent years by USA and the University of Southern Mississippi.

"I am looking forward to it becoming open to the public," he said.

Moore hopes the museum will open sometime in 2014. "Every time we unlock the gate, someone wants to come in and take pictures of the house and the cannons," she said.

Money is always an issue. "We need funds to operate the museum, hire a curator, develop educational tools, exhibitions, and maintain facilities," Moore said.
