Hurricane Delta live updates: Reports of power outages, damage begin in Mississippi

Lici Beveridge ,  Giacomo Bologna ,  Keisha Rowe   | Mississippi Clarion Ledger

The Clarion Ledger will have ongoing coverage of Hurricane Delta. We're providing this critical information for free. To support our work, please consider subscribing.

10:45 p.m.: Delta downgraded to Cat 1 storm

The National Weather Service in Jackson said Hurricane Delta has been downgraded to a Category 1 storm as it continues to move inland.

Wind gusts of up to 45 mph continue to be reported in some areas of Louisiana and Mississippi.

Franklin County Emergency Management Director Mark Thornton said while there have been several trees damaged, no people or structures have been harmed by the storm.

"The only pictures I've seen are pictures of timber," he said. "It hasn't been any buildings."

According to a tweet, the National Weather Service in New Orleans is monitoring the heaviest rains associated with the storm, which are currently affecting central and northeastern Louisiana, but could impact southern Mississippi overnight.

— NWS New Orleans (@NWSNewOrleans) October 10, 2020

9:45 p.m.: Thousands now without power in southern Mississippi

More power outages have been reported throughout southern Mississippi as Hurricane Delta continues moving northeast through Louisiana and the Mississippi Valley.

More power outages are expected tonight and into Saturday as Delta brings tropical storm-force winds to the region. Late Friday, the most impacted counties included:

  • Wilkinson: 1,723
  • Amite: 1,612
  • Adams: 318
  • Simpson: 270
  • Jefferson: 200
  • Franklin and Pike: 115

No additional damage has been reported.

7:10 p.m.: Power outages, damage reports begin to come in throughout state

Hundreds of customers in Mississippi are without power Friday evening as Hurricane Delta begins to move inland from the southwestern Louisiana coast.

According to the latest report, more than 700 customers have lost power in Amite County as of 7 p.m. Friday. Nearly 50 customers have also reported power outages in Claiborne County.

In Franklin County, officials are investigating the possibility that a tornado may have come through the area. According to a report from the county's emergency manager, treetops were snapped in the southeastern area of the county near Berry Town Road.

Officials from the National Weather Service and the National Hurricane Center expect more high winds to impact western and central Mississippi throughout the night and into early Saturday morning. Flooding is also expected throughout the region as Delta continues its track toward northern Mississippi

The National Weather Service in New Orleans is reporting Hurricane Delta has made landfall in southwestern Louisiana as a Category 2 storm.

5:20 p.m.: Hurricane-weary Natchez residents preparing for Hurricane Delta landfall

Kevin Long, 38, sat on the porch of the Under-the-Hill Saloon, a bar that overlooks the Mississippi River in Natchez.

Long, an electrician and construction superintendent, typically works on an oil rig in the Gulf of Mexico, but he’s only worked six days in the past month due to storms and hurricanes.

“We’ve been hit pretty hard here in the last month,” Long said.

He said he’s ridden out worse storms on the rig.

One hurricane made the rig lean so far that the milk in his cereal bowl started pouring out, Long recalled. Still, he said he’s taking Hurricane Delta seriously — and he hopes other people are, too.

Long picked up water and canned food Friday and tested his generator. 

“You have some that don’t care,” Long said of the mood at the saloon.

Down the road, a trio of Louisiana residents watched the rain pick up from the front of their hotel.

Peggy Henry, 58, Byron Parsley, 59, and Jordie Carrier, 33, all hail from Eunice, a town in central Louisiana that was expected to be hammered by Delta, so they headed north for shelter. Parsley works as an electrician at a casino where Carrier fixes the slot machines.

This is the second time this year they have evacuated. Last time, they hunkered down in Hattiesburg to avoid Laura.

Carrier said normally they only have to evacuate “once every few years.” Twice in one year is “bizarre,” he said, chalking it up as another oddity in the year 2020. 

Still, Carrier said he was trying to stay positive.

“You gotta make the most of it,” he said.

All three were surprised at the friendliness of the Natchez community.

“Everybody here’s so polite,” Parsley said.

4 p.m.: Hurricane-force winds to enter Mississippi late Friday

The National Hurricane Center anticipates hurricane-force winds will reach southwestern Mississippi Friday night. 

According to an update released at 4 p.m., high winds and rain are expected to impact Adams and Wilkinson counties starting at 8 p.m. Friday. Wind gusts are expected to reach the Jackson metro area around 8 a.m. Saturday.

Heavy rain and river flooding is also expected along the central Gulf Coast and into the Lower Mississippi Valley, the NHC said.

2:30 p.m.: Natchez ready for Delta's arrival

Natchez Mayor Dan Gibson said he doesn't have to "reinvent the wheel" when it comes to handling natural disasters.

The city, which sits on a bluff overlooking the Mississippi River, has had to prepare for a good a number of disasters, he said, so city staff, police and sheriff's departments know what do to before a storm hits.

Gibson said the city is working with Adams County Emergency Management to prepare for Hurricane Delta.

Adams County EOC Director Robert Bradford gives the city routine updates and city officials will be at the EOC and police department command center until Delta's threat has passed.

"Our people know where to go and what to do," Gibson said. "We'll be in the cross hairs between midnight and 4 or 5 in the morning."

Gibson said most businesses except casinos will already be closed by the time Delta passes through.

"We've already alerted the casinos to any oncoming weather so they know to hunker down and keep their patrons there if there’s a tornado or anything like that," he said.

Gibson said he and Bradford have been in touch with other agencies like Entergy and the American Red Cross, which is managing the shelter.

He said, and the city's public works department is ready and well-equipped to keep roadways clear for first-responders.

Bradford said he has been in touch with nursing homes, hospitals and "others in the emergency arena" to make sure they have what they need ahead of the storm.

The Adams County community safe room opened at 9 a.m. and will remain open for the duration of the storm, Bradford said.

The shelter can support around 55 to 60 people with social distancing in effect. If the shelter fills up, another shelter will be opened. Health department officials will be on hand to administer rapid testing to anyone seeking shelter.

1:40 p.m.: Summit residents prepare for storm

Artie Faucett, 63, was filling up gas canisters in the back of his pickup at a gas station in Summit ahead of Hurricane Delta.

“I already got some,” Faucett said. “I’m getting 10 gallons.”

He saw an update of the radar and decided it was time to test his generator, then head to his 89-year-old father’s home and test his.

The potential for strong winds with speeds of 50 or 60 mph were concerning to Faucett.

His father lives “7 miles through the woods” and is not in good health, Faucett said. Only a few weeks have passed since he last checked his father’s generator ahead of a different storm.

“I’ll be glad when hurricane season is over,” Faucett said, noting it’s been “one after another.”

A neighbor who lives in a trailer was unaware of the hurricane brewing until Faucett said he warned him.

“I told him he better get prepared,” Faucett said. “... They call me the mayor in my little neighborhood.”

1:30 p.m.: State Fair to close early Friday, open later Saturday

The Mississippi State Fair will close its gates at 10 p.m. Friday and will not reopen until noon Saturday as a safety precaution for Hurricane Delta, but activities will be allowed to continue "to allow (people) to finish up," said Commissioner of Agriculture and Commerce Andy Gipson during a news briefing on social media.

Although the fairgrounds will be closed, the fair's livestock shows will continue as scheduled, Gipson said.

The coliseum will remain open for anyone camping overnight on the fairgrounds. Social distancing will be enforced, he said.

1 p.m. McComb

As of lunchtime, no resident had come to the Pike County Community Safe Room in McComb, but the shelter was ready for Hurricane Delta — and the pandemic.

There were stacks of water bottles, blankets and coats as well as COVID-19 tests.

Members of the Red Cross, county emergency management, Mississippi Department of Health and Mississippi National Guard were on site, wearing masks, ready to accommodate about 150 people if needed.

SFC William Scott demonstrated one of the rapid coronavirus tests on hand that would allow staff to know whether someone had the virus within 15 minutes. Part of the building was cordoned off as an isolation room for folks who tested positive or refused to take the test.

“We’re taking extra, extra cautions just to make sure everything’s OK,” Scott said.

Pattie Taylor, a 64-year-old volunteer from Enterprise, Alabama, said this was her fifth deployment of the year with the Red Cross.

“I work with some great people,” she said. “Wonderful people.”

— Giacomo Bologna, reporting from McComb

Noon: Vicksburg implements curfew

The city of Vicksburg implemented a curfew that will be in effect from midnight to 7 a.m. Saturday, Mayor George Flaggs said.

"We want to use all precautions in preparation for this bad weather that’s coming through," he said. 

The city also has delcared a state of emergency in case it is needed for damage, power outages and other needs arising from the storm.

"This was a quick preparation," Flaggs said. "We put everything on the table to be ready, but we are hoping and praying (it won't be needed). I would rather under-promise than over-deliver."

10 a.m.: Wind, rain beginning in Mississippi

Hurricane-force winds are being felt about 150 miles out from the radius of Hurricane Delta, National Hurricane Center Director Ken Graham said.

The effects of the hurricane are beginning to be felt in southwestern Louisiana, with "treacherous conditions" and "life-threatening values" arriving later Friday as Delta makes landfall Friday evening.

Graham said motorists should use extra caution if traveling at night since flash flooding is likely as heavy rains pelt western and central Mississippi, especially in areas west of Interstate 55.

In Mississippi, tropical storm warnings remain in effect for Adams, Amite, Claiborne, Franklin, Issaquena, Jefferson, Warren and Wilkinson counties.

Those areas could expect between 4-8 inches of rain with sustained winds of 25 to 35 mph with gusts of up to 55 mph.

Central Mississippi and the Pine Belt, which includes Hattiesburg and the Jackson metro area could see 2-3 inches of rain and sustained winds of 20 to 25 mph with gusts of up to 40 mph through Saturday afternoon.

The hurricane is expected to weaken over land, making its way across northeastern Louisiana Saturday morning, moving into northern Mississippi Saturday night, bringing more damaging winds, flash flooding and the possibility of tornadoes.

8:30 a.m.: 4-8 inches of rain, 35-45 mph winds expected

The National Weather Service in Jackson said areas of flash flooding will become likely and some roads could be flooded or closed later Friday into Saturday as 4 to 8 inches of rain is expected across southwestern, western and central Mississippi.

Some structures potentially could be threatened by high water. Minor river flooding also is possible as Hurricane Delta makes its way across the state. 

Delta could bring sustained winds of 35 to 45 mph with gusts of 50 to 60 mph in some areas Friday night and into Saturday, which could result in downed trees and power lines, leading to widespread power outages.

7:30 a.m.: Southwestern counties under tropical storm warning

Several Mississippi counties remain under a tropical storm warning ahead of Hurricane Delta's anticipated landfall Friday night: Adams, Wilkinson, Amite, Warren, Issaquena, Franklin, Jefferson and Claiborne.

Pike, Walthall, Pearl River and Hancock counties were removed from a tropical storm watch Friday morning. Storm surge warnings for Mississippi also were canceled.

Much of the state remains under wind and flash flood advisories until 1 p.m. Saturday. A coastal flood advisories in effect for Hancock, Harrison and Jackson counties.

6 a.m.: Mississippi begins to feel effects of Delta

Hurricane Delta is heading toward a Louisiana landfall on Friday and will find its way to Mississippi as it moves inland Friday night.

The storm is expected to bring heavy winds, storm surge and strong rain to south Louisiana. The storm was a Category 3 storm with 120 mph winds as of the 4 a.m. update from the National Weather Service.

The storm's path is forecasted to move through Mississippi as it continues on its inland track, with tropical storm-force winds reaching the state as early as Friday afternoon.

A hurricane warning is in effect for High Island Texas to Morgan City Louisiana.

4 a.m. Thursday: Delta remains Cat 3, but weakening is forecasted

As of 4 a.m. Friday, the category 3 storm was located 200 miles south of Cameron, Louisiana, and was moving North at 12 mph with sustained winds of 120 mph.  

The NHC forecast track has narrowed to show a Louisiana landfall. Some weakening is expected as the storm nears the coast. Hurricane force winds extend outward up to 40 miles from the center of the storm and tropical force winds extend up to 160 miles from the center.    

Forecasters say the storm could weaken ahead of landfall.
