Big Al is more than just the man behind the shake-ups

CHARLESTON — More than shake-ups, snow cones and cotton candy are dispensed at the bright yellow Big Al’s food trailers.

That has been the goal since Charleston’s Al Krietemeier started selling shake-ups in 1995.

“We’re selling a product, but we’re really selling service to people,” Krietemeier said. “We try to greet our customers, and we try to make them feel at home because they’re paying pretty good money for what they’re buying.

“I think we’ve got a good product,” he added. “A lot of people say ours is the best. I don’t know that it is, but we do some things that help our product be right at the top.”

One thing that makes visiting Big Al’s different is Krietemeier. He will be sitting on a cooler of ice next to the stand looking for the opportunity to talk to people as they walk through EIU’s Celebration, the IHSA state track meets, the Coles County Fair or any of the other sites in the surrounding counties that Big Al’s visits between March and September.

“He’s just a people person,” said Mattoon’s Chris Walden, who works in vending and carnivals at state fairs in Wisconsin, Minnesota and Texas. “If you go to some of these small events, it’s people that he’s never known before and they know him from visiting with him. Over the years, he’s kind of almost a legend at lot of different places.

“The shake-ups are unbelievable, but he’s a big part of it,” Walden added. “People will come and get a shake-up and visit with him for a while, too. Personality and work ethic is what’s made Al Krietemeier successful with Big Al’s Shake-ups.”

Walden is also Krietemeier’s son-in-law, so he works Big Al’s occasionally when his own carnival business is not taking him all over the Central time zone.

There is an abundance of topics that Krietemeier can discuss with his customers.

“We’ll talk about anything and everything. He has one of those eclectic minds that just soaks up everything,” said Wally Carlson, pastor at Charleston’s Wesley United Methodist Church, where Krietemeier is a member.

Krietemeier, 67, built up a wealth of life experience before starting to specialize in shake-ups when he started Big Al’s, which began its 18th season on March 30.

He was a legendary fast-pitch softball catcher. He was a Methodist minister. He was a teacher for 27 years first at Stewardson-Strasburg, then he left his hometown of Strasburg to spend his final 17 years teaching at Charleston Junior High/Middle School. His years teaching included coaching, and all those experiences brought together have contributed to his success selling shake-ups.

But it was his summer job in the early 1970s that led him most directly to being at a carnival, festival or fair every weekend each summer. Krietemeier worked for Pepsi three consecutive summers from 1972-74.

“Part of my job was we had a wagon and a tent that we loaned out to homecomings and small towns,” he said. “One of my jobs was to go set them up and put the product out there and tear them down. I just liked the people that I worked with. I always thought that someday I’d like to do that.

“I had dreams of running a root beer custard stand when in college, and I never had the money to buy one,” he said. “I was able to get a wagon for a pretty cheap price and basically just built it from there.”

Krietemeier got some help from P.J. Rodgers, whose family runs a french fry trailer. Rodgers occasionally drove the bus for Krietemeier’s basketball team after games. They had a conversation that led to Rodgers finding a reasonably priced food trailer that Krietemeier converted into the first Big Al’s.

Krietemeier now has four trailers, though he does not use them all simultaneously. Coles County restricts the number of days each trailer can be used, so the trailers in use are rotated, Krietemeier said.

“He’s honest and he’s fair and he’s an extremely hard worker,” said Randy Harpster, who coached with Krietemeier for six years at CJHS and taught with him even longer. “He was excellent with putting people in position to succeed. He always put his players in the best position to be successful, and I’m sure he does that with the people that work for him as well.”

There have been close to 85 employees at Big Al’s during the past 18 years, including Lisa Lewis, who has worked for Krietemeier the last 12 years.

“He’s a very caring and giving person. He puts other people before himself,” Lewis says. “He’s a good boss — no, a great boss.”

His lemon, lime, orange and strawberry shake-ups have gained notoriety, but there is much more to the man known as Big Al. It does not take long to find that out. Just ask him, and he is more than glad to talk.

It’s part of the Big Al’s experience.

And he hopes everyone’s experience is positive.

“The most enjoyable thing is meeting people and seeing people and knowing that they’ve walked away with a product they like and that they’re going to enjoy,” Krietemeier said.

DUI case dismissed on improperly obtained evidence

CHARLESTON -- Driving under the influence charges were dismissed when the prosecution agreed that police shouldn't have stopped the man charged.

The traffic stop was what led to the suspicion that Jesse R. White was driving while intoxicated. However, a review of the video recording from a police officer's squad car showed that White went through an intersection on a yellow light, not a red light as accused.

White, 33, for whom court records list an address of 16561 E. County Road 420N, Charleston, was charged with driving under the influence after an Eastern Illinois University police officer made the traffic stop in Charleston on Jan. 14. The DUI charges in the case were felony offenses because White has prior convictions for the offense.

Defense attorney Todd Reardon filed what's called a motion to suppress evidence, claiming the evidence indicating White was intoxicated at the time wasn't obtained properly.

Specifically, the motion said there was no "reasonable suspicion of criminal activity" because White didn't actually commit a traffic violation and shouldn't have been stopped by police. That meant any evidence against White obtained after the stop shouldn't be used in the case against him, the motion contended.

During a court hearing on the motion, Coles County State's Attorney Brian Bower agreed with the motion's contention after he reviewed the squad car video. Circuit Judge Mitchell Shick then granted Bower's motion to dismiss the charges.

"Everything after the stop becomes inadmissible," Bower said, meaning there was no usable evidence of intoxication that then could be used to try to convict White.

The police officer didn't do anything wrong and likely saw the red traffic light just after it changed from yellow, leading to his decision to stop White's vehicle, Bower said. It took the review of the squad car video to show that the light was yellow when White drove past it, he said.

Bower said the decision to agree to the motion and dismiss the case was made by one of his assistant state's attorneys, Jesse Danley. Danley handles traffic cases but wasn't available to attend White's hearing, so Bower filled in and said he supported Danley's decision.

Fisherman rescued on Lake Charleston

CHARLESTON (JG-TC) -- The Coles County Dive Team rescued a fisherman Sunday afternoon after his boat capsized on Lake Charleston.

Emergency responders were dispatched to the scene at 1:26 p.m. Sunday after the fisherman's cries for help were heard by an angler on the causeway, said Charleston Fire Chief Pat Goodwin. The witness could not see where the cries were coming from but called 911 to report them, he said.

Charleston firefighters soon arrived at the scene, Goodwin said. Their arrival was followed by the Coles County Dive Team, which is hosted by the Lincoln Fire Protection District.

Goodwin, who serves with both the dive team and Lincoln fire, said the team members got their boat in the water and saw the fisherman's small sailboat capsized about two-thirds of the way out in the lake. He said they found the man clinging to the back of his boat and rescued him by 1:40 p.m.

The fisherman, who was wearing a life jacket, was suffering from hypothermia as a result of being in the water for approximately half an hour, Goodwin said. The man was taken by ambulance to Sarah Bush Lincoln Health Center for treatment.

Goodwin credited the witness, Seth Allen, calling 911 when he heard the cries for help and the emergency responders' quick response with saving the fisherman's life.

Emergency responders form the Charleston Police Department, Coles County Sheriff's Office, and the Illinois Department of Natural Resources Conservation Police also assisted at the scene.

Casey-Westfield to play with heavy heart

CASEY -- The IHSA Class 1A second-round football playoff game of Casey-Westfield at Bismarck-Henning, which is set for 2 p.m. Saturday, took an unexpected turn Friday; a turn that affects both the Casey and Westfield communities.

Anna (Orrrell) Buenzli, the mother of one of Casey's senior captains, Parker Buenzli, a 6-4, 320-pound lineman, died unexpectedly Friday morning.

While the pep session Friday night was canceled the game is to go on.

"She was one of our biggest supporters," said Casey-Westfield coach Jim Sullivan. "Anna almost single handily raised money for our new football jerseys this past year. She believed in the spirit of Warrior football and the community. Parker's success on and off the field brought her a great amount of joy and pride. The team will go on with the game and play it for Parker and her. I know her spirit will be the with team Saturday. We will miss her. The Casey-Westfield football family will remain strong for Parker in his time of need."

The game is to be broadcast on WHQQ, 98.9 FM and on internet television at www.citylinktv.com/channel/Casey-Westfield.

Casey enters after a thrilling 34-29 victory over Milford/Cissna Park, a team that is in the same conference as Bismark-Henning the Vermilion Valley. 

"I was happy with our resiliency," said Sullivan. "We made mistakes, but we never quit. Isaac Elkins (quarterback) took the team on one of the best drive's in our school's long playoff history."

Elkins, who is 78-of-133 for 1,598 yards and 19 touchdowns, completed three straight passes for a total of 57 yards on the six-play, 59 yard drive that ended with just 32 seconds left on a 12-yard pass to Evan Blankenship.

"We made mistakes that are correctable," said Sullivan whose team finished No. 6 in the final rankings. "I am thankful that we had another week to work on them."

Bismarck-Henning is 8-1 and have won seven straight games. Its only loss was to Paxton-Buckley-Loda, 21-20 in overtime. The Blue Devils beat Milford 31-21 Oct. 17.

"They remind me a lot of Lawrenceville," said Sullivan. 

The winner takes on the victor of the LeRoy vs. Carrollton game. If Casey wins the quarterfinal contest would be at home Nov. 15.

While Casey is making its 14th straight playoff appearance and going for its 12th straight quarterfinal appearance, Bismarck-Henning is making its sixth consecutive appearance, but has not advanced passed the second round. The school is 6-15 in playoff games with four coming under current coach Mark Dodd, who is 42-10 in his sixth year.

The Blue Devils won their first playoff game, 42-0 over Tri-County. B-H is led by two 1,000 yard rushers in Donnie Powell (5-11, 197) and Mo Abbed (5-10, 185). Powell has 159 carries for 1,772 yards and 15 touchdowns, while Abbed has 161 carries for 1,266 yards and 23 touchdowns.  The team averages 391.8 yards rushing and 47.9 yards passing for a total of 439.7 yards per contest.

"Powell and Abbed are great football players," said Sullivan. "We have to improve each week to take another step."

Defensively, the Blue Devils, who average 9.3 points per game compared to 12.9 for the Warriors, play a 44 and a 43 and are very strong and athletic according to Sullivan. 

When asked it it was a good thing Casey's game went down to the wire and if that was an advantage since Bismarck's game didn't  Sullivan said, "Every game is different. You can't really compare one score to another. It is all about match-ups."

The Blue Devils have 99 tackles for a loss and are led by Jackson Darby (6-2, 240) and Jarod Noel (5-11, 190) with 17 and 14 respectively. Noel and Braedon Lappin-Pratt (6-3, 270) average 5.9 and 5.8 tackles per game.

Casey's defense is led by Dylan Lawrence (6-1, 175) and Trey Thompson (6-0, 200) with 5.8 and 5.5 tackles per game respectively. Thompson also leads the ground game offensively with an average of 93.5 yards per game.

Dylan Allen is the Blue Devil quarterback and was also last year during a 9-1 season. Allen is 30-of-66 for 479 yards and eight touchdowns with the coach's son Dylan Dodd (6-0, 185) the top receiver with 15 receptions for 217 yards and four touchdowns.

Cody Hollensbe and Corey Carver (6-5, 215) lead the Warriors in receiving with 27 and 26 catches respectively.

Casey has allowed an average of 175.8 yards per game, while the Blue Devils' lowest rushing total was 285 yards.

The Blue Devils have forced an average of 2.4 turnovers per game.

"If we are going to advance we can't turn the ball over and expect to win," said Sullivan whose team has forced 1.5 turnovers per game. "We must play with effort and do our individual job."

Steeples reportedly involved in fight with deputies at jail

CHARLESTON -- A murder suspect fought with sheriff's deputies at the Coles County jail Thursday morning after he reportedly became upset about having to share his cell block with a black inmate.

Deputies had to use a Taser electroshock weapon to subdue Larry A. Steeples and two officers received minor injuries during the incident, Sheriff Darrell Cox said.

Cox said the incident began when Steeples was in a visitation room meeting with his attorney, Lupita Thompson. Steeples became angry when he told Thompson he was being housed in a cell block with a black inmate and referred to the inmate by a racial epithet before deputies tried to remove him, Cox said.

Steeples, 53, is jailed on charges of first-degree murder and is accused of shooting and killing Gina Giberson at her home in Charleston on April 7.

Cox said the sheriff's office plans to send reports about the incident to the State's Attorney's Office for possible aggravated battery charges against Steeples because of the injuries to the deputies.

He said one deputy received a cut on a finger and another received a small puncture wound during the attempt to subdue Steeples. Steeples was eventually returned to his cell and wasn't injured, Cox said.

Cox also said there are no changes planned in how Steeples is monitored at the jail because he's already classified as a high-risk inmate with corresponding security measures in place.

After deputies secured Steeples following Thursday's incident, they placed electroshock devices on his legs that can be activated remotely if needed to subdue an inmate, Cox said. He said Steeples might have to also wear those when he's taken to the county courthouse for future court appearances.

Steeples is scheduled for hearings on Monday and on Nov. 17, during which an issue concerning his mental fitness for trial will likely be addressed.

In September, Circuit Judge Mitchell Shick granted Thompson's motion requesting that Steeples be examined for his mental fitness for trial.

A report from the expert who conducted the evaluation has to be filed for hearings on the fitness issue to take place. However, the contents of the report, including its conclusion on the fitness issue, are sealed and not open to the public.

Mental fitness for trial addresses whether a defendant understands the charges and can assist with the defense and isn't the same as an insanity defense.

If Steeples is found mentally unfit for trial, he could be held in a facility for treatment until that changes or longer if his condition doesn't change.

However, in September Thompson also received court permission for Steeples to be examined for other mental issues that might affect his case.

At that time, Thompson said one reason she wanted the examination was to try to determine if Steeples might have thought he was acting in self defense when he shot Giberson.

Steeples was located and arrested at Giberson's home at 207 E. Vine Ave., Charleston, after police responded to a 911 call from there, according to police testimony during an earlier hearing in his case.

Steeples told officers he "put five in her," matching the number of gunshot wounds Giberson received, according to the testimony. She died later at Sarah Bush Lincoln Health Center.

At the time, Steeples lived at a nearby residence on Vine Avenue and the shooting marked at least the fourth incident involving him and Giberson reported to police in about a five-month period.

Steeples has been jailed since his arrest and his bond is set at a level that would require $500,000 for him to be released. He could receive up to life in prison if he's convicted.

Woman died of gunshots; house searched

BLOOMINGTON — Bloomington police continued to remain close-lipped Thursday in the case of a businesswoman gunned down in her east-side office.

Pamela Zimmerman, 53, was found after police were called at 7:16 a.m. Tuesday to check on her well-being by a family friend. She was pronounced dead at the scene about an hour later.

Anyone with information is asked to contact Bloomington police Detective Michael Johnson at 309-434-2369 or Detective Tim Power at 309-434-2579.

Police executed a search warrant Wednesday evening at a Normal residence in connection with the case but declined to say what, if anything, was found. The home was not that of the victim.

No one was in custody in connection with the case after the search, police said, adding they could not comment on whether a weapon has been found.

A police squad car and uniformed officer also were stationed Wednesday at the crime scene at Washington Park office complex, formerly known as Doctor's Park, at 2103 E. Washington St.

"The circumstances surrounding Pam's death remain suspicious and the Bloomington Police Department continues to investigate this incident," said Coroner Beth Kimmerling on Wednesday, noting the wounds were not self-inflicted.

"No further specific details will be released as to not jeopardize the pending investigation," she said.

When asked via email whether there is a risk to the public's safety, police spokeswoman Sara Mayer replied, "Everything possible is being done regarding the investigation."

In reply to other emailed questions, she said, "No additional information is available at this time."

Police also did not explain why they waited nine hours after they found the body to issue a news statement saying they were investigating a "suspicious death" at a business at a busy intersection.

Bloomington Police Chief Brendan Heffner did not respond to email requests for comment.

People who knew Zimmerman at the office building where she worked are remembering her as a consummate professional who loved her family, friends and her job as a Certified Public Accountant.

She was the mother of three high school-age children and a successful businesswoman. She was an active member of St. Patrick's Catholic Church and involved in Children's Discovery Museum and St. Jude's Research Hospital.

"She was a very nice person," said Dr. Emil Verban Jr., a dentist in the Washington Park office complex. "Nobody said anything bad about her."

"She was one of the most professional, nicest people on Earth," said his assistant, Hope Perry. "I used her services and she will be missed."

After closing Tuesday when Zimmerman's body was found, Verban's office reopened on Wednesday.

According to her business website, Zimmerman had more than 15 years of experience as a Certified Financial Planner. She attended Maroa-Forsyth High School where she was valedictorian and senior class president, graduating in 1979. She attended Eastern Illinois University in Charleston with a double major in accounting and management. Her hobbies included skiing, biking and volleyball.

Rankin wins Coles County sheriff's race

CHARLESTON -- Jimmy Rankin was a convincing winner in the race for Coles County Sheriff on Tuesday, outdistancing opponent Greg Voudrie by more than 2,000 votes.

Rankin, the county Republican candidate, received 57 percent of Tuesday's vote with a total of 8,450 with the unofficial total. Voudrie, the Democratic candidate, received 6,312.

Rankin is a rural Charleston resident and currently a deputy with the sheriff's office and received much of his support from his hometown area.

Individual precinct totals weren't available Tuesday but Rankin took a large, early lead as Charleston-area precincts reported. The margin narrowed somewhat toward the end of the night when Mattoon precincts reported, adding votes for Voudrie, a Mattoon resident and retired Mattoon police administrator.

"Obviously, Republican support in Coles County goes a long way," Voudrie said after the final results were in. He added that he wished Rankin "the best of luck" in the office.

Ranking couldn't be reached for comment late Tuesday.

Rankin will take office Dec. 1. He will replace current Sheriff Darrell Cox, a two-term Republican who didn't seek re-election to the office.

The sheriff's election was the only contested race in Coles County on Tuesday, though two other county officials and seven Coles County Board members were unopposed and elected.

County Clerk Sue Rennels received 13,096 votes and county Treasurer George Edwards received 12,693; both are Republicans.

The county board members elected included Republican Marc Weber in board District 3, who received 371 votes Tuesday. Weber is a former member who will return to the board after incumbent Democrat Dick Goodrick didn't seek re-election.

Current members, all Republicans, made up the other county board candidates on Tuesday's ballot. They were Cory Sanders, District 4, 1,510 votes; Paul Daily, District 6, 1,248 votes; Nancy Purdy, District 7, 939 votes; Jan Eads, District 9, 845 votes; Stan Metzger, District 10, 903 votes; and Brandon Bell, District 11, 685 votes.

Man gets 20 years in prison for stealing vehicle

CHARLESTON -- A judge Thursday ordered a 20-year prison sentence for a man who attacked another man and stole a pickup truck from the 69-year-old Coles County resident during a run from a shooting in southern Illinois.

John L. Cunningham, 43, received the sentence for what the case's prosecutor called "a complete disregard for any other person" during the crime spree nearly two years ago.

Thursday also marked the end of the case in Coles County in another way as Cunningham's wife, Janet S. Cunningham, 45, pleaded guilty to charges in connection with the truck theft and another vehicle theft and also received prison time.

The Cunninghams lived in Windsor at the time of the incidents. John Cunningham is scheduled for trial in January on charges in Johnson County, where the first reported crime took place.

On Thursday, Circuit Judge James Glenn said he considered the Johnson County crimes and added that he was "not convinced" that Cunningham wouldn't commit more crimes in the future. The judge also said a lengthy prison was needed to protect the public.

Coles County Assistant State's Attorney Tom Bucher asked Glenn to impose the maximum sentence possible, 30 years in prison.

"Mr. Cunningham is a very violent and dangerous man," Bucher said. Noting how Cunningham committed more crimes while on the run after the alleged shooting, Bucher said he showed "a complete disregard for any other person."

Defense attorney Bryan Robbins noted that Cunningham was eligible for probation but admitted that wasn't appropriate and recommended an eight-year prison term.

Robbins said Cunningham has taken responsibility for his actions and asked Glenn to consider that he's still presumed not guilty of the charges in Johnson County.

In a statement to Glenn, Cunningham said he has a "problem reacting without thinking" and he'd "give anything to change what happened."

He also said he would compensate the man for the stolen pickup truck. Glenn, noting Cunningham's finances and the time he'll be in prison, responded by saying, "I don't see how."

In Coles County, Cunningham pleaded guilty in September to a charge of robbery from a victim age 60 or older. He also pleaded guilty to charges of possession of a stolen vehicle for the man's pickup truck and the van taken in Johnson County.

Evidence presented Thursday indicated that on Dec. 3, 2012, John Cunningham shot and injured another man, his wife's cousin, during an argument over whether the man stole items from property Cunningham owned in Goreville in Johnson County.

The Cunninghams then fled the shooting scene but soon wrecked their car, after which they stole a van from the home of a woman after Cunningham threatened to shoot her, the evidence showed.

They then drove the van until the next day when it ran out of gas in a rural Coles County location near Ashmore. After approaching and getting an offer for a ride from the 69-year-old pickup truck owner, Cunningham hit him in the head and the couple stole the truck, according to the evidence.

The Cunninghams were located in Kentucky and arrested there two days after the Johnson County incidents.

Thursday's evidence also included a video recording of Illinois State Police officers' interview with Janet Cunningham shortly after the arrest.

Though her version of the events changed over the course of the questioning, eventually including an admission of more involvement, she claimed she mostly did what her husband forced her to do.

"He's just abusive," she said. "I do what I'm told."

While making his sentencing recommendation, Bucher said the interview showed that Cunningham wasn't only a threat to strangers but was also a "very dangerous wife-beater."

Janet Cunningham pleaded guilty on Thursday to Coles County charges of possession of a stolen vehicle. With the agreement reached in her case, she was sentenced to five years in prison.

The agreement also allows her Coles County sentence to run at the same time as the five-year prison she received recently when she pleaded guilty to charges in Johnson County.
