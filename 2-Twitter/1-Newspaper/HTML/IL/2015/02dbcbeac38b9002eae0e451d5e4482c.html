Duckworth draws big crowd in visit to Champaign

  • By Nicole Lafond nlafond@news-gazette.com
  • Dec 30, 2015
  • Dec 30, 2015 Updated Jun 27, 2019
  • Facebook

  • WhatsApp

CHAMPAIGN — When Tammy Duckworth caught wind of the vast flooding across central Illinois, she wanted to come see the damage for herself.

So off to Champaign it was Tuesday for the U.S. Senate hopeful, who said her office has made considerable efforts to provide federal dollars to help with flood abatement.

"My thoughts and prayers are with the people who are praying their sump pumps don't give out," she said during a noontime stop at downtown Champaign's Cafe Kopi.

Duckworth, who has represented Illinois' 8th district in the U.S. House since 2013, is seeking the Democratic nomination to challenge Republican U.S. Sen. Mark Kirk in November.

She drew a big-name crowd of 50-plus Tuesday, with state Rep. Carol Ammons, D-Urbana, state Treasurer Mike Frerichs, Urbana Mayor Laurel Pressing and Champaign County State's Attorney Julia Rietz among the fellow Democrats who turned out for the meet-and-greet.

Ammons introduced Duckworth and threw her support behind the disabled Iraq war veteran, who will face Andrea Zopp, head of the Chicago Urban League, and football star turned state Sen. Napoleon Harris in March's primary.

"First and foremost, she's a female. We could certainly use more female representatives in the Senate," Ammons said later of her reasons for backing Duckworth. "She's also an experienced public servant, and we really want to share with our community what her vision is. ... Plus, she needs Champaign County to win, so we want Champaign-Urbana to be a critical part of any electoral politics in the state of Illinois."

Speaking to the crowd for a little less than an hour, Duckworth talked about growing up in a low-income household, shared her campaign platforms and took questions from the audience.

"I'm out running for Senate because I believe truly in how great this country is and the chances it gives people who work hard," she said. "The hardest I ever worked was when I was poor, when my family was trying to make ends meet, when my mother was working and my father was working and I was working. But we didn't give up, and America didn't give up on us either."

Other topics touched on:

Health care

The first female double amputee from the Iraq war referenced her own "major health-care crisis," saying: "I know what it's like. I know that so many of our families are just one bad medical diagnosis or one bad accident away from bankruptcy. If I had been injured in a car accident as opposed to while in the military, I don't know that my family could've afforded my medical bills, and I certainly wouldn't be running for office. ... I'm very much committed to supporting health care for everyone.

"We live in a great nation, and we live in a great state, but sometimes, for people in this nation, we don't get to see the benefits of a lifetime of hard work."

Syrian refugees

Politicians need to "stop with the fear mongering," said the Thailand-born Duckworth.

"The fact of the matter is Senator Kirk is doing exactly what the terrorists of ISIS want him to do, which is to react in fear, curl up in the fetal position and reject the humanitarian aid that should be going to Syrian refugees," she said. "We need to remember that the Syrian refugees are victims. These are women and children and victims of torture. ... For us to shut our doors as a nation to people who are victims of butchery goes against the very grain of what our nation is, but more importantly — and I'm speaking purely from a military perspective — it does exactly what the terrorists want.

"The terrorist wants the world to believe that the U.S. hates Muslims and that we are a closed nation that doesn't follow through on our word."

Criminal-justice reform

"We have problems in this country. We have black and brown people who, when they call the police for help, they can't be sure they're going to get the help they need, and that is wrong," she said. "We have to look at serious criminal justice reform. The fact that we have people who get higher drug sentences for minor drug offenses than they do for having illegal weapons is wrong.

"We need better training for the police. We need independent investigators when we have police involved in shootings. We need shorter jail sentences, especially when it comes to mandatory minimums, which really swings the power toward the prosecutor, as opposed to the person being charged."

There's a real need and this is not something that cannot be done overnight."
