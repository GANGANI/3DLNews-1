The Re-Write on Education Spending

  I recently saw an article from www.townhall.com by Kyle Olsen

What Teachers Unions Won't Tell You About School Layoffs

and I couldn't help but give it a rewrite for fact-checking purposes.

Fundamentally, the argument over funding for public education boils down to whether or not you believe that "free" basic education is a worthwhile investment for the betterment of society. Mr Olsen seems to think that a bargain bin teacher and materials in a sardine can of a classroom is just as acceptable, if not preferable, as our existing educational system. I do think that there is a laundry list of problems with the current system in America, but I don't think that de-funding "unnecessary" programs like technology, music/arts, and early childhood or special education is the way to fix it. On the other hand, I don't think it can be fixed by simply throwing money at the problem - generations of trust-fund-babies have shown that method to be counter-intuitive. It takes a combination of dedicated parents and teachers, access to resources, and specialized adaptation of the learning process to the students strengths and weaknesses to give the best educational experience possible - these are things we all know (provided we've read/done the research) how we get there is the big question.

My redux of Kyle Olsen's article follows:

"The media and education establishment’s hair has been on fire over the thousands of layoffs that are occurring in American public schools. They’ve bought into the union line that school funding is in crisis, when in reality, spending is unsustainable."

  The author of this article seems to thing that funding and spending are wholly separate entities. However, it doesn't matter if the cost of education is unsustainable under the current structure because it still costs that much to get a decent education. Sure, you can make cuts (removing music education, physical education, after-school activities, special ed, class sizes, etc). Consider Grandma on a fixed income from retirement - even if her bloodpressure meds are too expensive to continue paying for (in the longterm, aka financially unsustainable) should she just stop taking her medication or continue getting the care she needs for however long she can afford it? Education is just as life or death as any medication on the market (black or white). If you have no education, that means no skills and no ability to support yourself and/or contribute to society. We already have a flailing and failing educational system in the US, but further restricting access to necessary programs and resources by way of de-funding is not the answer. 

"Because of collective bargaining agreements, many school districts’ hands are tied and layoffs are the only option. They can’t save money by changing employee health insurance policies, or obtaining salary freezes or wage concessions, because the unions won’t allow it."

  This is simply not true. For example, in Wisconsin, the Teacher's Union (as well as all other major state unions) had agreed to the necessary salary cuts/freezes, mandatory holidays/furloughs, reduction of benefits, etc, but the state assembly stuck behind Gov Walker and the party-line to destroy the ability of the unions to even come to the table. The Unions said, "we'll take the cuts for the good of the state", Gov Walker said: "la la la, can't hear you! guess we'll have to revoke/rescind all union charters and contracts." Obviously, that's the Cliff's Notes version of the whole discussion, but you can find video and text of the whole thing if you don't believe me. (normally I'd link it, but I"m only familiar with Colorado and federal sites for transcripts)

"The Obama education stimulus package accomplished two things: it temporarily maintained artificially large school employment levels and created the layoff “crisis” that school boards are now grappling with. You see, the stimulus lasted for two years and provided money to keep unnecessary staff on the job. But then the money was cut off and schools could no longer afford to keep extra teachers on the payroll."

Exactly what counts as "unnecessary staff"? The author of the article cleverly avoids giving any explanations or examples on that note & simply assumes that any teacher or other school staff is expendable. According to the chair of the Education Comittee for New York City, the cuts would be: 19% of technology and career teachers, 15% of music teachers, 9% of elementary teachers, and 9% of social studies teachers will be lost due to the current budget plan. These aren't unnecessary staff like paying janitors overtime because some kids put gum on the bottom of the seats of a bus that just has to be pristine. These are real teachers that provide the only education that most children get (as for-profit education swallows up the hopes of the middle and lower-class kids being able to afford college). This basic 12 years of childhood education is what prepares most people for adulthood - not something we can skimp on like eating out less or cancelling premium channels on our tv service. For kids that come from low-income and broken homes, school is often a refuge from a very unpleasant home life and shows them a way to get ahead in life by attaining greater skills through education. http://www.documentcloud.org/documents/205343-nolan-letter.html

"Census figures, first dissected by the Education Intelligence Agency’s Mike Antonucci, show that government school employment rates have been increasing as student enrollment has been decreasing.

“The latest Census Bureau report provides details of the 2008-09 school year, as the nation was in the midst of the recession. That year, 48,238,962 students were enrolled in the U.S. K-12 public education system. That was a decline of 157,114 students from the previous year. They were taught by 3,231,487 teachers (full-time equivalent). That was an increase of 81,426teachers from the previous year.”

No wonder there’s a “crisis” – so many people and so little work, and a lack of tax dollars to keep everyone employed."

It is a small wonder that someone against education would put such a small value on smaller classroom sizes, but decades of scholarly research show that one-on-one education is the best, but the smaller the classroom size the better if you can't get one-on-one. This is why homeschooling is so effective, even when the teaching parent(s) do not have a college degree. When you can focus all your energies on a single student, that student gets the greatest chance to succeed and really learn for a lifetime. When class sizes get too big (as most classrooms are), it becomes a rote memorization exercise rather than teaching children how to learn and be learners for life. Those critical thinking skills and problem solving skills go a very, very long way in both academia and the workforce.

"But that’s strange, because, as Antonucci points out, per pupil spending has continued to rise across the nation.

“Per-pupil spending rose 2.6 percent, and spending on employee compensation (salaries and benefits) rose 2.3 percent. The United States average for per-pupil spending was $10,499, with 25 states spending more than $10,000 per student.”

Those facts are such stubborn things. Unions would have us believe that the resources taxpayers invest in education are not sufficient to maintain quality schools. But their arguments must be taken with a grain of salt. If we need fewer teachers, the role and power of teachers unions will suffer. As Detroit Public Schools Emergency Financial Manager Robert Bobb said, “the greatest resistance (to school reform and budget cuts) comes from the guardians of the status quo who still guard the status quo long after the status quo has lost its status.”

I would certainly hope that the cost of the payroll for educators would at least try to keep pace with the Cost of Living and inflation - thus, there would be a net increase in the amount of money spent on the payroll of educators over time. Perhaps the author thinks that teachers don't have to pay as much for a mortgage on a house, or loan on a car, or for groceries and utilities, as do those who hold jobs within the private sector? Otherwise, how could he suggest it improper to give out COLA's? (Cost Of Living Adjustment) I'm certain that he expects his own salary to increase as his cost of living increases, but what's good for him isn't good for those in the position to train and (partly) rear our future generations apparently.

"Society should be focused on maintaining the necessary number of teachers for today’s student population, instead of keeping a bunch of educators on the public payroll for no particular reason."

His closing statement should read: Society should be focused on maintaining the necessary quality of teachers for today's student population, instead of keeping a bunch of legislators on the public payroll for no particular reason.

America needs to take ownership of not just the deficit that is left to our children and grandchildren, but of how those children and grandchildren are educated and raised to continue on with society. Real problems aren't easy to fix or done quickly, we need to groom a culture of learning that will look for new and creative ways to work with problems and improve upon what has been done in the past.

Lowering our investment in education guarantees us a more moronic future.
