Rehabilitated birds returned to wild

  • By Eric Fleischauer Metro Editor
  • Jul 11, 2018
  • Jul 11, 2018 Updated Jul 11, 2018
  • 0
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Print
  • Save

Two birds that overcame serious injuries because of the efforts of volunteers regained their freedom this week.

A young, red-tailed hawk on Tuesday hesitated in the gloved hand of Limestone County resident Jodie VanBeukering, studying the familiar environment at Wheeler National Wildlife Refuge. Then, its itinerary determined, it rocketed over the nearby field and out of sight.

On Sunday, a barred owl rescued five months ago by Decatur resident Roy Gambino waited impatiently as volunteer Michelle Eddy opened the cardboard box that held it. With no prompting, it burst from the box, flying at first awkwardly and then with more confidence into dense woods near Redstone Arsenal.

Both raptors had completed successful rehabilitation at Alabama Wildlife Center at Oak Mountain State Park, near Birmingham.

“This is goose-bump happiness,” VanBeukering said moments after the hawk’s release. “It’s just a thrill. All of that work from all those people, this combined effort, all came to that one moment of him successfully flying off, and knowing he has a really good chance of survival. It’s a thrill.”

The hawk, stunned and bloody, was carried to the Wheeler visitors’ center after it got hit by a car. A volunteer transported it to the Alabama Wildlife Center, where it underwent intensive treatment culminating in rehabilitation in a flight cage. As with the barred owl, the hawk was released only after it consistently caught live prey in the cage.

Gambino discovered the injured owl as he drove into Redstone Arsenal. He carried it to a veterinarian, who contacted the wildlife center. Staff and volunteers nursed the owl back to health at the center, although it remains blind in one eye as a result of head trauma.

“It’s over so quickly. They fly and they’re gone,” Eddy said after she released the owl. “But it’s the best feeling.”

The volunteers try to always release the birds near where they were found.

“To give them the best chance of survival, we want to bring them back to familiar territory,” Eddy said. “They know where they can find food, water and shelter. Plus, we don’t want to infringe on the territory of other birds.”

VanBeukering said it’s likely the hawk was hunting rodents when it was hit by a car.

“What draws the mice and such to the edges of the road are the things we throw out of the car that we think are biodegradable, like apple cores and leftover food,” she said. “That attracts the mice, and then of course the raptors go after the mice. They’re focused on the mice, not the cars, and many get hit.”

Another common injury comes when birds get tangled in fishing line that’s left in the water rather than being disposed of properly. An owl injured by fishing line is what led VanBeukering to get training at the Alabama Wildlife Center three years ago.

Some friends discovered the owl, apparently dead, hanging from fishing line in a tree. Upon realizing it was alive, they retrieved it and took it to their house. After some quick research, they contacted the Alabama Wildlife Center’s rescue number. Before a center volunteer came to pick up the owl for transport, VanBeukering got to see it.

“I opened up the box to see him and I saw those big, huge, brown, liquidy eyes — and that was it,” she said. “I decided I want to be one of these people. I want to be somebody who does the transport, even if that’s all I can do.”

Success stories like those this week, however, are not the norm. Eddy captured and transported a bald eagle found in February at Swan Creek Wildlife Management Area. The eagle could not fly, but otherwise seemed strong and healthy. Two months later, after causing itself additional injuries while at the Southeastern Raptor Center at Auburn University, it had to be euthanized.

“Even if there’s nothing we can do to help them, I don’t get discouraged,” Eddy said. “And when it does work out, like now, there’s nothing better.”

VanBeukering said it can be frustrating. Capturing the injured birds is usually difficult, and sometimes impossible. Frequently, the injuries are such that rehabilitation is not an option.

“It’s a very low success rate because so many of them are so badly injured,” she said. “When we have days like this when we get to release, it makes every single transport worth it, and makes it worth it for all the many volunteers. It’s a labor of love.”

Raptor hotline

Raptor hotline

Alabama Wildlife Center, located at Oak Mountain State Park near Birmingham, operates a hotline for injured raptors. People who discover an injured bird of prey should call the nonprofit at 205-663-7930, extension 2. The line is monitored seven days a week, from 8 a.m. to 8 p.m.

For more information or to volunteer, go to www.awrc.org.
