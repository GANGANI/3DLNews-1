    Our Greatest Hits: As he retired, Dr. J reflected on his career — and a legacy launched with the Virginia Squires

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on X (Opens in new window)
    By Frank Vehorn and Pilot Online
    PUBLISHED: April 17, 2020 at 11:59 a.m. | UPDATED: April 20, 2020 at 2:28 p.m.

    Editor’s note: As we wait for the sports world to return, we’re occasionally looking back at some of our favorite Virginian-Pilot and Daily Press Sports stories. The following story appeared in The Pilot on April 19, 1987 — the day Julius Erving played his final regular-season basketball game. The Philadelphia 76ers and Dr. J lost in the NBA playoffs’ first round that year to Milwaukee.

    — Jami Frankenberry, sports editor

    —–

    Julius Winfield Erving II arrived in Norfolk to begin his professional basketball career on April 5, 1971, with the nickname of “Dr. J” and an assortment of spectacular dunk shots.

    That magnificent career — at least the regular-season portion of it — ends today when Erving’s Philadelphia 76ers play in Washington.

    Back in 1971, only a few people — his playground friends back on Long Island and a few fans at the University of Massachusetts, where he had spent the three previous seasons without receiving national attention — knew him as Dr. J.

    “I had a friend in high school that I called ‘Professor’ because he wanted to be a lawyer,” explained Erving. “He called me ‘Doctor.’ The nicknames had nothing to do with basketball.”

    Few people knew about Erving’s spectacular ability to dunk the basketball, either. College rules at that time prohibited the jam.

    Thus, the signing of Erving, who would become one of pro basketball’s greatest and most entertaining players, by the Virginia Squires of the old American Basketball Association generated virtually no excitement.

    Earl Foreman, a Washington lawyer who had moved his ABA franchise to Virginia the previous year, lured newsmen to the press conference to announce Erving’s signing by promising “news of national importance.”

    Few were impressed.

    The arrival of Erving was noted in a newspaper column the next day under the headline: “Nothing Earth-Shaking.”

    The “national importance” was that Virginia had signed an undergraduate who did not claim “Hardship.”

    Foreman was criticized by the media for signing “another 6-6 forward” instead of a center, the team’s most apparent need.

    But it did not take Squires coach Al Bianchi long to discover the vast potential of Erving, whose large hands (measuring 12 inches from wrist to fingertip) enabled him to hold the basketball like others might a large grapefruit.

    “What struck me most about Julie when I first met him was his maturity, and his huge hands. They were the hands of most great players that I’ve seen through the years,” said Bianchi, now an assistant coach for the Phoenix Suns.

    “I had no idea he would be so polished,” Bianchi had said after the team’s rookie camp in early June.

    “This kid had all the qualities to become a superstar,” added John Kerr, the Squires’ vice president and a former NBA center.

    As for Erving, he never had any doubts.

    “Maybe the people in Virginia hadn’t heard about me,” he said. “That’s because the basketball program at UMass was not that big and our schedule pretty much was confined to New England. But I never doubted that I could play. I knew pretty much what I could do.”

    What Erving could do convinced Bianchi to trade all-star forward George Carter shortly after preseason camp opened in September.

    Carter was the second-leading scorer for the Squires the previous season, when they won the regular-season title in the ABA East.

    “If Julius can do as well as we think, he’s got to play,” explained Bianchi.

    Eight days later, Erving proved he could play better than anyone dreamed.

    The Squires played their first preseason game in Petersburg against Kentucky, which featured 7-2 rookie center Artis Gilmore.

    Bianchi was eager to see how other teams would try to defend against Erving’s quickness and leaping ability.

    “I imagine other clubs at first will try to match a tall forward with Julius,” said Bianchi. “But there is not a forward in the league quick enough to stay with him.

    “When someone tries to play smaller, quicker players against Julius, he is going to jump right over them.”

    In that first exhibition game, Erving became an instant hit with the fans.

    He thrilled them in pregame warmups with acrobatic, one-handed dunks. The show didn’t end when the game began, either.

    After missing his first shot as a pro, he came back a minute later to leap over Gilmore for his first field goal.

    By the third period, when he helped the Squires wipe out a 13-point Kentucky lead, Erving was flashing over-the-head bank shots, soaring one-handed dunks and a few jump shots.

    Playing all but three minutes of the game, Erving scored 30 points and claimed a game-high 13 rebounds.

    “I had heard he (Erving) was good, but I didn’t know he was that good,” said Colonels veteran forward Cincy Powell. “I’d hate to play him when he gets a couple years of experience.”

    Meanwhile, Erving showed little emotion about his spectacular debut, reflecting a quiet professionalism that would mark his career.

    “I need to improve on defense, and a couple of times I should have pulled up and shot instead of going for the basket.” he said.

    But, clearly, he enjoyed the excitement he had generated among the fans.

    “They were just reflex moves,” he said about his flamboyant dunk shots.

    “I was aware of the crowd’s reaction. I like to hear them react like that because that means they are enjoying life and appreciate the way we are playing.”

    By the time Erving returned to his native Long Island, when the Squires played the New York Nets at the old Island Garden on Nov. 13, he was being called, “Perhaps the best rookie in pro basketball since Lew Alcindor (Kareem Abdul-Jabbar).”

    The cheerleaders from Erving’s Roosevelt High School attended the game in uniform to cheer for the Squires. More than 50 fans came from the University of Massachusetts to watch the game.

    Erving, as usual, did not disappoint.

    He scored 29 points and had 17 rebounds as the Squires defeated the Nets 127-123.

    “It meant a lot to win here,” said Erving. “But I wasn’t nervous. I slept all day trying to get mentally ready for the game.”

    As productive as Erving was, he was not the Squires’ big star.

    Former University of North Carolina guard Charlie Scott, who had shared ABA Rookie of the Year honors the season before, was top gun.

    It was not unusual for Scott and Erving to score more than half of the team’s total points, as they did in a 124-120 victory over the Carolina Cougars on Dec. 30, 1971.

    In that game Erving scored 33 points and Scott 31. The previous night, Scott had scored 50 points and Erving 28.

    Scott and Erving made the East All-Star team, which defeated the West 142-115 on Jan. 30, 1972. Scott and Erving each scored 20 points.

    As bright as the Squires’ future seemed in January, with a pair of young superstars, the foundation began to crack only a short time later.

    Scott jumped the team to sign with Phoenix of the rival NBA in March.

    Erving a month later signed what was first said to be a “future” contract with the Atlanta Hawks of the NBA.

    Erving had signed for a $500,000 contract with the Squires, but much of the money was not immediately accessible to him.

    Foreman declined to renegotiate the contract.

    Meanwhile, Atlanta was offering a $250,000 bonus if Erving would sign a $2 million contract.

    “Honestly, I couldn’t even visualize that kind of money,” Erving said at the time.

    “I’m not saying that I’m worth that much money. But I know the market and what other players are getting. I know what the fans want to see and I know my own capabilities.”

    Erving said he and Foreman could have settled their differences “in one afternoon.”

    Instead, Foreman even declined to personally guarantee the terms of Erving’s contract, saying that his guarantee was “not worth a pile of dirty jock straps.”

    It was that statement that convinced Erving that the future was not bright for him in Virginia.

    “After hearing that, and knowing what other teams were offering me, I decided my future was elsewhere,” he said.

    Word that Erving had signed a second pro contract was leaked on April 12, 1972, as the Squires prepared to meet New York in the championship round of the NBA East Division playoffs.

    Erving averaged 37 points per game as the Squires defeated the Floridians in the first round of the playoffs.

    “I’m shocked,” said Erving’s teammate George Irvine.

    So were other members of the team.

    Erving insisted that he would complete the remaining three years of his Squires contract before honoring his newest contract with Atlanta.

    He also promised that his signing would not disrupt the team’s effort in the playoffs.

    On both counts, he was wrong.

    “I don’t appreciate the signing being released at this time,” said Bianchi, “and I’ve told Julius that.”

    New York defeated the Squires in the playoffs.

    During the summer, Erving was convinced by his agent, Irvin Wiener, to establish residence in Atlanta and to jump his Virginia contract.

    Erving filed suit asking that his Virginia contract be terminated because he claimed he was not represented properly when he signed it.

    Reporting to the Hawks’ training camp in September, Erving also touched off a bitter dispute inside the NBA.

    Erving had been drafted by the Milwaukee Bucks, who claimed NBA rights to him after he had fulfilled his Virginia contract.

    NBA commissioner Walter Kennedy sided with Milwaukee and fined Atlanta $50,000 for playing Erving in three exhibition games.

    “I enjoyed the time I spent with the Hawks,” Erving said recently. “I was playing with two superstars, Lou Hudson and Pete Maravich. It was great.”

    Erving, though, didn’t burn any bridges in Virginia.

    He said his dispute with Foreman was strictly financial and that otherwise he enjoyed playing for the Squires.

    Thus, when the courts ruled that Erving could only play in Virginia, he returned on Oct. 20, and there was no animosity between him and his teammates or Virginia fans.

    Erving’s contract dispute with Foreman was to be decided later by a special arbitrator, according to the court ruling.

    The Squires already were four games into the season when Dr. J returned, and they had not been able to win without their young superstar.

    Erving rejoined the team in New York and, without a single practice session, scored 26 points to lead a 130-120 victory over the Nets in Uniondale.

    “I’ve never disliked playing with this team in Virginia or for this coach (Bianchi),” Erving said after the game.

    “My problem was elsewhere and could have been eliminated, but wasn’t.”

    Erving was rewarded with a new, richer contract upon his return.

    It still wasn’t enough for the financially troubled Squires to keep their superstar from wanting more, or looking elsewhere.

    Erving played the remainder of the 1972-73 season with the Squires without further controversy.

    The Squires, however, were rebuilding, following the retirements of veterans Doug Moe, Ray Scott and Adrian Smith.

    Newcomers included guards Mike Barr and David Twardzik and forward William Franklin, all of whom were selected to complement Erving’s talents.

    It was Dr. J’s show, but a season in which he was bothered by injuries and received very little scoring support from his teammates.

    Other teams quickly realized that stopping Erving meant stopping the Squires.

    At times opponents assigned two or three players to Erving, who still managed to average 30 points.

    In a team meeting before a Dec. 7 game against Indiana, the Squires decided that too much was being asked of Erving, and that others should contribute more to scoring.

    At halftime of the game in Richmond, the Squires had three players in double figures, including Erving with 10.

    The more balanced Virginia attack prompted Indiana to relax its sagging defense around Erving and to pay attention to the other players in the second half.

    With only one player covering him, Dr. J scored 24 points in 12 minutes and the Squires went on to a 132-129 victory. Erving had 41 points, 31 in the second half.

    “We feel we should be giving the Doc more support,” said Barr. “But heck, when he plays like he did in the second half, it seems he doesn’t need anyone else.”

    Later in the season, leading the league with a 31.7 scoring average, Erving tried to make others more involved in the scoring.

    By that time, the Squires had added another budding superstar, George Gervin.

    In a game at Scope against the Dallas Chaparrals on March 7, Erving told Gervin that “I’m not going to shoot; I’ll set you up for shots.”

    As some 6,000 fans wondered what was happening, Erving took only six shots in the first half.

    In the fourth period, with the score close, Erving decided that enough was enough. He tallied nine consecutive points to stash away the victory.

    “They (Dallas) had started to come back on us, and I thought I needed to get a little rally going,” said Erving, who finished with 29 points.

    The Squires, winless in four season-opening games without the Doctor, learned how much they missed him again at the end of the regular ’72-73 season.

    Erving, who had injured his back and ribs during a game against Carolina in mid-March, missed the final eight games of the regular season. The Squires won only two of the games without the Doctor.

    In one of the victories, Erving was put in charge of coaching the team while Bianchi scouted Kentucky, the team Virginia would meet in the playoffs.

    In Erving’s coaching debut, the Squires beat New York 121-106, an important victory in that it came in the final game of the regular season and prevented the Squires from having a losing record.

    “When I used to officiate kid games, they called me the ‘phantom official’ because I’d just let them play and not blow the whistle,” said Erving.

    “I had intended to be the ‘phantom coach,’ but as the game got going, I got more involved.

    “I could see things taking place out there and I pointed them out. I did much more talking and hollering than I do when I’m playing.

    “But I don’t know about this coaching. It was fun. But if we had lost, it might have felt different.”

    When someone complimented Erving for his timely substitutions, he grinned, “I was just putting in the people fans behind me were hollering for,” he explained.

    The Squires finished the regular season at 42-42, 15 games behind East champion Carolina. They were 2-10 in games played without Erving, 40-32 with him.

    Erving returned for the playoffs, but he was not on top of his game.

    When the Squires dropped behind 3-1 in the best-of-seven series, Erving hit only 8 of 21 shots and scored 20 points, a dozen below his average, in the 108-90 defeat.

    The Squires were eliminated from the playoffs 114-113 in the next game, which would be the last time Erving would wear a Virginia uniform.

    The Doctor scored 31 points, but only eight in the final half when Kentucky rushed from 13 points behind to win on its home court.

    For most of the season, Erving had played with a pulled stomach muscle. Then came the badly bruised ribs near the end of the regular season, and during the playoffs he pulled a ligament in his right ankle.

    “I tried to push myself out there (on the court),” Erving said. “But they (the Colonels) would catch me when I tried to break away. They couldn’t do that when I was well.”

    When the season ended, Erving talked as if he would be back for a third year with the Squires, although an arbitrator had yet to decide the legality of his Squires contract.

    “If the court says the contract is valid, I’ll be back with the Squires for at least two more years,” Erving said.

    “I think next year we (Squires) should change some things on the team, and I’ll give my suggestions to Coach (Bianchi).”

    But the biggest change on the team was that Erving would not return. On July 31, 1973, Foreman completed a complex transaction that sent Erving to the New York Nets.

    Foreman said he received “in excess of $1 million” for Erving, who signed a contract with the Nets that was worth $2.1 million.

    Nets owner Roy Boe also purchased the contract that Erving had signed with Atlanta.

    Erving left the Squires with more vocal regret than excitement.

    “My parents are a lot happier than I am, because their boy is going to be playing at home (on Long Island),” said Erving.

    “But it’s not that emotional for me. I’m leaving a lot of good friends in Virginia, and the players and the coach I spent two years with.

    “I am getting something good, with this contract, but I am leaving something that also was good.”

    Erving also was leaving his opportunity to return to Atlanta in two years, the saddest part of the deal for him.

    “I liked the Atlanta team and I thought the city offered a young black man a lot of opportunities,” Erving recalled recently. “I would have enjoyed playing there.”

    Foreman remembered telling Erving when the deal with New York was struck that “I hope you will be happy.”

    He said Erving only replied, “What is happiness?”

    Foreman said he decided to sell Erving to the Nets because Erving was demanding that his salary be doubled.

    “He said if his salary was not doubled that he would sit out the next two years and then play in Atlanta,” claimed Foreman. “It was legalized blackmail.”

    Erving denied he ever made such a threat.

    “I would have stayed in Virginia for the last two years of my contract,” he said. “No way would I have sat out for two years.”

    Foreman later admitted he had been “told that by Julius’ agent, and not by Julius because Julius would not discuss business with me.”

    Foreman insisted that he offered to double Erving’s original salary, but that the deal was not accepted.

    “Julius would have made $235,000 and $280,000 if he had played out his final two years,” Foreman said. “That was more than twice the terms of his original contract.”

    Foreman also said money was needed to keep the financially troubled franchise afloat, and that the deal ensured that Erving would remain in the ABA.

    Foreman seriously considered folding the Virginia franchise, because he was fearful that fans no longer would support a team without Erving.

    On that count, he was correct.

    With fan support dwindling, Foreman had to sell Gervin and Swen Nater, the latest rookie star, to finish the 1973-74 season.

    The Squires ended that season with a 28-56 record while Erving was leading the Nets to the ABA championship and leading the league in scoring with a 27.3 average.

    Foreman sold the Squires at season’s end to local investors, who lasted only two years before they too surrendered, folding the franchise shortly before the merger with the NBA.

    Ironically, when the merger came, Nets owner Boe was in financial trouble, too, having failed to recover from the price he had to pay the Squires and the Hawks to obtain Erving.

    Boe sold Erving to the Philadelphia 76ers for $3 million before the Nets played their first game as a member of the NBA, and New York fans never forgave him.
