Portland committee debates allowing non-U.S. citizens to vote in local elections

by WGME

Tue, December 21st 2021 at 5:38 PM
Updated Tue, December 21st 2021 at 5:41 PM
Man voting (WGME)

    TOPICS:

    Maine,United StatesPortlandUnited StatesBilly Bob FaulkinghamBeth StickneyPat WashburnCasco BayCities In The United States

PORTLAND (WGME) -- Should Portland residents be able to vote in local elections even if they’re not a U.S. citizen? That’s the question up for debate Tuesday night in front of a Charter Commission Committee with the public set to weigh in.

The proposal would mean that asylum seekers, permanent residents, and other immigrants could vote in city elections. Proponents say the measure would give all Portland residents a voice.

"It's a way to include new Mainers in the process,” said Pat Washburn, at-large member of the Portland Charter Commission.

  • Also read: 'I can't afford to pay': Daycare dilemma over Portland hazard pay

Washburn drafted the proposal going before the Charter Commission's Election Committee.

“It's important to me because I believe in democracy and justice,” Washburn said.

Maine's constitution currently allows municipalities like Portland to let non-citizens vote in local elections.

But not everyone supports it.

“No United States citizen should ever have their vote canceled out by a non-citizen,” said Rep. Billy Bob Faulkingham, (R)-Winter Harbor.

Faulkingham says he's offered bills twice to change the constitution.

“We just can't get anywhere near the support to pass this. You're going to keep seeing this pop up until we put an end to it,” Faulkingham said,

Maine Business Immigration Coalition Executive Director Beth Stickney is generally in favor of these laws, but she says there have to be precautions.

  • Also read: 'It doesn't taste right': Some Wiscasset residents concerned to drink tap water

“If they were mistakenly handed a ballot for state and federal elections and were to vote, that makes a person deportable,” Stickney said.

She believes there should be repeated warnings to potential voters in multiple languages. There's also the worry that voter lists could be used by immigration officials.

“I just think they need to make sure they do it properly if they're going to do it,” Stickney said.

Washburn says she's put in some safeguards, but if this moves forward, some residents will have to decide if they want to risk it.

“But at least the vote will be available,” Washburn said.

The public hearing will start at 6 p.m. on Tuesday via Zoom.
