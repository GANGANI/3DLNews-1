Cine trascendente: 'Roma' is an exquisite Mexican drama

by Ryan Painter

Fri, December 14th 2018 at 8:39 AM
Updated Fri, December 14th 2018 at 12:35 PM

“Roma”
5 out of 5 Stars
Director: Alfonso Cuarón
Writer: Alfonso Cuarón
Starring: Yalitza Aparicio, Marina de Tavira, Diego Cortina Autrey
Genre: Drama, Biography
Rated: R for graphic nudity, some disturbing images, and language

SALT LAKE CITY (KUTV) - Synopsis: A year in the life of a middle-class family through the eyes of one of their maids.

Review: I didn’t know anything about Mexico in the ‘70s. It’s not like they teach us these things in American class rooms. I still don’t know a lot about Mexican history, but because of “Roma” I have a greater sense of the country’s economic divide and the Corpus Christi massacre of June 10, 1971.

I was there watching these things through the innocent gaze of a child. They weren’t significant in the moment, not to a little boy who would not understand the weight of poverty or El Halconazo. Meaning would come with age.

The first time I watched “Roma” I was overwhelmed. The film features the most immersive sound design that I’ve ever experienced. You can easily just close your eyes and get lost in the sounds of the domestic discontent, the waves as they hit the shore and the stirring of the old theater where lovers escape from the afternoon heat.

However, to do so would be to close your eyes to some of the most beautiful black and white cinematography. It shouldn’t feel inviting, the lack of color in modern films is often a barrier that keeps the audience at bay. It is a device used to create a sense of distance. Its use in “Roma” has the opposite effect. It lures you in and before you know it you’re head over heels and down into the rabbit hole of the Roma district of Mexico City.

These are the streets where Cuarón grew up, the home he came of age in. It is the broken marriage, the motherly presence of the hired help, the dog feces that stained the enclosed driveway that the family’s Ford Galaxy could barely squeeze into.

Then there are the political aspects of the film, which are all subtext. The social struggle, the protests and the deaths.

The second time I saw the film I didn’t worry about the things I didn’t know; it was likely that the children didn’t know any more than I did. To them, it was just background noise. Having shed the obligation of understanding everything, I enjoyed the film even more.

In writing this review, I’m reminded of how I felt when I watched “Cinema Paradiso” for the first time. The whirling sound of a projector (never mind that I probably watched Giuseppe Tornator’s ode to movies and love on VHS in my bedroom) and the flickering of light against the screen. “Roma” is a movie that begs to be seen in a theater because it is made by a person who clearly loves the widescreen medium as much as the devoted audiences that fill the art house theaters to watch “The Bicycle Thief,” “Canoa” and “Zombi 2.”

Netflix knows this, believes this. That’s why “Roma” is getting a limited theatrical release along with debuting on the streaming service. Seeing the film in a theater will provide the best experience, but getting to see the film at all is more important. A few weeks ago, I attended a screening where lead actress Yalitza Aparicio explained that few, if any, from her town would be able to see the film if it wasn’t shown on Netflix because they would have to travel hundreds of miles just to find a theater that would be showing it.
