Ohio to receive over 52,000 fewer Pfizer vaccines next week than originally planned

by Matthew Thomas

Fri, December 18th 2020 at 1:21 PM
Updated Sat, December 19th 2020 at 12:50 PM
A second round of COVID-19 vaccines were administered at the Ohio State Wexner Medical Center on Dec. 15, 2020. (WSYX/WTTE)

    TOPICS:

    AmatoHealth Medical PharmaMODERNA, INC.Mike DeWineOhio,United StatesPFIZER INC.PfizerThe Associated Press

COLUMBUS, Ohio (WSYX/WTTE) — Ohio will receive fewer Pfizer COVID-19 vaccines in its second shipment than was previously estimated, the Ohio Department of Health (ODH) confirms.

Coronavirus vaccines began rolling out this week to health care workers and nursing homes. The next shipment of Pfizer COVID-19 vaccines will be much lower than expected.

MORE: Ohio governor meets with US surgeon general after 2nd COVID-19 vaccine is approved

Ohio was supposed to receive 123,000 additional doses next week. Now the state expects 70,200 doses, according to Melanie Amato, press secretary for the Ohio Department of Health. That is a nearly 43% decrease in doses.

The decrease was previously reported by the Columbus Dispatch.

Amato said Ohio received its first shipment in full.

Earlier this week, Ohio Governor Mike DeWine said he expects to receive 123,000 Pfizer vaccines and 201,900 Moderna vaccines for a total of more than 420,000 vaccines by Christmas. That number is now unlikely.

The news comes as other states and governors reported similar, unexplained decreases.

At least 13 other states reported between 20% and 40% decreases in next week's Pfizer shipment. Two senior Trump administration officials told the Associated Press that the decrease is a misunderstanding and that states will receive their full allocations.

One official told the AP that the initial numbers of available doses that were provided to states were projections, not fixed allocations.|

Both officials said the federal government made changes to the delivery schedule at the request of governors to space out the shipments and make administering the vaccines more manageable.

"They will get their weekly allocation, it just won't come to them on one day," one official said.

Later on Saturday, the Army general in charge of getting COVID-19 vaccines across the United States apologized for “miscommunication” with states.

Pfizer said it has not had any production issues and is delivering vaccines as ordered by the federal government.

The Moderna vaccine is expected to get the green light from the Food and Drug Administration soon. Once emergency-use authorization is granted, vaccinations could begin as early as Monday.
