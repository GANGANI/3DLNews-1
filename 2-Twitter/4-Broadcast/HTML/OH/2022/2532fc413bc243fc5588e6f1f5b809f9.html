'Bring it on' says history-making Chief Justice Maureen O'Connor to critics

by Darrel Rowland

Tue, December 27th 2022 at 5:39 PM
Updated Tue, December 27th 2022 at 5:55 PM
13
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
Maureen O'Connor, Ohio's first woman chief justice, is clapped out by court staffers lining the building's Grand Concourse after hearing her final case. (Ohio Supreme Court)

COLUMBUS, Ohio (WSYX) — Justice Jennifer Brunner recalls the moment clearly.

She was sitting in the front row alongside Chief Justice Maureen O’Connor at a Statehouse event last summer when a Republican state legislator from Cincinnati who was unhappy with O’Connor’s rulings in the bitter court battle over redistricting took the mic.

"Representative Bill Seitz talked about the possibility of impeachment of the chief justice," Brunner told WSYX ABC 6 On Your Side.

"She crossed her arms and under her breath she said, 'Bring it on.' I don’t think it bothered her a bit"

Bring it on.

Love her or despise her, that’s Maureen O’Connor — whether it’s swimming with Great White Sharks off the coast of South Africa or swimming upstream against her fellow Republicans who harshly condemned several of her rulings and even removed her portrait from party headquarters.

Former Ohio Gov. John Kasich, not exactly Mr. Popularity among the Ohio GOP himself, admires her nonpartisan approach.

"She was clearly not a partisan, right? And she’s highly intelligent," said Kasich, who was sworn into office by O’Connor about 12 years ago.

"I liked her a lot because I always knew where she was. You know politicians always try to weave and bob and all that. She was never that way. She was straight at you. I had great respect for her," he said.

The controversies of the past couple of years are putting an unusual exclamation point to the career of perhaps the most accomplished woman in Ohio political history.

O’Connor, 71, forced to step down after reaching the court’s mandatory retirement age of 70, became the first female chief justice in state history in 2011. When she joined the court in 2003, it became majority female for the first time.

She was the first woman to serve as Summit County prosecutor and the second woman lieutenant governor. She was also the first woman to head the state department of public safety, leading Ohio’s homeland security after the 9/11 attacks.

"Every job I’ve had, I’ve just been so lucky, so blessed to have the positions that I’ve had over the years. And I think that I left every office that I’ve had better than what it was when I found it," she said in a one-on-one interview with 6 On Your Side in the Supreme Court's law library.

"That gives me a lot of satisfaction because that was my goal. That’s public service. That’s how you serve the public."

Former Gov. Bob Taft: 'She is fearless'

In all, O’Connor has served in statewide office longer than any woman since Ohio became a state in 1803.

Former Gov. Bob Taft started that historic run when he chose her as his running mate in 1998, and later watched her performance on the state’s highest court.

"First of all, the headline is (that she's) first woman to serve as chief justice, and she was a remarkable chief justice," said Taft, who now teaches at the University of Dayton.

"She is fearless. ... She really puts the interests of the public, what’s right for the people of Ohio, ahead of partisan politics, ahead of petty concerns. And just tries to call them as she sees them."

Former Justice Paul E. Pfeifer, who administered the oath of office to the new chief justice more than a decade ago, said O’Connor’s long-term legacy is assured — despite what he calls the recent “bare knuckles” political brawls.

"Right now, as she leaves, Democrats in the state love her and the Republicans claim they don’t even know how she got there," said Pfeifer, who is now executive director of the Ohio Judicial Conference after 24 years on the court.

"But that’s near-term legacy, not long-term."

'I don’t concentrate on the fact I am a historical person. I know I am'

The final tussle came over where to put O’Connor’s official portrait. The state commission that decides such issues voted unanimously to locate it with those of other historical figures in the Supreme Court building’s Grand Concourse — the large main hallway just outside the courtroom.

But the seven justices have the final decision. The three Democrats wanted O'Connor's portrait on the Grand Concourse. But the three other Republicans voted against displaying it there.

"I was just being consistent from when I was on the board of governors for the state bar. I, to be honest, didn’t want Tom Moyer’s portrait in this hallway," said Republican Justice Patrick Fischer about O'Connor's predecessor, for whom the Supreme Court building is now named.

"It wasn’t per se the portrait, it was the integrity of the history of the building. And it was the same for Tom."

Fischer said he wanted a special room honoring all women justices in which O’Connor’s portrait would be displayed.

Once again, O’Connor showed her “bring it on” approach, breaking the court’s 3-3 tie to assure that her smiling portrait would go alongside those of what she notes are all unsmiling white men.

"Why I think it’s important to focus on me being the first female is not to pat me on the back, and say, 'wow, that was a great thing you did.' It’s the example that was set — for boys AND girls," she said, noting the field trips of school kids who often visit the court facility on South Front Street.

"All they saw were old white men up on the wall, not a single woman."

In brief, she belongs.

"I don’t concentrate on the fact I am a historical person. I know I am."

And for those who say the grand court hallway is reserved for historic figures?

"History is not static. History is continuously being made. And I’m part of history."

O'Connor wants to help boot elected officials out of the redistricting process

What legacy does she hope to leave?

"I hope people would remember me as someone who was fair and took my responsibility to heart," she said.

"This was my guidepost, my north star for all my career. It’s not about me, it’s never been about me. It’s about what I can do in the position that I hold, with the wonderful staff that I’ve been privileged to have."

While she can't imagine seeking elective office again, O'Connor does want to be involved in revamping the crucial legislative and congressional redistricting process.

"The (November) election was held with an unconstitutional map. That’s what we have here in Ohio: a completely gerrymandered legislature," O'Connor said.

Ohio voters, she added, did not get what they thought they were voting for when they approved constitutional amendments to revamp the process a few years ago.

"From my vantage point, we need a constitutional amendment to reconstitute the redistricting commission, to get politicians out of the redistricting commission, and put in people that are not elected officials and have their representation balanced."

'Yes, there are threats to democracy'

And about those calls to impeach her? She calls them irresponsible political stunts with potentially serious consequences.

"Some legislators were trying to get some attention in calling for my impeachment, and obviously (Rep. Bill) Seitz was interested in the same thing. Some people say that he was the puppet master for that," O'Connor said.

"Don’t these people understand history? Don’t they understand the type of our democracy? And as strong as our democracy is, it becomes very frail when this kind of stuff is voiced.

"They give the wrong kind of impression of what it means to have checks and balances. And they’re doing it for political (reasons), maybe appeal to their base...

"I think it can be a dangerous stunt. I think it reflects very poorly on the members of the legislature that engaged in that."

But it's more than just one judicial figure getting harassed for one set of decisions that go against the party in power. Similar things are happening across the U.S., with the public being deliberately misinformed by political leaders seeking to advance their cause, the outgoing chief justice said.

When asked if she fears for the future of democracy, she replied, "I think that’s up for discussion now."

"Yes, there are threats to democracy. There are threats to the judiciary. I worry about threats to the independence of the judiciary, such as ‘we’re going to impeach you if we don’t like your vote.’ That’s just childish."

But when disgruntled politicians voice such threats, O'Connor said, that's not only "irresponsible" but "dangerous."
