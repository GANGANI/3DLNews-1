Here are the top stories of 2022 on KUTV.com

by Daniel Woodruff, KUTV

Fri, December 30th 2022 at 11:50 AM
Updated Fri, December 30th 2022 at 3:14 PM
12
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
Top 10 stories of 2022 on KUTV.com. (Image: Craig Hart/KUTV)

SALT LAKE CITY (KUTV) — The year 2022 has been busy, and KUTV 2News was there every step of the way with compelling, important coverage of big local, national, and international stories.

Some of those stories that featured prominently this year included high inflation, Utah’s wild housing market, the local impact of the war in Ukraine, the ongoing drought, the overturning of Roe v. Wade, and state lawmakers moving to ban transgender athletes from competing in girls’ sports.

Other stories also captivated the public’s attention. Here’s a countdown of the most popular stories on KUTV.com in 2022:

10. Susan Powell's father 'confident' some of Susan's remains have been discovered

In February, Chuck Cox, whose daughter Susan Powell disappeared from her West Valley City home in 2009, expressed confidence that a search of a West Desert mine may have unearthed some of Susan’s remains. Searchers found bones and a pair of pants at the bottom of a mine. However, a few weeks later, a DNA analysis revealed the bones were not human.

Susan's husband, Josh Powell, has long been suspected in her disappearance and death, with many speculating he disposed of her remains in the desert. However, he was never charged in the case as he killed himself and his two sons in Washington state in 2012.

9. U alumna with children shares shock after seeing topless fans at Utah football game

A graduate of the University of Utah and season ticket holder shared her shock with KUTV 2News about seeing two topless women at a football game at Rice-Eccles Stadium in September.

Melea Johnson said seeing the women whose torsos were covered in body paint was appalling, especially since she had her two young children with her at the game.

8. Salt Lake schools superintendent asked to resign

The KUTV 2News Crisis in the Classroom team led the way reporting on major turmoil in the Salt Lake City School District. In July, reporter Chris Jones broke the news that Dr. Timothy Gadson was being asked to resign after being placed on paid administrative leave. He had been on the job for just one year.

During his tenure, there were allegations of inappropriate workplace behavior, a controversial trip, and questions about some of his hires.

After a lot of back and forth, Gadson ultimately agreed to step down, but he left with a separation agreement worth more than $200,000.

7. Additional murder charge filed against suspect in Utah 'purge' killings

A Utah County man was charged with murder after allegedly telling authorities he heard a voice in his head telling him to “purge the city” before killing two people in July.

According to charging documents, Christian Taele, 28, admitted he beat up Ryan Hooley, 49, before stabbing him to death in a shopping center parking lot in Spanish Fork. Taele told detectives he did not know Hooley.

After killing Hooley, investigators said Taele went to Ogden where he killed Tyler Belinti, 23, at an apartment complex.

6. Oliverson family files lawsuit against Little League Baseball, bunk bed maker after fall

The story of a 12-year-old baseball player from Santa Clara who fell from his bunk bed at the dormitory at the Little League World Series in August quickly captured the country’s attention.

Easton Oliverson was hospitalized in intensive care after sustaining a head injury which required surgery and therapy.

In September, his family announced they were suing Little League Baseball and Savoy Contract Furniture, the bed maker, alleging the bed was “dangerous and defective.”

5. 'Put the baby down!' - Body cam video shows desert hostage standoff with Utah police

KUTV 2News obtained police body camera footage in June that showed a pursuit and standoff that began in St. George and ended in the desert of northwestern Arizona.

The incident, which took place in February, ended when a police sniper shot the suspect, Oscar Alcantara, 30, while he was holding a baby he had allegedly kidnapped.

Alcantara was killed. The baby was not hurt.

4. Lehi charter school hasn't decided fate of teacher at center of TikTok video controversy

A teacher at a charter school in Lehi, Renaissance Academy, came under criticism in May after a video she posted showed her describing a game with students that included conversations about sexuality, angering some parents.

After an hours-long meeting May 11, administrators held off making a decision on Jenna Hall’s employment at the school.

A few days later, the school’s executive director announced Hall’s resignation – which she had previously submitted before the controversy – would take effect immediately.

3. Family of 12-year-old boy who took his own life asks community to teach kindness

A Tooele County family shared their heartbreaking story after their 12-year-old son, Drayke Hardman, took his own life after he was bullied at school.

His parents spoke with KUTV 2News and said they took all the steps they could to try to resolve the problem. They pleaded for kindness and urged other parents to watch for signs of aggression in their children.

Several Utah Jazz players reached out to Drayke’s family to offer condolences and even donated money for his funeral expenses.

2. Students protest outside school after dozens of girls not allowed in homecoming dance

Students gathered outside American Leadership Academy, a charter school in Spanish Fork, after the weekend of the school’s homecoming dance where students said nearly 60 girls were kicked out because of their dresses. Some students said they felt judged by their body types.

However, school administrators released a statement refuting the students’ claims, saying the number of students turned away was 14, and six of them later returned to the dance. Still, administrators said they “regret that any student interpreted the enforcement of the dress code as an intent to body shame any of the students.”

1.15 million pounds of concrete poured overnight at Utah construction site

That’s a lot of concrete.

Our most popular story of the year involved Layton Construction crews pouring truckloads and truckloads of concrete July 16 at the site of a new high-rise residential building at 200 East and 300 South in Salt Lake City.

According to the company, it took 11 hours to pour the concrete in what they said was one of the longest continuous concrete pours in state history.
