State takes control of Providence Public Schools

by ASHLEY CULLINANE, NBC 10 NEWS

Fri, November 1st 2019 at 7:12 AM
Updated Fri, November 1st 2019 at 6:57 PM
Interim Providence school superintendent Fran Gallo and Rhode Island Education Commissioner Angelica Infante-Green speak to reporters on the first day of the state's takeover of city schools, Friday, Nov. 1, 2019. (WJAR)

PROVIDENCE, R.I. (WJAR) — "What happens on Nov. 1?"

That's the question NBC 10 News has been asked by parents, students, and even employees of Providence Public Schools.

The state intervention began Friday, during which the state will assume control of the school budget, program and personnel of the district's 41 schools.

Major changes during the intervention, which is expected to last at least five years, will include changes to curriculum, school culture and the potential closures of crumbling schools, Education Commissioner Angelica Infante-Green said.

While 'change does not happen overnight', the state has said, Interim Superintendent Fran Gallo said the first day of the intervention matters.

"Day one does matter. Day one puts us all in a direct communication with the commissioner, brings her to the district much more frequently, provides some staff we have been lacking and definitely makes a difference in how we communicate, and how the general academic leadership moves forward and what they roll out," Gallo said. "I think we'll see a difference day one."

As made clear in Providence, the decision to take control of a school district comes with heavy criticism and fears of what intervention will truly bring.

This year, the New Jersey Board of Education ended a contentious state takeover that lasted about 22 years, though the state had been keeping a close watch on the troubled city schools for years.

Another New Jersey school district, Paterson, was under state control for nearly 30 years, during which the state fired the district superintendent was fired, dissolved the school board and demoted top district administrators.

In an interview with NBC 10 Thursday, Gallo said – though not under her purview – she does not anticipate major personnel changes, but rather some reorganizing.

"My job while I was here and while I am here is to really thin out central office, to move, to really not push people out the door, but push people into schools," she said. "We have a significantly large central office staff and it's really important the expertise of teaching shouldn't be in central office. It should be at the elbows of teachers in the classroom. If we have coaches here, or special supervisors here, who really could provide so much on-the-ground support, then they need not to be here, but rather in the schools."

The school system will also begin the state intervention without a turnaround superintendent. On Oct. 15 and again on Oct. 22, Infante-Green said she was negotiating a contract with one candidate and they would be hired 'very shortly'.

RIDE's spokesperson said this week should the candidate not take the job, Infante-Green is in talks with other potential superintendents.

"It was fully understood that there could be by November 1, someone in place,” Gallo said. "That seems like a hard time of year to get a permanent replacement. I think as the year progresses, we'll see much more and we'll hear more about -- I call him a 'super supe' -- I believe the right term is turnaround superintendent – I do believe we will have a strong turnaround superintendent very soon."

Infante-Green has been tight-lipped about who the candidate is, but said they are not local.

Meanwhile, Gallo's plans are a bit more uncertain.

"My expectation is that I will keep my promise and be here for 90 days," she said.

Gallo's 90 days means 90 school days she is present for, which would come to an end Jan. 9.

"I'm not sure I'm staying on a consultant. That all depends. That all depends on the 'super supe'. He may not need anybody to stay on. To certainly transition, well, of course, walk around, get to know everybody -- that’s a different thing. To literally stay on as a bird in his ear, I don't think so. I think when this new person comes in, he's going to be ready to go."

The Center for Justice, which filed a legal motion on behalf of students for requesting a formal role in takeover plans, applauded the commissioner's final order.

RIDE said it plans to hold a news conference at 8 a.m. Friday.
