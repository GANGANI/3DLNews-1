Number of flu cases spike across New York state this week

by CNYCentral

Thu, December 27th 2018 at 4:29 PM

    TOPICS:

    Animal VirologyFlu SeasonHealthInfluenzaInfluenza PandemicMedicineMicrobiologyRTT

Cases of influenza in New York state have seen a sharp rise across the state in recent weeks, Gov. Andrew Cuomo's office said on Thursday.

The number of flu cases rose by 72 percent over the past week and new cases were reported in 58 out of 62 counties.

The governor's office said the number of patients hospitalized with confirmed influenza was 363 — a 41 percent increase from last week.

"With an increase in flu cases in every corner of this state, I want to remind New Yorkers who have not been vaccinated against the flu that it is not too late to take this critical action," Gov. Cuomo said. "Flu season is in full-swing, and as the number of cases and hospitalizations continue to rise, we must do everything we can to protect ourselves and our loved ones."

According to the New York State Department of Health, the number of influenza cases reported this week in central New York are:

  • Onondaga County: 9
  • Cayuga County: 4
  • Oswego County: 11
  • Cortland County: 1
  • Tompkins County: 1
  • Madison County: 3
  • Oneida County: 10

Last week, State Health Commissioner Dr. Howard Zucker declared that influenza is prevalent in New York State. This announcement put into effect a regulation requiring that healthcare workers who are not vaccinated against influenza wear surgical or procedure masks in areas where patients are typically present.

"We strongly encourage anyone who has not yet gotten the flu vaccine to get one immediately to combat this significant threat to public health," Zucker said. "Getting vaccinated protects you as well as the people around you, including those who are more vulnerable, like babies and young children, elderly people and people with chronic health conditions."

In addition, it's important to practice good hand-hygiene.

  • Unlike some viruses, influenza is easily killed by soap and hot water.
  • Wash your hands often with soap and hot water for at least 20 seconds to protect yourself from germs and avoid spreading them to others.
  • Carry an alcohol-based hand sanitizer to use when soap and water are not available. Choose a product with at least 60 percent alcohol.
  • Do not cough or sneeze into your hands. Instead, cover your mouth and nose with a tissue. People with the flu are infectious for up to 7 days after symptoms begin.
