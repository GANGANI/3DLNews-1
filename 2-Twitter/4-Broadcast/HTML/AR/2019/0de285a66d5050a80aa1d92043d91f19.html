Little Rock doctor accused of using own sperm for artificial insemination; lawsuit filed

by Marine Glisovic

Thu, November 7th 2019 at 6:27 AM
Updated Fri, November 8th 2019 at 10:13 AM
5
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
A Little Rock man who was looking for the missing pieces to his family tree has made a shocking discovery (Photo: KATV)

LITTLE ROCK, Ark. (KATV) - A Little Rock man who was looking for the missing pieces to his family tree has made a shocking discovery.

Matthew Smith's journey has caused heartache for his family. Not only did they figure out that his dad is not his biological father, the lawsuit the Smith family filed claims the doctor they sought help from for artificial insemination used his own sperm.

About a year ago, Matthew Smith's curiosity led him to a point of no return.

"I was very close to my grandfather, but I didn't know any farther up the family tree than that," Smith said.

Smith turned to multiple genealogy sites like 23andMe in hopes of connecting his family tree. But, he couldn't match his DNA to his dad Steven.

"It came back and it didn't show us being related in any way," Smith explained.

After multiple attempts, the family shrugged it off assuming Steven's chemo treatments altered his DNA.

"That they had had trouble conceiving and had done the partner insemination for me to be born, but that didn't throw any red flags or anything," Smith said.

Upon further research, Smith came to a shocking discovery.

"Any resource I could use I did," Smith said. "But I started looking at the family trees. I started seeing names that kept reappearing in family trees. So, when I saw those names I started tracing their lines down and they only match in one point."

When asked where that was, Smith said, "Dr. Wood."

Dr. Gary Wood, a Little Rock OBGYN, still has an Arkansas medical license. The man the Smith family used for artificial insemination treatment 40 years ago and now the lawsuit filed claiming the doctor used his own sperm.

"I would like to know why he did it," Rebecca Smith, Matthew's mother, said. "Why? Because, you know, if he didn't think that there was a possibility for Steven and I to do using his sperm, why didn't he tell us this? Why didn't he give us some options? Why didn't he let us make the decision?"

They now want a legal decision.

"You feel dirty in a way," Rebecca said. "I know there was nothing I could have done but you just feel violated."

Matthew Smith reached out to Dr. Wood hoping to keep it out of the courts, but the response he says he got through an email was pure denial.

Wood reportedly claimed he had a vasectomy years before Smith was born. But, he also had no intention of submitting his DNA. In the email, Wood reportedly added that all records from his practice had been destroyed.

"It could be fixed immediately," said attorney Chip Baker. "All he has to do is submit DNA and there are lots of places he can do it. As long as we know it's his DNA, we're perfectly willing to live with that result."

Baker says there is no criminal statute on the books in Arkansas to go after Wood, but filing a civil suit will help get the paternity test the family is seeking.

KATV reached out to Dr. Wood via email and phone but have not heard back.

A previous version of this story incorrectly identified the medical procedure that led to Smith's mother becoming pregnant.
