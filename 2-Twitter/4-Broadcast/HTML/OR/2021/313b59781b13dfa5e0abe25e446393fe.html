Review: 'The King's Man' reminds me that I don't remember what I liked about 'Kingsman'

by Ryan Painter

Tue, December 21st 2021 at 1:21 PM
Updated Tue, December 21st 2021 at 1:24 PM

The King’s Man
2.5 out of 5 Stars
Director: Matthew Vaughn
Writers: Matthew Vaughn, Karl Gajdusek, Mark Millar
Starring: Ralph Fiennes, Gemma Arterton, Rhys Ifans
Genre: Action, Comedy
Rated: R for sequences of strong/bloody violence, language, and some sexual material

SALT LAKE CITY (KUTV) – Synopsis: With the fate of the world in the balance, Orlando Oxford forms a secret organization known as “The King’s Men” to neutralize a shadow organization intent on pushing the world into war.

Review: It might sound like snark, but I’m struggling to remember what it was that I liked about 2014’s “Kingsman: The Secret Service.” I know that I disliked 2017’s “Kingsman: The Golden Circle” because it abandoned the British sensibilities of the original in favor of something more American. “The King’s Man,” a prequel set in “the early years of the 20th century,” is decidedly more British and it really doesn’t work for me at all.

With an advertising campaign that highlight’s the film’s irreverent behavior, you might expect an action film that ramps up the violence and avoids political correctness (or British properness) in a way that is intended to push boundaries and offend audiences across the political spectrum. “The King’s Man” isn’t that bold. It’s not even that much of an action film until the final act.

That’s not to say that the film doesn’t have its shocking moments. It does. They just don’t have any lasting power. Nothing sticks. I know that I should be emotionally invested in Orland Oxford (Ralph Fiennes) and his desire to protect his son Conrad (Harris Dickinson). I’m not. I know I’m intended find the way the narrative weaves in and out of actual world history clever. I don’t. Maybe I’m exhausted by real-world conspiracy theories to the point where I can’t enjoy the fictionalized ones.

Again, I find myself asking, “What did I like about the original film?” “Why do I expect to be amused and feel disappointed when I’m not?” “When will this movie actually be about the Kingsman organization?” "How seriously am I supposed to take this?" “Shouldn’t I be feeling a sense of anticipation?” “Isn’t this supposed to be fun?” “Do I even care about the Kingsman enough to sit through its origin story?”

Enjoying a film shouldn’t be this difficult.
