Housing, drought relief, illegal cannabis suppression to be discussed at special session

by Frances Lin

Mon, December 13th 2021 at 4:43 AM
Updated Mon, December 13th 2021 at 5:07 AM
Oregon Capitol. (KATU)

PORTLAND, Ore. — Oregon lawmakers will head to the state capitol for a special session Monday, December 13.

"Successful special sessions line up all the votes and what they’re going to vote on before the session starts. And it looks like that’s the case right now," said Jim Moore, political analyst.

The urgent needs of some Oregonians will be front and center of the discussion.

One of the pressing topics that could have immediate implications are winter evictions.

Political experts said the housing funding proposal is expected to pass. And if it does, it could take some pressure off of tenants who are at risk for eviction right now.

Representative Julie Fahey, chair of the Housing Committee, said more than 10,000 Oregonians are at risk of eviction.

"The reason for this is that there have been a basically a road block in terms of getting all of the people who have had rent issues because of COVID through the system," Moore said.

"Even a short period of homeless or housing instability can do generational damage," said representative Fahey, "we don’t think anyone should be evicted while assistance is on the way to help them.”

$215 million is proposed to prevent winter evictions, "it’ll help about 8,000 families that are facing eviction right now," Moore said.

As for drought relief, "a big part of that is to go towards those people who are affected by the heat dome," Moore explained, "and so, people who’ve had longtime agricultural businesses there. Another crucial part goes to something that’s been building for a number of years, there’s a water crisis in the climate basin in south-central Oregon."

If passed, $100 million will be provided.

Both housing and drought relief are expected to pass.

"There could even be a deal, republican votes may go for the rental assistance one, if they’re a democratic votes, to go for the agricultural one, to deal with the drought," explained Moore.

As for illegal cannabis suppression, "we’ve now legalized marijuana. And so there are now legal places where that happens and yet there’s still the illegal cannabis grows going on out in the woods and things like this," Moore said, "it’s impacting our market for legal marijuana, but it’s also having an effect on law enforcement, they need more funds so they can put more resources into cracking down on these illegal grows.”

If passed, $25 million will be provided for a statewide plan.

And as for the Afghan refugee resettlement, "a lot of states across the country with the end of the u.S. Occupation in Afghanistan and pulling out, have opened up their doors, saying we will take Afghan refugees," Moore said.

If passed, $18 million will be allocated to help local groups with those efforts, "so it would go for instance to help churches, who do that anyway, to help them expand what they’re doing, to help local agencies in different counties and things like that, to open more doors, get more refugees in here," said Moore.

The special session starts at 8:00 a.m. Monday morning.

Anyone can introduce something not on the agenda.

KATU will be following this special session.
