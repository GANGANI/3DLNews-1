Full Video: Both sides give views on Amendment 2 at Eyewitness News Town Hall

by STAFF REPORTS

Sen. Eric Tarr, R-Putnam, chair of the state Senate Finance Committee, pictured at left, and Kanawha County Commission President Kent Carper participate Monday in a Town Hall debate on Amendment 2. (WCHS)

CHARLESTON, W.Va. (WCHS) — With the public debate about Amendment 2 raging, Eyewitness News hosted an online Town Hall where both sides on the issue made their case about the proposed constitutional amendment on the Nov. 8 ballot in West Virginia.

The “Your Voice, Your Future” Amendment 2 Debate Town Hall streamed at 7:30 p.m. Monday, Oct. 24, on wchstv.com and on the station's Facebook page.

Video of the Town Hall can be viewed below or click here if you are on mobile device.

Participants in the Town Hall were Sen. Eric Tarr, R-Putnam, chair of the Senate Finance Committee, who is urging passage of the amendment, and Kanawha County Commissioner Kent Carper, who is speaking out strongly against the amendment.

Eyewitness News Lead Political Reporter Kennie Bass was the moderator.

Amendment 2 would give the Legislature the authority to regulate property taxes, which the state Constitution currently gives to the state’s 55 counties as a guaranteed revenue stream.

If voters pass Amendment 2, the state Senate GOP leadership has said it wants to eliminate the tax on personal automobiles and on machinery and equipment used in the operation of a business.

Approving the amendment does not automatically authorize any tax cuts. That move would still need to go through legislative action.

Carper and representatives of many other county governments have argued that doing away with the guaranteed funding stream for counties would wreck their budgets. The Senate GOP says it has a plan to replace the money for counties, and they actually would get additional funding.

Gov. Jim Justice has spoken out against the amendment, saying it would kill his plan to reduce the state’s income tax. Tarr and Senate President Craig Blair support the amendment, saying it would grow the state's economy.

Justice has said a constitutional amendment is not required to get rid of the car tax, and he has a plan to get rid of the car tax without a constitutional amendment. He said residents would receive a rebate. Senate leaders say Justice’s car tax plan would not eliminate the tax, is confusing and is not really a rebate.
