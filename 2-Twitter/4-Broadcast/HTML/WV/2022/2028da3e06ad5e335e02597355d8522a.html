NTSB investigator says plane cleared for landing moments before deadly Marietta crash

by JEFF MORRIS

Wed, October 19th 2022 at 8:29 AM
Updated Thu, October 20th 2022 at 8:03 AM
3
VIEW ALL PHOTOS
“We are going to be looking at the human, the machine and the environment,” Aaron McCarter with the National Transportation Safety Board says Wednesday during a news conference in Marietta, Ohio. (WCHS)

MARIETTA, Ohio (WCHS) — An investigator for the National Transportation Safety Board said the pilots of a plane in a deadly crash in Marietta, Ohio, had reported no problems and had been cleared for landing at the Parkersburg airport about 40 seconds before witnesses described seeing the aircraft sharply descend and crash.

“We are going to be looking at the human, the machine and the environment,” Aaron McCarter with the federal agency said Wednesday at a news conference in Marietta.

Video of the news conference can be viewed below. Click here if you are on the Eyewitness News app.

Eric S. Seevers, 45, of Parkersburg and Timothy F. Gifford, 49, of Orient, Ohio, were killed when their 1974 Beechcraft King Air E90 crashed about 7 a.m. Tuesday into the parking lot of the Pioneer Buick GMC dealership on Pike Street, the Ohio State Highway Patrol said.

Multiple vehicles and buildings were damaged.

"Our thoughts and prayers go out to the friends and family of the two pilots onboard," McCarter said.

McCarter said a team of investigators and representatives of the manufacturer of the aircraft and technical advisers from Canada from the company where the engine was manufactured are at the scene.

The lead investigator said the goal is to have a preliminary report completed in 10 days with the basic facts that are gathered at the scene. McCarter said he hopes to have a factual report drafted within nine to 12 months with a probable cause of the crash and then have the NTSB sign off on it.

“The end game is to determine the probable cause. We determine the cause so we can make recommendations, so this doesn’t happen again,” McCarter said.

Investigators will be looking at the pilots’ background to find out their level of experience and how familiar they were with the aircraft and the area in which they were flying.

McCarter said they also will be doing a weather study. He said there were some reports of icing in the area, and he wants to look at the deicing capabilities of the plane.

The plane did not have a black box, but he said there was equipment in the cockpit that could provide useful data if the equipment survived the crash. A drone is being used to provide mapping of the scene and details about the crash.

The NTSB has been able to obtain several videos of the crash, and McCarter asked anyone in the public who has video, saw or heard the crash to step forward and contact the federal agency because the information could be useful in determining the cause of the crash.
