Missouri Missing Christmas Trees remember those missed over the holidays

by Elise Person

NEW BLOOMFIELD — Through what she calls “labors of love”, Marianne Asher Chapman, founder of the local nonprofit organization, Missouri Missing, uses Christmas tree ornaments inspired by her children to remember those who are not home with their families this holiday season.

Almost ten years ago, Chapman was inspired by her children’s homemade ornaments from 1980 to begin making her own ornaments as a way to remember those who have gone missing. After Chapman’s daughter had gone missing 18 years ago, she said she felt compelled to care for those missing ever since then.

As her Christmas tree ornament tradition continued, her Trees of Love and Hope grew to become more.

“It started out with just missing people, or missing and found deceased or whatever. But then after a while, I started adding other people that we just miss,” Chapman said.

Chapman has ornaments for many members of her family that have passed away over the years, as well as for many others who have passed away. However, Chapman said the trees are primarily for those who have gone missing.

“So, I have the big tree for the adults, but I’ve always had the folk-art tree for the children. Some of the children, they’re missing with their parent. So, I hang those adults with their child on the children’s tree because I just can’t separate them,” Chapman said.

With the names of over 200 people on her two Christmas trees, Chapman has made ornaments for those who have gone missing both locally and nationally.

“We have Carl DeBrodie, he went missing a few years ago. Of course, little Darnell Gray. Really the children’s tree is really way important to me,” Chapman said.

She tries her best to share photos of the ornaments with the individual’s families and said she hopes this tradition will bring some peace to those who are missing a loved one over the holiday season.

“Hopefully, it will just make them feel a little bit better. That is my intention. I know that if somebody made something with my daughter’s name on it, I would probably cry, I would feel so touched by that,” Chapman said.

Each year, Chapman takes requests for ornaments from Dec. 1 through Dec. 20.


Chapman said she will keep her Christmas Trees of Love and Hope up until the start of the new year.
