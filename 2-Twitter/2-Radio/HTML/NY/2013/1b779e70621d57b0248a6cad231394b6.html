Syracuse defense will have to contend with Penn State's deep talent pool at tight end

  • Published: Aug. 28, 2013, 11:30 a.m.
By
  • Nate Mink | nmink@syracuse.com

Penn St Football.JPG

Jesse James (left) and the Penn State tight ends are a big part of Bill O'Brien's offense. Syracuse's defense must limit their production in Saturday's season opener at MetLife Stadium.

(AP Photo/Centre Daily Times, Nabil K. Mark)

Syracuse, N.Y. — For decades Penn State has been known as Linebacker U, a fertile program filled with top collegiate talent at the position.

But in just his second season, Penn State coach Bill O'Brien has transformed another position into one of the most potent in the Big Ten, and it's quickly starting to gain national focus.

Can Penn State stake a claim to Tight End U?

That may be a bit premature, but it's trending that way with the stockpile of threats at that position. Where Syracuse sees its strength in numbers at running back, Penn State has similar depth at tight end, making it one of the biggest matchups to watch Saturday when the Orange square off against the Nittany Lions at MetLife Stadium.

"They're playmakers," junior linebacker Dyshawn Davis said. "Basically, they rely on them for everything on the offense."

Here are the names to remember for Saturday:

  • Kyle Carter, a first-team All-Big Ten selection last season, is well past recovering from his season-ending wrist surgery and poses a challenge to the Syracuse defense with his speed, hands and ability to stretch the field. He caught 36 passes for 453 yards and two touchdowns in nine games.
  • Jesse James, a sophomore, came on strong in the second half of last season, grabbing 13 passes for 274 yards and four touchdowns over the final six weeks of the season. In those six games, he recorded long gains of 31, 35, 42 and 41. James, 6-foot-7, 257 pounds, carries his weight well and will be a tough cover given his size and speed.

James and Carter were the two players both Davis and defensive coordinator Chuck Bullough specifically mentioned on Tuesday night when asked about the tight ends.

You can be sure Bullough has circled No. 87 (Carter) and No. 18 (James).

Matt Lehman is former walk-on who, at 6-foot-6, 262 pounds, is another red zone target for whoever lines up at quarterback for Penn State. He finished with 24 receptions, 296 yards and three touchdowns in 2012.

That trio collectively is one of the best units in the Big Ten, maybe even the country, and that's not even counting freshman Adam Breneman, rated by Rivals as one of the top 3 tight ends in the 2013 recruiting class. O'Brien has said that Breneman will play this year.

Has Bullough, who spent the previous two years in the NFL coaching linebackers for the Cleveland Browns, ever seen a grouping this loaded?

"Yeah," he said, a smile starting to form, "when we played the Patriots."

Of course, O'Brien was the former offensive coordinator in New England and deployed Rob Gronkowski and Aaron Hernandez to form one of the best combos in the league.

It was widely expected that O'Brien, in implementing the Patriots offense at Penn State, would prominently feature the tight end.

He did.

"That's one of the tougher positions in our program to learn, second only to quarterback, especially on offense I'm talking about," O'Brien told reporters earlier this month. "These guys are involved in all different facets of the game. They're involved in the running game, they're involved in protections sometimes, and obviously they're involved in the passing game."

Identifying where the tight ends line up is only half the battle for Syracuse on Saturday. O'Brien has mixed them in the backfield, in the slot and out wide.

Then the ball is snapped, leaving the defense with the second half of the task: Stopping Penn State's most potent position.
